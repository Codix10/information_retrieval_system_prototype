Despite the threat of global warming, few studies have investigated inter- and intra-specific thermoregulatory variation in bats at high temperatures. Those from Europe and Africa suggest that individuals from warmer roosts tolerate higher temperatures than those from comparatively cooler roosts. However, because comparable data are lacking, it remains unclear whether these patterns apply more generally. We predicted that bats occupying cooler roosts (e.g., tree-cavities) would initiate evaporative cooling at lower air temperatures compared to those from warmer roosts (e.g., bark). We also predicted that, compared to males, females would: (a) have higher heat tolerance limits, and (b) be more conservative with water due to their warmer roost preferences during the breeding season. We conducted flow-through respirometry experiments in an Australian semi-arid ecosystem to make inter-specific comparisons of males from five bat species (Chalinolobus gouldii,Vespadelus vulturnus,Nyctophilus geoffroyi,N. corbeni, andOzimops petersi), and intra-specific thermoregulatory patterns withinC. gouldii,V. vulturnusandN. geoffroyi. Our results indicate that both inter and intra-specifically, the use of warmer, thermally unbuffered roosts is positively correlated with higher heat tolerance limits and greater evaporative cooling capacities. Understanding this variation can improve conservation decisions in ecosystems under the greatest threat of global warming.

In arid environments, many small endotherms (mammals and birds) regularly contend with the risk of dehydration and hyperthermia1–3. The high surface area to volume ratios of small endotherms, facilitates high heat and water exchange with the environment, particularly when environmental temperatures exceed body temperatures (Tb3–5;). During such times, many small endotherms employ behavioural thermoregulation to minimise exogenous heat gain (e.g., burrowing6,7; tree-hugging8; and shade seeking9,10. Nocturnal species such as bats that rest in tree-hollows or under bark during the heat of the day, however, have limited capacity for behavioural thermoregulation.
At high air temperatures (Ta) and roost temperatures (Troost), bats rely on varying physiological thermoregulation strategies to avoid hyperthermia and dehydration11,12. For example, in southern Africa where certainTroost= 50–60 °C, some bats can dissipate 121–227% of their metabolic heat through evaporative cooling13,14. Alternatively, Commerson’s leaf-nosed bats (Macronycteris commersoni) uses torpor to decrease endogenous heat production at highTareducing the need for evaporative cooling15,16. In deserts, water conservation is critical, and to reduce evaporative water loss (EWL), some bats use facultative hyperthermia (skin temperatures [Tskin] > 45 °C) or delay the onset of active evaporative cooling until high temperatures (e.g.,Ta> 42°C17,18).
Thermoregulatory patterns and heat tolerance of bats can vary due to geographic distribution, and roost choice19–22. Species confined to arid habitats maintain lower EWL and metabolic rates compared to species with broader distributions21,23. For example, at highTaa desert specialist batOtonycteris hemprichiiuses 30–50% less water for cooling than more widely distributed sympatric species22. Roost microclimate also influences thermoregulatory strategies. In southern Africa, three bat species that occupy caves and cave-like structures initiated evaporative cooling at lowerTathan three that occupy comparatively warmer roosts14. Further, during highTa,Sauromys petrophilus, a bat roosting in exposed rock crevices whereTroostexceedsTa, maintains lower resting metabolic rates (RMR) and produce less endogenous heat thanNycteris thebaica, a bat roosting in well buffered tree cavities19.
Thermal physiology can also vary intra-specifically, particularly when the sexes experience different selection pressures18,24. For example, during the summer reproductive season, male bats often roost solitarily or in small groups25,26. In contrast, females select warm microsites to form large maternity colonies where their endogenous heat production further increases theTroostby > 7°C27–29. In addition to the comparatively higherTroostduring the breeding season, females must allocate water reserves for lactation30,31and this may impose evolutionary pressures for higher heat tolerance and water conservation. To date, females from six bat species have been recorded to delay active evaporative cooling until higherTacompared to males18,32,33. In Australia, femaleNyctophilus geoffroyialso maintain lower RMR and thus lower energy expenditure at highTacompared to males18. However, very few studies have investigated inter- and intra-specific differences in bat thermoregulation and more studies are needed to establish whether the observed patterns are widespread.
Some multispecies comparisons of bat thermal physiology at highTaexist from Africa and Europe14,33, but none from Australia. Therefore, we investigated the inter- and intra-specific thermoregulatory patterns of insectivorous tree-roosting bats inhabiting the same semi-arid ecosystem in Australia:Chalinolobus gouldii,Vespadelus vulturnus,N. geoffroyi,N. corbeni, andOzimops petersi. Although all five species are classified as tree-roosting,C. gouldii,V. vulturnus, andO. petersiare considered obligate arboreal cavity dwellers17,34,35. The exfoliating bark roosts preferred byN. geoffroyiandN. corbeni36are less buffered fromTa, with higher maximumTroostand diurnalTroostfluctuations than tree hollows37–39, allowing for comparisons along fine scale ecological differences.
Using flow-through respirometry experiments to measure physiological variables [RMR and EWL] at highTa, and recording subcutaneous temperature (Tsub) during the experiments, we tested the hypothesis that variation in roosting and reproductive ecology would be reflected in thermal physiology. Inter-specifically, we predicted that species using more unbuffered roosts (e.g., bark), and likely exposed to greater fluctuations in diurnalTroost, would tolerate higher experimental temperatures than those that use buffered roosts (e.g., hollows). Intra-specifically, we predicted that females, using warmer maternity roosts, would possess higher heat tolerance limits and have a more conservative water economy than males. By using these data, we aimed to understand the vulnerability of these sympatric species to heatwaves that are predicted to increase in both severity and frequency in the future as a function of global climate change40.

We provide detailed data on the heat tolerance and thermoregulatory strategies of Australian insectivorous bats from a semi-arid ecosystem. Our results suggest that heat tolerance and evaporative cooling capacity vary among the five tree-roosting species, and between sexes. We found support for our prediction that females have a higher heat tolerance limits and prioritise the conservation of water. In particular, femaleV. vulturnusandN. geoffroyisignificantly delayed the onset of evaporative cooling until higher temperatures than males.
The maximumTsuband experimentalTas tolerated by the study species are similar to values reported for species occupying warm ecosystems and microsites14,18and thereby indicate adaptations to tolerate high environmental and roost temperatures. In Australian deserts, free livingN. geoffroyiandO. petersihave been recorded defendingTskinof 44.9 °C and 45.8 °C, respectively17,18. The highestTsubwe measured forO. petersiandN. geoffroyiwas similar to data from free-ranging bats, which adds to the evidence that these bats rely on facultative hyperthermia (i.e., the voluntary elevation ofTbabove normothermic levels)   in parts of their range17,18. This thermoregulatory strategy facilitates water conservation and reduces dehydration risk, critical in arid environments41,42.
AlthoughV. vulturnuswere the least tolerant to high temperatures they still maintained a maximumTsubcomparable to the most heat tolerant speciesN. corbeniillustrating the species’ tendency to rely on facultative hyperthermia as a thermoregulatory strategy17,43. Interestingly,V. vulturnus’ pattern ofTsubincreasing linearly withTais similar to a thermoconforming pattern, which has been observed in other small bats such asPipistrellus pipistrellusand maleP. pygmaeus33. The linear increase inTsubwithTaand reliance on facultative hyperthermia could be due toV. vulturnus’ low mass, and consequent high surface area/volume ratio, facilitating increased environmental heat gain whenTa>Tband consequently challenges defendingTb3,5. Allometrically, the water flux in endotherms is positively correlated withMb, thus the water reserves ofV. vulturnusare comparatively lower44,45while their mass specific EWL is higher than larger species46. In other words, forV. vulturnus, it may be unfeasible to mitigate the high heat influx using evaporative cooling because of extremely high surface area/volume and low water reserves44–46. AtTa= 40 °CV. vulturnushad the second lowest mass specific EWL (10.9 mg H2O g− 1h− 1), but at its HTL of 44 °C, there was a fourfold increase in mass specific EWL (45.2 mg H2O g− 1h− 1). Thus, forV. vulturnus, delaying the onset of active evaporative cooling reduces the risk of dehydration at the expense of relying on facultative hyperthermia and risking deleterious hyperthermia. This pattern of heat tolerance is worth investigating in other very small endotherms, particularly regarding a potential thresholdMbat which thermoconforming becomes favoured over thermoregulating.
The five species we tested are classified as “tree-roosting” but can be broadly subcategorised as roosting within cavities (C. gouldii,V. vulturnus, andO. petersi) or under exfoliating bark (N. geoffroyiandN. corbeni)17,35,36,47. During summer,Troosts under bark are generally higher during the day and less stable than those in tree cavities38,48due to bark’s low insulative properties49,50, and high degree of solar exposure51. Species using cooler, stable roosts have lower RMR inflection points and also exhibit higher metabolic water production at a givenTacompared to species from warmer microhabitats14,19,52. To the best of our knowledge,O. petersiexclusively roost in tree cavities35,53and this species exhibited the lowest inflection point for RMR, suggesting that it begins to spend energy for thermoregulation at lowTas. It may be that, of the five tree roosting species we investigated,O. petersiprefers cavities that are cooler and well buffered during the heat of the day35,54and therefore faces comparatively lower selection pressure for contending with acutely high or fluctuating daytimeTroosts37–39. The correlation between cooler, stable roosts and thermal physiology is further reinforced by our inter-specific EWL results with the EWL inflection point ofO. petersibeing the lowest we recorded. AtTa= 40 °C, a 10 gN. geoffroyiwould only use 90.7% of the water used by the similar sizedO. petersi. As opposed toN. geoffroyithat likely contends with higher diurnal temperature fluctuations during summer due to their exposed bark roosts,O. petersiis likely exposed to consistently lower diurnalTroostand thus begins evaporative cooling at lower temperatures. Similar patterns of a lower EWL inflectionTaand a greater EWL rate at relatively lowTas have been reported inN. thebaicaoccupying a cooler, well-insulated roost compared toS. petrophilusoccupying a comparatively warmer roost19.
AtTas > 42.9 °C, all species we tested could dissipate more heat than they produced; another characteristic of bats occupying warm ecosystems13,14. Within these five species, the obligate cavity roostingO. petersidissipated 100% of its MHP byTa= 39.5 °C, indicating its use of evaporative cooling at relatively lowTroosts - a phenomenon observed in species occupying cooler roosts even in warm ecosystems14,20. However, not all cavity roosting species conformed to the pattern of lower EWL inflectionTaand a greater EWL rate at relatively lowTas. AlthoughC. gouldiiandV. vulturnusare cavity roosting species36,47, they exhibited the two highest inflectionTas for EWL, meaning they tolerate the highestTabefore the onset of evaporative cooling. ForC. gouldii, this could be due to greater water reserves due to higherMb. In mammals, the total body water reserves scale positively withMb56, allowing them to potentially delay evaporative cooling until highTa, after which they have large amounts of water available to dissipate MHP. Alternatively, unlike other obligate cavity roosting species,C. gouldiireadily use artificial bat boxes that can often exceed the temperatures of natural roosts12,56. In fact, in urban areas, boxes are used almost exclusively byC. gouldii, suggesting that, they can effectively tolerate high temperatures57. It would be worthwhile to compare the heat tolerance and evaporative cooling capacities ofC. gouldiiacross a latitudinal gradient to elucidate the extent of their physiological flexibility in the heat.
Previous studies indicate that, perhaps due to variation in selection pressures pertaining to reproductive ecology, some female bats tolerate higherTas than males18,33. In the three species that we had appropriate sample sizes to allow inter-sexual comparisons (C. gouldii,V. vulturnus, andN. geoffroyi), the percentage of females (36–70%) tolerating the maximum experimentalTawas always higher than males (14–42%), similar toNyctalus noctula,P. pygmaeus, andP. pipistrellus from Poland33and a population of desert adaptedN. geoffroyi, where 45% of females, but only 16% males, could tolerateTa= 46 °C18. Being able to tolerate higher temperatures could be advantageous to females when contending with the inherently high temperatures of maternity roosts and the heat generated by gestation/lactation, and the other members of the maternity colony27,29. At higherTas, femaleN. geoffroyiincreasedTsubmore gradually compared to males, a pattern similar toN. noctulafrom Poland, an arid population ofEptesicus fuscusfrom Canada and other populations ofN. geoffroyi18,32,33,58. This supports the notion that females may face strong selection pressures to minimise metabolic heat production and effectively dissipate it during highTas, allowing for water and energy conservation while lowering the risk of hyperthermia18,33.
In both femaleV. vulturnusandN. geoffroyi, the onset of evaporative cooling began at a significantly higherTathan in males. Furthermore, femaleV. vulturnusmaintain EWL 13–18% lower than males across all experimentalTas above inflection points. Lower EWL rates by females have been reported inL. cinereusuntil 40 °C61and inL. noctivagansacross all experimental temperatures20. Maintaining lower EWL rates allows female bats to conserve their body water reserves and allocate it towards lactation20,58. FemaleN. geoffroyimaintained a lower EWL rate than males untilTa= 40 °C but then increased it significantly above the males’ at higherTas; a pattern absent in femaleC. gouldiiandV. vulturnus. In addition to the need to allocate water towards lactation, femaleN. geoffroyialso contend with highTroosts18,25,28. Thus, femaleN. geoffroyiface selection to conserve water until all other avenues of thermoregulation have been exhausted, at which point they have to use high EWL rates to dissipate heat and avoid lethal hyperthermia. Furthermore, this similarity in the intra-specific thermoregulatory strategy between three geographically distinctN. geoffroyipopulations (the Sturt desert ~ 560 km and the Northern Tablelands ~ 1000 km from this study site)18,58, suggests similar selective pressures on females across populations. In the case of males, increased evaporative cooling from lowTas could be to mitigate chronic heat exposure related deleterious effects to sperm quality60.
We provide further evidence that thermoregulatory patterns, and physiological adaptations differ both inter- and intra-specifically in bats, while adding support to the contention that females have higher heat tolerance and better conserve water reserves than males, likely as a result of varying reproductive pressures. The abundant, perennial water sources in our study area allows bats to replenish water lost during the day, but for species such asN. geoffroyiandN. corbeniinhabiting unbuffered roosts, increasingTas could still overwhelm their daytime evaporative cooling capacity. Given the unique thermal physiology ofV. vulturnus, roost cavities with stableTroosts are essential for this species’ persistence. However, asTas increase due to climate change these species would face increased dehydration risk and sub-lethal fitness costs61,62. Furthermore, as heat waves become more frequent, the risk of fire will increase leading to the destruction of critical roost trees. It is therefore important to identify roost trees and ideal roost microclimates as these facilitate the protection of existing habitats.

All experiments were performed following relevant guidelines and regulations. The study has been reported in accordance with ARRIVE guidelines.
Our study was conducted during the austral summer (16 December 2023 – 26 February 2024) at Calperum Station, Renmark, South Australia (−34.05026, 140.71060). Calperum Station is located on the Murray River floodplain with perennial sources of water in the form of the Murray River and Lake Woolpolool. The riverine vegetation is dominated byEucalyptus camaldulensisandE. largiflorens, while the understory is comprised ofDuma florulentaorAtriplex rhagodioides. Mean rainfall for the months of December, January, and February between 1995 and 2025 are 20.6, 18.9 and 17.3 mm, respectively63. The daily meanTafor the site ranged from 14.4 to 31.3 °C (December), 16.5–33.8 °C (January) and 16.0–32.7 °C (February) during the last 29 years63. Since 1995, the highest recordedTafor these three months are 48.6 °C (20 December 2019), 47.1 °C (14 January 2001), and 48.2 °C (7 February 200966). During our study, we recorded theTaof the site using an iButton (Thermochron iButton, DS1921G, Maxim Integrated Products Inc., Sunnyvale, CA, USA) deployed in the shade at 2 m height (sensu19,34.
We captured bats using mist nets and harp traps (Faunatech Austbat; Wildlife Services UK) placed across potential flyways within the riverine forest. We used both 3 m high mist nets (Ecotone, Poland) and a triple-high system comprised of an extendable pole system reaching 9 m (Bat Conservation & Management, Pennsylvania, USA). We immediately aged (adults were distinguished using the fusion of phalangeal epiphyses29, sexed and placed bats in clean cloth bags, and then transported by vehicle (3–5 min) to the field laboratory for further processing. We retained only non-reproductive adults in our study; young-of-the-year and reproductive females were immediately released. We captured:C. gouldii(10 females, 12 males),V. vulturnus(12 females, seven males),N. geoffroyi(11 females, 14 males),N. corbeni(seven males), andO. petersi(nine males).
At the field laboratory, we measured the mass (to the nearest 0.1 g), and forearm length (to the nearest 0.1 mm) of each individual. We offered each individual water using a pipette and fed them mealworms (Tenebrio molitor) until they gained ≥ 10% body mass (Mb). After feeding and watering, we implanted a temperature-sensitive passive integrated transponder tag (PIT-tag; Biotherm, Biomark, Biose ID, USA) subcutaneously between their scapulae. PIT-tags were previously calibrated in a water bath to the nearest 0.1 °C between 25 and 50 °C against a precision mercury thermometer (traceable to NIST). To ensure all individuals were post-absorptive, we obtained gas exchange measurements at least six hours after feeding64,65.
We used a flow-through respirometry system to measure evaporative water loss (EWL), oxygen (V̇O2) and carbon dioxide (V̇CO2). We constructed airtight respirometry chambers from glass jars (0.5 L forN. geoffroyiandV. vulturnusand 0.75 L forC. gouldii,N. corbeni, andO. petersi). The inside of the chamber was covered in wire mesh, allowing the bat to crawl, stretch, and adopt natural roosting postures. The bottom wire mesh platform was elevated ~ 2 cm from a layer of mineral oil (~ 1 cm) to prevent water in excreta from interfering with H2O readings14,1,8. We used a type T thermocouple probe (0.1 °C accuracy) connected to a digital thermometer (Omega Handheld Digital Thermometer HH81A) to measure theTawithin the respirometry chamber. We placed the respirometry chamber in a customised temperature-controlled chest (WAECO mobile refrigeration unit: 14 L) that was controlled manually. To recordTsub, PIT-tag readings were logged throughout the experiment using a racket antenna placed adjacent to the respirometry chamber and connected to a PIT-tag logger (HPR+; Biotherm, Biomark, Biose ID, USA).
We used two respirometry systems in our study. In each system, dried atmospheric air was supplied to the system from the outside using an aquarium electromagnetic air pump with a maximum capacity of 30 L min− 1and a column of silica used to scrub water vapour. This air was then split into baseline and chamber channels. A needle valve regulated the baseline air flow while a mass flow controller (Aalborg-GFC17 controlled by an Aalborg totalizer-TIO-LAA2, Orangeburg, NY, USA) was used to regulate the chamber airflow. We placed the air inlet near the top of the chamber, while the air outlet was placed below the mesh platform to maximise air mixing. Immediately before entering and immediately after exiting the chamber, air was filtered using polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) 0.2 μm membrane filters (Sterlitech Corporation, WA, USA). We adjusted the flow rates at eachTaaccording to the species,Mb, and behaviour of the individual, and ranged from 0.46 to 2.96 L min− 1. We used Bev-A-Line IV tubing (Thermoplastic Processes inc.) within the system.
In the first system, excurrent air from the baseline and chamber was split using a multiplexor (MUX3-1101-18 M, Sable Systems) in manual mode. This air was subsequently pulled through a CO2/H2O analyser (LI-840 A, LI-COR, Lincoln, NE, USA) and an oxygen analyser (Sable Systems, FC-10). Air was subsampled using a sub-sampler (SS4, Sable Systems). In the second system, we directly fed the baseline and chamber channels into a field metabolic system to measure O2, CO2, and H2O (FMS, Sable systems, Las Vegas, NV, USA). We spanned the H2O sensor of the LI-840 A and the FMS using air saturated with H2O at two dew point temperatures and zeroed using N2gas21. We also spanned the CO2/H2O analyser and FMS for CO2using a known CO2concentration (2000 ppm) and zeroed using N2gas. We used an analog-digital converter (UI-2, Sable Systems, Las Vegas, NV, USA) to digitise the voltage outputs from the first system. We used Expedata software (Sable Systems; sampling interval: 5 s) to record the final digital outputs from both systems.
We conducted experiments during the diurnal rest phase of the bats (i.e., daylight hours) following established methods33,66and each experiment lasted 4.5–5.5 h in total. We placed bats in the chambers atTa= 28 °C to acclimate for one hour. After acclimation, individuals were exposed to incrementally higherTas in stepped states, which is analogous to the sliding cold exposure protocol used for summit metabolism measurements, but in reverse67. This stepped method produces results indistinguishable from steady-state measurements without the deleterious effects of prolonged exposure to high temperature68. We initiated measurements atTa= 28 °C, andTawas subsequently increased to 40 °C in 4 °C increments and thereafter in 2 °C increments. Once an individual had been exposed to each experimentalTafor 20–30 min and then onceTsub, V̇CO2, and EWL had stabilised at constant values, we recorded a 5-min sample.
Throughout the experiments we used an infra-red video camera (IP camera; Shenzhen Electronics, Guandong, China) to continuously monitor individuals. If an individual displayed behaviours such as prolonged ceaseless movements/chewing, lack of coordination in body movement or uncontrolled increase inTb, indicating the onset of hyperthermia and/or dehydration and that the individual had reached what we considered its ‘heat tolerance limit ’ (i.e., the maximumTaan individual tolerated at rest )14,33, the experiment was immediately terminated, and individuals were removed from the chamber. Immediately after experiments, we weighed bats and offered waterad libitiumfrom a pipette. We then placed individuals in cloth bags, allowing them to rest, and fed them mealworms and more water one hour later, and once more before release at the site of capture. The total holding time of bats was < 24 hours. This protocol has been used to measure the HTL and evaporative cooling capacities of bats14,18,20, other small mammals and birds43,69. During our study, we recaptured a maleN. geoffroyi(two days post initial capture) and a femaleC. gouldii(14 days post initial capture). A maleO. petersiwas recaptured two, 11, and 39 days post initial capture. All recaptured bats displayed normal behaviour, were in good body condition, and none had a loss > 6%Mbat initial capture.
We corrected analyser drift and lag using Expedata software. By using the lowest stable 5-min periods of CO2and water vapour at eachTa, V̇CO2and EWL was calculated using Eq. 9.5 [V̇CO2= Excurrent flow rate * (Fractional excurrent CO2) – Incurrent flow rate * (Fractional incurrent CO2)] and 9.6 [EWL = Excurrent flow rate * (Fractional excurrent H2O) – Incurrent flow rate * (Fractional incurrent H2O)]75, assuming 0.803 mg H2O ml−1vapour75. As individuals were post absorptive, we assumed a respiratory exchange ratio = 0.7176. V̇CO2was converted to metabolic rate (W) assuming 27.8 J CO2ml−177. We calculated whole animal EWL (EWL) and whole animal RMR (RMR) for each individual and rates of EWL were converted to evaporative heat loss (EHL; W) assuming a latent heat of vaporisation of water of 2.406 J mg−1at 40 °C78. Metabolic and evaporative scope were defined as the ratio between highest recorded average value of RMR or EWL at a given experimental temperature and lowest recorded average values.
All statistical analyses were performed in R 4.4.279. For physiological responses in relation toTa, we created segmented regressions and identified inflection points using thesegmented.lmepackage75. We used an analysis of variance (ANOVA) to compare the segmented regression to a linear regression without an inflection point. In all cases, except the relationship betweenTsub~Tafor both sexes ofV. vulturnus, the p-value and difference in residual sum of squares and degrees of freedom in the results of the ANOVA between the two models indicated that the segmented regression was a significantly better fit.
To examine inter-specific differences among the five species, we compared the physiological data recorded from males as we could not collect data from females ofO. petersiandN. corbeniwithin our sampling period. We compared the slopes of each species-specific linear model above the species-specific inflection points of the males. For each comparison, if the 95% confidence intervals of the inflection point did not overlap, we considered them to be significantly different14,76.
To test for intra-specific differences between sexes, we compared males and females fromV. vulturnus,N. geoffroyi, andC. gouldii. As above, we fit linear mixed effect models to the physiological data above the sex-specific inflection points using thenlmepackage77. In the global model of each physiological variable,Ta,Mb, sex, and their interaction terms were used as fixed effects. To account for repeated measurements (i.e., measurements at multipleTavalues from each individual) we included individual identity as a random factor. We performed model reductions by first removing the interaction terms and, based on null hypothesis testing, we selected the predictor variables to be retained in the final model78,79. We calculated estimated marginal means forTsub, EWL, RMR, and EHL/metabolic heat production (MHP) using the emmeans package80. Significance was assigned atα= 0.05 or in cases where confidence intervals did not overlap in all analyses.
