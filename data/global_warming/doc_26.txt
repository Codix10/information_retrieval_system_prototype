Developing climate-resilient crops is important for sustainable agriculture and maintaining food security. In this study, we simulated severe global warming in a customized growth chamber, revealing a significant advancement in rice life cycles and a notable reduction in grain quality in modern Japanese cultivars in a temperate region. While our evaluation is limited to certain temperate japonica cultivars, the results underscore the value of simulating less observable severe global warming environments for a comprehensive understanding of their impact on rice cultivation. Expanding this approach to diverse varieties and regions will offer a more realistic view of global warming’s impact on rice production.
Keywords:global warming, rice, artificial environment, phenotyping
Decreased production of crops due to climate change has been predicted scientifically. While climate-resilient crops are necessary to ensure food security and support sustainable agriculture, predicting crop growth under future global warming is challenging. Therefore, we aimed to assess the impact of realistic global warming conditions on rice cultivation. We developed a crop evaluation platform, the agro-environment (AE) emulator, which generates diverse environments by implementing the complexity of natural environmental fluctuations in customized, fully artificial lighting growth chambers. We confirmed that the environmental responsiveness of rice obtained in the fluctuation of artificial environments is similar to those exhibited in natural environments by validating our AE emulator using publicly available meteorological data from multiple years at the same location and multiple locations in the same year. Based on the representative concentration pathway, real-time emulation of severe global warming unveiled dramatic advances in the rice life cycle, accompanied by a 35% decrease in grain yield and an 85% increase in quality deterioration, which is higher than the recently reported projections. The transcriptome dynamism showed that increasing temperature and CO2concentrations synergistically changed the expression of various genes and strengthened the induction of flowering, heat stress adaptation, and CO2response genes. The predicted severe global warming greatly alters rice environmental adaptability and negatively impacts rice production. Our findings offer innovative applications of artificial environments and insights for enhancing varietal potential and cultivation methods in the future.
Ensuring a stable food supply is crucial for food security. Climate change due to increased greenhouse gas emissions can significantly affect the food supply because of decreased production. Maize (corn), wheat, rice, and soybean yields steeply decrease when temperatures exceed certain thresholds (1). To date, 103 crops have been reported to provide up to 90% of the calories in the human diet, four of which (rice, wheat, maize, and potato) account for 60% of the human energy supply (2). Climate change may reverse the progress in the fight against hunger. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) highlighted that climate change increases the food security risks for the most vulnerable countries and populations (3,4). Sustainable development of crop- and region-specific adaptation and mitigation strategies is necessary to meet the demands of the growing world population and ensure food security.
The IPCC reports (https://www.ipcc.ch/) provide a scientific basis for climate change, describing the projected changes in global average temperature and CO2concentration at different levels (scenarios) based on models incorporating socioeconomic and technological factors and climate policy. Although crop yield projections incorporating climate change predictions are uncertain (5,6), combining crop models with the best consensus of climate change projections reflecting different global warming scenarios enables global and local prediction of crop yields and reductions (7–9). Therefore, technologies enabling rapid and precise breeding should be developed using the adaptation strategies of crop plants to fluctuating external environments to combat global warming because quantitative differences in environmental responses underlying interspecific variation can significantly affect performance (10).
Rice (Oryza sativa) is one of the staple foods grown in a wide range of regions in Asia and other parts of the world. Cultivated varieties mostly belong to one of two subspecies,indicaorjaponica. Whileindicais widely distributed in low-latitude tropical regions of Asia,japonicais longitudinally distributed to high latitudes up to temperate regions. Climate change adversely affects both the subspecies. Recent advances in understanding the molecular mechanisms of heat stress responses in rice, a model crop, highlight the impact of high temperatures on reproduction and grain quality (11). However, the combined effects of high temperatures and CO2levels, which represent future global warming scenarios, on rice cultivation remain unclear. In cultivars for both subspecies, the effect of high temperatures during the reproductive stage has been empirically reported in the field (12,13). Under free-air CO2enrichment conditions, the beneficial effects of CO2fertilization on rice yield are less affected by increasing ambient temperatures (14). Although these findings are important for understanding how climate change affects rice cultivation, more direct approaches, such as environment evaluation under global warming, are needed to clarify rice adaptability to realistic global warming conditions.
Growth chambers with artificial environments are commonly employed for evaluating crop potential. Historically, natural sunlight serves as the primary source for plant growth. However, recent advancements in Light Emitting Diode (LED) technology for plant growth now offer high-power alternatives that efficiently stimulate plant photosynthetic potential. Here, we developed a unique crop evaluation platform equipped with high-power LEDs that can be used year-round and applied to produce complex environmental controls resembling seasonal trends and climate change in artificial environments. We evaluated crop growth and productivity robustness by emulating different global warming scenarios in growth chambers where we cultivated rice cultivars under realistic global warming conditions and obtained scientific insights into rice growth in severe global warming environments. Our findings reveal different ways to use artificial environments and provide relevant insights for future improvements in varietal potential and cultivation methods.
To evaluate crop performance across a wider range of environmental conditions than in the conventional growth chambers, we developed indoor growth chambers comprising 17 rooms that simultaneously produce diverse environments (SI Appendix, Figs. S1 and S2 and Tables S1 and S2). These chambers would provide a wide range of environmental control that can facilitate the creation of diverse environments including seasonal changes and severe growing conditions. Controlled environmental facilities have become standard tools for emulating diverse environmental conditions close to the complexity of natural environments (15). In addition to typical control parameters (i.e., temperature, relative humidity, light intensity, and CO2concentration), our system, hereafter referred to as the agro-environment (AE) emulator, enables the meteorological data and geographic information input to emulate natural environments.
Using rice as our model, we investigated the capability of the AE emulator system to simulate past natural conditions using historical meteorological data. We chose Tsukuba (36° N, 140° E; Japan) as the study location and classified the average daily temperature data for the past 31 y into five patterns using nonhierarchical clustering (Fig. 1A). The clustering depended on the daily temperature change curve and timing of the highest daily temperature during the test period. We selected 1 or 2 y from each cluster and emulated them in the AE emulator using meteorological data (air temperature, relative humidity, and global solar radiation) at 1-h intervals obtained from the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA), day-length data calculated from geographic information, and CO2concentration data. The AE emulator system accurately reconstructed seasonal changes that differed slightly among years by processing hourly input data on a minute basis, thereby creating diverse air systems coupled with seasonal day-length changes (Fig. 1BandSI Appendix, Fig. S3). The successive control of temperature and day length, which contribute significantly to rice productivity under natural conditions (16,17), strongly indicated that the AE emulator could work as an artificial environment generator that considers the natural environment’s complexity to investigate crop adaptability in each environment.

We collected data on the agronomic traits of five modern Japanese cultivars, encompassing 18 traits (Fig. 1CandDandSI Appendix, Fig. S4). The cultivars belonging to temperate japonica within thejaponicasubspecies have adapted to high-latitude temperate regions during domestication, with various combinations of known flowering-time natural variations (SI Appendix, Table S3). To verify whether rice grows properly in our system, we compared the three environmental responses exhibited in the emulator with those in the field using data on flowering time (days to heading, DTH), culm, and panicle lengths (Fig. 1C). Although we used meteorological data obtained from the Tsukuba weather observatory, the phenotypic values of the five emulator-tested cultivars, each with distinct photosensitivities, closely resembled those observed in the Yawara field (35°59′ N, 140° E), near Tsukuba. The exceptions were a slight delay in DTH and an increase in culm length. Flowering time plasticity in our settings highly depended on seasonal cumulative photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) values (instead of solar radiation in the natural environment) and average temperature (Fig. 1D), which greatly influence DTH under natural field conditions. Correlation analysis of trait values between the emulator and natural field showed relatively high similarity in DTH compared to those in culm length and panicle length (SI Appendix, Fig. S5) despite using the meteorological data from Tsukuba, which does not precisely match Yawara. Therefore, we concluded that the emulator could simulate the range of variability of environmental responses occurring in nearby fields, even though not precisely matched in location.
In our real-time emulation, cultivation differed from conventional crop cultivation in the field (realism), including variations in soil, pots, and sunlight (wavelength and intensity), presenting experimental limitations for approaching realistic outdoor conditions (18,19). However, rice (Koshihikari) grown in the emulator exhibited normal characteristics similar to that grown in the field (SI Appendix, Table S4). Among the eight emulation years, a record-breaking cold year (1993) was included, which induced evident growth defects such as remarkably delayed flowering time, a dramatic decrease in panicle number, and reduced biomass (dry matter) (Fig. 1DandSI Appendix, Fig. S4). This suggests that the emulated environments can determine crop potential by mimicking fluctuations occurring in previously unfavorable environments.
Future climate change projections have raised concerns regarding changes in areas suitable for crop cultivation because of rising temperatures (7). We selected an appropriate environment to investigate the effects of global warming through a multisite evaluation, using four locations on mainland Japan because of the availability of historical data and the presence of satellite fields of the National Agriculture & Food Research Organization near the JMA weather observatory. The emulation of four locations, including Tsukuba, generated location-dependent environmental changes, particularly for air temperature and day length (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). The observed differences in the DTH of the five cultivars among the emulated environments of the four locations were similar to those observed in the natural fields (SI Appendix, Figs. S7Aand S8), indicating that rice responded to climate change depending on the emulated geographical differences to confer flowering-time diversification. Culm and panicle length showed a weak correlation between the emulator and natural field values (SI Appendix, Figs. S7BandCand S8). The observed limited correlation in culm and panicle length may be related to the limited reproduction of other potentially decisive growing conditions such as light quality, planting density, and soil conditions, other than photoperiod and temperature. We compared yield-related and grain-quality traits with productivity (SI Appendix, Figs. S7 and S9). Tsukuba, emulated in the AE-emulator, stood out as the most productive location, with the highest biomass and panicle weight per plant among the four emulated locations. Our results also showed that emulation- or cultivar-specific growth failures were observed at the other emulated locations (SI Appendix, Figs. S7 and S9). The northern site (Daisen) exhibited low yield-related trait values, accompanied by small stature and reduced panicle numbers in both Koshihikari and Hinohikari. At the southeast site (Chikugo), flowering times of Koshihikari and Nipponbare in the AE emulator coincided with late August, corresponding to the period when the average daily temperature is frequently >30 °C. The correlation between maximum temperature days of approximately 35 °C in Japan’s summer and increased rice sterility (13) suggests that the convergence of flowering time with high temperatures during emulated summers may result in reduced panicle weight and unfilled spikelet development. In summary, we concluded that the AE emulator can be utilized for the evaluation of crop potentials under unfavorable environmental conditions, such as hot and cold for rice, occurring in the past, present, and future, which is challenging to achieve in conventional growth chambers.
We simulated an environment that reflects future global warming in Tsukuba. We implemented the Tsukuba meteorological data using a set of climate scenarios known as the representative concentration pathway (RCP), used by climatic researchers to provide possible scenarios for the evolution of atmospheric composition (20,21). Using the mean meteorological data from 1990 to 1999 as the control environment from the literature (22), we investigated two types of simulation scenarios: RCP2.6 (lowest impact) and RCP8.5 (highest impact) at the end of the 21st century. Contrary to the control environment, we set 1.4 and 4.5 °C rises in chronological sets of air temperature values and 426 and 850 ppm CO2concentrations for RCP2.6 and RCP8.5 (RCP8.5_CO2), respectively (Fig. 2AandB).

To elucidate the synergistic effects of simultaneously rising temperature and CO2concentration on rice growth, two environments, RCP2.6 with a CO2concentration of 850 ppm (RCP2.6_CO2) and RCP8.5 with a CO2concentration of 426 ppm (RCP8.5), were investigated (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). As the temperature increased, plant height growth speed and tiller number increased only with high CO2application in the five varieties (Fig. 2C). Furthermore, compared to those in the control conditions, maximum tillering stage timing advancement was the greatest under severe global warming conditions (RCP8.5_CO2;Fig. 2C), and DTH advancement consequently reached 9 to 19 d, depending on the variety (RCP8.5 and RCP8.5_CO2;Fig. 2D). Consequently, the flowering time of all five cultivars coincided with the timing of rapidly rising and constantly high average temperature, substantially exceeding 30 °C in the RCP8.5. In addition to the dramatic changes in these phenological responses, panicle weight was remarkably reduced in rice grown under RCP8.5 (SI Appendix, Fig. S11), indicating a negative effect on grain traits.
Deleterious effects of severe global warming on rice grain yield and quality were observed (Fig. 3). Although the RCP2.6 simulation did not strongly influence the panicle traits irrespective of the CO2, the temperature rise in RCP8.5 dramatically increased unfilled spikelet formation, maximally reducing the single panicle weight by about 35% (Fig. 3A). The occurrence of chalky grains, indicating rice appearance quality, remarkably increased in the temperature condition reflecting the RCP8.5 scenario, and these effects were likely enhanced in the RCP8.5_CO2environment (Fig. 3B–DandSI Appendix, Fig. S12). This suggests that the deleterious effect on grain quality was increased by the negative synergy between high temperatures and high CO2concentrations. While the occurrence of chalky grains in Akitakomachi grown under the RCP8.5_CO2environment (nearly 40%) was approaching that estimated for the same cultivar by rice growth models in the RCP8.5 scenario (9), our real-time emulation of global warming additionally showed the greatest impact, reaching an approximately 85% reduction in grain appearance in Koshihikari, the current leading variety in Japan (Fig. 3BandD).

To determine the physiological status of plants under emulated global warming, we analyzed the leaf transcriptomes of vegetative rice plants (Koshihikari and Nipponbare) grown under five different temperature and CO2concentration combinations, including the RCP scenarios. The principal component analysis suggested that the expression profiles were considerably changed by environments reflecting the RCP8.5 scenario (Fig. 4A). The MA plots revealed that the transcriptome dynamics, expressed as the number of statistically significant differentially expressed genes (DEGs), markedly increased in both cultivars in the RCP8.5_CO2environment (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). Common transcriptomes originating from 1301 DEGs to the two cultivars were classified into five clusters (Fig. 4BandCandDataset S1). Known genes characterized as being induced by heat stress treatment, including one gene selected as a DEG, exhibited similar expression profiles. Specifically, four homologous genes regulating heat stress adaptation (Os03g0161900, Os10g0419300, Os09g0526600, and Os04g0568700;HSFAandHSFBfamilies) showed strong expression in both RCP8.5 and RCP8.5_CO2(23–25) (Fig. 4D). The clusters (C01 and C02) included key genes involved in stomatal closure in response to external signals (including high CO2), such asSLAC1(Os04g0574700),SAPK10(Os03g0610900), and the abscisic acid (ABA) receptor (OsPYL; Os03g0297600) (26–29) (Fig. 4E). The results suggested that gene networks for heat stress and excess CO2adaptations operate simultaneously in rice leaves continuously exposed to severe global warming (RCP8.5_CO2). Precocious induction of florigen (RFT1), possibly coupled with related floral regulator genesGhd7,Ehd1, andHd3ain leaves under RCP8.5_CO2conditions (Fig. 4FandSI Appendix, Fig. S14), implied a strong impact on the phenotypic plasticity required to allow for optimal flowering time in rice photoperiodic flowering (30). The GO enrichment analysis using individual clusters further revealed that stress signaling-related biological processes, such as “protein amino acid phosphorylation” (Gene Ontology [GO]: 0006468), were associated with up-regulated DEGs (cluster C02), whereas GOs related to metabolic processes, such as “cellular nitrogen compound metabolic process” (GO: 0034641), “cellular carbohydrate metabolic process” (GO: 0044262), and “cellulose biosynthetic pathway” (GO: 0030244), were associated with down-regulated DEGs (clusters C03 and C05;Fig. 4GandSI Appendix, Table S5). The transcriptome dynamics under the realistic future global warming scenario confirmed an unbalanced daily physiological status compared to one operating under current fluctuating environments (Fig. 4H).

Under global warming, inconsistencies in the regional adaptability of existing varieties to rising temperatures could decrease food production and quality. Our real-time emulation of severe global warming climate scenarios in a temperate climate region (Tsukuba) demonstrated that the combination of high CO2with rising temperatures synergistically stabilizes such disastrous situations, leading to a maximum of approximately 35 and 85% decrease in panicle weight and an increase in the occurrence of chalky grains in the currently leading rice variety, respectively (Figs. 2and3). Although the recent rice production prediction under the RCP8.5 scenario showed a similar tendency by incorporating CO2data (9), our real-time emulation also highlighted rice growth and development acceleration in a realistic future environment.
Our results strongly indicated that rising temperatures during the reproductive and seed-ripening stages are a major cause of yield loss. Rising summer temperatures severely induced unfilled spikelet formation (Fig. 3A). Based on the RCP8.5, as the maximal daily temperature during summer appeared to be constantly around 35 °C (Fig. 2AandB), rice adaptability for anthesis exhibited in the studied varieties can no longer be compatible with future global warming in Japan’s temperate regions. Our results also suggested that synchronizing shifted flowering time with the rising temperature and high-temperature period in summer may contribute to spikelet sterility during anthesis, potentially jeopardizing the regional adaptability of varieties supporting local paddy farming. High temperatures have been known to induce spikelet sterility inindicavarieties grown in tropical regions (12). Therefore, ongoing breeding approaches for high-temperature tolerance (31–34) to improve spikelet fertility during anthesis could serve as a viable climate adaptation strategy that can be applied to a wide range of varieties. However, to assess rice productivity accurately under global warming, the reproduction of tropical climate conditions in the AE emulator needs further clarification. In a high-yieldingindicavariety, an increase in nighttime temperature causes a decrease in grain yield without spikelet sterility (35). Grain yield declined by 10% for each 1 °C increase of mean minimum temperature in tropical cultivation (16). Despite minimum temperature increases of 1.4 and 4.5 °C (Fig. 3) in the construction of global warming scenarios in a temperate region using temperate japonica cultivars, no noticeable decrease was observed in the number of differentiated spikelet numbers per panicle, implying a limitation in the current assessment of rice production using the AE emulator. To show the impacts of global warming more accurately, accumulating data on the evaluation of various rice cultivars encompassing rice diversity and implementing artificial weather replication in various growing regions is necessary.
Compared to high temperature alone, the combination of high CO2and temperature resulted in considerably poorer performance (Figs. 2and3). Transcriptome information representing the physiological status in the emulated severe global warming further confirmed their synergistic effect on the expression of various genes, including those involved in stress response and metabolism (Fig. 4). The DEG analysis also revealed the upregulation of genes related to the mechanism of excess CO2adaptation, which reduces stomatal opening (36) in addition to the upregulation of heat stress adaptation genes (Fig. 4DandE). Therefore, a prominent increase in atmospheric CO2concentrations might limit CO2absorption in rice leaves. CO2utilization is directly related to nitrogen acquisition and influences developmental processes. While nitrogen fertilizers can increase food production, the remaining nitrogen in the soil is emitted into the environment as harmful greenhouse gases. Limited CO2utilization by plants in high CO2concentrations with rising temperatures will consequently accelerate global warming by increasing greenhouse gases released from agriculture. Modification of stomatal opening may mitigate limitations on CO2utilization that may occur with global warming. Recently, genetically modified rice plants have been developed with H+-ATPase (OsA1/AHA1) overexpression with enhanced CO2absorption capacity because of increased stomatal opening (37). Additionally, the knockout ofPYLs encoding ABA receptors by genome editing facilitated rapid CO2absorption by affecting stomatal movement (38). In both cases, enhancement of CO2absorption can contribute to yield increase. Since stomatal opening reduces the leaf surface temperature through transpiration, it could simultaneously mitigate the heat stress response in leaves. However, enhancing CO2absorption by stomatal opening may also increase water loss from both plants and paddies. Genetic modification of CO2utilization by enhancing CO2absorption, considering a trade-off between water retention in the plant bodies and water loss from stomata, will be necessary to implement in cultivation areas, especially in those dependent on rainwater and poor irrigation under real paddy farming.
The decline in rice yield and quality due to global warming poses a significant threat to food security, especially in regions heavily reliant on rice as a staple food. The FAO has called for sustainable rice production that reduces the impact of climate change (39). Crops should be improved for climate resilience using adaptive strategies, such as water use efficiency, fertilizer use, and pest management, tailored for specific growing regions. Our findings underscore the importance of traits such as CO2absorption capacity and high-temperature tolerance for local adaptability. A comprehensive understanding of rice quantitative traits can effectively connect increasing genomic knowledge with diverse improvement needs in rice (40).
The AE emulator could contribute to both adaptation and mitigation research and reduce the risks to rice production caused by global warming by mirroring the paddy field environment. Establishing a paddy field ecosystem coupled with real-time monitoring techniques of CO2and methane levels (41,42) in the AE emulator can provide digital information to evaluate the performance of developing climate-resilient rice varieties, including CO2utilization ability and nitrogen use efficiency along with assessing the efficacy of reduced methane emissions through extended drainage. This approach offers unique indicators crucial for developing region-specific sustainable agriculture within a climate-resilient framework, which has been a widely accepted practice in the history of the Asian region, including Japan.
To date, more than 100 crops have been reported worldwide (2). An international initiative has been launched to expand environmentally regenerative agriculture on a global scale (https://regen10.org). Owing to the ongoing global changes, the positive outcomes of environmentally regenerative agriculture are essential for ensuring food security for people, nature, and climate. Applying the AE emulator to field crops as a platform for providing science-based indicators will be crucial for the future assessment of the environmental and ecological impacts of agriculture in the face of climate change.
In this study, we used five rice cultivars (O. sativaL.), Koshihikari, Akitakomachi, Hitomebore, Nipponbare, and Hinohikari. Seeds were sterilized using a seed disinfectant containing 5% ipconazole (Kumiai Chemical Industry Co., Ltd., Japan) for 1 d, washed, and incubated with tap water for 3 d. Seedlings were grown in a nursery room under daily cycles of 14.5 h light at 26 °C and 9.5 h dark at 22 °C. Seedlings were planted in 1/10,000 a Wagner pots filled with synthetic granular soil (Sumitomo Chemical Company Co., Ltd., Japan) after 30 d. Pots were grown in a plastic container measuring 640 mm (length) × 380 mm (width) × 145 mm (height). Two sets of containers with 13 pots were placed in one room. Door openings were minimized by monitoring plant conditions through a small window in the growth chamber door (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Access to the door was primarily restricted to irrigation and weekly growth data acquisition.
The AE emulator is a customized growth chamber that combines TGL-1-5SN (Type A) or TGL-1-2SN (Type B) with a weather reconstitution program (Espec Mic Co., Ltd., Japan;SI Appendix, Fig. S2and Table S2). Hereinafter this program is referred to as PemuController. Historical weather data (air temperature, relative humidity, and global solar radiation) of the indicated years, recorded at 1 h intervals, were downloaded from the JMA as CSV files. The files were then uploaded to PemuController. The PemuController communicated with the individual rooms of the growth chamber to transmit the set values. The temperature and relative humidity set values changed linearly every 5 min, transitioning from the value at a given time to the value 1 h later, based on the acquired hourly data. Day length was calculated based on input latitude, longitude, and altitude to determine sunrise and sunset times. The CO2concentration was controlled with an increasing set value. Except in the global warming simulation, the CO2concentration was set at 412 ppm (Fig. 2). For the emulation of past years in Tsukuba, we used the meteorological data from the indicated years. In the multilocation experiment, the meteorological data for Daisen, Inada, and Chikugo were substituted with those for Akita, Joetsu, and Kurume due to limited available past weather data near local weather stations recorded at 1-h intervals by JMA.
With LED lighting, there were slight differences in the maximal PPFD values (100% power) in individual product lots in PFQ-600DT (NK System, Japan;SI Appendix, Fig. S15). To mitigate this lot bias, we set the maximum PPFD value of the individual LED units to 540 µmol m−2s−1, 900 mm from the top of the growth room, before performing the experiment. The dimmer provided with the LED unit was used, and the light intensity at 1% was 88 ± 2.2 µmol m−2s−1, from which it was adjusted as stepless.
Flowering time in DTH was recorded as the date when the first panicle appeared. The rice plants were harvested 40 d after heading. Before harvesting, the final culm length and panicle number were recorded, and the plants were packed in an envelope and dried at 30 °C for 3 d. Phenotypic plant traits [dry weight (biomass), panicle length, and panicle weight per plant] were measured, whereas phenotypic traits related to yield (panicle, branch number per panicle, spikelet number per panicle, and unfilled spikelet number per panicle) were measured from the top three panicles of each plant. The dehulled rice grains of each panicle were processed using a Rice Analyzer RGQI 100 B (Satake Corp., Japan) to evaluate grain quality. The graphs representing trait values were visualized using the R package “ggplot2” (ver. 3.3.6).
Since rice plants were well acclimated to the treated conditions, the growth effects were noticeable. Samples were collected 27 d after transplantation under control, RCP2.6 with or without 850 ppm CO2, and RCP8.5 with or without 850 ppm CO2, and then powdered in liqN2. RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) was performed as described previously (43) with slight modifications. Total RNA was extracted using the ISOSPIN Plant RNA Kit (Nippon Gene, Tokyo, Japan). RNA concentration was measured using a Qubit fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, United States). mRNA was purified using oligo-dT beads (New England Biolabs Japan Inc., Tokyo, Japan), and a sequencing library was constructed using the NEBNext Ultra II Directional RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (New England Biolabs Japan Inc., Tokyo, Japan). The library was sequenced using the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with S4 flow cells to produce 150 bp paired-end and dual index reads.
The reads were preprocessed using CLC Genomics Workbench ver. 22.0.2 (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). After quality trimming, the raw reads were mapped to the Oryza sativa-Nipponbare-Reference-IRGSP-1.0 genome assembly and gene set (Ensemble Plants Release 25) (44) using the following parameters: mismatch cost of two, insertion/deletion cost of three, length fraction of 0.8, similarity fraction of 0.8, and maximum number of hits for a read of 10 (Dataset S2). The CLC Genomics Workbench output was subsequently analyzed using the R software (ver. 4.0.2). After excluding the genes with counts <1, the count data were normalized to transcripts per million (TPM) using the CLC Genomics Workbench. The TPM data were log2-transformed or z-transformed as needed and used for the analyses.
A principal component analysis was performed based on count data that normalized against the trimmed mean of M values and library size using the R “prcomp” function and the first and second principal components (PC1 and PC2) were visualized. The Wald test was performed based on raw count data using the “DESeq” function in the R package “DESeq2” (ver. 1.30.1). Genes with an adjustedP-value (padj) <0.05 were extracted as DEGs against the control groups. These genes were included in thek-means cluster analysis, which was performed based on the TPM data that z-transformed using the “k-means” function in R. The appropriate number of clusters (k= 5) was determined using the elbow method (SI Appendix, Fig. S16). Heatmaps were prepared based on the TPM data that z-transformed using the R package “ComplexHeatmap” (ver. 2.6.2).
The web-based tool agriGO (http://systemsbiology.cau.edu.cn/agriGOv2) was used for the GO enrichment analysis. The genes used for this analysis are summarized inDataset S1.
For quantitative real-time PCR, 1,000 ng RNA was subjected to cDNA synthesis using a ReveTra Ace cDNA synthesis kit with Genome Remover (Toyobo, Japan). Thereafter, qRT-PCR was performed with a THUNDERBIRDTMNext SYBR qPCR Mix (Toyobo, Japan) using the primers listed inSI Appendix, Table S6and detected using Viia7 (Thermo Fisher, Applied Biosystems, USA). The cycle program for the PCR was performed following the manufacturer’s instructions (50 °C 2 min, 95 °C 30 s, 95 °C 5 s / 60 °C 30 s × 40 cycles, 95 °C 15 s, 60 °C 1 min, 95 °C 15 s).
