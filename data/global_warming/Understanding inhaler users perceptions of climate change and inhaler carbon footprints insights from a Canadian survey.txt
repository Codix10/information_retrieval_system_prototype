People with lung diseases are vulnerable to climate change, yet metered dose inhalers (MDIs) have significant climate impacts. We sought to understand inhaler users’ perspectives on climate change and awareness of inhaler climate implications.
Canadians (aged ≥16 years) who reported using an inhaler in the previous 6 months were invited via health organisations’ newsletters to complete an online survey (November 2024–March 2025). Multivariate regression models assessed the association between sociodemographic factors and climate change risk perception, disposal practices and awareness of inhaler climate implications.
There were 343 respondents (median age 71 years (IQR: 64–76 years), 65% female, 80% MDI users). Individuals were concerned (45%) or very concerned (39%) about climate change, but only 20% were previously aware of inhaler climate implications. Advancing age (OR 0.58, 95% CI 0.45 to 0.74) was associated with being unaware of inhaler climate implications, while a university education (OR 4.86, 95% CI 1.77 to 16.62) and living in a large urban area (OR 2.81, 95% CI 1.21 to 7.45) were associated with greater awareness. Most respondents reported disposing of their inhalers in garbage or recycling bins (70%) and provincial pharmacy return programmes were underused (27%) when available. Nearly all MDI users (92%) were willing to switch to a lower carbon footprint device. Advice from healthcare providers was valued in supporting treatment decisions.
Inhaler users are concerned about climate change but lack awareness of inhaler climate impacts. Sustainability interventions that promote education, safe use of low carbon devices and recycling could reduce inhaler-related climate impacts.
Keywords:Inhaler devices, Asthma, COPD
Metered-dose inhalers release potent greenhouse gases that contribute to climate change, yet the very patients who use these devices to treat their lung disease are the most vulnerable to climate change. Guidelines recommend preferential use of inhalers with low carbon footprints, but patient awareness and perceptions of this topic have not been studied.
Our national survey found inhaler users are highly concerned about climate change and its health impacts but have limited awareness of the environmental impacts of inhalers. Once made aware, inhaler users are overwhelmingly willing to use lower carbon devices and to participate in recycling programmes.
Interventions to reduce inhaler carbon emissions have been provider-focussed but must include patient education and tools to enable shared decision-making.
In 2019, the WHO declared that air pollution and climate change were the number one threat to global health.1Our lungs are particularly vulnerable to the effects of climate change, with climate-related events such as changes in temperature, humidity, air pollution and wildfires contributing to new cases of respiratory diseases and exacerbating existing cases.2,4Healthcare for individuals with respiratory diseases paradoxically contributes to climate change, with metered-dose inhalers (MDIs), the most common treatment for respiratory diseases, having a significant carbon footprint.5 6
The carbon footprint of inhalers varies dramatically by device type. MDIs have the largest carbon footprint due to the use of propellants which contain hydrofluorocarbons, a potent greenhouse gas (GHG).5Although the exact figure has been a subject of debate,7a commonly cited metric states that the production, use and disposal of a single MDI device (eg, salbutamol) is estimated to release GHG equivalent to a 137 km journey in a gas-powered vehicle.8Dry powder inhalers (DPIs) and soft mist inhalers do not contain propellants and thus have carbon footprints that are multifold lower than that of MDIs.9Despite the availability of DPIs in each drug class, MDIs represent the vast majority of prescribed inhalers in many countries including Canada.6
Although there is growing awareness and interest among respiratory organisations and healthcare professionals in reducing inhaler carbon emissions,10 11little consideration has been given to the patient perspective. Guidelines and position statements from professional societies, led by the British Thoracic Society, National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network and later echoed by the Global Initiative for Asthma and the Canadian Thoracic Society, recommend considering patient preference and carbon footprint mitigation when selecting an inhaler device.12,14However, little is known about patients’ awareness of the climate implications of their inhalers or their willingness to use low carbon devices to reduce the environmental footprint of their inhaler treatment.
To our knowledge, awareness, attitudes and perceptions regarding climate change and its downstream health impacts have not formally been assessed among individuals with respiratory diseases and there has been little investigation into the perceptions of inhalers in this context.15,18We sought to understand inhaler users’ perspectives on climate change, awareness of inhaler-related climate implications, willingness to switch to inhaler devices with lower carbon footprints (among MDI users) and supports they would desire when considering such a switch. We also sought to identify existing inhaler disposal practices and preferences for inhaler recycling programmes. Our overarching goal was to characterise patient perspectives required to guide future sustainability interventions in respiratory medicine.
We conducted a cross-sectional, national, online, voluntary survey of inhaler users in Canada.
The survey was iteratively developed by our research team, which included a person with lived experience, two adult respirologists, one paediatric respirologist, one internal medicine specialist, three family physicians, three pharmacists, two respiratory therapists, one nurse and one certified respiratory educator. A member of the research team who is fluent in French translated the survey.
After an initial prototype was developed, four members of the research team and four research students uninvolved in this study independently pilot tested the online survey for logic and to determine the estimated complete time (~10 min). The survey was also vetted by leaders of the organisations who disseminated the survey, ensuring that all questions were appropriate for the intended audience (see below).
The survey was administered online via the website‘SurveyMonkey’. It was made available for 17 weeks (14 November 2024–14 March 2025). Respondents were able to review their responses prior to submitting the survey.
Members of the research team created a comprehensive list of patient-facing national or provincial non-profit organisations (including patient organisations for each major respiratory disease treated with inhalers), professional societies, associations and charities in Canada with audiences that captured individuals who use inhalers. A member of the research team then reached out to each organisation through their online contact information or through a personal email contact, explaining the purpose of the study and the request for circulation of an invitation to complete the questionnaire to their membership. A text version of the questionnaire was shared with each responding organisation on request, and any suggestions were considered by the research team, before dissemination.
Individuals were eligible to participate if they self-identified as meeting the following criteria: (1) age 16 years or older, (2) live in Canada and (3) used an inhaler in the last 6 months. A pre-formatted invitation (seeonline supplemental file 1) was provided to each organisation for circulation. The invite included eligibility criteria and explained that the survey would take approximately 10 min to complete and that participants would be entered into a draw to win one of three $C100Amazongift cards. All participants provided electronic informed consent prior to completing the anonymous survey.
Our research team included a person with lived experience, who provided feedback on the language used and the suitability of survey questions for the intended population, facilitated advertisement of the survey through their provincial lung association and assisted with interpretation of the findings. Leadership team members of the public-facing organisations who disseminated the survey also provided feedback on the survey content.
The Checklist for Reporting Results of Internet E-Surveys is available in theonline supplemental file 1along with a copy of the survey. In summary, the survey contained questions relating to participant demographics, self-reported respiratory diagnosis, inhaler devices used in the previous 6 months, the eight-item validated Climate Change Risk Perception Index (CCRPI),19health impacts of climate change (adapted from van Baalet al20), inhaler disposal practices, awareness and prior discussions with healthcare providers on inhaler carbon footprint, willingness to use/switch to lower carbon devices, necessary supports when considering device changes and preferences for inhaler recycling programmes. Prior to questions relating to the environmental impact of inhalers, respondents were provided with a brief educational summary to ensure that participants without prior knowledge on the topic were able to fully participate in the survey.
All available response data were analysed. Descriptive statistics were used to calculate the mean (SD) or median (IQR) for continuous variables and the frequency or proportion for categorical variables. Free text responses were categorised using a conventional content analysis approach.21
The first three digits of the respondents’ postal code were used to determine whether individuals lived in a rural area (<1000 residents), or in a small (1000 to 29 999), medium (30 000 to 99 999) or large (≥100 000) population centre, adapted from Statistics Canada’s Postal Code OM Conversion File (Reference date March 2025), which is based on data licensed from Canada Post Corporation.
A total score for the CCRPI was calculated by summing scores for all eight items. A personal score was calculated by summing responses for questions 1, 2, 6 and 8, and a global score was calculated by summing responses for questions 3, 4, 5 and 7.19
Multivariable linear regression was used to assess the association between sociodemographic factors and total CCRPI score. Multivariable logistic regression was used to assess the association between sociodemographic factors, CCRPI total score and awareness of inhaler carbon footprints. In the subgroup of respondents from Canadian provinces with government-mandated pharmacy medication return programmes (British Columbia, Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, New Brunswick and Prince Edward Island),22multivariable logistic regression was used to assess the association between sociodemographic factors, climate change risk perception and sustainable disposal practices (returning inhalers to the pharmacy when they are empty, expired or no longer needed). Among MDI users, multivariable logistic regression was used to identify factors associated with a willingness to switch to an inhaler with a lower carbon footprint. All statistical analyses were conducted using R statistical software (RStudio V.2024.12.1.563)23with a significance level of 0.05.
We invited a total of 13 organisations to share the questionnaire with their membership, and 10 organisations consented. This included five national and four provincial non-profit/charitable respiratory organisations and one patient advocacy organisation (online supplemental table 1). Organisations circulated invitations to complete the survey via electronic newsletters (n=7), email blast (n=1) and announcements in virtual community forums (n=3). Not all survey invitation recipients were eligible for the survey. Since the eligible population for each organisation was not known, response rates for each organisation are provided out of total invitations sent (online supplemental table 1).
A total of 343 individuals (65% women; age range 17–90 years (median 71 years, IQR: 64–76 years)) responded to the survey (English: n=284, French: n=59), with a completion rate of 92% (n=315). The most common respiratory diagnosis was chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) (43%), followed by asthma (27%) and asthma/COPD overlap (18%) (table 1).

Responses to the CCRPI are depicted infigure 1. The overall perceived risk of climate change was high (median CCRPI total score: 33 out of 40, IQR: 27 to 36), with most individuals being concerned (45%) or very concerned (39%) about climate change. In a multivariable analysis (online supplemental table 2), individuals who had or were unsure if they had already experienced health changes due to climate change had higher CCRPI scores compared with people who had not experienced health changes due to climate change (p<0.0001). Individuals with a university education scored 1.98/40 points higher on the CCRPI total score (95% CI: 0.08 to 3.88) compared with individuals with an elementary or high-school education. Similar trends were noted for the CCRPI personal and global scales (online supplemental table 2).

Most individuals were also concerned (55%) or very concerned (23%) about thehealth effectsof climate change (figure 2A), particularly extreme weather events (eg, heat waves, wildfires). Approximately one-third of individuals (31%) had already experienced changes to their health which they believed were connected to climate change (figure 2B). The most common health changes were respiratory-related (figure 2C), with 50% of individuals experiencing worsening respiratory symptoms, primarily due to wildfire smoke (39%) and heat or humidity (27%). Reported behaviour changes to mitigate this included increased inhaler use, spending less time outdoors and reduced physical activity.

Most inhaler users (80%) were unaware that some inhaler devices have a higher carbon footprint than others. In a multivariable model (figure 3,online supplemental table 3), a 10-year increase in age was associated with not being aware of inhaler carbon footprints (OR 0.58, 95% CI 0.45 to 0.74). Awareness of inhaler carbon footprints was higher among individuals with a university education (compared with elementary or high-school education) (OR 4.86, 95% CI 1.77 to 16.62) and among individuals living in a large urban population centre (100 000 or greater) (compared with rural areas) (OR 2.82, 95% CI 1.21 to 7.45).

Most individuals agreed (48%) or strongly agreed (23%) that it is important to consider environmental impact when choosing an inhaler, while 24% neither agreed nor disagreed. However, only 5% had ever discussed the carbon footprint of inhaler(s) with their healthcare provider.
Among MDI users (n=257), 92% indicated that they would be willing to switch to an inhaler with a lower carbon footprint if their healthcare provider agreed and it could offer a similar health benefit. Concerns about the effectiveness of a new inhaler device were the top reason for not being willing to switch (60%). Individuals also had concerns about the cost of different inhaler devices (30%), not knowing how to use a new device (10%), not wanting to learn how to use a new inhaler device (10%), satisfaction with their current inhaler (10%), concerns regarding side effects (5%) and concerns about difficulty using DPIs due to physical ability or needing to use an MDI because of a need for a spacer device (5%) (online supplemental figure 1).
Among MDI users (n=255) advice from their healthcare provider was the most helpful support when deciding whether to switch to an inhaler with a lower carbon footprint (78%). Individuals also indicated that information on how well other inhalers work (75%) would support their decision-making, as would information on cost (53%) and training on how to use a new inhaler device (49%) (online supplemental figure 2).
Most respondents indicated that when their inhaler is expired, empty or no longer needed, they dispose of their inhaler in the garbage (55%) or recycling (15%). Among participants who lived in provinces where there are government-mandated medication return/disposal programmes at pharmacies (via incineration22) (n=271), only 27% returned their inhaler to the pharmacy. In a multivariable model (figure 4,online supplemental table 4), only higher CCRPI total score was associated with returning inhalers to the pharmacy (five-point increase OR 1.33, 95% CI 1.05 to 1.72). Although the majority of patients (94%) were unaware of inhaler recycling programmes, interest in participating in such programmes was high, particularly for pharmacy-based programmes (98%), followed by programmes in physicians’ offices or hospitals (42%) and mail-in programmes (24%).

This study assesses perspectives on climate change among individuals who use inhalers. Understanding existing knowledge and willingness to change in this population is particularly important given its vulnerability to climate change and the paradoxical environmentally-mediated deleterious health impacts of the very inhalers they use to treat their condition.
We found that most people who use inhalers are concerned about climate change and its resulting health impacts (as measured by the CCPRI). Climate concern in this population was relatively higher than the general population19 24 25and concern for extreme weather events and air pollution/quality was higher than the general Canadian population.26Aligning with studies in the general population, personal experience with extreme weather events was associated with higher CCRPI scores.19 25 27Nearly one-third of individuals in our study had already experienced climate-related health changes, primarily respiratory in nature, most often attributed to wildfire smoke exposure and extreme heat. This is alarming, although not surprising, since Canada experienced its worst wildfire season on record in 2023, with nearly all regions in the country experiencing smoke and poor air quality.28Rapidly rising temperatures, wildfires and extreme heat are grave and immediate population health concerns. Given their heightened vulnerability, effective patient-facing resources focused on mitigation and safety strategies during these events are urgently needed for individuals with respiratory disease.
Despite significant concerns about climate change, awareness of the environmental impact of inhalers was paradoxically low. This finding mirrors that of a small Canadian survey of individuals with asthma or COPD,17where only 13 out of 61 respondents (21%) were aware that different inhaler devices had different carbon footprints. Reassuringly, when made aware of the issue, >90% of MDI users were willing to switch to inhaler devices with lower carbon footprints. Accordingly, the first component of any behavioural intervention aiming to reduce avoidable MDI use must start with patient education about the nature, magnitude and circular impact of the problem. Our finding that awareness is lower in older individuals, those with lower educational attainment and those living in rural areas suggests that targeted educational campaigns will be required (eg, conventional media campaigns for older individuals and materials tailored for lower health literacy for individuals with less education).29
However, given the complex factors involved in selecting inhaler devices safely and effectively, reducing GHG emissions will require active engagement of healthcare providers.30In fact, almost 80% of respondents indicated a need for advice from a healthcare professional when considering switching their device. This complex decision is well-suited for a shared-decision making approach, facilitated by point-of-care tools such as decision aids and/or conversation aids to support climate-conscious inhaler selection,31such as the British Thoracic Society/National Institute for Health and Care Excellence/Scottish Intercollegiate Guideline Network decision aid on asthma inhalers and climate change.32This is particularly important given that non-consensual switches are associated with poor clinical outcomes.33 34At the same time, given the importance of device education in ensuring safe therapeutic initiations and/or transitions, increased access to interprofessional health team members such as certified respiratory educators will be required. It is also important to note poorly controlled disease contributes to excessive GHG emissions35due to overuse of rescue inhalers—the main driver of inhaler emissions in Europe and Canada6—and moderate to severe exacerbations, which are highly carbon intensive events.36In fact, the climate impact of reducing misdiagnosis (and associated unnecessary inhaler use) and achieving good control of airways diseases such as asthma and COPD (through medication optimisation and self-management) is comparable to that of low carbon device selection.31These basic care principles must be emphasised in any patient/provider educational campaign and should take precedence over device switches given that inhalers only constitute a fraction of carbon emissions attributable to the care of patients with asthma and COPD.7Additionally, advancements in next-generation propellants with near-zero global warming potential37will significantly reduce the carbon footprint of MDIs, ultimately mitigating the need for switches solely to reduce the carbon footprint.
Beyond changing prescribing decisions, inhaler recycling also warrants prompt attention. Globally, 1.5 billion inhalers are manufactured annually.38Their disposal creates immense metal and plastic waste, releases leftover medications and propellants (which leak into the atmosphere with improper disposal), contributing to a myriad environmental toxicities (including marine ecotoxicity from DPIs).39Aligning with other reports in Canada17and the UK,40we found that most (70%) of inhaler users dispose of their devices in the garbage or household recycling (ending up in landfills). To address this, a UK mail-based inhaler recycling programme collected 20 049 inhalers in a 12-month period, 77% of which were MDIs, diverting 119.3 tonnes of carbon emissions.41Similar initiatives are required in other jurisdictions, though our respondents preferred pharmacy, hospital or physician-based programmes to mail-in programmes. We also found that those with a higher climate risk perception were more likely to participate in inhaler disposal programmes, yet only 27% of individuals with access to such programmes actually used them, despite 98% of respondents expressing an interest in participating. This suggests that any recycling programme will require creative strategies to ensure public awareness and ease of return, such as partnerships with manufacturers (eg, package inserts) and pharmacies (eg, signage/reminders at the point of prescription) or even nominal deposit return programmes.
We aimed to gather diverse perspectives from inhaler users using convenience sampling. Our sample was geographically diverse, with representation proportional to provincial population sizes and inclusion of rural and urban voices, across official Canadian languages, education and income levels. Limitations include an overrepresentation of women, limited ethnic diversity and an older age distribution, corresponding to a large proportion of respondents with COPD. Given that younger age is associated with greater climate anxiety42and engagement in pro-environmental behaviour,43our findings of high climate change risk perception and willingness to engage in mitigation strategies may be even more pronounced in the broader population of inhaler users. On the contrary, as women have been shown to have higher concern for the health impacts of climate change compared with men,26our sample may reflect a population with greater concern. Additionally, self-selection bias may have contributed to a sample that is more environmentally aware and concerned, while targeting dissemination through patient-facing organisations may have resulted in a sample that is more educated and engaged. However, it is notable that even in this sample, only 20% of respondents were aware that MDIs have a higher carbon footprint than other devices. We also received responses which challenged the veracity of climate change itself, serving as a reminder of the sensitivity with which healthcare providers must approach this topic with their patients.
Future research must consider to what extent providers across the continuum of care are currently aware of carbon footprint disparities between inhalers, and whether they consider these to be important, let alone whether they are actively engaging their patients in these discussions. Survey data should also be complemented by qualitative research capturing attitudes towards mitigating the environmental impacts of inhalers and barriers/enablers to corresponding required behaviour changes, among both patients and providers. We also note that this field is evolving rapidly, with active research and development of next-generation propellants with near-zero global warming potential.37
In summary, our survey reveals significant concerns about climate change among inhaler users, along with a high degree of willingness to consider lower GHG devices and to participate in recycling programmes, but a low degree of prior awareness of the actual climate impacts of inhalers and availability of safe disposal programmes. Accordingly, there is a need for corresponding education in the inhaler user population, through novel public-facing strategies and provider counselling. The latter will likely require point-of-care tools such as conversation aids, which can facilitate ‘green informed consent’44regarding inhaler therapy, while balancing safety and patient choice, and minimising the risk of inducing ‘green guilt’ among patients who prefer or require an MDI.12 13
