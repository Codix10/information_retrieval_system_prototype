Rapid warming in the Gulf of Maine (GoM) is contributing to widespread shifts in the abundance, distribution, and quality of marine prey communities. These shifts cascade up the food web and will ultimately affect the fitness and viability of seabirds, the impacts of which are expected to be especially pronounced at the southern edge of their ranges. Using integrated population models, we analyzed 26 years (1998–2023) of demographic data for Atlantic Puffins (Fratercula arctica; ATPU) and Razorbills (Alca torda; RAZO) nesting at the southern edge of their breeding range in the GoM. Additionally, we examined the environmental and demographic drivers of demographic rates and how these relationships may have changed over time. We found that key demographic rates in both species are linked to warming‐related stressors. In ATPU, adult survival declined with increasing summer length, and productivity was negatively associated with winter sea surface temperature anomalies. RAZO first‐year survival was positively associated with chick wing chord growth, which itself declined over time with a parallel increase in the proportion of lower‐quality prey in the diet. Summer length had a strong negative effect on productivity in this species. Finally, we found no evidence indicating that these climate‐demography relationships were changing over time, suggesting current stationarity in these species' responses to ocean warming. Our findings suggest that specific warming‐related factors, such as summer length and winter sea surface temperature anomalies have negatively impacted ATPU and RAZO demographic rates. Given the apparent stationarity of the identified climate–demography relationships over the study period, these relationships may be expected to persist under continued warming in the GoM. For cold‐adapted species like ATPU and RAZO that are already living close to their thermal limits in the GoM, these insights are especially urgent.

We used integrated population models to analyze environmental drivers of population dynamics for Atlantic Puffins (Fratercula arctica) and Razorbills (Alca torda) nesting at the southern edge of their breeding range in the rapidly warming GoM. We found that key demographic rates in both species are increasingly linked to warming‐related stressors and that the strength and direction of these climate‐demography relationships have shifted over time.

Climate change is rapidly transforming marine ecosystems, with ocean temperatures rising faster than many species can adapt (IPCC2021,2022). These physical changes are driving widespread shifts in the abundance, distribution, and quality of marine prey communities, particularly zooplankton and forage fish, which cascade up the food web and ultimately affect the fitness and viability of marine top predators such as seabirds (Hollowed et al.2013; Hastings et al.2020; Dahms and Killen2023). In many cases, seabirds are experiencing lower reproductive success and survival in response to these prey shifts (Kitaysky et al.2010; Barbraud et al.2018; Fayet et al.2021). Globally, more than half of seabird species are declining, and nearly half are threatened with extinction (Croxall et al.2012; Paleczny et al.2015; Dias et al.2019; Phillips et al.2023), highlighting the urgency of understanding how environmental changes affect key demographic rates.
Cold‐adapted seabirds may be particularly vulnerable to warming because of their limited thermal tolerance and specialization on cold‐water prey (Furness and Tasker2000; Orgeret et al.2021). In the Northern hemisphere, these vulnerabilities are often magnified at the southern edge of breeding ranges, where warming is typically most pronounced, and populations have limited scope for further poleward shifts (Jeglinski et al.2024). The Gulf of Maine (GoM) is a global warming hotspot (Pershing et al.2015), where cold‐water seabirds such as the Atlantic Puffin (Fratercula arctica; hereafter ATPU) and Razorbill (Alca torda; hereafter RAZO) breed at the southern extent of their range (Figure1A; Lavers et al.2020; Lowther et al.2020). ATPU is currently listed as globally vulnerable (BirdLife International2018), with severe declines in parts of its range, while RAZO remains of least concern globally (BirdLife International2021), but may face increasing risk in rapidly warming regions.

In the GoM, a prey regime shift in 2010 (Mills et al.2013; Pershing et al.2015,2018) was characterized by energy‐rich (i.e., high lipid) cold‐water forage fish like Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) being largely replaced by lower‐quality warm‐water prey such as hake species (Merlucciidae/Phycidae/Lotidae) in the diets of ATPU and RAZO (Scopel et al.2019; Depot et al.2020; Kennerley et al.2024). This shift had been associated with reduced reproductive success, particularly for ATPU (Scopel et al.2019). Additionally, prolonged periods of warm waters, known as marine heatwaves (Hobday et al.2016) have become more frequent and intense since approximately 2010 (Mills et al.2013; Pershing et al.2018; Gulf of Maine Research Institute2024). Earlier demographic studies of the ATPU and RAZO populations on Machias Seal Island (hereafter MSI; Breton et al.2005,2006; Lavers, Jones, Diamond, and Robertson2008) in the GoM relied on relatively short time series that pre‐dated or only slightly overlapped with the 2010 regime shifts and subsequent marine heatwave events. Additionally, the focus of these studies was primarily on adult demographic rates, leaving other critical life stages, such as juveniles, underexplored. Durham et al. (2024) revisited population dynamics of ATPU and RAZO at MSI using a longer‐term dataset (1998–2019), offering valuable insights into temporal trends in key demographic rates. However, their analysis did not incorporate environmental covariates, limiting the ability to evaluate how demographic rates are correlated with ocean warming or shifting prey dynamics.
In this study, we build upon Durham et al. (2024) by coupling long‐term demographic data with environmental and demographic covariates to investigate the drivers of population dynamics of ATPU and RAZO at the southern edge of their ranges. Specifically, we expand the dataset from 22 to 26 years (1998–2023), facilitating detection of long‐term trends and climate signals—a key requirement for understanding climate impacts on long‐lived marine species (Sydeman et al.2012; Orgeret et al.2021; Pistorius et al.2023). We also include first‐year survival estimates with associated covariates, helping to address the current knowledge gap regarding environmental impacts on the juvenile life stage (Orgeret et al.2021).
Our objectives were twofold: (1) to identify environmental and demographic factors (i.e., state factors that may affect demographic rates) associated with variation in demographic rates (i.e., survival and reproductive probabilities) of ATPU and RAZO, and (2) to assess whether the strength or direction of these relationships has changed over time. Long‐lived species like seabirds typically buffer short‐term environmental variation via adult survival, with population growth more sensitive to adult survival than to reproductive output (Lebreton and Clobert1991; Saether and Bakke2000). First‐year survival can be more environmentally sensitive than adult survival but generally has lower elasticity in long‐lived species. In contrast, reproductive success (i.e., productivity) often responds acutely to environmental stress and may serve as an early indicator of population vulnerability (Layton‐Matthews et al.2023; Durham et al.2024). We therefore predict that, compared to adult survival, productivity will show a stronger response to warming‐related changes in the GoM, and that these responses may have intensified over time, in line with accelerating ocean warming.
By explicitly modeling time‐varying relationships between climate and demography in these cold‐adapted seabirds, our study provides critical insight into the vulnerability of range‐edge populations in rapidly warming marine ecosystems. Understanding these dynamics is essential for predicting future population trajectories and informing conservation efforts in a changing ocean.
This analysis incorporates datasets described in Durham et al. (2024); however, we extended the temporal scope by 4 years to cover 1998–2023 (Durham et al.2024covered 1998–2019). Additionally, we assessed the influence of environmental and demographic covariates on adult survival, first‐year survival, and productivity (TablesA1–A3) based on prior evidence (e.g., Kitaysky and Golubova2000; Becker et al.2007; Sorensen et al.2009; Sandvik et al.2012; Schmidt et al.2015; Scopel et al.2019; Clairbaux et al.2021; Laurenson et al.2025) and ecological relevance, and retained those with strong and moderate support. Here, we define demographic rates as survival and reproductive probabilities, while demographic factors are state variables that may affect those demographic rates. Final covariates were summer length, winter sea surface temperature anomalies (WSSTA), and wing chord growth rate. ATPU adult survival was modeled as a function of summer length, and productivity for this species was modeled as a function of WSSTA. First‐year survival in RAZO was modeled as a function of wing chord growth rate, while productivity was modeled as a function of summer length. See Section2.2and TablesA1–A3for tested covariate justifications and Section2.3.1for the variable selection procedure. We did not find any significant relationships between our tested covariates and ATPU first‐year survival or RAZO adult survival, these demographic rates are still estimated.
Demographic data for ATPU and RAZO were collected on MSI (44°30′ N, 67°06′ W; Figure1) by the Atlantic Laboratory for Avian Research at the University of Brunswick (Diamond and Devlin2003; Diamond2021; Atlantic Laboratory for Avian Research2025). These data included breeding pair counts, capture‐mark‐recapture (CMR) records, and productivity estimates. Here, we summarize details for the collection of the demographic data used to estimate adult survival, first‐year survival, and productivity along with the collection of our retained demographic covariate—wing chord growth rate. Full methodological details for demographic data collection can be found in Durham et al. (2024) and Atlantic Laboratory for Avian Research (2025).
Data sets used for integrated population models. Breeding pair counts (FigureA1A,B) were derived from systematic surveys of breeding burrows. Given the differences in nesting behavior (i.e., ATPU typically nest in distinct underground burrows while RAZO typically nest within rock crevices), potential nests were categorized differently according to species. For ATPU, nests were classified as occupied (egg or chick present), unoccupied (empty), or unknown (contents could not be determined), while nests for RAZO were classified as occupied (egg or chick present) or unknown (i.e., nests with an adult present but unable to determine if an egg or chick is present). The number of occupied burrows per area surveyed was extrapolated to the entire breeding area of the colony as a proxy for the total number of breeding pairs (Durham et al.2024). Six surveys were conducted between 1998 and 2023 for each species, and extrapolated values were used to estimate annual breeding population sizes (FigureA1A,B).
CMR data (FigureA1C,D) were collected annually and used to estimate apparent survival probabilities (the probability that an individual survives and remains in the study area) for both adults and juveniles. Individually numbered stainless steel bands were affixed to chicks and adults, and annual re‐encounter events were recorded in subsequent breeding seasons (FiguresA1C,DandA2). Re‐encounters occurred through resightings from research blinds and physical recaptures at burrows and during trapping events. Over the study period, a total of 10,913 ATPU (7711 chicks and 3202 adults) and 2563 RAZO (1734 chicks and 829 adults) were banded. Each breeding season from 1999 onward was treated as a CMR re‐encounter occasion. Of the individuals banded as adults, 70.3% (ATPU) and 67.2% (RAZO) were re‐encountered at least once. Of the individuals banded as chicks, 39.6% (ATPU) and 36.4% (RAZO) were re‐encountered at least once.
Productivity data were collected annually by monitoring approximately 75–100 marked burrows per species per year. Burrows were chosen based on location and accessibility to researchers and marked with semi‐permanent wooden stakes. If a burrow became perpetually inactive or collapsed, it was removed from the sample, and a new burrow was added given the criteria listed above. Marked burrows were monitored each year for activity (i.e., presence of an egg), and active burrows were then monitored for hatch, enabling estimation of chick age. After hatching, burrows were regularly monitored (approximately weekly) for chick development (see below), and healthy chicks were considered fledged if the burrow was found empty after two consecutive weekly visits once they had reached ~35 days (ATPU; Harris and Wanless2011) or ~15 days (RAZO; Lavers et al.2020). The number of chicks fledged per monitored burrow was used to estimate annual per‐capita productivity (FigureA1E,F). In 2020, due to delayed access to colonies, data on egg‐laying were unavailable. To estimate the number of active burrows needed to produce the number of fledglings counted that year, we divided the number of fledged chicks by the average productivity rate of 0.50 (Durham et al.2024), assuming a roughly 1:2 fledgling‐to‐active‐burrow ratio.
Wing chord growth rate was recorded as a proxy for body condition, and measurements were taken during routine productivity burrow visits. Wing chord was defined as the distance from the wrist joint to the tip of the unflexed primaries. At least two measurements were recorded during a chick's linear growth phase (days 10–30 post‐hatch for ATPU: days 5–15 post‐hatch for RAZO), allowing for calculation of individual growth rates (Atlantic Laboratory for Avian Research2025).
We used summer length to represent thermal conditions in the GoM during the breeding season and WSSTA to represent thermal conditions during the non‐breeding season. During the breeding season (May–August for ATPU, May–July for RAZO; FigureA3), these species are usually tied to the colony and therefore limited in their foraging range by the need to return to incubate their egg or feed their chick (Orians and Pearson1979). During the non‐breeding season (September–April for ATPU, August–April for RAZO; FigureA3), individuals disperse away from the colony but primarily stay within the GoM (Baran et al.2022; Dodds2024). As such, thermal conditions during the breeding season may influence reproductive investment and chick provisioning (Gjerdrum et al.2003; Carroll et al.2015; Schmidt et al.2015), while non‐breeding season conditions may influence survival and subsequent breeding decisions (Sandvik et al.2005; Robinson et al.2013; Schmidt et al.2015; Desprez et al.2018; Layton‐Matthews et al.2023; Laurenson et al.2025).
Summer length and WSSTA data were obtained from the ‘ecodata’ R package (Bastille and Hardison2018; Northeast Fisheries Science Center2025) to represent conditions within the Gulf of Maine Ecological Production Unit (EPU; Figure1B) during 1998–2023. Although MSI is not within the bounds of the Gulf of Maine EPU, inter‐annual trends in summer length and WSSTA are all highly correlated among EPUs surrounding MSI (R2> 0.60). Summer length was derived from the “Transition Dates” ecodata dataset, which calculates the number of days between spring and fall thermal transition dates using the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's (NOAA) high‐resolution daily sea surface temperature (SST) data (1/4° grid). The spring transition date represents the onset of warming (increasing SSTs), and the fall transition date indicates the end of the warm period. WSSTA values (with winter defined as January—March) were obtained from the “Seasonal SST Anomalies” ecodata dataset, which uses NOAA Optimum Interpolation SST Version 2 data to calculate seasonal deviations from the long‐term mean (LTM).
Our demographic covariate, wing chord growth rate (described in Section2.1above), served as a proxy for chick body condition and was correlated with chick diet (see Section2.5below). Note that while wing chord growth rate was collected for both species, we did not find a significant relationship between this covariate and any demographic rates for ATPU. As such, the information presented here refers only to RAZO.
Adults carry prey visibly in their bills when returning to the colony, allowing diet information to be collected through feeding watches conducted from research blinds throughout the breeding area (FigureA4A; see Atlantic Laboratory for Avian Research2025for detailed protocols). During 3‐h observation periods, observers recorded each prey delivery, noting the prey species, number of items, and item length relative to the adult's bill. Prey length (mm) was estimated by multiplying the observed length in bill units by the mean adult bill length (approximately 35.2 mm; Lavers et al.2020). We converted prey length to biomass using length–mass relationships as described in Scopel and Diamond (2018) and Scopel et al. (2018) using fresh prey items dropped by adults on the colony (FigureA4B). Annual diet composition was calculated as the proportion of total delivered biomass made up by each prey category. Prey types included herring (Clupea harengus), sandlance (Ammodytes spp.), rock eel (Pholis gunnellus), haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus), hake (Merlucciidae/Phycidae/Lotidae), gadoids (Gadidae), pollack (Pollachius virens), butterfish (Peprilus triacanthus), larval fish, squid (Loligo pealeii;Illex illecebrosus), and an “other” category comprising species contributing < 1% of total biomass (FigureA4C).
We adapted the integrated population model (IPM) previously developed for these species by Durham et al. (2024) with two key modifications: (1) the inclusion of variable selection to evaluate the effects of environmental and demographic covariates on demographic rates; and (2) the incorporation of time‐varying covariate effects to assess how those influences may have shifted over the study period. A summary of the modeling framework is provided here; refer to Durham et al. (2024) for detailed methodology.
We used a female‐based IPM to jointly estimate population size, productivity, and apparent survival by synthesizing breeding pair counts, CMR data, and productivity data. The IPM consisted of three submodels: (1) a state‐space matrix population model for breeding pair counts; (2) a zero‐inflated gamma‐Poisson model for CMR data; and (3) a binomial generalized linear model for productivity data.
We analyzed the count data using a state‐space model parameterized by productivity (f), first‐year apparent survival (α1), and adult apparent survival (α2; Figure2). The process model tracked the number of individuals in each age class through time, with the assumption that individuals begin breeding at age four (Lavers, Jones, and Diamond2008; Breton and Diamond2014). Due to delayed maturity in these species, there is no explicit data to estimate first‐year survival. As such, first‐year survival is treated as a hidden parameter and estimated within the model by sharing information across datasets (Schaub and Kéry2021).

Potential covariates (TablesA1–A3) were incorporated into the model as a linear function of the demographic rates and were retained if their associated regression coefficients had 80% or 95% credible intervals (CRIs) that did not overlap zero, indicating moderate or strong support, respectively (Gelman et al.2014; Hobbs and Hooten2015). Due to the limited number of annual data points, we focused on linear trends, as our main objectives were to assess: (1) whether the overall influence of the covariate on the demographic rate was positive or negative over time, and (2) how this effect may change across years. As such, covariate effects were modeled in two ways: with a time‐invariant slope of the covariate effect, representing a consistent, long‐term relationship between each covariate and the demographic rate across the entire study period, and with a time‐varying slope of the covariate effect, which allowed the strength and direction of these relationships to change from year to year. As such, the time‐varying slope is estimated indirectly through the combination of (1) random deviations in the demographic rate that introduce variation for each time step in the relationship between the demographic rate and environmental covariates, and (2) the priors on theαβandββparameters. The priorsNormal1,0.001imply that, before observing the data, the model assumes the effect of the covariate on the demographic rate does not change dramatically from year to year unless strongly indicated by the data. In summary, the time‐varying slopes show how the effect of the covariate on the demographic rate changes over time using a linear trend to estimate how the relationship between the demographic rate and covariate evolves. The parametersαβandββgovern how the slopes evolved, and the priors help constrain this evolution and ensure that the model does not overfit or produce unrealistic trends. Finally, the model compares the observed data (e.g., demographic rate at each time step) to the predicted demographic rate, which depends on the time‐varying slope, and estimates the parameters that best explain the data.
This approach enabled us to assess not only the average influence of environmental and demographic covariates on survival and productivity, but also to explore how these relationships may have shifted in response to interannual variability in those conditions. To determine the latter, we examined whether the rate of change of the slope (ββ) was significant (i.e., credible intervals did not overlap zero). By capturing both stable and dynamic patterns, we were able to gain a more nuanced understanding of the ecological processes influencing ATPU and RAZO population dynamics over time.
We included the following covariates on (1) ATPU adult survival: summer length in yeary, influencing survival from yearyto yeary+ 1; (2) ATPU productivity: winter (January–March) sea surface temperature anomaly (WSSTA) in year y; (3) RAZO first‐year survival: wing chord growth rate in yeary, influencing survival from yearyto yeary+ 1; and (4) RAZO productivity: summer length in yeary. Because young birds often do not return to the colony until breeding age (~4 years; Lavers, Jones, and Diamond2008; Breton and Diamond2014), the final three estimates of first‐year survival were excluded from the covariate regressions. For adult survival, only the final year estimate was excluded due to known downward bias in terminal survival estimates from CMR data (Lebreton et al.1992; Sandercock2020). As described, we did not find any significant relationships between our tested covariates and ATPU first‐year survival and RAZO adult survival, and therefore only present estimates of those demographic rates and no climate‐demography relationships.
We implemented the IPM using NIMBLE (de Valpine et al.2017,2023a,2023b) called from R (R Core Team2023; see Data Availability statement for model code and data access). We specified vague priors for most parameters unless informed priors were needed to reach convergence. Specifically, the immigration rate plus noise parameter (ω) was assigned a Beta (3,7) prior based on previous CMR estimates of immigration rates (Lavers et al.2007; Whidden2016). Priors for the slope and intercept of time‐varying covariate effects were Normal (0, 0.01). For the ATPU models, we ran three chains with 500,000 iterations each, a burn‐in phase of 50,000, and a thinning rate of five, resulting in 250,000 posterior samples. For the RAZO models, we ran three chains with 300,000 iterations each, a burn‐in phase of 50,000, and a thinning rate of three, resulting in 250,000 posterior samples. Convergence was assessed using the R‐hat statistic (≤ 1.01; Gelman and Hill2007; Gelman et al.2014) and visual inspection of chains. Goodness of fit (GOF) measures for IPMs are not well studied, and currently, no overall GOF test exists (Newman et al.2014; Schaub and Kéry2021). However, for specific details on sub‐model diagnostics and assumptions, see Durham et al. (2024). Additionally, for this study, we compared the demographic rate estimates from the null model (i.e., no covariate effects) to the fixed and time‐varying models to validate the fit of the covariate models. Posterior estimates are summarized by their means and 95% CRIs unless otherwise indicated. As described above, we exclude the final three juvenile survival estimates from reported results because they are likely to be downward‐biased terminal values (Lebreton et al.1992; Sandercock2020). For adult survival, the terminal estimate was excluded from the covariate and post hoc analyses for the same reason; however, given new evidence that survival in the final year may indeed have been lower than expected, we now include this estimate, along with terminal population size and growth, to evaluate the potential population‐level consequences of reduced adult survival. Results stated are from the time‐invariant analysis with covariates unless otherwise stated.
To assess long‐term changes, we conducted post hoc linear regressions for first‐year survival, adult survival, and productivity over time for both species. To compare demographic rate synchrony between species, we looked at correlations between corresponding demographic rates. For RAZO, we additionally examined relationships between wing chord growth rate and chick diet variables (FigureA4C) using correlation and regression analyses and evaluated the temporal trend in wing chord growth rate itself. Finally, to further quantify temporal change in the effect of covariates on demographic rates, we calculated the posterior probability that the effect in the final year was greater than or less than that in the first year. For each Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) iteration, we compared the difference in time‐varying slopes between the final time step and the initial time step. The posterior probability of an increase or decrease was estimated as the proportion of posterior samples for which the final‐year effect was greater than or less than the initial‐year effect.

Our study provides evidence that specific warming‐related ocean conditions in the Gulf of Maine (GoM) may be linked to declines in key demographic rates in ATPU and RAZO. Using nearly three decades of CMR data, we show that: (1) warm winter sea surface temperature anomalies negatively affected ATPU productivity; (2) summer length negatively affected ATPU adult survival and RAZO productivity; (3) RAZO first‐year survival was positively associated with chick wing chord growth, which itself declined over time in association with the proportion of lower‐quality squid in the diet; and (4) the climate‐demography relationships have not significantly strengthened or weakened over time. The evidence for the climate‐demography relationships found for RAZO was stronger than that found for ATPU, indicating potentially closer coupling between RAZO population dynamics and climate‐demography relationships.
Overall, we found relatively few significant environment‐demography relationships for either species despite the large number of covariates tested. One reason for this is likely lack of information about, and heterogeneity within (Bjørnstad and Hansen1994), the study species. For example, while tracking studies indicate that most individuals stay within the GoM during the non‐breeding season, these results are based on limited data and indicate that not all individuals follow this pattern (Baran et al.2022; Dodds2024). Environmental conditions during the non‐breeding season have been found to influence demographic rates (Calvert et al.2009; Fayet et al.2017; Desprez et al.2018; Reiertsen et al.2021; Strøm et al.2021; Snell et al.2025) and can have important carry‐over effects (Sorensen et al.2009; Salton et al.2015; Szostek and Becker2015). However, the time lags associated with carry‐over effects can range from months to years (Sandvik and Erikstad2008; Zador et al.2013) and these effects are further complicated by the fact that individual quality (Léandri‐Breton et al.2024) and favorable conditions in subsequent seasons can buffer the effects of unfavorable seasonal conditions (Steenweg et al.2022). Likewise, unfavorable conditions in consecutive seasons can compound the carry‐over effects (Jones et al.2024; Häkkinen and Pettorelli2025).
This inherent complexity in ecological systems offers another explanation for the lack of strong relationships (or any relationships) between some of the demographic rates and tested covariates. Population models are necessarily simplifications of multidimensional systems, which have emergent properties that often cannot be determined through studying the individual components (Oro2013). However, the ability to detect environment‐demography relationships is improved when using long‐term datasets of individually marked animals (Frederiksen et al.2014; Orgeret et al.2021). Because this study draws on over 20 years of data and includes thousands of marked individuals, we can be reasonably confident that the identified relationships reflect genuine links between environmental factors and demography. As such, the negative impacts of climate‐related stressors on key demographic rates may be a signal of future impacts on population growth and persistence as the GoM continues to warm. Below, we summarize and attempt to explain the climate‐demographic relationships we found to be significant.
In ATPU, we found evidence for an average negative effect of longer summers in the GoM on adult survival in the subsequent non‐breeding season. While previous studies (e.g., Breton and Diamond2014) linked spring SST to overwinter survival, our analysis emphasizes the role of sustained warm conditions spanning the full spring‐to‐fall period. Interestingly, however, we did not find significant relationships between this demographic rate and components of summer length such as spring SST, summer SST, spring transition, or fall transition. These findings suggest that prolonged periods of warm temperatures may be more influential than a single warm season alone. Indeed, longer duration marine heatwave events can affect seabirds at multiple stages of their life cycle, such as spring or fall migration and breeding (Piatt et al.2024).
Warm SST in spring and summer may reduce prey availability during the chick‐rearing period as fish species shift their distribution, or dive deeper, to find cooler water (Rose2005; Nye et al.2009; Pinsky et al.2013; Poloczanska et al.2016). Supporting this, longer summers have been found to have a strong effect on both the abundance and biomass of many fish stocks in the Northeast US Continental shelf (Henderson et al.2017). Additionally, fish increase their burst speed in warmer water (Cairns1988; Grady et al.2019), making them harder to catch. Reductions in prey availability or accessibility in the GoM during the breeding season, especially during anomalously warm years, are supported by shifts seen in ATPU chick diets (Kress et al.2016; Scopel et al.2019; Kennerley et al.2024), reductions in chick body condition (Major et al.2021, Major et al.2024; Lescure et al.2023), and post‐fledgling survival (Kress et al.2016). When prey is not easily accessible by breeding adults, they must spend more time and energy foraging to find adequate prey for their growing chicks (Ronconi and Burger2008; Burke and Montevecchi2009; Fayet et al.2021; Lescure et al.2023). Additionally, a recent study on ATPU diet during the breeding season found that ATPU adults tend to forage on lower‐quality prey than their chicks (Kennerley et al.2024). Taken together, high SST during the pre‐breeding and breeding periods may mean that breeding adults forage more for their chicks and eat less high‐quality prey themselves. For this reason, poor foraging conditions during the breeding season are linked to reduced body condition (Lescure et al.2023) and reduced survival after the breeding period (Harding et al.2011; Cohen et al.2014; Wanless et al.2023).
Additionally, as spring transition occurs earlier and fall transition later in the GoM (Staudinger et al.2019), the critical post‐breeding period during which seabirds undergo energetically expensive molt (Darby et al.2022) and recover from the previous breeding season (Anker‐Nilssen et al.2018) is shortened. As such, a prolonged period of warm waters during the spring through fall period may reduce body condition in adults who are then unable to recover over the shortened winter period (Fayet et al.2016). Taken together, these results suggest that the duration of warming may be more impactful than the magnitude of warming. However, we note that this climate‐demographic relationship had somewhat weak statistical support.
Productivity declined with specific aspects of SST in both species, though the drivers differed. In ATPU, productivity was weakly and negatively influenced by winter SST anomalies, suggesting carry‐over effects of poor pre‐breeding foraging conditions. Warm winter conditions are associated with reduced adult body condition (Scopel et al.2019), due to reduced prey availability and/or accessibility (Rose2005, Nye et al.2009, Pinsky et al.2013, Poloczanska et al.2016), which can lead adults to defer or abandon breeding to prioritize self‐maintenance (Saether and Bakke2000). This is consistent with findings elsewhere (Robinson et al.2013; Durant et al.2003) and particularly striking given that the GoM represents the southern limit of ATPU breeding range, where warm anomalies may be more detrimental. In RAZO, summer length had a strong negative effect on productivity, while spring transition was not a significant predictor on its own. Given that productivity is measured from approximately May through July, these results suggest that conditions during both the spring and summer periods are influential to breeding success in this species. Somewhat similar results have been found in black‐legged kittiwake (Rissa tridactyla), where breeding success was negatively affected by stronger pre‐breeding stratification and higher breeding season SST (Carroll et al.2015). However, our results suggest that the duration rather than the magnitude of ocean warming is most influential. As in ATPU, these effects are likely indirect, mediated through reduced prey availability and increased parental effort (Wanless et al.2023). As conditions increasingly cross physiological or ecological thresholds, seabirds may face mounting constraints on successful reproduction, especially those at the warm edges of their range.
RAZO chick wing chord growth was positively associated with first‐year survival, consistent with hypotheses that longer wings enhance post‐fledging diving and foraging efficiency (Morrison et al.2009). We found that wing chord growth has declined over time and is negatively associated with the proportion of squid biomass in the RAZO chick diet. Squid, a low‐lipid prey species, has become increasingly common in chick diets as warm‐water incursions into the Gulf of Maine have intensified since the regime shift in 2010 (Mills et al.2013; Scopel et al.2019). However, because squid still comprise a small fraction of the total biomass in their diet, their increasing proportion alone is unlikely to explain the reduced growth of RAZO chicks. Instead, this pattern may indicate a general decline in prey availability, with squid serving as a “last resort” food source, such that an increased proportion reflects scarcity of other prey. As ocean temperatures rise, many cold‐water fish stocks are shifting northward and into deeper waters to remain within thermal limits (Nye et al.2009; Pinsky et al.2013; Kleisner et al.2017), thereby reducing their accessibility to seabirds (Peck et al.2004; Grémillet and Boulinier2009; Weeks et al.2013; McDuie et al.2018). Thus, our results may point to a warming‐mediated mechanism: decreased availability of cold‐water prey species may lead to lower chick growth rates, which in turn would reduce post‐fledging survival.
In addition to changes in abundance and distribution due to climate change, it should be noted that commercial fisheries in the GoM have historically depleted prey stocks (Cohen and Langton1992; Witman and Sebens1992; Fogarty and Murawski1998; Harris and Tyrrell2001; Read and Brownstein2003), which has created additional pressures for foraging seabirds (Kress et al.2016; Grémillet et al.2018; Votier et al.2023). Therefore, future studies should also account for the impacts of commercial fisheries when trying to understand the drivers of population dynamics in seabirds (e.g., Rolland et al.2009; Barbraud et al.2018; Gibson et al.2023).
ATPU productivity was most sensitive to climate variation, while RAZO first‐year survival was more strongly linked to our demographic covariate. These patterns may reflect contrasting chick development strategies. ATPU chicks grow slowly over 6 weeks and fledge independently; thus, productivity integrates much of their developmental trajectory. In contrast, RAZO chicks grow more rapidly and fledge underdeveloped, relying on paternal care at sea (Sealy1973; Gaston and Jones1998). This likely shifts mortality from the chick stage into the fledgling period, where it is reflected in first‐year survival estimates rather than productivity metrics. As such, our study highlights the importance of species‐specific life history when interpreting demographic responses to environmental change. Despite divergent responses to specific environmental covariates, we found high demographic rate synchrony between survival rates.
The relatively weak association between our climate covariates and ATPU demographic rates compared to RAZO was surprising. However, population dynamics of more generalist species tend to be less tightly linked to the dynamics of their preferred prey as compared to more specialist species (Closs et al.1999). Thus, the population dynamics of ATPU are unlikely to be tied to a single prey species but rather linked to a broad suite of prey types. This reliance on many different prey types is potentially masking an overall climate signal (Jenouvrier et al.2022) given that climate change is impacting fish species differently (Nye et al.2009; Mills et al.2013). Indeed, warming waters favor more temperature species (Nye et al.2009; Mills et al.2013), which ATPU can take advantage of by prey switching (Kennerley et al.2024). However, this prey switching may not be enough to offset the negative effects of exceptionally poor prey availability (Kennerley et al.2024).
Although unusually low estimates of adult survival in the final year of our study may reflect a downward bias common in terminal CMR estimates, they could also indicate a genuine reduction in survival during 2022–2023. Both species showed a decline in survival in 2021–2022, followed by an additional drop the next year. The GoM experienced record‐high temperatures in 2021, with an annual sea surface temperature anomaly of 12.27°C (Gulf of Maine Research Institute2024), coinciding with the lowest productivity recorded in our time series (Figure4C,D). These post‐2021 decreases in adult survival, population size, and population growth may therefore reflect carry‐over effects of this exceptionally warm year.
Mass mortality events caused by prey shortages have been observed in other seabird species during marine heatwaves (Jones et al.2018,2024; Piatt et al.2020,2024). However, no such die‐offs have been documented for ATPU or RAZO following 2021. An alternative explanation is widespread breeding deferral. Breeding is energetically costly under poor conditions (Fayet et al.2021) and can reduce adult survival (Hamer et al.2001; Dobson and Jouventin2010; Landsem et al.2023). Long‐lived species, including ATPU and RAZO, often skip breeding to enhance survival (Jenouvrier et al.2005; Nevoux et al.2010; Cubaynes et al.2011; Reed et al.2015; Schmidt et al.2015; Shoji et al.2015), and nonbreeders are unavailable for resighting or recapture (Kendall et al.2009). If the recent reductions in apparent survival and population growth reflect temporary skipping rather than mortality, these parameters should rebound as additional years of data are incorporated.
At the same time, climate‐driven declines in productivity and first‐year survival may now begin to manifest at the population level. In long‐lived seabirds, population responses to environmental change are often delayed by the age at first reproduction (Thompson and Ollason2001; Sandvik et al.2012). Our results may therefore represent the earliest detectable population‐level impacts of climate on these species. Continued monitoring and the integration of additional years of data will be crucial for determining whether the observed declines reflect true demographic change or short‐term behavioral responses.
Despite relatively high probabilities that the effects of environmental covariates on ATPU adult survival and RAZO first‐year survival were weakening over time (i.e., moving toward zero), there was no statistical support for a long‐term trend in these relationships, indicating that the effects were largely stationary over the study period. However, we note that statistical insignificance does not necessarily indicate biological insignificance. As such, the apparent weakening of the annual effects of summer length on ATPU adult survival and wing chord growth rate on RAZO first‐year survival may still have population‐level implications. For example, both species have shown behavioral flexibility in their foraging (St. John Glew et al.2019; Lescure2021; Kennerley et al.2024), which could be demographically buffering the impacts of climate change to some degree (Grémillet et al.2012; Killeen et al.2025). However, because the current evidence for temporal trends in climate effects on survival in either species is weak, further studies are needed to assess potential nonstationary influences of these and other climate factors.
Our results indicate that certain aspects of warming, such as summer length and winter sea surface temperature anomalies have had a consistent influence on seabird populations in the Northwest Atlantic. Given the apparent stability of the identified climate–demography relationships over the study period, these relationships may be expected to persist under continued warming in the GoM. Mechanistic links, such as the role of prey quality and chick growth in survival, or the carry‐over effects of foraging conditions on productivity and adult survival, offer tractable targets for monitoring and management.
The bottom‐up influence of prey availability mediated through warming may explain these findings; however, other factors, such as competition with commercial fisheries cannot be ruled out and should be explored further. As climate change is only one threat among many, mitigating other more direct impacts such as overfishing should be a focus of management actions moving forward through the implementation of Marine Protected Areas and fisheries closures. There is mounting evidence that these measures have positive effects on over‐exploited fish stocks (Montevecchi2023), which are crucial to seabird survival and persistence. Moreover, climate‐informed recovery strategies for Northern Hemisphere forage fish have been identified as a critical component of effective seabird conservation in the region (Sydeman et al.2021). While continued warming may reinforce existing climate–demography relationships, proactive management of forage fish through spatial protection and adaptive, climate‐informed policies may buffer seabird populations against the compounded effects of climate change and other anthropogenic pressures.
