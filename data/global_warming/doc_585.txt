Global climate warming has been threatening community biodiversity and ecosystem stability worldwide. Bipartite network is a type of ecological networks defined by interactions between two trophic level species, such as plants and herbivores, where species connections occur exclusively between these groups. However, the mechanisms underlying the effects of increasing temperature on bipartite systems structure and species richness remain poorly understood. Using 156 bipartite networks analyzed under a dynamic modeling framework, we examined the individual and interactive effect of temperature (gradient from 0 to 50 °C) and mean body-mass ratio (gradient from − 3 to 6) on the community persistence and connectance. We found that ecosystems biodiversity increased linearly with rising temperature when mean body-mass ratio was low (i.e., resource biomass > consumer biomass). Conversely, under high mean body-mass ratios (i.e., consumer biomass > resource biomass), ecosystems biodiversity exhibited a negative response to temperature elevation. Network connectance (i.e., the proportion of possible feeding links that are realized), however, decreased with increasing temperature at low mean body-mass ratios. At high mean body-mass ratios, community connectance displayed a concave relationship with temperature. Overall, our results demonstrate that the body-mass ratio between consumers and resources serves as a key indicator for predicting the response of biodiversity and structure in antagonistic bipartite networks (specifically, herbivore-plant systems) to global environmental change.

Climate warming is a major threat to global ecosystems stability through its impacts on biodiversity1. Although significant researches have identified key drivers of biodiversity patterns, the coupled effects of temperature and species-specific traits on bipartite networks (i.e., ecological networks linking two different groups of species) architectures remain critically understudied2–5. Moreover, understanding temperatures responses of both ecosystems biodiversity and network structural properties is becoming more and more important in predicting ecosystems resilience under anthropogenic global climate change6,7.
Although studies demonstrate the interactive and individual effects of temperature and resource productivity on biodiversity in complex food webs8–12, the responses of bipartite networks architecture to global warming remain largely unstudied. In addition, the interplay between temperature and consumer-resource body mass asymmetry, and its consequences for bipartite networks complexity, is poorly resolved. Empirical studies indicate that warming disproportionately increases metabolic-related biomass losses, as elevated temperatures accelerate metabolic expenditure, creating a thermal energetic mismatch where individuals cannot maintain sufficient energetic gains to counteract losses1,13–15. Such bottom-up energetic constraints translate into consumer extinction and food web instability16. Hence, theoretical and empirical work paradoxically suggests thermal stabilization: at lower temperatures, warming buffers destabilizing oscillations, usually produced by strong consumer-resource interactions in ecosystems where organisms have low metabolic rates, thus preventing species loss11,15,17,18. Furthermore, in highly complex communities, climate change modulates the relative strength of consumer-producer interactions, thereby altering the context-dependent outcomes of warming7,19. Collectively, these findings highlight the critical, yet context-dependent, roles of temperature and network structure in governing network biodiversity and functioning, while highlighting the unaddressed mechanisms specific to bipartite networks.
The consumer-resource body mass ratio is a fundamental indicator that affects the strength and result of trophic interactions, thereby shaping food web architecture and ecosystem functioning20. Furthermore, climate warming in natural ecosystems modulates species body mass distributions within food webs by altering species-level body mass21,22. Crucially, temperature and species body biomass interactively reshape ecological network structures, thereby affecting biodiversity14,23,24. Current research predominantly examines temperature-driven shifts in food web size structure to infer stability responses to warming21,25. These studies focus on how consumer-resource biomass ratios affect ecosystem stability, reporting contrasting effects from ecosystem stabilization to destabilization26–28. It lacks consensus on whether such ratios confer net resilience or vulnerability. Particularly for bipartite networks, the joined effects of temperature and consumer-resource biomass ratios on biodiversity and structural properties remain critically poorly understood, impeding mechanistic explanation of these context-dependent outcomes.
The combined effecs of temperature and body mass ratio are predicted to cascade through ecological networks, fundamentally altering their properties7,29. Warmer temperatures increase the metabolic demands of consumers, potentially intensifying top-down pressure on resources. Simultaneously, if species is predicted to become smaller, the allometric scaling of interaction strengths (e.g., attack rates, handling time, conversion efficiency and predator–prey mass ratio) and the mean consumer-resource body mass ratios that modulate them are also altered. These shifts in individual size and size-structured interactions can ultimately reconfigure the entire architecture of bipartite networks.
Moreover, this framework and predictions are based on the foundational principles of the Metabolic Theory of Ecology30,31and the Temperature-Size Rule16,29,32. The Metabolic Theory of Ecology posits that the metabolic rate of organisms, which affects most biological processes from resource consumption to population growth, scales with body mass and temperature. Consequently, larger organisms have higher absolute metabolic demands, which can influence interaction strengths by forcing them to increase energy uptake; however, this compensatory mechanism may fail beyond a critical temperature threshold. Temperature shifts can fundamentally alter the flux of energy through food webs by changing the metabolic demands of consumers relative to the production rates of resources. Furthermore, the Temperature-Size Rule suggests that climate warming can result in reductions in individual body size within populations, which in turn can influence the body-mass ratios that structure consumer-resource interactions. However, how these physiological and morphological responses scale up to mediate the structural architecture of entire bipartite networks under warming remains a critical, unresolved issue. This study integrates these theoretical frameworks into a dynamic bipartite network model to bridge this gap between individual-level physiology and system-level patterns.
Additionally, beyond simple network connectance, other structural properties of bipartite networks, particularly nestedness and modularity, are crucial determinants of their stability and response to perturbations. Nestedness characterizes a structure where specialists interact with subsets of the species that generalists interact with, a pattern hypothesized to promote ecosystems community persistence by buffering against coextinction cascades33–37. In contrast, modularity reflects the extent to which a network is organized into semi-independent subgroups of highly interconnected species, potentially localizing perturbation impacts (e.g., secondary species losses) and thereby limiting their propagation across the network38. Given that climate warming and body-size ratios are expected to alter species interactions and extinction sequences, we also evaluate these two fundamental architectural features to provide a more comprehensive understanding of how warming reorganizes bipartite network architecture.
Consequently, we developed a parsimonious yet comprehensive population dynamics model for bipartite networks that integrates temperature and mean body mass ratio based on previous frameworks12,17,21. We find that the mean body mass ratio and temperature-driven changes in bipartite networks architecture while generating diametrically opposite biodiversity responses, providing a unified model for predicting how the interactive effects of temperature and mean body-mass ratio reshape the bipartite networks biodiversity and structures. This framework thus provides an analysis for understanding the bipartite networks biodiversity and maintenance of complexity under climate warming, delivering a accurate analytical approach to predict structural properties and biodiversity patterns in natural ecological communities.
We leveraged 156 bipartite networks as initial datasets to analyze structural properties response to temperature (Fig.1). The richness of these empirical networks span from 9 to 94 species (Fig.1A). To incorporate these architectural metrics into the model and facilitate direct comparison, we standardize them to a common scale of 0 to 1. The observed decrease in connectance with increasing network size (Fig.1A) aligns with the well-established theoretical expectation that species interaction capacities are limited, causing the realized connectance to decline as the number of potential links grows quadratically with size. In contrast, neither nestedness nor modularity show a consistent relationship with network size (Fig.1B,C). The substantial variation in these metrics across our initial networks is ecologically significant. Statistical comparison confirmes that the vast majority of networks exhibit significantly higher nestedness and modularity than expected in random networks, confirming that our dataset is composed of structured, non-random networks. This variability confirms the architectural diversity of our initial dataset and mitigates potential selection biases in our models simulations, as our models are not initialized with networks of a particular structural type.
The interactive effects of warming and mean body mass ratio on community persistence in bipartite networks exhibit pronounced strong volatility (Fig.2). Temperature and mean body mass ratio exert strong interactive effects on community persistence and networks connectance as shown in Fig.2. Specifically, species richness in bipartite networks reaches its maximum under both the highest temperature with intermediate prey–predator mass ratios and the lowest temperature with the maximum mass ratios. However, the lowest species richness occurs at the lowest temperature combined with the smallest mass ratios. In contrast, bipartite networks connectance peakes under low temperature and the smallest mass ratio conditions, while the lowest connectance is observed at the highest temperature with intermediate mass ratios. Combined together, these drivers generate complex, non-monotonic responses in the proportion of persisting species. Critically, the thermal response of species persistence depends fundamentally on the mean body mass ratio: at maximal body mass ratio (i.e., very large consumers relative to resources), maximal species persistence occurred at 0 °C (Fig.2A), whereas at 50 °C, it emerges at intermediate biomass ratios (centered ≈ 0–1) (i.e., consumers and resources of comparable size). Furthermore, whether warming increases or decreases community persistence is critically contingent upon the community mean body-mass ratio. Notably, the maximum community persistence across all simulated scenarios reaches only 0.5 across temperature gradients, indicating that thermal stress alone could drive species population extinction cascades eliminating half the initial network community. This pattern is driven by dynamic reassembly of networks community composition. For instance, under extreme temperature and mean mass ratio conditions (Supplementary Fig.S2andS3), we find that rapid collapse of consumer species due to excessively high metabolic demands.

These nonlinear patterns contrast with the linear responses observed under different mean body-mass ratios, demonstrating that the mean body-mass ratio does not slightly attenuate but can fundamentally alter the functional form of temperature effects on network structure. Importantly, maximum community connectance occurs at minimal biomass ratio and extreme temperatures. Conversely, at thermal maxima temperature (50 °C) with intermediate mean body mass ratios, network connectance reaches its lowest values, showing trophic network disintegration where only resource species persist following complete consumer species extinction. This is likely explained by the high metabolic demands of consumers at elevated temperatures and the suboptimal foraging efficiency at intermediate body-mass ratios.

Temperature-driven mediated changes in bipartite network biodiversity exhibit species body mass ratio-dependent response patterns. Under low mean body mass ratios, the proportion of persisting species within community increases monotonically with rising temperatures (Fig.S1). At the low temperature and low body mass ratio extreme, we observe high connectance but low persistence. Conversely, under low temperature and high body mass ratio conditions, both persistence and connectance are low. On the contrary, when consumer biomass exceeds resource biomass, species richness declines monotonically with warming. The high temperature and low body mass ratio scenario yields the high species persistence but intermediate connectance. Moreover, at body mass equivalence (i.e., similar consumer and resource body mass), community persistence in the bipartite networks follows a concave biphasic response, namely, initially decreasing then increasing with adding temperature. Furthermore, the increase in the proportion of persisting species accelerates with higher mean body mass ratios, evidenced by significantly steeper slopes in linear regressions. Conversely, when consumers biomass is less than producer biomass (mean body mass ratio < 1), bipartite networks connectance decreases monotonically along rising temperatures, a pattern inversely related to species biodiversity responses. At elevated biomass ratios, community connectance exhibits a concave biphasic response to warming, initially declining then increasing. Concomitantly, at mean body mass ratio parity (value = 1), community connectance shows the steepest warming decline, indicating the maximal thermal sensitivity under balanced species biomass conditions. Additionally, as the mean body mass ratio increases from very low (10−3) to very high (106), the relationship between climate temperature and community persistence shift from strongly positive to monotonically negative, indicating that the mean body mass ratio modulates the effect of temperature on structural outcomes of bipartite networks—including species richness and networks connectance. These findings establish that temperature responses of bipartite network architecture are fundamentally mediated by consumer-resource biomass ratios, revealing how interspecific biomass structure modulates temperature impacts on ecological networks through energy allocation constraints. Using an extensively parametrized bipartite network dynamics model, we dissect the mechanistic basis of thermal responses in species biodiversity and network architecture. Our analyses reveal that both mean body mass ratio between consumers and resources and temperature drive intricate, interactive dynamics in both community persistence and connectance. Strikingly, these co-drivers exert divergent effects on community persistencevsnetwork connectance, enhancing species survival while destabilizing interaction networks under identical thermal-biomass regimes. To unravel this paradox, subsequent sections will be analyzed: (i) how temperature-mediated shifts in consumer-resource energy flux dynamics alter interaction strengths13,15,39, and (ii) the cascading impacts of consumer extinction cascades on trophic link restructuring1,40.
Ecosystem biodiversity and structural complexity can be enhanced by reducing the mean consumer-resource body mass ratio21,41. For individual consumers, lower body mass ratios where consumers are much smaller than resources increase accessible prey biomass, thereby elevating community species persistence probabilities under environmental perturbations. This is because a sufficient abundance of resource species is available to provision resources for consumers, reducing their extinction risk. The increase in species persistence under climate warming at low consumer-resource body mass ratios can be explained by the Metabolic Theory of Ecology. High temperatures accelerate the intrinsic growth rates of plant species, stimulating the production of basal resource for consumers. When consumers are relatively small and therefore have high mass-specific metabolic rates, this warming induced resource enhancement effectively mitigates the inherent high energy demands, thus preventing starvation and supporting a larger diversity of consumer species. This, therefore, aligns that ecosystems stability is enhanced when production outweighs consumption22,29,42,43. Furthermore, rising temperatures accelerate metabolic rates across food web compartments13. This climate warming-induced metabolic acceleration changes the allometric scaling of reproduction rates, which can result in a desynchronization of population dynamics between consumers and resources. The thermal-driven increase in reproduction rates propagates through communities, progressively increasing bipartite networks species richness as reproductive gains outpace extinction losses, particularly under mean body mass ratios, below an energetic stability thresholds (approximately log10(body mass ratio) < 0–1, corresponding to consumers being smaller than or similar in size to resources)43–46.
In addition, elevated mean consumer-resource body-mass ratios (i.e., larger consumer biomass than resource biomass) force consumers to expand dietary breadth due to energy deficits1,47. When prey diversity is constrained, consumers face mortality by starvation due to resource scarcity. Critically, warming accelerates species metabolic rates across trophic levels, increasing further the risk of mortality42,48. For example, plants species suffer compounded stress from autotrophic respiration costs and intensified herbivory, while consumers populations experience two main mortality drivers, that is, metabolic exhaustion and prey scarcity. Therefore, these synergistic stressors generate monotonic biodiversity declines under warming at high consumer-resource mean biomass ratios, at the opposite of the positive effect observed the positive diversity-temperature relationships observed at low mean body mass ratios.
While the primary focus of this study is on bipartite networks biodiversity and connectance, our models also have tracked changes in nestedness and modularity (data not shown). We have not observe consistent, strong responses of these structural metrics to temperature or body-mass ratios across our models simulations. This suggests that while these factors dramatically alter the number of ecosystems species and networks links, the emergent organization of those remaining links, whether nested or modular, may be affected by more complex stochastic or evolutionary processes not captured in our current model. Thus, future frameworks incorporating interaction rewiring rules could provide further insight into these structural dynamics.
Furthermore, our model reveals that while bipartite community persistence increases with warming under low mean body mass ratios, networks connectance decreases monotonically. This decoupling arises because warming expand the persisting species pool, thereby increasing absolute trophic link numbers. However, community connectance is constrained by its quadratic scaling with species richness22, as well as energetic and geographical limits on the total number of interactions that can be maintained. The ecological meaning of this mathematical relationship is that there is an upper limit to the number of trophic interactions that can be sustained in a system due to the above-mentioned constrains. The sublinear increase of species links fails to match the species richness quadratic term expansion, resulting in community connectance decline despite greater number of interactions. This demonstrates how bipartite networks mean body mass ratio mediates thermal responses on biodiversity and community connectance in ecological networks.
Under larger mean body mass ratios, species metabolic constraints reduce both species persistence and networks connectance as temperatures increase. The complex response of network connectance at high body mass ratios emerges from the interplay between metabolic costs and resource limitations predicated by the Metabolic Theory of Ecology31. Initially, warming increases metabolic rates of large consumers, leading to their already important energy deficit because of scarce prey (i.e., high body mass ratio). Thus, it may lead to starvation-driven extinctions of the most vulnerable consumers, collapsing trophic links and reducing network connectance. For example, at lower temperatures (0–30 °C), plant-dominated communities exhibit sparse interactions due to fewer trophic links so less dense networks, driving bipartite connectance decline with initial warming. Conversely, at higher temperatures (30–50 °C), it promotes consumers survival, triggering extensive trophic rewiring that escalates networks connectance with further warming. Importantly, peak networks connectance values occur at minimal predators-resources body mass ratios and low temperature, demonstrating how cold, size-symmetric conditions foster complex network structures by maximizing species interaction redundancy, and minimizing populations metabolic rates transfer costs across consumers-plants species prey relationships49–51. At extreme temperatures, however, if few surviving plant species thrive, they might support a limited but high network connectance with generalist consumers capable of exploiting them, resulting in a partial recovery of highly connected bipartite networks29. This explains how climate warming restructures bipartite networks by filtering species based on their metabolic efficiency.
Our work elucidates how mean consumer-resource body mass ratios and temperature both drive of community biodiversity and structure responses in bipartite networks, and this paper establishes that their effects are mediated by interspecific biomass structure. This integration provides a novel mechanism for the emergence and maintenance of complex ecological systems. However, given the rarity of pure bipartite architectures in nature, where most ecosystems form multi-trophic-level complex food webs such as omnivory, detritivory which recycles dead organic matter and alters energy pathways, future research may prioritize complex trophic food webs. Specifically, assessing how omnivory-mediated trophic networks topology modulate temperatures stability thresholds represents a critical frontier for predicting climate change impacts on ecosystem functionality.
A critical consideration arising from our work is the ecosystem context dependency of our findings. Our model parameters are tuned to represent systems where allometric scaling of metabolic and foraging processes is pronounced, which is characteristic of many aquatic ecosystems. In terrestrial ecosystems, the applicability of these exact scaling exponents might be modulated by different plant defense strategies (e.g., structuralvs.chemical), the life-history traits of woody plants, and the endothermy of many large herbivores. Future work could incorporate these ecosystem-specific nuances to test the generality of the body-mass ratio modulation effect across a broader range of ecological contexts.
Our model is grounded in the dynamics of consumer-resource interactions, specifically representing antagonistic bipartite networks such as herbivore-plant systems. In addition, the framework parameters and scaling temperature utilized in our model (e.g., attack rates, metabolic rates) are almostly derived from and validated in aquatic systems for ectotherms14,15,21. While these scaling interactions provide a well-established theoretical foundation, we acknowledge that their application to terrestrial systems, particularly involving large vascular plants (e.g., trees) and endothermic consumers, may still require future research due to potential differences in energy allocation and constraints52–54. Therefore, the primary interpretation of our results should be framed within aquatic herbivore-plant networks. Moreover, while this paper utilizes empirical network structures to ensure ecological realism, future work could complement the findings by employing dynamically assembled or randomly generated networks to further generalize the relationships between body-mass structure, temperature, and network architecture.

The allometric scaling parameters and constants used in this model are primarily derived from meta-analyses of predator–prey interactions58–61. Their application here to plant–herbivore and host-parasite networks rests on the foundational assumption that the scaling of metabolic and foraging processes with body mass and temperature follows general principles that are largely universal across consumer types, as posited by the Metabolic Theory of Ecology. While we acknowledge that specific interactions may vary, this generalized approach allows us to isolate and test the theoretical effects of body-mass ratios and temperature on network architecture, using a unified bioenergetic framework that has been successfully applied across diverse ecosystem types.
Nestedness represents a fundamental structural property characterizing the organization of interactions within bipartite networks35. As a core metric in ecological network theory, nestedness quantifies the extent to which specialist species interact with subsets of the partners of generalists34. In other words, it measures the proportion of specialist species that have an interaction with a resource shared with generalist species. While traditionally applied to binary interaction data, contemporary indices also accommodate weighted interaction strengths. Crucially, the nestedness metrics considered in this study, including body mass ratios and temperature, do not represent simple variants but differ fundamentally in their derivation, algorithmic implementation, and sensitivity to network properties36. Nevertheless, all capture the hierarchical asymmetry inherent in bipartite networks. Nestedness is quantified utilizing the NODF algorithm37in R software. For the initial 156 empirical bipartite networks, we quantify the nestedness using the NODF algorithm. We compute nestedness indices for initial bipartite networks using the software bipartite package in R (v4.0.2), with statistical significance assesses utilizing null model comparisons37. To account for bipartite networks size and connectance, we calculated the Z-score of the observed NODF value relative to a distribution of 100 null models generated using the algorithm, which randomizes the interaction matrix while preserving the row and column sums representing the number of links per species. This standardized effect size allows for comparison across networks of different size.
Modularity quantifies the degree to which a bipartite network is organized into distinct subgroups, or modules, characterized by densely interconnected species within modules and sparser connections between modules38. Each module comprises tightly interconnected sets of plant and herbivore species, with within-module interactions substantially exceeding between-module interactions. Modularity is quantified using the algorithm62designed for bipartite networks, implemented in the R software. To evaluate modularity, we implement the simulated annealing algorithm of Guimerà & Nunes Amaral, (2005) via the igraph package in R software. This method uses an iterative heuristic to search for the network partition that maximizes the modularity value by increasing the density of links within modules and reducing the number of links between models. The statistical significance and strength of modularity are assessed by comparing the observed modularity value against a distribution of 100 values obtained from null models generated utilizing the algorithm. The Z-score of the observed modularity value is used as the standardized measure of modularity. Therefore, higher modularity values, ranging from 0 to 1, indicate pronounced compartmentalization. This ecological segregation may confer resilience against perturbations by localizing natural and anthropogenic disturbance impacts, a key networks structure underlying ecosystems stability63.
The species richness of plants (SP) and consumers (SA) in the initial bipartite networks are not randomly generated for this paper. Instead, they are intrinsic properties of the 156 empirically bipartite networks used as the initial networks for the simulation. The initial biomass densities of plant species are randomly assigned values between 0 and 1, whereas consumer species’ initial biomass densities are set to one-eighth of the mean biomass density across all plant species. To ensure that the results are robust to variations in initial conditions and do not depend on a specific arbitrary starting point, we repeat all simulations for each parameter combination using distinct sets of initial biomass densities. Furthermore, the summed biomass density of plant species is constrained to equal the system’s total carrying capacity, preventing unrealistic resource overexploitation and better approximating natural resource constrains. Prior to formal numerical simulations, comprehensive pre-trials demonstrate that all cases reach steady-state dynamics by 900 years. Consequently, we conduct all numerical simulations for 1000 years to guarantee convergence to equilibrium species population dynamics in the bipartite networks. During simulations, species with biomass densities falling below the extinction threshold, namelyor, are considered ecologically nonviable due to higher vulnerability to environmental stochasticity. Therefore, these species are considered extinct and, consequently, removed from the simulation system.
We systematically examined the response mechanisms of bipartite networks to temperature across a gradient from 273.15K to 323.15K (equivalent to 0–50 °C) and log10mean body-mass ratios from -3 to 6 (in unit increments). Upon networks stabilization, community persistence is quantified as the proportion of persisting species during the end simulation phase (years 990–1000) relative to the initial species pool. Concurrently, community connectance is calculated as the realized proportion of possible trophic links (L/S2, L is the number of realized feeding links, S is the survival species). We note that this differs from the traditional definition of connectance, which uses the initial species richness to define the number of possible links. These response variables of community persistence and connectance are analyzed using generalized linear models in OriginPro 2021 to explore the individual and interactive effects of temperature and the log10-transformed mean body-mass ratio: (i) the interactive effects of temperature and log10mean body-mass ratio, and (ii) their individual contributions to biodiversity (via community persistence) and community connectance.
To initialize the model simulations, this work utilizes a large compilation of empirically documented antagonistic bipartite networks (specifically, host-parasite and plant–herbivore networks). A total of 156 unique networks are sourced from published papers64,65. These networks are selected to encompass a wide range of sizes and networks connectance to ensure the generality of the findings. The use of empirical networks as starting points grounds the dynamic simulations in realistic ecological structures.
