Plant-associated microorganisms significantly contribute to plant survival in diverse environments. However, limited information is available regarding the involvement of endophytes in responding to climate change and their potential to enhance host plants’ adaptation to future environmental shifts.Pinus armandi, endemic to China and widely distributed in climate-sensitive regions, serves as an ideal subject for investigating microbiome interactions that assist host plants in climate change response. Despite this, a comprehensive understanding of the diversity, community composition, and factors influencing endosperm endophytes inP. armandi, as well as the response of these endophytes to climate change, remains elusive.
In this study, transcriptome data from 55P. armandisamples from 13 populations were analyzed to evaluate the composition and diversity of active endosperm endophytes and predict their response to future climate change. The results revealed variations in community composition, phylogenetic diversity, and interaction network between the northern and southern groups. Temperature and precipitation correlated with endosperm endophytic species richness and diversity. Under projected future climate conditions, the northern group exhibits greater genomic vulnerability and anticipates increased threats, reflecting a corresponding trend in endosperm endophytes, particularly within the Ascomycota community.
The consistent threat trend from climate change impacting both hosts and endophytes emphasizes the potential importance of host-related fungi as crucial indicators for predicting future climate impacts. Meanwhile, this study establishes an initial framework for exploring host-microbial interactions within the context of climate warming and provides valuable insights for studies related to plant protection.

Climate change emerges as the paramount challenge confronting humanity [1], profoundly affecting Earth’s biodiversity [2] and leading to unprecedented losses [3–5]. Extensive research underscores climate change’s substantial threat to woody plants, manifesting in extinction rates of up to 30% in extreme cases [6,7]. Generally, plants respond to shifting climatic conditions by adjusting their geographic distribution to align in the most suitable climates [8] or by modifying their genetic material to acquire new biological traits [9]. Nevertheless, the inherent fragility of plant genomes constrains their adaptive capacity. Consequently, climate warming may precipitate reductions in population numbers and genetic diversity loss [10,11]. Microorganisms play a pivotal role in shaping the resilience of all living organisms to climate change [1]. Operating as a ‘secondary genome’ for plants [12,13], climate-sensitive microorganisms may serve as indicators of host response to climate change. Endophytes, a subgroup of plant-associated microorganisms, have received significant attention in recent years [14,15], with research exploring their effects on plant stress resistance, growth, and development [16,17]. However, the response of endophytes to climate change and their potential to bolster plant adaptation to these changes remains unexplored.
Serving as a repository for plant microbiota during vertical transmission form parents and progeny, seeds contribute microbial genes and functions, facilitating adaptation to local biological and abiotic conditions [18]. Seed microorganisms, in turn, play a dual role in influencing seed viability by either inhibiting germination [19,20] or diminishing dormancy [21,22]. Additionally, beneficial microorganisms originating from seeds provide protective support throughout plant growth [23]. This has substantial implications for plant population dynamics, evolution [24], and the overall adaptation of entire forest ecosystems to changing environmental conditions [25]. understanding of seed microbiomes significantly lags behind that of rhizospheric (root) and phyllospheric (leaf) microbiomes [26,27]. Moreover, environmental factors [28], including temperature [14], precipitation [29], and soil properties, significantly influence endophytic community and activity [30,31]. These factors may also correlate with variations in host genetic material and endophytic environment [32]. Projected climate change is expected to alter environmental factors and host genetic material [11], potentially leading to changes in microbial community composition and structure in response. Therefore, exploring the seed microbiome is crucial for enhancing our understanding of plant adaptation to future climate change.
Trees, given their extended growth periods, are particularly susceptible to adverse adaptations, with climate change likely occurring over an individual organism’s entire lifespan [33]. The microorganisms in trees with long growth periods contribute to the adaptation of hosts to climate change, forest regeneration, and the overall ecosystem health. A comprehensive study of pathogens and other microorganisms in tree seeds will enhance our ability to predict and manage forest ecosystem regeneration, while advancing our understanding of the adaptive evolution of trees [32]. To date, there has been greater emphasis to endophytic fungi in crop seeds than in tree species [32]. This attention gap leaves endophytic variations in tree species largely unexplored.
Pinus armandi(also namedPinus armandiiinFlora of China, corrected in International Plant Names Index (https://www.ipni.org/n/262780-1)), an endemic tree species in China, widely distributed in climate-sensitive regions, the Qinling-Daba Mountains in central China and Himalayan-Hengudan Mountains and Yungui Plateau in Southwest China [34]. Notably, it displays significant geographic variation and intraspecies genetic differentiation, resulting in the delineation of populations into three distinct groups: northern, southern, and the transition zone [34,35]. Previous investigations onP. armandihave documented substantial variations in cold resistance, phenological period, and seed dormancy between its southern and northern provenances [34]. Due to its significant intraspecific genetic differentiation and distinct provenance characteristics,P. armandirepresents a valuable model for investigating plant-microbiome interactions, with its seed endophytic strains potentially acting as a crucial reservoir of beneficial microorganisms. However, the diversity, community composition, and influencing factors of endosperm endophytes inP. armandi, particularly the distinctions between northern and southern groups, remain poorly understood. Additionally, limited investigations have been conducted on the response of endophytes to climate change.
Previous research indicates that plant transcriptome datasets of host plant species can reveal significant signatures of endophyte community composition [36]. Moreover, extracting microbial information from plant transcriptome data ensures data stability and consistency, reduces research costs, and improves data utilization [36]. To understand the composition and diversity of active endosperm endophytes and their potential to enhance host plants’ adaptation to climate change, this study analyzed transcriptome data from 55P. armandisamples across 13 populations, comparing endophyte composition and diversity between northern and southern groups. This study also investigated correlations between environmental factors and endophytic composition and diversity, and predicted genetic offset and changes in microflora composition under future climate conditions. The research contributes to our understanding of endosperm endophytes inP. armandiand establishes an initial framework for investigating host-microbial interactions in the context of climate warming.
This study employed raw transcriptome data from 55 individualP. armandiendosperm samples, with Sequence Read Archive (SRA) accession numbers listed in TableS1, previous published by Liu et al. (2019) [34], to extract microorganism information. These samples, collected from 13 distinct populations, were obtained from separate trees. The seeds were stored in sterile, sealed at -20 °C. Population distributions are shown in Fig.1A and TableS1. A rigorous procedure was employed to prevent endosperm contamination by foreign microorganisms. Seeds underwent treatment with 8% sodium hypochlorite or 2% hydrogen peroxide for 30–60 min, followed by rinsing with distilled water, immersion at room temperature for 24–48 h, and subsequent mechanical opening to extract uncontaminated endosperm by removing the embryo.

The raw data were processed using Trimmomatic v. 0.32 [37] for adaptor sequence removal and low-quality read filtering with the following settings: LEADING: 20, TRAILING: 20, SLIDINGWINDOW:8: 20, MINLEN: 36, HEADRCOP: 0–10 (The specific length was set according to the quality of raw data for each transcriptome), followed byde novoassembling with Trinity v2.15.1 [38]. The resulting sequences were aligned to the NCBI NT database (2022,https://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast/db/FASTA/) using blastn, with “evalue” parameter set to 1e-5 and “perc_identity” set to 0.85, to identify microbial sequences. Detailed microbial species information for each sample is provided in TableS2and sequence information is shown in Data1and2. To evaluate sequencing adequacy, six samples were randomly selected, and varying numbers of sequencing reads from their clean data were assembled to obtain microbial sequences and comparison diversity. The results showed no significant difference in Shannon index when the number of reads was 25 million and 30 million, as detailed in Fig.S1. Subsequently, based on the population genetics study by Liu et al. (2019) [34], the 13 populations were categorized into three groups:P. armandi_S (the southern group),P. armandi_M (the transition group) andP. armandi_N (the northern group), for the following analysis.
The fungal and bacterial species diversity in threeP.armandigroups was assessed through alpha diversity (Shannon and Simpson diversity indices) and beta diversity (Bray-Curtis dissimilarity) at the genus level. The diversity index for each population is the average of its samples. Statistical analysis was performed in R 4.3.0 (https://www.r-project.org/), using the “vegan” package for alpha and beta diversity calculations and the “ggplot2” package [39] for visualization. Alpha diversity significance was tested with the Wilcoxon test in “ggpubr”, and beta diversity significance was assessed by permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PerMANOVA) in “vegan”.
To assess the phylogenetic diversity of endosperm endophytes inP. armandi, ITS sequences for fungi and 16S sequences for bacteria were extracted fromhttps://unite.ut.ee/andhttps://www.arb-silva.de/, respectively (Data3and4), based on the genus list of endophytes derived from RNA-seq data (TablesS3andS4). Genus-level phylogenetic trees for fungi and bacteria were constructed using the Maximum Likelihood (ML) method in RaxML-HPC2 on XSEDE (8.2.12), employing the GTR model with a bootstrap iteration parameter set to 1000 [40]. The analysis was conducted on the CIPRES online platform (https://www.phylo.org/) and the resulting phylogenetic trees were visualized using iTOL (Interactive Tree Of Life website (https://itol.embl.de/)) [41]. Phylogenetic tree files are provided in Data5and6. Diversity and distribution of genera within fungal and bacterial phyla (TableS5), along with the species richness of each genus (TableS6), were tabulated and presented using iTOL. Phylogenetic diversity (PD) calculations were performed in R, employing the “picante” [42] and “ape” [43] packages. To mitigate the impact of richness, we also computed the standardized effect size of PD (SES. PD) using the R package “picante” [44]. Additionally, we calculated the proportion of Basidiomycetes in each population and using the “ggpubr” R package to draw box plots for group comparisons. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) and Tukey Test in R were applied to assess the significance of group differences. PerMANOVA based on Bray-Curtis distance was used to assess differences in the community composition of Ascomycota and Basidiomycota between northern and southern groups, while the “pairwiseAdonis” R package was employed for paired test.
The Molecular Ecological Network Analyses (MENA) [45] was employed to construct the network based on genus-level endophytic species richness, excluding unidentified species due to the qualitative nature of this study. Due to the limited number of populations forP. armandi_M, which does not meet the requirement of the MENA software, onlyP. armandi_S andP. armandi_N were analyzed separately. Network construction used data present in more than 15% of samples, employing Pearson correlation coefficients and logarithmic transformation in the calculation. Random matrix theory (RMT) was applied to automatically identify the appropriate similarity threshold prior for network construction [45]. All analyses were performed using the MENA pipeline (http://ieg2.ou.edu/MENA/), and Gephi 0.10.1 (https://gephi.org/users/download/) [46] was employed for graphical representation.
Modularity of each network was assessed using a greedy modularity optimization method. Detailed information for each network is provided in TableS7. Different nodes exhibited diverse topological roles in the network [47], defined by two parameters: within-module connectivity (Zi) and among-module connectivity (Pi) [45]. Nodes were categorized into four types based on these parameters: ‘network hubs’ (Zi> 2.5 andPi> 0.62), ‘module hubs’ (Zi> 2.5 andPi≤ 0.62), ‘connectors’ (Zi≤ 2.5 andPi> 0.62), and ‘peripherals’ (Zi≤ 2.5 andPi≤ 0.62) [48,49].
Applying the concepts of membership and phylogenetic redundancy degree, we identified the core genera in the endosperm endophytes ofP. armandi(TablesS8andS9). Specifically, the core genera were operationally defined as those present in at least one sample from each group. The visualization of overlapping genera (i.e., core genera) across the three groups was conducted using the Evenn online tool [50].
Employing the FungalTraits database [51], we annotated phenotypes of endophytic fungi at the genus level, identifying primary lifestyle types, and determining nutritional classifications. Subsequently, we evaluated distinctions in life types and pathogenicity of fungi between theP. armandi_S andP. armandi_N groups.
Geographical coordinates of sampling sites were utilized to extract 41 environmental factors (TableS10), comprising 19 climatic factors from WorldClim (https://worldclim.org/), 19 soil factors from the FAO Soil Portal (http://www.fao.org/soils-portal/soil-survey/soil-maps-and-databases/harmonized-world-soil-database-v12/en/), and three altitude factors from the USGS Earth Explorer (https://earthexplorer.usgs.gov/). The description of environmental factors is shown in Abbreviations. The correlation between these factors and microbial richness was assessed through regression analysis with the “lm” function in R. We reduced the initial set of 41 factors to 11 weakly correlated ones (|r| < 0.8) to mitigate collinearity among environmental factors for subsequent mantel analysis. The Mantel test was conducted using the “vegan” package in R, employing the Spearman method with 9999 permutations to evaluate associations between environmental factors and microbial composition.
Gradient Forests (GF) is currently widely applied in assessing the genomic vulnerability of different tree groups amidst rapid climate change, providing crucial insights into species’ responses to climate and environmental changes and facilitating the exploration of adaptation strategies [11]. In this study, we employed the GF model to analyzed genetic offset and endosperm endophytic composition offset inP. armandito determine whether their responses align under climate change. Endophytic composition offset, akin to genetic offset [52], is herein defined as a measure of the extent of adjustment required in endophytic composition to accommodate new climate conditions. Populations exhibiting the highest endophytic composition offset necessitate the most significant adjustments. The host genetic dataset comprised single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) from Liu et al. (2019) [34] (Data7), with adaptive and neutral sites identified from these SNPs. The endophytic dataset consisted of a species composition matrix generated in this study. Genotype-environment association (GEA) analyses were conducted to detect climate-related candidate SNPs using two methods based on selected environmental factors. First, we applied Redundancy Analysis (RDA) with a two-tailed cutoff of three standard deviations (P= 0.0027) to identify SNPs potentially involved in local adaptation [53]. Second, the latent factor mixed model (LFMM) was used to identify candidate SNPs [54], with the number of latent factors set to 2 according to the population structure from Liu et al. (2019) [34]. To minimize false positives, the False Discovery Rate (FDR) was set at 0.05. All SNPs identified by RDA and LFMM were classified as adaptive, with the remaining sites considered neutral. Future climate data under three periods (2041–2060, 2061–2080, 2081–2100) were acquired from WorldClim using the Model for Interdisciplinary Research on Climate version 6 (MIROC6) in Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 (CMIP6), considering three climate scenarios—SSP1-2.6, SSP3-7.0, and SSP5-8.5—representing “low”, “high”, and “very high” CO2emissions, corresponding to specific representative concentration pathways (RCPs): RCP2.6, RCP7.0 and RCP8.5. To reduce collinearity, only one environmental factor was retained when the correlation coefficient greater than 0.8 (|r|> 0.8) and subsequently analyzed using the “gradientForest” package by Smith and Ellis (2013) [55]. Geographical distribution was mapped using ArcGIS 10.6.1.
The 55 transcriptomes fromP. armandiyielded an average of 61.44 million raw reads per sample, with Q30 values ranging from 89.04 to 96.47%, and an average of 60.56 million clean reads per sample.De novoassembly produced 52,307 to 167,691 transcripts per sample, with mean lengths of 841.5 to 1267.48 bp and N50 scaffold lengths of 1418 to 1937 bp. Detailed transcriptome information is shown in TableS1. A total of 1147 Operable Taxonomic Units (OTUs) were identified across all samples. This included 469 OTUs of fungi, representing 267 genera, 153 families, and 7 phyla (Table1). The southern group (P. armandi_S) comprised 295 OTUs from 180 genera and 7 phyla, while the northern group (P. armandi_N) consisted of 320 OTUs from 187 genera and 6 phyla (Fig.1B and D; Table1). In the bacteria domain, 678 OTUs were detected from all samples, distributed among 295 genera, 146 families, and 18 phyla (Table1). TheP. armandi_S group exhibited 410 OTUs, spanning 188 genera and 15 phyla, whereas theP. armandi_N group featured 394 OTUs, belonging to 189 genera and 15 phyla (Fig.1C and E; Table1).

The alpha diversity of endophytic fungi and bacteria amongP. armandi_S,P. armandi_M, andP. armandi_N groups, as determined by the Shannon index, exhibited no significant differences (Fig.1F and G). This observation was corroborated by similar findings from the Simpson index analysis (Fig.S2). The phylogenetic reconstruction includes 260 fungal genera and 289 bacterial genera. Among the fungi, 72.69% of the genera belonged to Ascomycetes, 22.69% to Basidiomycetes, and 4.62% to Chytridiomycetes, Mortierellomycota, and Mucoromycota (TableS5). In all three groups,Aspergillus, a genus of Ascomycetes, exhibited the highest OTU richness (Fig.2A). In the bacterial phylogenetic tree, 48.79% of the genera were classified under Pseudomonadota, with Actinobacteriota representing 21.11% (TableS5). Overall,Bradyrhizobium, a genus of Pseudomonadota, exhibited the highest OTU richness (Fig.2B). Additionally, the proportion of Basidiomycetes inP. armandi_S was significantly higher than inP. armandi_N (P= 0.045; Fig.2E). Seed endophytes are noteworthy due to their direct impact on seed quality, potential transmission from parent to progeny, and presence in the plant embryo [30,56,57]. Our investigation of endosperm endophytes inP.armandi, a unique tree species in China, revealed no significant differences in both alpha diversity and beta diversity among microorganisms in different distribution regions (Fig.1F-I). These findings suggest the stability of endosperm endophytes within the endosperm habitat.
First, this stability was supported by the consistent predominant fungal and bacterial phyla across various groups. Ascomycota and Basidiomycota were identified as predominant fungal communities, while Pseudomonadota and Actinobacteriota exhibited relatively high richness among endophytic bacteria inP.armandiendosperm endophytes (TableS6). These results are consistent with previous findings in endophyte studies involving various plant species [16,58–61]. Notably, Ascomycota, commonly found in plants [62–64], particularly within the Nectriaceae family, have been reported to confer fitness benefits to the host plant [65,66]. This fungal microbiota likely represents functionally selected groups by the host plant [67].
Second, a substantial number of core microorganisms were shared among the northern, southern and transition groups ofP. armandi, further supporting the stability of endosperm endophytes in this species. The presence of core microorganisms suggests a conservative selection and filtration process by the host [58]. For instance,Penicillium, identified as a core fungal group across three groups, is associated with the production of various alkaloids [68]. Additionally, several core genera with reported antifungal properties, such asBipolaris,Alternaria,Cladosporium,Colletotrichum,Trichoderma,Aspergillus, influence the physiological activities of the host to enhance resistance [68]. Besides, the conservative and beneficial characteristics exhibited by the seed microbiota may suggest the potential need for protective layers in the host seed [23]. Notably,Sphingomonas, a core bacterial genus inP. armandi, has been consistently recognized as a common bacterium associated with host plants since 1998 [69]. Importantly,Sphingomonasconstitutes a core component of the seed endophytic bacterial community in the rice disease resistance phenotype, significantly contributing to the shaping of bacterial disease phenotypes across generations [17]. This genus has been associated with promoting plant growth and enhancing plant resistance to biotic and abiotic stressors [70–72]. The presence of pathogens in seeds has been known as early as the early 1940s [73], and we detected pathogenic fungi inP. armandiseeds based on phenotypic annotation. However, pathogenicity is not an inherent trait of microorganisms, it is context-dependent [74,75]. Additionally, as noted earlier, many endophytes in the endosperm produce resistance compounds, potentially inhibiting pathogen pathogenicity. The diversity and dominant microbiomes exhibited no significant differences across different regions with the same species, consistent with some recent studies. Similarly, inCycas panzhihuaensis, biogeography hardly exerts an impact on microbial community variation, with almost no differentiation in dominant microbiomes based on the geography of the host cycad. Additionally, the conservatively core genera of seed endophytes, comprising a relatively large number, may contribute to the non-significant differences in seed endophytic diversity.
Third, across all groups, we observed that the endosperm endophytic bacterial network consistently displays lower modularity, while the fungal network demonstrates higher modularity (Fig.3A-F; TableS7). Generally, microbial relationships significantly influence the diversity and function of microbial networks, and changes in co-occurrence networks can impact functionality and stability [76,77]. Modularity, an indicator of a network system’s resistance to external interference, is positively correlated with increased resistance [78]. In our investigation, the fungal network within endosperm endophytic strains ofP. armandiexhibits higher resistance than its bacterial counterpart (Fig.3C and F; TableS7). Additionally, our observations suggested a prevalence of antagonistic interactions among endosperm endophytes, aligning with previous studies highlighting stronger competition among microorganisms in seed microhabitats compared to some bulk, root, and stem environment [79]. We speculate that this phenomenon may be caused by the limited size and nutritional resources of seeds relative to other tissues; however, further verification is needed.
In addition to the shared characteristics mentioned above, distinct features were observed among endosperm endophytes of different groups ofP. armandi. First, in terms of bacterial taxonomy, Acidobacteriota, Deinococcota, and Gemmatimonadota were exclusive toP. armandi_N, while Dadabacteria, Fusobacteriota, and Verrucomicrobiota were unique toP. armandi_S (Fig.1E). Specifically, the higher prevalence of Basidiomycetes inP. armandi_S compared toP. armandi_N (Fig.2E) aligned with latitudinal differences in fungal species distribution [80]. Additionally, the composition of Ascomycota and Basidiomycota differed between the two groups (Fig.2F), suggesting that microbial variability is influenced by environmental selection [81]. Second, the phylogenetic diversity of endosperm endophytes varied across groups. In fungi, the SES.PD ofP. armandi_S surpassed that ofP. armandi_N (Fig.2C and D), indicating adherence to a latitudinal diversity gradient pattern, specifically, the Latitudinal Diversity Gradient (LDG) proposed by Pianka in 1966 [82]. Notably, studies of global distribution patterns of soil microorganisms have observed LDG applicability to overall fungal taxonomic diversity [83]. However, bacterial taxonomic diversity, contrary to the typical LDG, peaks at mid-latitudes and declines towards the poles and equator [83]. In this study, the sample sites were primarily concentrated in temperate areas, which explains the similarity of SES.PD inP. armandi_S andP. armandi_N. Third, microbial co-occurrence varied in different regions. Previous network studies suggested that closely connected groups generally possess lower interference resistance, and a shorter network transmission distance can expedite external interference, thus affecting overall community structure stability [79]. In fungal networks, theP. armandi_N exhibited a smaller average distance compared toP. armandi_S communities, indicating weak resistance in community structure (TableS7). However, in bacterial networks, bothP. armandi_N andP. armandi_S communities showed similar average distances (TableS7). This implies that environmental factors alone may not be the sole determinant of network composition; host selection effects may reduce bacterial community diversity and network complexity, potentially leading to decreased resistance to external influences [84]. Consequently, preserving the diversity and complexity of microbial interactions is crucial for sustaining plant diversity.
In conclusion, conservative host selection and filtration contribute to stable active endosperm endophytic diversity and core microorganisms in different groups ofP. armandi. However, differences in community composition, phylogenetic diversity, and interaction network among groups may be related to host genetic variation, the environmental influences on the host, microecological environmental differences, and the ecological adaptation of the host. Additionally, the microbial community detected in this study reflects transcriptional expression, and some microbial members in the seed may be inactive or dormant and not detected [31,85]. Therefore, the commonalities and differences mentioned refer to actively expressed in endosperm endophytes inP. armandithat coexist and may function within the seed.
Host plant microbiomes are shaped by a confluence of environmental and host-associated factors [58], and microbial activity responds to variations in temperature, humidity, and nutritional conditions [30,31,85]. An investigation into active endophytes in the endosperm ofP. armandirevealed a negative correlation between Bio6 and endophytic fungal richness (Fig.5A), confirming the constrained adaptability of microbial communities to elevated temperatures [2]. This assertion is corroborated by prior studies, Walker et al. (2018) [86] revealed soil microorganism’s limited adaption to temperature warming in both short-term and long-term experiments. Geothermal warming intensifies the growth and respiration of soil microorganisms, leading to reduced biomass, altered microbial activity [86], and significant shifts in bacterial and fungal communities [2]. Extending beyond soil microorganisms, studies indicated that the species richness of root endophytes exhibits asynchrony with increasing temperatures. Specially, a sudden decrease in root endophytic fungal OTUs richness occurs when the mean annual temperature exceeds approximately 1.40 °C or the mean temperature of the coldest quarter surpasses approximately −8.26 °C [87].
Precipitation positively influenced active bacteria inP. armandiendosperms, with endophytic bacteria richness showing a correlation with Bio16 (Fig.5B), highlighting the significant role of seasonal precipitation in shaping bacteria richness [88]. Variations in precipitation are vital components of global change, profoundly impacting plant growth and plant-microbe interactions, particularly plant-microbe symbiosis [89]. Natural variability in seasonal rainfall alters water and nutrient availability in ecosystems [90,91], with profound implications for ecosystem functioning [91–93], and influencing plant growth and biomass. Changes in plant internal microenvironments and nutrients subsequently affect endophyte diversity and activity. A previous study examining the impact of global change factors on soil microbial diversity has found a positive correlation between precipitation and microspecies richness; however, under climate warming scenarios, increased rainfall may have adverse effects on microbial diversity [88].
Additionally, our analysis revealed that the beta diversity of endosperm endophytes was primarily associated with latitude and environmental factors, particularly temperature (Fig.5C and D). This climate change also affects the host itself, especially in alpine ecosystems where temperature limits plant growth and distribution [94,95], making them more sensitive to temperature [96]. Regional temperature variations influence microhabitats within plants, impacting endophytic community composition [32]. The host may undergo genetic changes during adaptation to the environment [11], leading to changes in microbial communities, including the emergence of host genotype-specific endophytes [97].
Distinct determinants exist for bacterial and fungal community drivers. Soil bacteria composition correlates closely with environmental variables [83], while soil fungi composition is influenced by both geographical distance and environmental variables, affecting distribution patterns [83]. In this study, the fungal community structure showed negative correlations with most soil factors and positive correlations with most climate factors, longitude and latitude (Fig.5C). We suppose that temperature and soil variation across the region’s gradient create the differences in the host’s microenvironment, influencing subtle variations in fungal community composition. Temperature seasonality impacts fungal abundance and shapes community composition [87]. However, Bergmann and Busby (2021) [60] found that endophytic fungal community composition inPseudotsuga menziesiivar.menziesiiseeds was influenced by latitude and longitude rather than climate, possibly due to the distinct microclimates within the distribution range ofP. armandi. In bacterial communities, Bio6 and Soi1 correlated with community structure (Fig.5D), emphasizing the significant influence of temperature and soil on endosperm microbial communities.
Seed endophytes primarily spread through plants [97], but vertical transmission of bacteria into seed endosperm through meristem colonization has been consistently detected in seeds [98]. For example,Methylobacterium extorquens, also identified in this study, has been reported to colonize pine meristem cells before transitioning to the gametophytic stage [99]. Consequently, regional temperature changes may indirectly impact host selection. Additionally, a classic study reported fewer culturable isolates from overwintered seeds compared to non-overwintered seeds [100]. Although the southern and northern provenances ofP. armandihave significant differences in cold resistance, further research is needed to confirm whether it is related to internal microorganisms. In conclusion, climate factors affect species richness and beta diversity of microbial communities, with direct influences [2].
Over the past century, global warming, as documented by Tian et al. (2023) [101], has imposed novel selective stress on the genetic diversity of extant species, potentially diminishing species adaptability and impacting population function [102,103]. Increased temperatures elevate the risk of local extinctions and species range contractions [2]. Recent studies increasingly employed gradient forest analysis to evaluate genome vulnerability to climate change [11,104]. Our study indicated that, regardless of the climate scenario in 2080 to 2100,P. armandi_N faces a greater genetic offset thanP. armandi_S (Fig.6; Figs.S6andS9), emphasizing an increased survival threat forP. armandi_N under future climate conditions and the need for heightened genetic variation for environmental adaptability. Similarly, under climate change, the active endosperm endophytes inP. armandi_N display greater offsets, signaling the need for substantial alteration in active microorganisms to adapt (Fig.6D-H). Ascomycetes of the fungi show higher offsets, withP. armandi_N hosting more threatened Ascomycete species, highlighting increased vulnerability to future survival challenges (Fig.6G). Both hosts and endosperm endophytes face parallel climate threats, suggesting shared susceptibility and similar responses in the future. This insight suggests that variations in the host endosperm endophytic community under climate change could serve as an indicator of the host’s response to climate stress. These findings underscore the importance of plant-associated fungi in predicting the impact of future climate change on terrestrial ecosystems [105–108].
In dynamic environments, forest trees persist through migration or phenotypic plasticity, or face extinction when environmental changes exceed their adaptive capacity [109]. Under long-term climate change periods, active endosperm fungal endophytes may transition from high-threatened Ascomycetes to low-threatened Basidiomycetes, potentially affecting host seed adaptation [27]. Global warming exacerbates the spread of plant diseases [110], with heritable endophytes in the endosperm potentially playing a crucial role in shaping disease resistance and assisting plants in managing future disease risks by simulating the plant immune system [111], mediating interactions [112], or producing compounds [113]. Simultaneously, climate warming is projected to elevate the complexity of microbial networks, closely tied to community functional potential and key ecosystem functions [114]. Protecting microbial “interactions” becomes pivotal for effective ecosystem management and anticipating the ecological consequences of future climate warming [114]. In conclusion, comprehending endophytes in tandem with host responses provides new insights into adaptive host phenotypes and conservation strategies under climate change.
This study reveals variations in the species composition of active endosperm endophytes inP. armandi, a Chinese endemic species, and their responses to future environmental changes. Significant genetic differentiation and distinct adaptations exist between the northern and southern groups. Correspondingly, community composition (notably the proportion of Basidiomycota), phylogenetic diversity, and interaction networks differ significantly, although active endosperm endophytic bacterial and fungal alpha and beta diversities show no significant differences across groups. Temperature and precipitation correlate with fungal and bacterial richness, respectively. Both climate and soil together contribute to diversity patterns. Under future climate change, the northern group faces heightened threats in both genomic vulnerability and endophytic community composition. This comprehensive investigation highlights the pivotal role of endophytic community composition and dynamics as a vital indicator of host response to climate change. Serving as the second genome of plants, the endophytic microbiota enhances plant growth adaptability, providing new opportunities for adaptive evolution rooted in the “evolutionary heritage” of seeds as plant parents. These insights contribute to advancing our comprehension of plant-microbial interactions and the adaptive dynamics of endophytic communities under climate change and provide valuable insights for plant protection studies.
