Delayed sowing (DS) is an important measure for adapting to global warming, but its effect on rice quality is poorly understood. To evaluate the influence of DS on the starch structure and quality of rice, this study conducted field experiments by sowing twoindicarice cultivars on two different sowing dates in both Hanyuan and Chongzhou, Sichuan Province, China, from 2017 to 2018. The delayed sowing period experienced a mean temperature reduction of 1.87–2.99℃ during the grain-filling stage compared to the conventional sowing period. The amylose content (AC), starch content (SC), branching degree (BD), proportion of amylopectin A chain (DP 6–12), 1022/995 cm–1ratio, Z-average molecular weight (Mz)/ number average molecular weight (Mn), and weight average molecular weight (Mw)/Mn were significantly (p< 0.05) and negatively correlated with both chalky rice rate (CRR) and chalkiness degree (CD) but positively correlated with appearance value (AV), taste value (TV), and comprehensive score (CS) of cooked rice. Meanwhile, starch granule average diameter (SGAD), volume-based percentage of large starch granules (D > 8 μm) (VPLSG), average amylopectin chain length (AACL), 1045/1022 cm–1ratio, proportions of amylopectin B2 (DP 25–36) and B3 (DP ≥ 37) chains, and crystallinity degree (CRD) significantly (p< 0.05) reduced with eating quality of rice but positively associated with rice chalkiness. Compared to conventional sowing, the 1022/995 cm–1ratio, amylose content (AC), BD, A chain proportion, Mw/Mn, and Mz/Mn were significantly (p< 0.05) increased by delayed sowing treatment in both study sites. DS also reduced the VPLSG, AACL, CRD, B2 and B3 chains proportions, and 1045/1022 cm–1ratio. This contributed to reductions in CRR and CD, as well as increases in the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice across study sites. No significance was observed in milling quality of rice. Therefore, delayed sowing improved the appearance and eating qualities of rice by altering its components and structural characteristics of starch.
Keywords:Climate change adaptation, Global warming, Delayed sowing, Rice, Rice quality, Starch structure
Rice is a staple food consumed by more than half of the global population [1–3]. China is the world’s largest producer and consumer of rice and over 60% of Chinese people rely on rice as a staple food [4,5]. Therefore, increasing grain yield has become the essential requirement in rice production in China. Meanwhile, with the rapid increase in living standards and income, consumers increasingly prefer high-quality rice [6–8]. Therefore, improving rice quality has become an important topic in rice production in China.
Rice quality is controlled by genetic characteristics and environmental conditions, such as temperature, light, and water [9–11]. Of these, temperature plays a vital role in determining the formation of rice quality [12,13]. It is reported that a mean temperature greater than 23 ℃ before heading and lower than 26 ℃ after heading improves rice quality [14]. However, the global temperature is predicted to increase 1.5 °C, with the possibility of exceeding 2 °C by 2030 [15]. This increased temperature would threaten agricultural productivity at global, regional, and local levels [16,17]. High temperatures can decrease the photosynthetic capacity of leaves by inhibiting rubisco activity and damaging the chloroplast thylakoid membrane structure [18,19], which results in accelerated leaf senescence and increased plant respiration [19,20]. Therefore, the growth and development of rice endosperm are disturbed by high temperatures by increasing the grain growth rate but reducing the activities of granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS), soluble starch synthase (SSS), and starch branching enzyme (SBE) [12,21,22], as well as shortening the grain filling duration. This results in a marked reduction in the AC and SC [12,21], which contributes to significantly increasing the both CRR and CD, but reducing the milled rice rate (MRR), head rice rate (HRR), and palatability of cooked rice [23,24].
A series of management strategies have been used to adapt rice production to warming climates [15,17,25,26]. One low-cost and widely used measure for overcoming the adverse effects of high temperature on rice production is adjusting the sowing date, which alters the environmental conditions during the growth season [27,28]. It is reported that the daily mean, maximum, and minimum temperatures, and daily solar radiation before heading are significantly increased under delayed sowing, while DS significantly (p < 0.05) decreased these temperatures and daily solar radiation after heading [14,29]. This contributes to a significant (p < 0.05) increase in the AC of rice by slowing down the progress of grain filling [30]. Therefore, both milling and appearance qualities of rice are increased by delayed sowing date by increasing the brown rice rate (BRR), MRR, and HRR, and reducing CRR and CD [14,27,31]. Moreover, DS decreases the onset, peak, and conclusion temperatures; gelatinization enthalpy; and peak and breakdown viscosities [31,32]. This results in variations in the cooking and eating quality of rice.
Starch, the largest component of rice, plays a pivotal role in the grain quality of rice [33–35], and is particularly sensitive to change in temperature condition. It is reported that the AC of rice has a negative correlation with stickiness and swelling power but a positive correlation with hardness, water solubility, and thermal characteristics [36,37]. Meanwhile, the starch fine structure is closely correlated with rice quality [35,38,39]. The CRR and CD increase with increased 1022/995 cm–1ratio, BD, and molecular weight polydispersity but decrease with increased gelatinization enthalpy and Mn [40]. Both thermal and pasting properties of rice are improved with increased ratio of 1045/1022 cm–1, VPLSG, and CRD [4,23,24]. High temperatures after heading downregulate the expression levels of GBSS, SSS, and SBE, which decrease the activities of these enzymes [22,41,42]. This increases the CRD, SGAD, and proportion of long-chain amylopectin but decreases the AC and proportion of short-chain amylopectin [33,41,43], which contributes to increasing the onset, peak, and conclusion temperatures; gelatinization enthalpy; and peak, trough, breakdown, and final viscosities, but decreasing the setback viscosity [23,24]. This results in an increased CD and reduced eating and cooking qualities [27,33,44]. Although many studies have been reported for the effect of high temperature at the grain filling stage on rice starch properties, most experiments have been performed in a phytotron, greenhouse, and field condition with Free-air Temperature Enhancement facility [4,12,24,45]. However, the rice growing environments are different between natural field and artificial climate chamber. One low-cost and widely used measure for overcoming the adverse effects of high temperature on rice production is adjusting the sowing date, which alters the environmental conditions during the growth season. However, the specific mechanisms through which delayed sowing date alters the fine structure of starch to improve quality remain unclear. In addition, most of these studies were conducted at a single stie. Therefore, these cannot comprehensively reflect the influenced of delayed sowing on structural characteristics of rice starch. Research at two sites is needed to accurately evaluate the effects of delayed sowing dates on structural characteristics of rice starch.
Therefore, we hypothesize that DS improves rice quality by altering the fine structure of rice starch. Thus, we conducted field experiments using two sowing dates and twoindicarice cultivars in Han yuan and Chongzhou, Sichuan Province, China, to (1) evaluate the influences of DS on rice quality, (2) investigate the influences of DS on the starch structural characteristics of rice, (3) and assess the correlation between the structural characteristics of starch and rice quality. The findings provide insights into the efficient utilization of rice starch and the improvement of rice quality by adjusting the sowing date.
The field experiments were carried out in Hanyuan (29°29ʹN, 102°36ʹE, and altitude 912 m) and Chongzhou (30°33ʹN, 103°39ʹE, altitude 572 m) in Sichuan Province, China, from 2017 to 2018. The organic matter contents of the test fields were 30.19 and 29.44 g kg− 1, total N contents were 1.94 and 2.02 g kg− 1, total P contents were 0.54 and 0.98 g kg− 1, total K contents were 4.60 and 4.61 g kg− 1, alkali-hydrolysable N contents were 90.96 and 60.37 mg kg− 1, Olsen-P contents were 162.96 and 119.81 mg kg− 1, exchangeable K contents were 112.32 and 164.04 mg kg− 1, and pH were 6.48 and 7.30 in Chongzhou and Hanyuan, respectively. As shown in Fig.1, the DS treatments resulted in a lower mean daily temperature during the grain-filling period (22.83–24.3 °C vs. 25.16–26.19 °C in Chongzhou and 21.80–22.54 °C vs. 24.02–24.79 °C in Hanyuan) compared to the conventional sowing treatments (CK). Expect for GuichaoⅡ in Chongzhou in 2018, DS treatments resulted in lower effective accumulative temperatures during the grain-filling period (654.40–698.80 °C vs. 705.50–742.60 °C and 620.60 °C vs. 679.80 °C in 2017 and 2018 in Chongzhou, and 632.10–656.50 °C vs. 672.80.90–695.40 °C and 552.00–578.30 °C vs. 606.30–612.90 °C in 2017 and 2018 in Hanyuan, respectively) compared to the CK. A two-factor split-plot design with three replicates was used in the experiment. The main plots were split by sowing date, including the conventional sowing dates (CK, March 30 in Hanyuan and April 3 in Chongzhou) and delayed sowing dates (DS, April 29 in Hanyuan and May 3 in Chongzhou). The subplots were split by rice variety, including Yixiangyou2115 and GuichaoⅡ. After 30 d, three seedlings were transplanted to each hill with a spacing of 0.30 × 0.20 m. The dimensions of each subplot were 4.40 m × 4.50 m, and a 0.30 m high and 0.20 m wide bank was constructed at each main plot to separate the movement of nutrients between different sowing date treatments.

At harvest time, rice grains (1 kg) were randomly sampled from each replicate plot for each year and stored under ventilated conditions for 3 months to ensure stable grain quality.
The BRR, MRR, and HRR were measured using the method outlined in GB/T 17,891 − 1999 high-quality paddy rice [46]. The CRR and CD were measured using a JMWT12 rice appearance quality tester (Dongfujiuheng Instrument Technology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) [14].
About of 50 g head rice of each treatment was pulverized into rice flour with three repetitions. The AC, SC, and amylopectin content (APC) of rice flour were measured using the method of Shi et al. [47]. The protein content (PC) was measured from the N content of the head rice with a conversion coefficient of 5.95 [14].
As in Chen et al. [48], the AV, TV, and CS of the cooked rice were determined using a rice sensory analyzer (STA-1 A 18; Satake Corp., Hiroshima, Japan). Head rice (30 g) was washed several times and soaked in water (at rice to water ratio of 1:1.6) for 30 min before cooking. The mixture was cooked in an electric rice cooker for 30 min and kept warm for 10 min. The cup was then placed in the STA-1 A device, which contains its own cooling apparatus, for 2 h. The taste value of the cooked rice was determined as follows: 7 g of cooked rice was removed from the cup at room temperature (20–25 °C), placed in a stainless steel sample ring and a rice cake sample was formed using a cooked rice press. The sample was placed in a measuring tank and inserted into the eating meter within the device. Each side of the rice sample was evaluated once. Hence, two measurements were acquired per sample.
For each treatment, starch samples were isolated from the head rice of 2018 rice with three repetitions according to the method of Deng et al. [40]. Head rice (20 g) was soaked in 100 mL of ultrapure water containing sodium metabisulfite and 10 mg g− 1alkaline protease at 42 °C for 24 h. The samples were homogenized and passed through a 200-mesh sieve. The slurry was collected and allowed to stand for 12 h. This step was repeated until purified starch was obtained. The starch samples were naturally dried and stored at room temperature until further use.
Starch granule morphology and size distribution were measured according to the methods of Deng et al. [40]. A 100 mg starch sample was covered with gold, and then observed and photographed using a Zeiss Merlin Compact scanning electron microscope (SEM; Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). Starch granule size was estimated using a Mastersizer 3000 laser diffraction particle size analyzer (Malvern Instruments Ltd., Worcestershire, UK) at a range of 0.1–3500 μm.
The branched-chain length distribution of amylopectin and its branching degree were analyzed using the methods of Li et al. [49]. and Zou et al. [50]. The branched-chain length distribution of amylopectin was measured using an ICS-5000 high–performance anion-exchange chromatograph (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) using a Dionex™ CarboPac™ PA 10 anion-exchange column. A Bruker BioSpin GmbH nuclear magnetic resonance spectrometer (Bruker, Rheinstetten, Germany) was used to measure the branching degree of starch samples.
According to the methods described by Deng et al. [40], the XRD of rice starch was measured using an X’Pert Pro X-ray diffractometer (PANalytical, Almelo, Netherlands) to determine the XRD pattern. CuK α was used as the X-ray source with 0.154-nm filtered radiation. A sample weighing 100 mg was scanned in the 2θ angle of 5–60° at a rate of 4° min− 1and a step size of 0.02°. Crystallinity degree was calculated using the MDI-Jade 5.0 software (Material Data, Inc., Livermore, CA, USA).
The FTIR spectrum of starch was scanned using a Nicolet iZ-10 FTIR instrument (Thermo Fisher Scientific) following the method of Deng et al. [40]. Starch samples (5 mg) were mixed with 250 mg KBr and pressed into film-coated tablets. The KBr was considered as the background of the tablet. Wavenumbers from 400 to 4000 cm− 1were measured at a spectral resolution of 4 cm− 1over 32 scans.
The molecular weight distribution of the rice starch was measured using a gel permeation chromatography-refractive index-multiangle laser light scattering detector (GPC-RI-MALLS) following the procedure of Gao et al. [51]. and Deng et al. [40]. The main analysis conditions were as follows: RI: Optilab Trex (Wyatt, Santa Barbara, CA, USA), MALS: DAWN HELEOSII (Wyatt, Santa Barbara, CA, USA), and Pump: Series 1500 Pump, waters. Three analytical column models (Ohpak SB-805 HQ (300 × 8 mm), Ohpak SB-804 HQ (300 × 8 mm), and Ohpak SB-803 HQ (300 × 8 mm)) were used at 60 ℃ with a 0.4 mL/min flow rate.
Sample characterization, measurements were performed in triplicate unless otherwise specified. Results were expressed as means ± standard deviations. Multi-factor analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to analyze the influence of sowing date on the starch structure and quality of rice using SPSS version 22.0 (IBM, Inc., Chicago, IL, USA). The least significant difference test (LSD) was used to measure the difference (p< 0.05) between the means of each treatment. Person’s correlation type was used to analyzed the correlation analysis between rice quality and structural characteristics of rice starch using Origin version 2022 (OriginLab, Northampton, MA, USA). Origin version 2022 (OriginLab, Northampton, MA, USA) was used to create the graphs.
All variables were significantly (p< 0.01) affected by location, variety, and sowing date (excluding brown rice rate) (Tables1and2). Significant (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) influences of interaction between variety and sowing date were observed on the CD, AV, and CS of cooked rice. Significant (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) effects of interactions of location, variety, and sowing date were observed on the BRR, CD, AV, and CS of cooked rice. The BRR, AV, TV, and CS in 2017 were significant (p< 0.05) higher than those in 2018, whereas HRR was significant (p< 0.05) lower. Hanyuan demonstrated significant (p< 0.05) higher MRR, HRR, AV, TV, and CS than Chongzhou, whereas CRR and CD were significant (p< 0.05) lower. The BRR, MRR, HRR, CRR, and CD of Yixiangyou2115 were significant (p< 0.05) lower than those of GuichaoⅡ, while AV, TV, and CS were significant (p< 0.05) higher. In 2017 and 2018, BRR and HRR were significantly (p< 0.05) higher in Hanyuan than those in Chongzhou, whereas CRR and CD were significantly (p< 0.05) lower. With the exception of BRR, MRR, and HRR of Yixiangyou 2115 in Chongzhou and BRR of Yixiangyou2115 in Hanyuan in 2018, DS had no significant influence on the milling quality (including BRR, MRR, and HRR). However, DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased CRR and CD of two cultivars at both study sites. Moreover, DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the AV (except for GuichaoⅡ in Hanyuan in 2018), TV, and CS of cooked rice across study sites and cultivars. These results show that DS improved the appearance and eating qualities of rice.

The morphologies of starch granules in all samples were similar: polyhedral and irregular shapes and sharp angles and edges (Figs.2A–B). Rice grown at a delayed sowing date had fewer starch granules with pitted and uneven surfaces than that at an early sowing date. Starch granule size distribution is presented in Table4. Significant (p< 0.01) influences of location, variety, sowing date, and interactions between location and sowing date were observed on all tested variables. Significant (p< 0.01) influences of interactions between location and variety were observed on all tested variables except for the number-based percentage of large starch granules (D > 8 μm). Additionally, there were significant (p< 0.01) influences of interactions among location, variety, and sowing date on all tested variables, except for the SGAD. The small (D ≤ 4 μm) and large starch granule average diameter (SSGAD and LSGAD) in Hanyuan were significantly (p< 0.05) lower than those in Chongzhou, which resulted in a decrease in the SGAD in Hanyuan. Yixiangyou2115 demonstrated significantly (p< 0.05) lower the volume-and surface area proportions of small and large starch granules and greater the percentage of medium (4 < D ≤ 8 μm) starch granules than GuichaoⅡ. This led to increasing the SSGAD but decreasing the MSGAD and LSGAD, which contributed to decreasing the SGAD in Yixiangyou2115. Compared with CK, DS significantly (p< 0.05) reduced the volume-, number-, and surface area proportions of small and large starch granules and increased the percentage of medium starch granules in samples obtained from both sites. This result increased the SSGAD but reduced the LSGAD, while the effect of DS on the MSGAD was slightly variable. For example, the delayed sowing date significantly (p< 0.05) increased the MSGAD for Yixiangyou2115 in Hanyuan and GuichaoⅡ in Chongzhou but not for Yixiangyou2115 in Chongzhou and GuichaoⅡ in Hanyuan. The data suggest that a delayed sowing date decreased SGAD.

Compared with CK, DS increased the proportion of amylopectin branch chains with a DP ranging from 6 to 18, whereas it decreased the proportion of amylopectin branch chains with a DP ranging from 20 to 60, in both the tested cultivars at both sites (Fig.3). The delayed sowing method resulted in a different distribution of amylopectin branch chains with varying DPs. The amylopectin chain length distribution was divided into four types: chains A (DP 6–12), B1 (DP 13–24), B2 (DP 25–36), and B3 (DP ≥ 37) [45]. The sowing date significantly (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) affected all tested variables except for the ratio of the B2 chain (Table5). DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the ratio of the A chain, but reduced the B2 and B3 chains ratios, except for the B2 chain ratio in GuichaoⅡ cultivar in Hanyuan. Compared with CK, DS resulted in a decrease in the AACL at both sites. The location, variety, sowing date, and the interaction between variety and sowing date significantly (p< 0.01) influenced the BD (Table6). The BD was significantly (p< 0.05) higher in Hanyuan than Chongzhou. Yixiangyou2115 exhibited significantly (p< 0.05) greater BD compared with GuichaoⅡ. DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the BD at both sites compared with that of the CK. These results suggest that the decrease in the ratio of long chains and the increase in the ratio of short chains for the delayed sowing date contributed to the increase in BD.

The XRD patterns of rice starch samples grown at different locations showed a typical A-type diffraction pattern (Fig.4–A). This phenomenon suggests that the starch crystal type remains unchanged despite DS but the crystallinity and stability of rice starch crystals are affected. Location, sowing date, and its interactions significantly (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) influenced the CRD (Table6). A significant difference was observed on the CRD, with Hanyuan showing significantly (p< 0.05) lower value than Chongzhou. DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased the CRD compared with CK. DS resulted in a significant (p< 0.05) decrease in the CRD of two varieties at both sites, when compared with that of the CK.

The crystalline and amorphous regions were measured using the 1045/1022 cm–1and 1022/995 cm–1ratios, respectively [50,51]. The transmittance FTIR spectra of the starch samples (Fig.4–B) showed no significant differences. However, the 1045/1022 cm–1ratio was significantly (p< 0.01) affected by variety, and the 1045/1022 and 1022/995 cm–1ratios were significantly (p< 0.01) influenced by the sowing date (Table6). The 1045/1022 cm–1ratio of Yixiangyou2115 was significantly (p< 0.05) higher than that of GuichaoⅡ, whereas the 1022/995 cm–1ratio was significantly (p< 0.05) lower. DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased the 1045/1022 cm–1ratio, but increased the 1022/995 cm–1proportion at both sites. The results indicate that delayed sowing reduced the short-range ordered structure and increased the amorphous structure in the external region of starch granules, which is in accordance with the lower CRD observed in the XRD analysis.
Sowing date, location (except for Mn), and the interaction between location and variety (except for Mn) had a significant (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) influence on the molecular weight characteristics of rice starch, namely Mn, peak molecular weight (Mp), Mw, Mz, Mw/Mn, Mz/Mn, and Z-average root mean square radius (Rz) (Table7). Additionally, interactions between variety and sowing date, and location, variety, and sowing date significantly (p< 0.05) influenced the Mp. The Mp, Mw, Mz, Mw/Mn, Mz/Mn, and Rz were significantly (p< 0.05) higher in Hanyuan than Chongzhou. The Mp and Mz were significantly (p< 0.05) lower in Yixiangyou2115 compared with GuichaoⅡ. DS markedly increased the Mp, Mw, and Mz and decreased the Mn compared with those of the CK. Therefore, the Mw/Mn and Mz/Mn ratios of both varieties significantly (p< 0.05) increased at both sites. Additionally, DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the Rz of both varieties (except for Yixiangyou2115 in Chongzhou) at both sites.

Temperature plays a vital role in determining the formation of rice quality [12,13]. It has been predicted that rice production will frequently and severely face high temperature in the future [14,15]. Therefore, it is of great significance to explore approaches for reducing the impact of high temperature on rice. This study evaluated the impact of delayed sowing dates on rice quality and starch structural characteristics by using a paddy experiment, with the objective of improving rice grain quality in southwest China by adjusting sowing dates accordingly.
Rice quality is controlled by the component and structure of its starch, which constitutes up to 90% of polished rice grains [34,52,53]. In this study, DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased APC and increased AC, leading to an increase in the SC and AAR in both varieties at both study sites (Table3). This is consistent with the 21.80–24.30 °C lower mean temperatures we recorded during the grain-filling period under delayed sowing (Fig.1), which is in agreement with the results reported by Deng et al. [14], who reported that DS reduced mean temperatures after heading. Lower temperatures after heading caused by delayed sowing date may have increased the GBSS activity and enhanced the expression of SBEIIb [22,41], leading to a marked increase in the AC [22,33]. This resulted in significantly increasing the SC and AAR. Moreover, DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased the PC, possibly due to the lower temperatures during grain filling, which decreased gene transcription connected with protein disulfide isomerase, glutenin, prolamin, and globulin, leading to decreasing the content of storage protein and amino acid [14,54].
This study indicates that rice grown at a later sowing date had fewer starch granules with pitted and uneven surfaces than rice grown at an earlier sowing date (Figs.2A–H). Lower temperatures caused by delayed sowing date may suppress the expression levels of α-amylase-encoding genes (i.e.,Amy 1 A,Amy 1 C,Amy 3 A,Amy 3D, andAmy 3E) and α-amylase activity, contributing to decreasing the formation of starch granules with pitted and uneven surfaces [55,56]. Additionally, elevated temperatures at the middle-late grain filling stage decreased the activities of enzymes responsible for synthesizing starch in rice endosperms, which might impede the proliferation and development of small starch granules that begin forming in the endosperms during the late grain filling stage [57]. DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased the percentages of small and large starch granules by number, volume, and surface area, which contributed to decreasing the SGAD (Table3) [45,58]. This was mainly attributed to the decreased percentages of large starch granules by number, volume, and surface area under the lower temperature during grain filling [22,58].
According to the literature, higher starch crystallinity is attributed to amylopectin, particularly intermediate and longer amylopectin branch chains and larger granules, although amylose disrupts the crystalline packing of amylopectin [45,59]. Our results suggest that DS significantly (p< 0.05) reduced the ratios of B2 and B3 chains and increased the A chain proportion, which contributed to a reduction in the AACL (Table4). An increase in the expression levels of SSS, GBSS, and SBE led to higher activities of these enzymes at lower temperatures during grain filling [22,42,60]. Additionally, substantial starch branching indicates many branch points [50]. DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the BD of both varieties at both sites (Table4), which might be due to the reduced long chains ratio and increased short chains ratio in the delayed sowing date (Table4) [23]. Moreover, DS significantly (p< 0.05) decreased the 1045/1022 cm–1ratio and CRD at both sites (Table4). The lower temperatures during grain filling on the delayed sowing date might decrease the B2 and B3 chains proportions and increase the A chain proportion and AC [24,45], which resulted in a decrease in the CRD and short-range ordered structure in the external region of starch granules (Table4). These resulted in decreasing the stability of starch crystals. Additionally, DS increased the Mw/Mn and Mz/Mn of the two cultivars at both sites, suggesting a wider starch molecular weight distribution (polydispersity) in the crops sown late at lower temperatures (Table4) [40,50]. We hypothesize that this greater polydispersity may physically inhibit the regular packing of amylose and amylopectin chains [61], contributing to the observed decrease in crystallinity.
DS has been shown to enhance the cooking, texture, milling, and appearance qualities of rice by reducing the temperature in paddy fields [27,28,32]. Chalkiness is influenced by starch synthesis and the fine structural characteristics of starch [40,62–64]. The variety with shorter chains of amylopectin was shown to have a better taste than other types of cooked rice varieties [24]. Studies have indicated that chalkiness levels increase with mean, maximum, and minimum temperatures during grain filling [10,14]. This study found that DS significantly (p< 0.05) reduced the CRR and CD but increased the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice, resulting in overall improvements in the appearance and eating qualities of rice (Table1). Meanwhile, the 1022/995 cm–1ratio, AC, SC, Mw/Mn, A chain proportion, BD, and Mz/Mn significantly (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) decreased with the CRR and CD, but increased with the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice (Fig.5). The SGAD, VPLSG, AACL, 1045/1022 cm–1ratio, CRD, and B2 and B3 chains ratios exhibited significantly (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) positive correlations with the CRR and CD, and negative correlations with the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice (Fig.5). Crucially, we found a direct and highly significant (p< 0.05 orp< 0.01) correlation between the key quality parameters (CRR, CD, AV, TV, CS) and the daily maximum, minimum, and mean temperatures during grain filling (Table8), providing robust statistical support for temperature as the primary environmental driver mediating the effect of sowing date. The above results imply that lower temperatures caused by delayed sowing date might prolong the grain filling duration but decrease the seed setting rate [14,65,66], increase the AC and A chain proportion [14,24], and reduce the ratios of B2 and B3 chains, SGAD [45], and VPLSG. These changes increased the 1022/995 cm–1ratio, Mw/Mn, BD, and Mz/Mn, and decreased the CRD and 1045/1022 cm–1ratio [23], which helped form the regular and closely packed starch granules [10,57,65]. These factors led to a reduction in the CD of rice and an increase in the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice, improving the appearance and eating qualities of rice.

From the above discussion, it reveals that the delayed sowing treatments resulted in a lower mean daily temperature during the grain-filling period compared to the conventional sowing treatments. The AC, 1022/995 cm–1ratio, BD, A chain proportion, Mw/Mn, and Mz/Mn were significantly (p< 0.05) increased, but VPLSG, AACL, CRD, B2 and B3 chains proportions, SGAD, and 1045/1022 cm–1ratio were significantly (p< 0.05) reduced by DS. This contributed to reductions in CRR and CD, as well as increases in the AV, TV, and CS of cooked rice across study sites, resulting in improving the appearance and eating qualities of rice. This approach is used as a simple and easy practice to achieve high rice quality by altering structural characteristics of starch.
However, the potential limitation is that the starch structural characteristics of rice were performed on samples from 2018. Due to the lack of consistency between years, the results of the one-year experiment may be limited in their representativeness. However, this experiment was conducted at two locations, which is equivalent to conducting repeated trials and thus also has representational significance. Additionally, we are currently in the process of collecting a second year of data to further strengthen our findings in a future publication.
Climate warming influences the phenology, biomass, grain yield, and quality of rice. Our results showed that delayed sowing can optimize the temperature by decreasing mean temperatures during grain filling. Compared with CK, DS significantly (p< 0.05) increased the AC, 1022/995 cm–1ratio, BD, A chain proportion, Mw/Mn, and Mz/Mn, but reduced the VPLSG, AACL, CRD, B2 and B3 chains proportions, SGAD, and 1045/1022 cm–1ratio. These led to reductions in the CRR and CD, as well as increases in the AV, V, and CS of cooked rice across study sites, respectively. The above results contributed to an improvement in the appearance and eating qualities of rice. This study provides highlights the DS as a simple and easy strategy to achieve high rice quality by altering structural characteristics of starch. This is helpful for breeder producing suitable rice varieties that adapt to changes in the environment. Further studies are required to investigate the differences in endogenous enzyme activity, gene expression, and metabolomics in rice quality by delayed sowing date.
