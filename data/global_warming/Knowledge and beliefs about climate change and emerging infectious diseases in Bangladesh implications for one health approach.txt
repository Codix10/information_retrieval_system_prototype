Bangladesh is a nation highly vulnerable to the detrimental effects of climate change on infectious diseases. Research on general population’s awareness and knowledge of this issue remains limited, making the One Health approach challenging due to factors such as low socioeconomic status, limited public health infrastructure, and gaps in climate-health literacy. To prevent and control climate-sensitive infectious diseases in Bangladesh, this study aimed to (i) assess knowledge and beliefs concerning climate change and infectious diseases and (ii) recommend One Health policies to reduce the burden of diseases.
A cross-sectional descriptive study was conducted among 1,905 participants in the northern region of Bangladesh from February to April 2024.
Survey data were collected using a pretested and structured questionnaire. To assess climate change knowledge, respondents were asked to accurately identify infectious diseases from a list of climate-sensitive infectious diseases, risk factors that increase the likelihood of infectious diseases, methods of transmission, signs and symptoms, and management and treatment. To assess identification of infectious diseases, respondents were asked about malaria, dengue, zika, chikungunya, tuberculosis, Lyme disease, influenza, SARS (severe acute respiratory syndrome), and HIV (human immunodeficiency virus). Participants’ knowledge was classified as high (scores above 75%), moderate (50% to 75%), and low (below 50%), and beliefs were categorized as sound (scores of 80% or more) and poor (scores of less than 80%). Descriptive statistics and ordinal logistic regression were used to analyze the data.
Of 1,905 valid responses, 70.9% of the respondents knew that climate change and infectious disease outbreaks are related. Only about a third of the respondents (27.9%) reported receiving information about climate change updates and methods to mitigate infectious disease risks. More than half of study respondents had good knowledge about climate change (57.7%). However, fewer were knowledgeable about emerging infectious diseases (21.2%) and about the effects of climate change on infectious diseases (30.3%). Place of residence (OR: 1.3, 95% CI: 1.1–1.6), gender (OR: 1.5, 95% CI: 1.2–1.8), income (OR: 1.5, 95% CI: 0.9–1.8), and education (OR: 11.4, 95% CI: 8.1–16.4) were significantly associated with respondents’ level of knowledge (P< 0.05) about climate change and how it affects infectious diseases.
This study offers an overview of public perception in Bangladesh to help prevent and control climate-sensitive infectious diseases. The results can be incorporated into the One Health approach to develop integrated and dynamic that promote education, strengthen surveillance, and address socioeconomic disparities.

There is a globally recognized increase in the burden and distribution of human infectious diseases linked to climate change [1,2] causing infectious diseases to remain a significant challenge within the One Health model [3–5]. Climate change impacts the fundamental spatial and seasonal limits of infectious disease transmission, and the survival, reproduction, dispersion, and transmission of disease pathogens, vectors, and hosts depend on climatic and meteorological conditions [2–7]. Over half (58%) of human pathogenic diseases have been exacerbated by climate hazards, including warming, flooding, drought, and storms [8]. Vulnerable populations, especially in low- and middle-income countries, face higher risks due to limited healthcare infrastructure and greater exposure to environmental hazards [9]. The One Health framework offers a promising, integrated approach to addressing these complex challenges by fostering collaboration among human, animal, and environmental health sectors [10]. One Health supports early warning and prevention through ecosystem-based surveillance [10]. Strengthening health equity via One Health includes precise resource allocation in low-income regions, cost-effective disease interventions, and sharing best practices. Given the multitude of variables involved in the dynamics of emerging infectious diseases, the One Health approach is essential for managing and preventing disease outbreaks [5,10]. However, significant gaps remain in research, policy, and implementation—particularly in the most at-risk regions and among the most vulnerable populations. Addressing these gaps through expanded research, cross-sectoral collaboration, and equitable adaptation and mitigation strategies is essential for building resilient and sustainable health systems in a warming world.
Studies have reported the spatio-temporal impact of climate variability on infectious disease transmission, including vector-borne, rodent-borne, food-borne, and water-borne diseases [11–13].
Although there are several global public health policies and initiatives to lower the health burden of infectious diseases, the present strategies, policies, recommendations, and measures are not explicitly designed to tackle climate-sensitive infectious diseases [11,14]. This is particularly evident in developing nations, such as Bangladesh, with low socioeconomic status (SES) and under-developed healthcare systems and workforce [14–17]. The country’s dense population, frequent flooding, and tropical climate create ideal conditions for the spread of infectious diseases. Bangladesh continues to face significant challenges from infectious disease outbreaks, including dengue, cholera, Nipah virus, COVID-19, and Zika [6],]. The country has experienced a dramatic rise in dengue outbreaks, with the 2023 outbreak being the largest on record [21–23]. In 2023 alone, there were over 321,000 reported cases and 1,705 deaths, making it the deadliest dengue season in the country’s history. The outbreaks are driven by the spread of different dengue virus variants (DENV-2, DENV-3, and previously DENV-4) [21], and are closely linked to climate factors such as increased rainfall and temperature [6,8,24,25]. Bangladesh is regarded as an endemic area for cholera, with an estimated annual incidence exceeding 100,000 cases, and is among the countries with the highest cholera risk [26]. Cholera continues to pose a significant risk, particularly during the monsoon season and following floods, due to the contamination of water sources. Nipah virus is a deadly zoonotic disease that has caused several outbreaks in Bangladesh since its first identification there in 2001 [27]. Outbreaks are sporadic but have high fatality rates, making Nipah a significant public health concern. Like the rest of the world, Bangladesh was heavily impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, which strained the healthcare system and affected the management of other infectious diseases [28]. Zika virus has been detected in Bangladesh, particularly in the capital, Dhaka [29]. While outbreaks have not yet reached the scale of dengue or cholera, the presence of the virus highlights the risk of future outbreaks and underscores the need for effective surveillance.
Climate change is a profound and growing driver of emerging infectious diseases, with the greatest impacts on the general population in climate-vulnerable countries, including Bangladesh [30]. Although scientific evidence links climate change and infectious diseases, limited research exists on the general population’s awareness and knowledge of this topic. One of the few such studies was conducted among healthcare workers at the Chinese Center for Disease Control [31]. Results indicated that only one-third of the workers had sufficient knowledge about how climate change affects infectious diseases, highlighting the need for specialized training and educational initiatives on One Health issues among healthcare workers [31,32]. Another similar study assessing knowledge, attitudes, and practices (KAP) among the public and government officials in Laos and Thailand found a low level of KAP regarding climate change and dengue [33]. Similarly, a multi-country study focusing on Myanmar, the Netherlands, Spain, the United Kingdom, and the United States found a knowledge gap among the general public from different occupational and national backgrounds about the impact of climate change on infectious diseases [3]. These previous studies indicate a generally low knowledge and awareness about climate change and its association with human health, particularly among certain groups.
Bangladesh’s geographical position and low-lying deltaic terrain make it one of the most vulnerable countries to both climate change and infectious disease outbreaks. The northern region is particularly susceptible due to its pronounced climate vulnerability and historical prevalence of infectious diseases, as well as a notable absence of regional data for targeted health interventions. Bangladesh also lacks the necessary regional data to create an effective climate-sensitive infectious disease management and prevention strategy. To identify the gaps in knowledge, it is important to assess the public sentiment about the effects of climate change on infectious diseases and the appropriate measures for disease prevention.
This study is the first known cross-sectional survey study conducted in the northern region of Bangladesh to evaluate knowledge and beliefs about climate change and a set of infectious diseases, (Malaria, Dengue, Zika, Chikungunya, Tuberculosis, Lyme disease, Influenza, SARS (severe acute respiratory syndrome), and HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) among the public among diverse socioeconomic backgrounds. While not all diseases addressed are endemic to Bangladesh (e.g., Lyme disease and Zika), their inclusion aims to evaluate general awareness and the public’s ability to recognize potential emerging threats under shifting climate conditions. The study was conducted among diverse socioeconomic backgrounds to examine differences in perception and awareness across demographic segments.
The study was designed to aid in (i) understanding the general public’s knowledge and beliefs about the association between climate change and infectious diseases, (ii) identifying areas of policy and practice where infectious disease response to climate change is lacking, and (iii) providing a recommendation for One Health policies to lessen the burden of diseases in Bangladesh.
This study was conducted from February to April 2024 in six districts of the Rangpur division in Bangladesh. The districts were selected for inclusion based on (i) a high prevalence of infectious disease and climate vulnerability during 2012–2018, (ii) feasibility, and (iii) logistics [6,15,18,19]. These locations were chosen to ensure representation of areas with heightened exposure to climate-sensitive infectious diseases and limited prior climate-health research. The selected districts were Dinajpur, Rangpur, Nilphamari, Lalmonirhat, Kurigram and Gaibandha (see FigS1in supplemental file 2). The study districts were selected using a multistage sampling method, whereby the Upazila (a third-level government administrative unit) from each district, one union (a local-level government administrative unit) from each Upazila, and one village from each union were randomly selected. Random selection was performed using a random number generator from a complete listing at each sampling stage. The inclusion criteria ensured a diverse representation of urban and rural populations, allowing for comparative analysis of knowledge disparities across different regions. The study region has a tropical environment with two seasons: the dry season from October to April and the southwest monsoon-dominated wet season from May to September with high temperatures, high humidity, and abundant rainfall. Infectious diseases are most spread during the monsoon season [11,16,18]. Further descriptions of demographic and socioeconomic characteristics of the study areas are provided in the supplement (see TableS1in supplemental file 2).

To get the required sample, we considered the level of significance, d = 0.05, z = 1.96,p= 0.5, and q = 1 –p= 0.5. The final sample size was determined to ensure statistical power in detecting significant associations between climate change knowledge, infectious diseases, and demographic variables.
A pretested, structured, and interviewer-administered questionnaire (see supplemental file S1) was used to collect the data. The questionnaire assessed information about climate change, infectious diseases, and the effect of climate change on infectious diseases. Infectious diseases were classified by their causative agents (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites) and by transmission routes (vector-borne, sexually transmitted, airborne), while signs and symptoms are the observable characteristics of an infection. Signs include objective findings, such as a rash or fever, whereas symptoms are subjective experiences, like pain or fatigue. Standardized systems, such as the World Health Organization’s International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11) [34], were used to categorize diseases. Survey questions were adapted from validated instruments used in previous climate change and infectious disease studies, ensuring relevance and reliability. The instrument was first pretested with 50 participants and exhibited reasonable internal consistency (α = 0.75) [16]. There were a week-long online training and monitoring of data collectors for data integrity and quality. The final version considered literature from studies about climate change [1–3,12,13,15–17,31–33] and emerging infectious diseases [1–3,6,18,19,35] and expert opinions on relativity, simplicity, and importance. The instrument consisted of 17 questions and included three parts: (i) knowledge and beliefs on infectious diseases, (ii) knowledge and beliefs on climate change, and (iii) knowledge and beliefs on the influence of climate change on infectious diseases (see supplemental file 1).
Respondents were asked to accurately identify infectious diseases from a list of climate-sensitive infectious diseases, risk factors that increase the likelihood of infectious diseases, methods of transmission, signs and symptoms, and management and treatment. Twenty-nine statements evaluated knowledge of infectious diseases, 9 statements assessed knowledge of climate change, and 8 questions focused on the impact of climate change on infectious diseases. These assessment criteria enabled the identification of specific knowledge gaps in climate-sensitive infectious disease awareness, aiding targeted intervention recommendations. All knowledge-related items included three response options - true, false, and don’t know.
Respondents’ beliefs on infectious diseases, climate change, and the link between them were assessed by a 15-item question, measured on a five-point Likert scale, with response options ranging from strongly disagree to strongly agree, and neutral. Summarized sound beliefs, as used in this study, were determined by respondents indicating both “agree” and “strongly agree”. Additionally, this study classified beliefs as “sound” and “poor” based on their total belief scores. A total belief score of 80% or more was considered a “sound belief”, while less than 80% indicated “poor beliefs” [16,33]. Belief scores were analyzed to examine the association between climate awareness, perception of disease risk, and willingness to adopt preventive behaviors.
For analysis purposes, individual responses for all knowledge items were recorded as 1 (for accurate answers) or 0 (for inaccurate answers and don’t know option) [33]. The score for each knowledge item was then normalized to a maximum score of 43, and every respondent was scored according to the percentage of the maximum score (scores higher than 75% = high, 50 to 75% = moderate, lower than 50% = low). Each respondent received three knowledge assessment scores corresponding to the three tested categories. A Likert scale was used to measure attitudes, where 5 represented strongly agree, 4 agree, 3 neutral, 2 disagree, and 1 strongly disagree [16,33].
Pearson’s chi-square, Fisher’s exact and Gamma tests examined the relationship between independent and dependent variables. The chi-square test was employed when the categorical variable had more than two categories, whereas Fisher’s exact test was utilized when the categorical variable had two categories (e.g., a 2 × 2 contingency table). These tests provided a robust understanding of categorical associations and demographic influences on climate change knowledge and infectious disease beliefs. An ordinal logistic regression model was used to identify significant predictors associated with respondents’ knowledge about climate change, infectious diseases, and the influence of climate change on infectious diseases. Independent variables included education level, income status, and geographic location, enabling targeted insight for public health interventions. IBM SPSS Statistics (Version 24.0) [36] was used for descriptive analyses, RStudio (Version 4.1.0) [37] for regression modeling, and ArcGIS (Version 10.8) [38] for mapping.
A total of 2,310 respondents were initially selected through simple random sampling, of which 205 respondents were excluded due to incomplete questionnaire responses and missing demographic information, specifically, those with more than 20% of responses missing were removed, resulting in a final sample size of 1,905. Among these, 1016 (53.3%) of whom were residents of rural areas and 889 (46.7%) of whom were residents of urban areas. Most were male (59.4%) and less than 30 years of age. Approximately one third (32.3%) had tertiary levels of education. Informational sources included television/radio (82.8%), newspapers (64.6%) and social media (61.5%). Further demographic and socioeconomic details of the respondents are provided in the supplement (see Table S2 in supplemental file 2).
There was accurate identification of infectious diseases among respondents, with malaria (64.5%), dengue (69.2%), and chickenpox (67.2%) correctly recognized. To assess health literacy more broadly, non-infectious diseases such as asthma (66.1%), obesity (85.6%), and diabetes (70.6%) were also included as comparative controls and were accurately classified. Regarding infectious disease risk factors, increased heat or temperature (70.5%) and excessive emissions of CO2(63.2%) were commonly recognized, while intensive farming was less frequently identified (17.8% rural, 23.7% urban). Most participants (84.8%) correctly identified coughing or sneezing as modes of transmission. However, only 30.0% recognized droplet transmission as distinct from airborne transmission, indicating a knowledge gap in differentiating transmission mechanisms. Respondents correctly identified fever (91.8%), chills (76.7%), muscle aches and headache (75.5%), and fatigue (72.5%) as signs and symptoms of infectious diseases. Finally, most respondents (77.8%) knew antibiotics are used to treat infectious diseases (Table1).

The majority of the respondents (76.5%) believed that the government should take measures to prevent infectious diseases from spreading due to climate change. Further, a majority (73.3%) concurred that research funding for climate change effects on infectious diseases should be increased. Most (73.9%) firmly supported the statement that the media should proactively raise awareness of climate change issues and infectious disease prevention and risk reduction. Finally, most respondents (72.4%) expressed sound beliefs towards the statement that people can personally receive information about climate change updates and engage in risk reduction for infectious diseases (Fig.2; Table6). Overall, belief scores about infectious diseases, climate change, and the intersection of the two, were consistently higher than knowledge scores across domains (Fig.2). While most respondents endorsed climate and disease mitigation efforts, fewer demonstrated a detailed understanding of transmission routes or climate-disease mechanisms.

Less educated respondents had significantly lower knowledge about climate change and infectious diseases as compared to those with tertiary education, suggesting education is a strong protective factor. Rural respondents, females were 1.3 times more likely to have low knowledge about climate change, indicating that gender and residence may influence awareness levels. Females had lower knowledge about climate change (Fig.3A) and infectious diseases (Fig.3B). Lower SES respondents were 1.5 times more likely to have low knowledge about climate change, highlighting economic disadvantage as a contributor to reduced awareness (see Table S4 in supplemental file 2).

The goal of this study was to assess knowledge and beliefs concerning climate change and infectious diseases in Bangladesh and to recommend One Health policies to reduce the burden of infectious diseases as related to climate change. To reduce the harmful effects of climate change on infectious diseases, it is crucial to consider Bangladesh’s capability to address the present and future challenges as related to infectious disease prevention and control [11,15,17,35]. Collectively, the study results show that most respondents surveyed had a generally low knowledge of climate change and the influence of climate change on infectious diseases. Lack of education was associated with poor knowledge about infectious diseases, a finding that aligns with similar studies conducted in China and Nepal [39,40]where the respondents demonstrated low knowledge of climate change and infectious diseases. Social and economic factors such as lower SES and inadequate public health training were consistently associated with reduced awareness in vulnerable communities across these contexts [39]. As SES remains a key determinant of knowledge, future efforts should focus on addressing these disparities to reduce the burden of climate-sensitive infectious diseases in Bangladesh.
Our study additionally indicated that when compared to rural respondents, urban respondents were more knowledgeable about infectious diseases and climate change. The finding related to infectious disease is consistent with a previous study conducted in Bangladesh [16] and can be attributed to the difference in education level among the urban versus rural respondents. The finding that urban participants have greater knowledge than rural is also closely aligned with other studies conducted in Bangladesh [16,20,41] and Northeast Ethiopia, where respondents’ awareness of climate change and its health effects was limited due to insufficient community-level training [42]. Our study finding that overall educated respondents were more knowledgeable on the topics of climate change and infectious disease is also in line with other studies [40,43,44] and may be due to their ability to comprehend information and read and increased engagement with the internet, social media, television, and books to collect information. People’s education level in northern Bangladesh is greatly influenced by the challenges posed in rural and remote areas, where socioeconomic disparities associated with geographic location may contribute to the differences in knowledge among urban versus rural respondents [45,46]. Diversity in socioeconomic position was also considered in a previous study from Singapore to examine the knowledge gap hypothesis on the issue of climate change [46].
Furthermore, our findings support that compared to male respondents, female respondents were less knowledgeable about the impact of climate change on infectious diseases. This can be explained by women in Bangladesh being, on average, less educated, particularly in rural areas [47,48]. Strategies such as providing more and better access to education, particularly for rural women, along with policy participation and inclusion, and access to resources, can position women as local advocates for healthier communities. Thus, gender-sensitive educational initiatives can play a critical role in improving awareness and strengthening community-level adaptive capacity. Identifying and addressing gaps in people’s knowledge of infectious diseases and their association with climate change is essential for enhancing community adaptive capacity. Several studies conducted in various countries, including China, Egypt, and Indonesia, have investigated people’s knowledge of climate change and its impact on health [31,49,50]. This study is the first to be carried out in Bangladesh to assess knowledge and beliefs regarding climate change and various infectious diseases. In Bangladesh, beliefs regarding climate change and infectious disease outbreaks are shaped by a complex mix of sociodemographic factors [51,52]. Education, SES, urbanization, age, gender, and cultural context all interact to produce distinct patterns of knowledge and belief [51,52]. Understanding these clusters is essential for designing effective public health interventions and communication strategies that resonate with diverse segments of the population. However, our findings can serve as a foundational reference for a future cohort study, enabling policymakers to develop initiatives and advocacies to improve the knowledge about the impact of climate change on infectious diseases. The findings of this study can also help policymakers, service providers, and other stakeholders build effective plans for preventing and controlling climate-sensitive infectious diseases.
Bangladesh’s population is becoming increasingly aware of the links between climate change and risks of infectious/insect-borne diseases. However, this knowledge has not translated into high policy priority or robust infection control measures [18]. The reasons are multifaceted: deficiencies in data and surveillance, competing health priorities, inadequate health systems, socioeconomic vulnerabilities, disjointed policy responses, and insufficient cross-sectoral integration [18–20]. To rectify this disparity, Bangladesh necessitates improved disease surveillance and data integration, investment in resilient health systems and infection prevention and control infrastructure, cross-sectoral policy integration and coordination, targeted public education and behavior modification initiatives, and the integration of climate adaptation into infection control and public health strategies.
Additionally, the study results have implications for the One Health approach and policies. Results support the need for government decision-making to be based on the concept of One Health approach. The One Health concept speaks for the inseparability of human, animal, and environmental health through an integrated vision of health care and is gaining global recognition to combat health concerns based on human-animal-environment interactions, particularly zoonotic diseases [4–54]. In Bangladesh, adopting the One Health approach can strengthen resilience and support sustainable strategies in addressing climate change challenges and infectious diseases. Existing national programs such as the Communicable Disease Control unit (DGHS), the Department of Livestock Services, and the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change provide institutional frameworks that could be leveraged for integrated implementation. For example, Bangladesh can make use of local communities and engage them in participating in disease education and prevention efforts about climate change and infectious diseases. Community-based participation can focus on education campaigns throughout schools, villages, and communities, as well as on involving community politicians, leaders, religious figures, and women’s groups. Finally, the findings of this study support that increased awareness of climate change and infectious diseases can be achieved through increasing the general education level of the population, practical training, school educational campaigns and community-level health systems in northern Bangladesh. There must be proactive implementation of public health decisions and necessary adaptive strategies to be effective. Practical and solid forecasting can provide direction for government policies and social behaviors, which are essential for addressing issues related to climate change, including population displacement, increased urbanization, and poor sanitation. Establishing direct associations between adverse weather events and communicable diseases is also necessary. Finally, weather-based early warning systems can be developed to improve monitoring and forecast the risk of disease outbreaks. For example, rainfall monitoring linked to seasonal dengue outbreaks can inform early warning systems and targeted public health interventions [25,55].
This study was carried out in the northern region of Bangladesh, and the findings might not apply to the country’s entire population, though Bangladeshis face significant disease and climate change risks regardless of where in the country they reside. Similar future studies across other regions, such as coastal or flood-prone areas could improve representativeness and allow comparative analysis. Additionally, there are study shortcomings related to the survey method employed towards data collection in this study. Namely, due to the cross-sectional nature of data collected, causal relationships cannot be established. Reliance on self-reported data may introduce recall bias or social desirability bias, potentially affecting response accuracy even though trained interviewers and anonymized responses were used to reduce such effects. Variations in literacy levels and access to climate-related health information across different socioeconomic groups may have influenced knowledge disparities and survey comprehension, warranting further investigation. Future research incorporating longitudinal studies and mixed-method approaches can enhance understanding of public perceptions and adaptation strategies. However, the urban-rural differences are not fully explored in this paper and will be explicated in a subsequent paper.
The findings of this study conducted in Bangladesh indicate that respondents with low education levels have low knowledge regarding the influence of climate change on infectious diseases. The study emphasizes the significance of a transdisciplinary One Health strategy, and increased efforts are required to promote climate change and infectious disease awareness programs through educational training and adaptation strategies. Integrating climate-sensitive disease education into national and community-level health programs, such as those led by the Communicable Disease Control unit, the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare, or the Ministry of Environment, can strengthen resilience against emerging health threats. Such strategies and measures can be incorporated into the One Health approach by developing integrated and dynamic policies to increase awareness among the general population. Strengthening healthcare and surveillance systems is also essential to control the spread of infectious diseases sensitive to climate change in Bangladesh and similar settings elsewhere. Future research should focus on regional collaboration across South Asian countries to develop shared frameworks for climate and health policy improvements. Without action, climate-related health risks may intensify whereas proactive, education-driven strategies can build long-term resilience.
