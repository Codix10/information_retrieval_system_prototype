The constant changes experienced in agricultural activities due to climate change pose a great challenge to melon production. Hence, this research examined the determinants of melon farmers’ adaptation strategies to cope with climate change hazards in southern–southern Nigeria. The research ultimately depended on primary data collected by using a set of questionnaires and interviews. The data were obtained from 260 samples retrieved from melon farmers by using multistage sampling techniques. The data were analyzed using the multivariate probit (MVP) model and partial eta squared test. The results of the MVP model showed that age (− 0.009), marital status (0.200), access to information on climate change (0.567) and crop insurance (0.214) were significant at the 0.01 level, while household size (− 0.030) was significant at the 0.05 level and determined the adoption of crop diversification. Educational level (0.012), extension contact (0.138) and access to credit (0.122) were significant at the 0.05 level, while access to information on climate change (0.415) was significant at the 0.01 level and determined the adoption of change in planting dates. Age (− 0.010) and access to information on climate change (0.381) were significant at the 0.01 level, while sex (− 0.139), marital status (0.158) and off-farm income (− 2.3E-7) were significant at the 0.05 level and determined the adoption of mixed farming. Farming experience (0.005) is significant at the 0.05 level, while access to information on climate change (0.529) and crop insurance (0.272) are significant at the 0.01 level and determine the adoption of drought-tolerant crop species. Access to information on climate change (0.536) is significant at the 0.01 level, indicating the adoption of improved crop species. Age (− 0.010), farm size (− 0.085) and crop insurance (0.206) were significant at the 0.05 level, while access to information on climate change (0.353) was significant at the 0.01 level and determined the adoption of off-farm job opportunities. The study recommends the availability and accessibility of credit, climate-smart agricultural practices, and the establishment of public‒private partnerships, among others.

Climate change is currently affecting farming activities and farmers’ livelihoods and ecosystems and presents a considerable development challenge for the entire developing country worldwide1. Climate change presents major challenges to researchers, scientists and policymakers. Scientific reports also show that climatic factors are changing rapidly due to an increase in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions2. An increase in the concentration of GHGs has escalated the mean temperature and changed the volume and distribution of precipitation globally. For instance, in sub-Saharan Africa, warming is anticipated to be greater than the global average, and in some parts of the region, there could be a reduction in precipitation3. Hence, the hazards associated with climate change include extreme events, such as droughts, floods, and heavy windstorms; limiting these hazards due to climate change has become a challenge for the global community at present, especially for melon farmers in South‒South Nigeria.
Some of the adaptation strategies to climate change with regard to crop production (including melon), as identified by Onoja4, include crop rotation, irrigation practices, mulching, intercropping, contour farming, contract farming, planting of crops along flood plateaus, planting of early maturing species of crops, crop planting diversification, soil conservation procedures, harvesting of rainwater, and changes in crop planting dates, among others.
Melon (Citrullus lanatus(Thumb) Mansf.) is a vegetable crop mostly grown in West Africa for edible nutrient-rich seeds and is commonly known as “Egusi” in Nigeria5. Melon is native to the West African region. It is commonly intercropped or interplanted with other crops, such as maize, yams and/or cassava, on peasant farms6. The common melon cultivars in Nigeria are “Bara”, “Serewe” (Citrullus lanatus(Thumb) Mansf) and “Bojuri” (Lagenaria abyssinica). The “Bara” species possess prominent thick, black or white colored seed edges. The lack of a noticeable seed edge in “Sewere” lacks a noticeable seed edge compared with “Bara”; this lack of a pronounced seed edge has caused “Sewere” melon seeds to possess a greater shelling proportion, hence yielding more edible endosperm than “Bara”, while “Bojuri” melon species possess larger and longer seeds than the two without pronounced seed edges5; hence, “Sewere” melon species are more common in southern southern Nigeria.
Melon seeds are an essential component of foodstuffs for the treatment of marasmus (calories deficiency), kwashiorkor (protein deficiency) and other diseases. Melon has the potential to enhance nutrition, increase food security, improve rural development and increase land use, provide affordable vitamins and micronutrients, particularly in rural areas, and potentially enhance infant nutrition due to its high protein and fat content7.
Despite the enormous nutritional and industrial importance of melon seeds, their production and yield are very low in Nigeria, particularly in the South‒South region, due to many factors, which include excessive changes in climatic factors such as rainfall and temperature. Many studies have been carried out on melon plants, such as Agba6, who examined the effect of nitrogen and phosphorus on the growth and yield of Egusi; Ibironke and Oyeleke7, who investigated the contributions of melon production to the livelihood sustainability of rural farming households; and Ogunsola et al.5, who examined the incidence of leaf blight disease in egusi melons, among others. However, there are limited reports and a lack of updates on climate change acclimatization strategies and melon production, especially in the southern region of Nigeria, where this research aimed to fill the knowledge gap. Hence, this research examined the determinants of melon farmers’ adaptation strategies to cope with climate change hazards in South‒South Nigeria in an attempt to intensify the pace of Nigerian people to achieve the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) of no poverty, zero hunger and climate action.
The theoretical explanation underpinning this research is the theory of regression. Regression theory is a principle used in regression statistical processes in finance, production and investment, among others, to ascertain the strength, firmness, attributes and estimations of actual relationships or correlations between dependent variable(s) and explanatory variables8. Hence, a regression model is capable of revealing changes in the actual dependent variable that are related to the variations in the explanatory variable(s). Simple linear regression, multiple linear regressions, multivariate regression and multinomial regression are the major kinds of regression. The research was carried out in South‒South Nigeria. The South‒South region is characterized by a terrain consisting of a natural delta of the Niger River and zones to the east and west of Nigeria. The natural boundaries of the South‒South region can be distinguished by their topography and hydrographic nature. The southern-southern region northern boundaries are close to the divergence of the Niger River at Aboh, and the western and eastern boundaries are near the Benin River and the Imo River, respectively. The South‒South region consists of six states: the Cross River State, the Edo State, the Rivers State, the Delta, the Akwa-Ibom State, and the Bayelsa State. The southern region is typically a low-lying marshy area with a dense network system of twisted rivers and creeks.
The region consists of four distinctive ecological zones defined by both landscape and hydrological characteristics: coastal sandy barricade crest, mangrove swamp, freshwater swamp and lowland rainforest zones9. The southern region of the country covers nearly 84,587 km2, which is approximately 9.3% of Nigeria’s total land mass, and the vegetation is characterized by tropical savanna, rainforest, mangroves and monsoons10. Hence, the climate in the South‒South zone favors the cultivation of cash crops such as coconut, cocoa, cashew, oil palm, kolaunt, gum arabic, sesame and rubber, among others. The arable crops cultivated in the zone included rice, cassava, maize, melon, yams and cocoyam. This research focused on melon production.
Research design provides insight into the total vital elements of the research. Hence, this research used a descriptive survey procedure to obtain and gather pertinent information on the associations and relevance of climate change adaptation strategies among melon famers, the independent variables comprising socioeconomic characteristics of the farmers, and adaptation strategies to climate change.
The questionnaire and interview schedule were employed as data collector instruments. The questionnaire was logically and clearly prepared and willfully and purposely designed to gather answers from the melon farmers. The interview schedule was used to obtain accurate information from the melon farmers as outlined in the questionnaire.
Multistage sampling procedures were used in the selection of respondents (melon farmers). First, three (3) states were selected using a purposive sampling technique (this purposive sampling technique used was based on high melon production in these states) from the six southern and southern states of the Akwa-Ibom, Bayelsa and Rivers State. The Agricultural Development Programme (ADP) divided each state into zones for administrative purposes11. Second, all the agricultural zones were selected in each state, for which twelve (12) agricultural zones were selected. Third, one (1) local government area (LGA) was selected from each agricultural zone using a purposive sampling technique (this purposeive sampling technique used was based on high melon production in these LGAs), for a total of twelve (12) LGAs. Fourth, three (3) communities were selected from each LGA population using a purposive sampling technique (this purposive sampling technique used was based on high melon production in these communities), for a total of nine (9) (for Akwa-Ibom State with 6 agricultural zones) communities and eighteen (18) (for Rivers and Bayelsa State with 3 agricultural zones each) communities from each state and thirty-six (36) communities. Finally, from each community, with the help of local extension personnel, a list of melon farming households was compiled, and ten (10) and five (5) melon-based farmers were selected (the rationale for selecting 10 and 5 melon farmers was based on the number of agricultural zones in the State and to have an equal number of 90 melon farmers from each State. Akwa-Ibom State has 6 agricultural zones, while Rivers and Bayelsa State have 3 agricultural zones each) according to the purposive sampling technique. A total sample of two hundred and seventy (270) melon-based farmers was selected for the study. A sample size estimator was used to determine the sample size needed for the study. Kuder-Richardson, KR-20, was used to measure the reliability of the questionnaire, while the multivariate probit (MVP) model was used to analyze the data obtained using SPSS 25.0. Validity is concerned with how well the collected data cover and satisfactorily describe the area of study to be examined. Validity simply means the data collected measure what is designed and intended to be measured13,14. The primary data were collected randomly in an open-ended questionnaire; the privacy, secrecy and confidentiality of the melon farmer respondents were stringent and consistent. The questionnaire absolutely and correctly captured the information contained in the data. Hence, face validity was assessed.
Face validity estimates and surveys the characteristics and content of the questionnaire in relation to feasibility, usefulness, readability, utility, suitability, and consistency of approach and typesetting with formatting, as well as the clarity and distinctiveness of the language utilized13. Hence, face validity simply assessed the relevance of the questionnaire as an instrument for determining whether the elements in the questionnaire were actually relevant, applicable, rational, unambiguous and explicit.
Reliability describes the consistency of the measurement. Reliability concerns the magnitude and range to which a measurement instrument provides firm, consistent and dependable results13. Hence, reliability is also associated with the repeatability of the result. Measuring reliability is vital for ensuring consistency across segments of a questionnaire. The Kuder–Richardson test and the KR-20 were used to measure the reliability of the questionnaire. The reliability and validity of the instrument (questionnaire) were assessed by using a pilot survey test with 10% of the respondents included in the study. Table5shows that age, with a coefficient of − 0.009 (pvalue of 0.008), is statistically significant at the 1% level and negatively impacts the likelihood of adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. A one-unit increase in the age of the farmer could reduce the chances of adopting crop diversification by 0.9%, suggesting that increasing the adaptation age would increase the chances of melon-based farmers adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. The negative coefficient for age shows that young melon farmers may diversify crops more than aged farmers do; this could be the result of young farmers innovating and trying new adaptation strategies to enhance their melon production. This result is in agreement with that of Enimu and Onome17, who found among farmers in Delta State that, regardless of whether older farmers are aware of new innovations, they are not willing to adopt new adaptation strategies. A partial eta squared of 0.029 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy.
Marital status, with a coefficient of 0.200 (pvalue of 0.002), is statistically significant at the 1% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. A unit increase in the marital status of the farmer could increase the chances of adopting crop diversification by 20%, suggesting that an increase in marital status would increase the chances of melon-based farmers adopting crop diversification as a means of adapting to climate change. This finding implies that married melon farmers could adopt crop diversification more than their single counterparts. A partial eta squared of 0.040 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. Household size, with a coefficient of − 0.030 (pvalue 0.031), is statistically significant at the 5% level and negatively impacts the probability of adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. An increase in the household size of a farmer could reduce the chances of adopting crop diversification by melon farmers when adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. The negative coefficient of household size implies that melon farmers with smaller household sizes are more prone to adopting crop diversification to cope with the effects of climate change. A partial eta squared of 0.019 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy.
Access to information concerning climate change, with a coefficient of 0.567 (pvalue of 0.000), is statistically significant at the 1% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. A one-unit increase in access to information related to climate change could increase the likelihood of adopting crop diversification by 56.7%, suggesting that adapting to climate change would improve the likelihood of melon-based farmers adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. This implies that timely access to information related to climate change could increase the rate of climate change adaptation strategies through crop diversification. The melon farmer who received information earlier could adopt the strategy than the farmers who may obtain the information late. These findings are in agreement with those of Gemeda, Korecha and Garedew18, who reported that access to climate information is correlated with crop diversification in Ethiopia. A partial eta squared of 0.094 was obtained, which indicates a medium effect on the use of crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. Awareness of farm/crop insurance, with a coefficient of 0.214 (pvalue of 0.012), is statistically significant at the 1% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. A unit increase in awareness of crop insurance could increase the chances of adopting crop diversification by melon farmers by 21.4%, suggesting that the awareness of crop insurance would improve the chances of melon-based farmers adopting crop diversification as a means of adaptation to climate change. This implies that transferring the losses that may be encountered in maize production is possible through insurance companies. A partial eta squared of 0.026 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of crop diversification as a climate change adaptation strategy. The multivariate probit (MVP) model results, consequently, provide appropriate support for simultaneous adaptation choices.
Table5shows that educational level, with a coefficient of 0.012 (pvalue of 0.028), is statistically significant at the 5% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting changing planting dates as a means of adapting to climate change. An increase in the year needed to reach the educational level of the farmer could increase the probability of adopting change planting dates by melon farmers when adopting change planting dates as climate change adaptation strategies. This means that the more years the melon farmers spent to reach the level of education, the more significant is the study and change in the planting date of maize for efficient productivity. This finding is inconsistent with those of Mwinkom, Damnyag, Abugre and Alhassan19, who obtained a negative coefficient among farmers in North‑Western Ghana. A partial eta squared of 0.020 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of changing planting dates as climate change adaptation strategies.
Access to information concerning climate change, with a coefficient of 0.415 (pvalue of 0.000), is statistically significant at the 1% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting change planting dates as climate change adaptation strategies. A unit increase in access to information on climate change could increase the chances of adopting change planting dates by 41.5%, suggesting that adapting to climate change would improve the chances of melon-based farmers utilizing change planting dates as climate change adaptation strategies. This finding is consistent with that of Mwinkom et al.19, who reported that climate change influences the change in planting time in Ghana. A partial eta squared of 0.052 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of changes in planting date as climate change adaptation strategies. With a coefficient of 0.138 (pvalue 0.024), agricultural extension contact is statistically significant at the 5% level and positively impacts the probability of adopting change in planting dates as a climate change adaptation strategy. An increase in agricultural extension contact could increase the chances of adopting climate change adaptation strategies to change planting dates. This implies that agricultural extension services offer vital and accurate information about climate change and are capable of providing good agricultural and management practices, including operational practices, to address climate change. Hence, farmers with better contact with the extension have a greater chance of altering planting dates for maximum yields even during climate challenges. This finding is consistent with Nhemachena, Hassan and Chakwizira20, who reported that increasing access to free agricultural extension services may considerably enhance farmers’ awareness of changes in climatic factors and, in addition, adaptation strategies to adapt to climate change hazards. A partial eta squared of 0.021 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the use of change in planting as a climate change adaptation strategy.
Access to credit facilities, with a coefficient of 0.122 (pvalue of 0.042), is statistically significant at the 5% level and positively impacts the possibility of adopting changing planting dates as a means of adapting to climate change. An increase in credit accessibility facilities could increase the chances of adopting changing planting dates by melon farmers when adopting changing planting dates as climate change adaptation strategies. This implies that for melon farmers to adapt to climate variability, productive financing is needed. A partial eta squared of 0.017 was obtained, which indicates a small effect on the usage of change in planting as a climate change adaptation strategy. According to the results of this study, climate change affects melon production in the study area. The multivariate probit (MVP) model, Kuder-Richardson (KR-20) model, variance inflating factor (VIF) and descriptive statistics were applied to estimate the determinants of melon farmers’ adaptation strategies to climate change hazards in southern-southern Nigeria.
Kuder-Richardson (KR-20) showed that the use of a measurement instrument is good for assessing the data, and the findings of this research are highly valid and reliable. The results of the multivariate probit (MVP) model showed that age, marital status, household size, access to information on climate change and farm/crop insurance determined the adoption of crop diversification. Educational level, access to information on climate change, extension contact and access to credit/finance determined the adoption of change in planting dates. Age, sex, marital status, off-farm income and access to information on climate change determined the adoption of mixed farming, crops and livestock rearing. Farming experience, access to information on climate change and farm/crop insurance determined the adoption of drought-tolerant crop species. Access to information on climate change determined the adoption of improved crop species. Age, melon farm size, access to information on climate change and farm/crop insurance determined the adoption of off-farm job opportunities. In conclusion, adapting to climate change by melon farmers could take the form of simultaneous adaptation choices. The study therefore recommends the following:
To efficiently adapt to the challenges posed by climate change, melon farmers in South‒South Nigeria need to employ a range of strategies and practices, such as crop diversification, climate-smart agricultural practices, easy access to climate change information and early warning systems, and capacity building and knowledge sharing;
There is a need to establish achievable strategies and programmes that could help melon farmers proactively adapt to climate change. For example, facilitating the availability and accessibility of credit at low interest rates for melon farmers can enhance farmers’ adaptation strategies. This could be done by government agencies, NGOs and other stakeholders;
The government should provide basic amenities for rural dwellers, such as good roads, educational and financial institutions and other needs, which could increase the possibility of adopting effective adaptation strategies;
There is also a need to establish new institutions, for instance, public‒private partnerships, which could collect research findings from farmers in the field and help melon farmers adapt to climate change for efficient production.
