Warming is altering the functioning of desert ecosystems in global drylands. Microbial communities are crucial for maintaining these ecosystems, yet how their co-occurrence networks and assembly mechanisms respond to warming remains unclear. Using 16 S and ITS rRNA amplicon sequencing, we examined bacterial and fungal community composition and structure. Further, we investigated cross-trophic bacterial-fungal interactions via inter-domain ecological network analysis. Warming significantly altered the diversity, composition, and structure of both bacterial and fungal communities. It increased bacterial network complexity but simplified the fungal network. Notably, warming enhanced cross-trophic interactions between bacteria and fungi, facilitating the maintenance of microbial hierarchical interactions, particularly bacterial network complexity. However, microbial keystone taxa declined dramatically under warming, 41.18% of these belonged to Ascomycota. Neutral community models and normalized stochastic ratio-based analyses revealed that deterministic processes dominated community assembly, with warming increasing their relative importance by 8–46%. This suggests a potential deterministic environmental filtering induced by warming. Collectively, these findings advance our understanding of the ecological mechanisms and microbial interactions underpinning rhizospheric communities in drylands under future climate change.

Drylands occupy over 41% of the global land area and represent critical biogeochemical engines [1]. However, climate models project that drylands will experience accelerated warming, increased water stress, and more frequent and severe droughts compared to other regions, potentially altering the drivers of biogeochemical processes [2,3]. For example, microbial communities that drive biogeochemical cycling in dryland are regulated by warming and are susceptible to changes in abundance and diversity under climate change [4,5]. Bacteria and fungi—two key interacting microbial groups—exhibit distinct adaptation processes and mechanisms due to inherent differences in their phenotypes, phylogenies, and life histories [6], which may be further affected by warming [5]. Given the spatiotemporal variability in microbial responses to simulated warming across regions and ecosystems [5,7,8], advancing our understanding of temperature-driven microbial dynamics and mechanisms is essential to accurately predict changes in ecological functioning under future climate scenarios [7,9].
The magnitude of microbial community responses to climate warming varies substantially across ecosystems and temporal scales. For example, notable alterations in microbial community structure occurred in temperate forest ecosystems after 20 years of warming [10], as well as in the structure of bacterial and fungal microbial communities in tallgrass prairie ecosystems exposed to prolonged warming [11]. However, no adjustments were observed in the taxonomic composition of soil bacteria in Mediterranean-type steppe ecosystems after 14 years of warming treatment [8]. These divergent responses likely arise because microbial reactions depend critically on local environmental factors, including substrate availability, rhizosphere allocation, taxon-specific temperature sensitivity, soil water, pH, nutrient availability, and vegetation types [5,12,13]. In arid habitats, warming often substantially reduces soil water availability [14], thereby diminishing microbial diversity [15]. Furthermore, enhanced aridity further drives global declines in the abundance and diversity of arid-land soil bacteria and fungi [4], primarily due to the well-documented inverse relationship between aridity and critical resources such as water and carbon [16].
Critically, microbial communities are characterized not only by their number and taxonomic composition, but also by the complex interactions among their constituents [17]—spanning mutualism, predation, and competition [18]. Microbial co-occurrence network analysis has emerged as a powerful tool for deciphering the complexity of the microbial communities and the intricate relationships among their species [19], and has been widely adopted to elucidate microbial interaction dynamics across environmental gradients [5,20,21]. Recent investigations have shown that the complexity of co-occurrence networks encompassing bacterial and fungal communities intensifies in subtropical forest ecosystems with climate warming [5], while species interactions weaken in alpine steppe ecosystems under the combined conditions of increased warming due to reduced water availability [13]. Meanwhile, warming has gradually increased the complexity and stability of microbial networks (bacteria and archaea) in a tallgrass prairie ecosystem [20]. In drylands, increased temperatures and the subsequent environmental changes, including reduced moisture levels, will act as significant factors against existing microbial species, potentially leading to a decrease in microbial network complexity or even collapse [20,22].
Another fascinating aspect concerns microbial community assembly, governed by both deterministic and stochastic ecological processes [23,24]. Deterministic processes, driven by environmental factors (e.g. pH, moisture and nutrients) and biotic interactions (e.g. competition, facilitation, mutualisms and predation), shape species composition based on niche theories. In contrast, stochastic processes, such as dispersal, drift and mutation, play a pivotal role in neutral theories of community assembly [25,26]. Several recent studies have examined how experimental drivers mimicking climate change affect microbial community assembly, with inconclusive results [5,24,27]. For instance, six years of warming shifted bacterial community assembly from stochastic to deterministic dominance in subtropical dry forests [5], while three years of warming decreased stochasticity in grassland bacterial assembly [24]. In drylands, climate warming may simultaneously enhance stochasticity via increased nutrient availability while favoring deterministic processes through exacerbated water scarcity [14]. Importantly, research has demonstrated that the microbial networks complexity and community assembly mechanisms is a crucial factor in ascertaining their links to community stability and ecosystem function [27,28]. However, the effects of warming on microbial interactions, co-occurrence networks, and assembly mechanisms remain poorly understood in climate-sensitive desert ecosystems, such as the Tengger Desert.
Accordingly, we conducted a field experiment simulating warming using open-top chambers (OTCs) to assess the response of bacterial and fungal communities in root and rhizosphere soils to elevated temperature. We used 16 S rRNA and fungal ITS gene amplicon sequencing to characterize bacterial and fungal community composition and structure. We inferred the relative importance of deterministic versus stochastic processes governing community assembly, constructed microbial co-occurrence networks, and assessed network complexity and microbial interactions. Herein, our main objectives are to: (i) compare the diversity and composition of bacterial and fungal communities in root and rhizosphere soils under warming; (ii) characterize the intra- (bacterial, fungal) and cross-trophic (bacteria-fungi) microbial co-occurrence networks under warming; (iii) determine the dominant ecological processes (deterministic vs. stochastic) governing bacterial and fungal community assembly under warming. Additionally, we identify keystone taxa within these networks under warming and evaluate their associations with key soil physicochemical properties and plant variables. Collectively, this study aims to elucidate how microbial communities in the Tengger Desert respond to warming across multiple organizational levels (taxonomy, interactions, assembly), expanding our knowledge of climate change impacts in arid ecosystems and improving predictions of microbial-mediated ecosystem functioning under future scenarios.
This study was conducted at the Shapotou Desert Research and Experiment Station of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (SDRES, CAS; 37°32′N, 105°02′E, and 1,330 m a.s.l.), situated at the southeastern edge of the Tengger Desert, northern China (Fig.1a). According to prior reports by Li et al. [29,30], the station experiences a continental monsoon and desert climate, characterized by a mean annual temperature of 8.6 °C and annual precipitation of 186 mm, most of which falls between May and September. Analyzing meteorological data from SDRES from 1955 to 2016, we observed a slight increase of 0.5 °C in average annual temperature and a slight decrease of 4 mm in average annual precipitation over the last decade. The vegetation is dominated by native mature shrubs and semi-shrubs, primarilyCaragana korshinskiiKom.,Ceratoides latensReveal et Holmgren,Artemisia ordosicaKrasch.,Reaumuria soongoricaMaxim.,Salsola passerinaBunge, andOxytropis aciphyllaLedeb. These are interspersed with native perennial herbs (Stipa brevifloraGriseb.,Carex stenophylloidesKrecz.,Cleistogenes songoricaOhwi), as well as annual and biennial species such asBassia dasyphylla(Fisch. et Mey.) O. Kuntze andEchinops gmeliniiTurcz.

The OTCs were established in this experiment to imitate the climate change observed between the last decade and the present, particularly the rising mean annual temperature. Specifically, the dimensions of the OTCs were modified to achieve a comparable temperature rise to that predicted. Ultimately, we selected three OTCs of different sizes from a series of existing devices and used them to simulate three treatments in the experiment. The three different sizes of OTCs are: a large octagonal column of 130 cm × 130 cm × 200 cm (+ 0.5 °C warming); a small octagonal column of 100 cm × 100 cm × 200 cm (+ 0.9 °C warming); a small quadrilateral of 100 cm × 100 cm × 150 cm (+ 1.5 °C warming). In addition, we also set up control groups that were at least 2 m away from the OTCs to minimize bias. These control plots replicated the OTC conditions and underwent identical pretreatment, installation, and recovery protocols, differing only in the absence of aboveground OTC structures. Air temperature, precipitation, and soil water at 0–5 cm depth in each OTC and the control group were continuously monitored throughout the experiment using an automated weather station (U30 HOBO data logger, Onset Computer Corporation, Bourne, MA, USA). Analysis of the data showed that the mean annual temperature of large, medium, and small OTCs increased by 0.5 °C, 0.9 °C and 1.5 °C, respectively, compared to the control. For additional details about OTCs, please refer to the study conducted by Li et al. [29].
Roots and rhizosphere soils were collected from individual treatment and control plants in mid-August 2023, two growing seasons after the establishment of the simulation experiment. The study focused on three herbaceous species observed in all treatments: the two-year-oldEchinops gmelinii, the annualBassia dasyphylla, and the perennialAllium mongolicum. Briefly, forEchinops gmeliniiandBassia dasyphylla, identical seeds (with no significant differences in shape, age, and thousand-grain weight) collected in 2021 were planted at a predetermined density in both the treatment and control, and the plants were grown from naturally shed seeds in the remaining years. In addition, the sameAllium mongolicumseeds were planted in the control and treatment and the seedlings were permitted to germinate and develop for about two years. At the peak of plant growth (mid-August), the roots and rhizosphere soil of plants in each treatment and control were collected as described by Zhong et al. [31] and Edwards et al. [32]. Excess soil was scraped off the roots by hand, leaving about 1 mm of soil adhering to the roots before being transported to the cooler (Fig.1b). In the laboratory, rhizosphere soils were separated from the roots according to the methods described below (Fig.1b), and all plant roots or rhizosphere soils from each treatment were mixed together to create a composite sample. Finally, for microbial community analysis, a total of 24 mixed DNA samples (4 treatments × 3 replicates × 2 compartments) were obtained. Measurements of soil pH, soil water content (SWC), total organic carbon (TOC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), total nitrogen (TN), available phosphorus (AP), available potassium (AK), ammonium nitrogen (NH4+-N) and nitrate nitrogen (NO3−-N), and total N and P concentrations in plant roots were made according to previous reports [33].
Microbial DNA was extracted from 0.5 g of fresh soil or 1 g of root samples using the E.Z.N.A.®Soil DNA Kit (Omega Bio-Tek, Norcross, Georgia, USA). To characterize the bacterial and fungal communities, the V3–V4 hypervariable regions of the bacterial 16 S rRNA gene were amplified with the primers 515 F (5′-GTGCCAGCMGCCGCGGTAA-3′) and 907R (5′-CCGTCAATTCMTTTRAGTTT-3′). For fungal analysis, the internal transcribed spacer 1 (ITS1) region was amplified using the primers ITS1F (5′-CTTGGTCATTTAGAGGAAGTAA-3′) and ITS2-2043R (5′-GCTGCGTTCTTCATCGATGC-3′) [34]. All polymerase chain reaction (PCR) reactions were performed in a 30 µL mixture containing 15 µL of 2 × Phanta Master Mix, 1 µL of each primer (10 µM), and 20 ng of template DNA. The thermal cycling conditions are as follows: 98 °C for one minute is the initial denaturation phase; 30 cycles of 98 °C for 10 s, 50 °C for 30 s, 72 °C for 30 s, and 72 °C for five minutes is the final extension phase. Following the manufacturer’s instructions, the amplicon was extracted from a 2% agarose gel and purified using the AxyPrep DNA gel extraction kit (Axygen Biosciences, Union City, CA, USA). The amplicon was measured using the Qubit®3.0 after purification. The pooled DNA products were employed to construct Illumina pair-end libraries using the Illumina genomic DNA library preparation protocol. The amplicon libraries were subjected to pair-end sequencing (2 × 250) using the Illumina Novaseq 6000 platform (Nanjing Geneponeer Co., Ltd.) as per standard protocols. The raw reads were deposited in the NCBI Sequence Read Archive (SRA) database (access number: PRJNA988244).
The raw tags were subjected to a qualitative filtration process under specific filtering conditions to obtain high-quality, clean tags following the Quantitative Insights into Microbial Ecology (QIIME) quality control process [35]. Specifically, the raw reads for each sample were trimmed, merged into paired-end reads, and low-quality sequences (including chimeras) were filtered using the UPARSE pipeline (version 7.1) [36]. Subsequently, the filtered sequences were processed in QIIME 2 using the DADA2 algorithm to generate high-resolution Amplicon Sequence Variants (ASVs), thereby offering higher resolution, improved specificity, and lower rates of false sequences compared to traditional Operational Taxonomic Units (OTUs). For bacterial taxonomic classification, the SILVA database (release 138.1) was queried using the Ribosomal Database Project (RDP) classifier [34]. Fungal species were classified taxonomically using the BLAST algorithm against the UNITE database (version 8.3). Ultimately, 2,391,714 bacterial sequences (with an average of 66,437, minimum of 52,189, and maximum of 78,645 sequences per sample) and 2,915,831 fungal sequences (with an average of 80,995, minimum of 65,432, and maximum of 94,310 sequences per sample) were obtained.
Unless otherwise specified, all data analyses were performed using R software (v.4.1.3; R Development Core Team, 2018). Normality and homogeneity of variances were first evaluated with the Shapiro–Wilk test and Bartlett’s test, respectively. For data pertaining to soil properties, plant attributes, and microbial community variables, if the assumptions for homogeneity of variance were satisfied, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted to assess differences among groups, followed by Tukey’s HSD post-hoc test (P< 0.05). If the homogeneity assumption was violated, the Kruskal–Wallis test was applied instead [37]. Differences in soil properties, plant characteristics, and microbial α-diversity among treatments were evaluated using ANOVA with Tukey’s HSD test at a significance level ofp< 0.05. The general differences in microbial communities among treatments, as measured by the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity, were compared using principal coordinate analysis (PCoA), analysis of similarity (ANOSIM), permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA), and multiresponse permutation procedures (MRPPs). We used the ‘ecodist’ and ‘vegan’ R packages to perform Mantel tests to determine the relationship between microbial communities and soil/plant variables [38]. We used the “vegan” package’s `cca()` function to conduct a canonical correspondence analysis (CCA) to evaluate how environmental factors impact and contribute to shaping microbial community structure.
Microbial co-occurrence networks under different treatments were analyzed using the ‘WGCNA’ package based on Spearman’s correlation matrix [39]. Briefly, ASVs with a relative abundance of less than 0.01% were excluded from the dataset to reduce the proportion of rare ASVs [40]. The R package “WGCNA” was then used to calculate all potential pairwise Spearman’s rank correlations (r) between these ASVs. In the network analysis, only robust (| r | >0.6) and statistically significant (p< 0.05) relationships were included. In addition, the topological features like the number of nodes and edges, average clustering coefficient, average path length, network diameter, graph density, network diameter, average degree, and modularity were computed using the ‘igraph’ package in R, based on a previous study. Finally, the networks were explored and visualized using the Gephi interactive platform.
To determine the keystone ASVs of each network, we analyze the connectivity of each node based on its within-module connectivity (Zi) and among-module connectivity (Pi) [28]. Keystone ASVs were grouped into four different categories: network hubs (zi >2.5; Pi >0.62; highly connected nodes both within their own module and to other modules, acting as central integrators of the entire network), modular hubs (zi >2.5; Pi < 0.62; highly connected nodes primarily within their own module, serving as local centers of activity), connectors (zi < 2.5; Pi >0.62; nodes with crucial links bridging different modules, facilitating inter-module communication), and peripherals (zi < 2.5; Pi < 0.62). Correlations between the Keystone ASVs of each network and soil or plant variables were determined using a heat map based on Pearson’s correlation analysis.
To assess the potential significance of stochastic and deterministic processes in the assembly of bacterial and fungal communities, neutral community models (NCM) and normalized random ratios (NST) were employed [41,42]. The estimate of dispersal between communities, ‘Nm’, is used in this model to calculate the relationship between frequency of occurrence and relative abundance in a given region. “N” is the meta-microbial community size (number of ASVs), and “m” denotes the migration rate of microbes, with higher values indicating that the species is less restricted by diffusion. The “R2” parameter crucially determines how well the model fits the NCM, where a higher R2value signifies a more closely matching neutral model, indicating that stochastic processes play a more dominant role in shaping community assembly, with less contribution from deterministic factors. A recently introduced index, NST, utilizes a 50% threshold to distinguish between deterministic (less than 50%) and stochastic (more than 50%) dominance [42]. A higher NST value exceeding 50% signifies a predominant role of stochastic processes, whereas a lower NST below 50% suggests deterministic processes are more significant.
Warming treatment significantly increased pH by 4.94%, 7.08%, and 8.43%, respectively, while decreasing SWC by 26.89%, 36.70%, and 50.00%, respectively, in comparison to the control (p< 0.05; TableS1). T1 and T2 treatments significantly reduced RN by 2.99% and 1.76%, respectively, whereas T3 treatments dramatically increased this response variable by 22.08% relative to ambient (p< 0.05; TableS1). However, TN, SOC, DOC, NH4+-N, NO3−-N, AN, AP, AK, AB, RB, RN, the ratio of AN to AP, and the ratio of RN to RP did not alter significantly among the different warming and the control treatments (p> 0.05; TableS1).
After rigorous quality control and exclusion of chimeric sequences, the 16 S rRNA and ITS gene sequencing of roots and rhizosphere soil generated 406,897, 462,492, 684,184, and 9,870,111 high-quality sequence reads, which were subsequently assembled into 1992, 8790, 2202, and 3158 ASVs, respectively (TableS2). The constructed rarefaction curves attested to the adequacy of the analyzed sequence volume in capturing the full spectrum of bacterial and fungal diversity.
For fungi, the Ace, Chao1, Simpson, and Shannon indices did not substantially differ between the control and warming treatments in the roots (p> 0.05); however, for bacteria and fungal in the rhizosphere soil, they did considerably increase (p< 0.05; Fig.S1). Warming treatments led to variable changes in root bacterial diversity indices, with some increasing and others decreasing compared to the control (Fig.S1). In contrast, warming treatments significantly increased the rhizosphere soil bacterial Ace index by 49.51%, 43.02%, and 13.35%, respectively, as well as the bacterial Chao1 index by 49.52%, 43.19%, and 13.32%, respectively, in comparison to the control (Fig.S1). In addition, the Ace index of rhizosphere soil fungi exhibited a pronounced increase of 54.71%, 63.74%, and 77.01% in the T1, T2, and T3 treatments, respectively, while the Chao1 index showed a similar trend, with an increase of 54.70%, 63.82%, and 77.10%, respectively (Fig.S1). The PCoA based on the Bray-Curtis distance revealed that the rhizosphere soil bacterial and fungal communities under warming conditions were entirely separated from those under the control treatment (Fig.2a, c; TableS3). The root bacterial community in the warming condition was completely isolated from the control treatment, and the microbial community was partially divided between the paired warming treatments (Fig.2b; TableS3). In contrast, no significant separation was observed in the root fungal communities between the warming and control treatments (Fig.2d; TableS3). The results of dissimilarity analyses, including PERMANOVA, ANOSIM, and MRPPs, indicated the rhizosphere soil bacteria (PERMANOVA,p= 0.001; ANOSIM,p= 0.001; MRPP,p= 0.001), rhizosphere soil fungi (PERMANOVA,p= 0.001; ANOSIM,p= 0.001; MRPP,p= 0.001), and root bacteria (PERMANOVA,p= 0.004; ANOSIM,p= 0.002; MRPP,p= 0.004) communities changed significantly among the control and warming treatments, and no significant differences observed in the root fungi community (TableS3). Additionally, variations in microbial composition were detected in all warming treatments (Fig. S2). The Mantel test results indicated that bacterial community composition correlated with pH, AP, and SWC (p< 0.05; Fig.3c; TableS4). In addition, NH4+-N, AN, SWC, and the ratio of AN to AP were also significantly related to the variation of fungi community composition (p< 0.05; Fig.3c; TableS4).

The topological characteristics of bacteria and fungi differed between the control and warming treatments (TableS5). Compared to the control, the bacterial network exhibited increased size and complexity under warming, as indicated by a consistent increment in the number of nodes, total links, average cluster coefficient, graph density, and average degree, while the average path length and graph diameter decreased (Fig.4a; Fig.S3; TableS5). In contrast, the number of nodes and the total number of links in the fungal network increased under the warming treatments compared to the control, even though the fungal network complexity did not increase in mean clustering coefficient, graph density, and average degree (Fig.4b; Fig.S3; TableS5). Additionally, as shown by the average path length and diameter values, the connections in the fungal network became looser under warming combined with reduced precipitation treatments compared to the control (TableS5). Topological features (e.g., node number, edge number, average degree, average cluster coefficient, and graph density) were higher in bacterial networks in the T1, T2, and T3 treatments than in fungal networks. On the other hand, other features (e.g., average path length and graph diameter) exhibited the opposite trend, indicating that in the warming treatments, bacteria harbored more complex microbial networks than fungi (Fig.4; Fig.S3; TableS5).

The modularity indexes of fungi under the warming treatments were 0.562, 0.721, and 0.514, respectively, while those of bacteria were all less than 0.4 (TableS5), indicating that the fungal networks were more modular. More specifically, the larger bacterial co-occurrence networks also had a higher node degree and betweenness than fungal networks (Fig.4; Fig.S3; TableS5), while a lower modularity value indicated marginally lower modularity in bacterial networks than in fungal networks. Generally, bacterial co-occurrence networks were larger, more connected, and less modular than fungal networks.
Compared to ambient, the proportion of rhizosphere soils in the fungal network reduced by 82.60%, 92.07%, and 90.97% under the warming treatments, respectively, while the proportion of roots increased by 2.75%, 2.25%, and 0.95%, respectively (Fig.4b). The positive interaction between root fungi and rhizosphere soil fungi in the warming was markedly higher than ambient (Fig.4b). In contrast, we found that the proportion of rhizosphere soil in the bacterial network was significantly larger than that of the root in both the warming and control treatments (Fig.4a).
In this study, we sought to disentangle the effects of warming on the ecological interactions between different trophic levels, such as bacteria and fungi. Therefore, we established bidirectional networks between the two microbial groups under different treatments (Fig.5) and evaluated their network topology and structural indices (TableS5). Enhanced interactions within the bacterial-fungal network were discovered under warming treatments (Fig.5; TableS5). For example, the number of nodes and total links in the bacterial-fungal network increased under warming treatment compared to ambient, but the network diameter decreased (Fig.5). In addition, organizational properties such as modularity in the bacterial-fungal network decreased under warming treatments, while others, such as the average clustering coefficient, increased (Fig.5; TableS5). Bacterial nodes in the bacterial-fungal network showed an increase under warming treatments, and connectivity in T1, T2, and T3 treatments was 23%, 23%, and 12% higher than the ambient conditions, respectively (TableS5). In contrast, the connectivity of fungal taxa in the bacterial-fungal network was reduced by 15.2%, 23%, and 23%, respectively, in the warming treatments compared to ambient (TableS5).

We conducted Pearson’s correlations between plant characteristics and soil properties to thoroughly understand the role of keystone taxa (Fig.6e; TableS7). ASV2679 and ASV2298, two Ascomycota keystone taxa, exhibited a substantial negative connection with AK and a significant positive correlation with AB, RB, TN, SWC, and NO3−-N under various treatments (p< 0.05; Fig.6e; TableS7). Furthermore, a significant positive association was seen between the Ascomycota keystone taxa (ASV594) and AP, whereas the Actinobacteriota keystone taxa (ASV1462) demonstrated a strong positive correlation with AB, RB, pH, AP, AK, and SWC (p< 0.05; Fig.6e; TableS7). The keystone ASV3328 exhibited a significant positive correlation with pH and RN while demonstrating a negative correlation with SWC (p< 0.05; Fig.6e; TableS7). In addition, other keystones, including ASV1535, ASV225, and ASV752, all displayed significant negative correlations with SWC and positive correlations with RN (p< 0.05; Fig.6e; TableS7). Meanwhile, most keystone taxas from the Actinobacteriota, Proteobacteria, Ascomycota, Patescibacteria, and Planctomycetota were detected as positively correlated with pH and NH4+-N and positively related to SWC and AK (p< 0.05; Fig.6e; TableS7).
The neutral community model accurately estimated most of the relationships between the frequency of occurrence of ASVs and their relative abundance changes (Fig.7). Compared to the control (R2= 0.66), the fit of the soil bacterial community to the NCM under different warming treatments was 0.50, 0.42, and 0.26, respectively (Fig.7). Further, the fit of the root bacterial communities to NCM also showed a decreasing trend under warming treatments, and the fit was decreased by 24.24%, 36.36%, and 157.14%, respectively, compared to the control (Fig.7). There were similar trends for fungi, where fit to the NCM was lower than for bacteria in all warming treatments and both the roots and rhizosphere soils (Fig.7).

Most of the NST values of soil bacteria and fungi under the warming treatments were less than the 50% threshold compared to the control and showed a decreasing trend (Fig. S4), indicating that warming enhanced the dominance of deterministic processes. The bacterial and fungal community assembly processes in the roots and rhizosphere soils exhibited similar patterns among the warming treatments (Fig. S4).
There was an inconsistent pattern in how different microbial groups and ecological niches responded to warming in terms of microbial community diversity, composition, and structure (Fig.S1; Figs.2and4). Our study showed that warming increased the diversity of bacteria and fungi in the rhizosphere soil (Fig.S1), contrary to earlier findings from coastal non-tidal wetlands, tall grass steppe, and Tibetan alpine steppe [13,15,22]. As opposed to the substantial modifications observed in cold temperate grassland, alpine treelines [12,43], and tropical grasslands [9], warming did not affect root fungal diversity in drylands (Fig.S1). In addition, significant changes in the composition of the microbial community were also brought about by warming (Fig.2); these findings were in line with earlier research conducted in tropical forests and various grassland ecosystems [7,22], but they were not the same as those of more recent studies conducted in Mediterranean-type grasslands, which revealed taxonomic-level microbial community resilience to warming [8]. These contradictory findings could be explained by differences in aboveground community composition and the associated effects of litter quality and inputs, and root secretions [12,22], variations in soil physicochemical properties such as pH, SWC and available nutrients (Fig.3c) [12,22], as well as ecosystem type variations [8,9,22]. In general, the observed patterns of consistent or inconsistent trends in microbial diversity and composition reflect the diverse reactions of microbial communities in different environments and suggest that drylands may be a vulnerable ecosystem in the face of future climate change scenarios.
Apart from microbial diversity, there were significant shifts in the interactions within each trophic level and among trophic levels under the warming treatment, particularly for the bacterial- and fungal-associated networks (Fig.4). In contrast to findings from previous research in grassland and wetland ecosystems [20,22], fungal networks showed lower complexity with increasing warming (Fig.4b), indicating that the composition of fungal communities may be less stable in the context of climate change [20]. Given that nearly all the energy-sustaining fungal microbes originate from plant-derived photosynthesis [8,44], warming-associated droughts (dramatic reductions in SWC) and declines in belowground biomass (TableS1) may limit plant photosynthetic C allocation and plant-microbe associations (e.g., mycorrhizal symbiosis) [45,46], disrupt microbial resource utilization and microbial interactions, and ultimately resulting in the simplification of microbial networks. At the same time, bacterial interactions showed high sensitivity to warming treatments, resulting in substantial modifications in the microbial network topology under both warming and control treatments (Fig.4a; TableS5). We found that bacterial and fungal networks possess distinct properties and exhibit varying responses to warming, with warming having a more profound effect on bacteria than the fungal networks (Fig.4), as evidenced by a simulated field drought experiment [21]. Further, we noted that given the crucial role of interactions between microbial communities in enhancing ecosystem performance [17], the decrease in microbial network complexity brought on by warming treatments, particularly for fungi, may impair the ability of drylands to provide multiple soil functions. Our results point towards a breakdown of this complex fungal co-occurrence network in drylands after two years at weaker levels of warming. Our findings may provide insights over longer time-frames than the duration of our warming treatments, as the temperatures we observed may represent the upper limit for the survival of many fungal species in drylands.
Interestingly, enhanced interactions between bacteria and fungi were observed under warming conditions (Fig.5), and bacteria and fungi retained their internal interactions (TableS5), implying that interactions between bacteria and fungi contributed to the maintenance of specific taxa within the microbial community, as described by recent studies [22]. For example, bacteria that maintained complexity in their networks, as evidenced by the node number and connectivity (TableS5), would have profited from increased positive interactions with fungi.Ascomycota(such as saprophytic and symbiotrophic fungi) increased under the warming conditions observed in this study (Fig. S2), which may further increase the degradation of organic matter by fungi, thus promoting bacterial growth and maintaining the increased bacterial network complexity under warming conditions [22,47]. But up until now, most studies on microbial co-occurrence networks in the context of global climate change have concentrated on specific groups (like bacteria or fungi). In contrast, only a few studies have evaluated other important groups of organisms at more typological trophic levels, like protists, archaea, and nematodes [17,48,49], which we know are often highly complex with myriad biotic interactions. We propose that, given the critical role that multitrophic network complexity plays in strengthening the connection between ecosystem function and biodiversity [50], rather than taking these two microbial groups into account separately, the cross-trophic interactions between fungal and bacterial communities here were frequently a better predictor of multifunctionality in nutrient cycling. Although the microorganisms we focus on here are only fungi and bacteria, our results suggest that we could better predict soil multifunctionality in drylands if we included information on more inter-trophic interactions or complexity. Consequently, the next frontier is to establish a link between the microbiota composition in dryland ecosystems, including microbial interactions among multi-trophic levels, and ecosystem functioning in light of global climate change.
Removal of microbial keystone species can drastically alter the structure and function of microbial communities because they are highly associated taxa that are essential to microbial communities [28,51]. Our results indicated that the warming networks had significantly fewer keystone taxa than the control network and a lower relative abundance of some keystone taxa than the control network (Fig.6a-d, TableS6). These findings imply that below-ground microbial networks in drylands are disrupted and may display unstable properties in the event of future warming. While our network analyses (refer to relevant section/results) suggested an initial increase in cross-trophic interactions under warming—potentially reflecting a transient community response to altered resource availability—the observed significant reduction in keystone taxa abundance and richness points towards a critical long-term destabilization. Keystone taxa are pivotal for maintaining network cohesion and functional redundancy. Their decline, particularly among functionally important groups likeAscomycota(see below), likely undermines network stability and increases vulnerability to further perturbations. Crucially, this erosion of keystone taxa could cascade to impair key ecosystem multifunctionality processes, such as organic matter decomposition and nutrient cycling, which are heavily reliant on stable microbial interactions and the functional roles of dominant fungal taxa. More importantly, we found that the majority of these keystone ASVs in the warming and control networks were from the same phylum ofAscomycota(mainly belonging to the familiesSordariomycetesandEurotiomycetes) (Fig.6e, TableS6). Most members of theAscomycotaare saprophytes, which can decompose hard-to-degrade organic matter and play an essential role in nutrient cycling and plant growth. Therefore, the keystone ASVs in these Phyla may be closely associated with soil or plant properties. Our results showed that the relative abundances of threeAscomycotaASVs (ASV1223, ASV85, and ASV336) increased significantly with warming, correlating negatively with soil pH and positively with SWC (Fig.6e, TableS7). Similarly, ASV2679 and ASV2298, twoAscomycotaASVs, also demonstrated a strong positive correlation with AB and RB in their relative abundances (Fig.6e, TableS7), highlighting their importance in plant growth. It is noteworthy that despite increases in specificAscomycotaASVs (e.g., ASV1223, ASV85, ASV336, ASV2679, ASV2298), the overall richness and abundance of keystone taxa declined under warming. This suggests that while some taxa might exhibit opportunistic growth or adapt to short-term warming-induced changes (e.g., altered SWC/pH or plant C inputs correlating with AB/RB), the broader community structure experiences a net loss of keystone entities essential for long-term network robustness and multifunctionality. The specific responses of individual ASVs underscore the functional importance ofAscomycotabut also highlight that their collective role as keystones is diminished under sustained warming pressure. These findings, when considered alongside the preponderance ofAscomycotain the fungal community observed in our study (Fig.S2), indicate that the dominant taxa of the microbial community in drylands may play a pivotal role in maintaining the stability of modules and networks. Protecting these dominant microbial groups, particularly the keystone fungi withinAscomycota, is therefore critical for safeguarding the stability and multifunctionality of dryland soil ecosystems under future climate change. The findings indicated a need to protect the dominant microbial groups, particularly fungi, in drylands under future climate change scenarios.
Unraveling the consequences of climate change on ecological communities and the fundamental principles driving them is a critical problem in ecology and global change biology [11,24]. Few studies have investigated the mechanisms underlying microbial community assembly despite previous studies showing significant effects of precipitation changes and climate warming on microbial diversity [52], activities [53], structure [5,20], and functional gene composition [8]. In our arid desert ecosystems, experimental warming increased mean annual temperature by 0.5–1.5 °C and reduced annual precipitation by 5–8% [30], so it may gradually exert strong selective pressure as a deterministic force to reduce stochastic processes, as evidenced in other studies [11,24,54]. The NCM and NST analyses further revealed that warming gradually increased the relative importance of deterministic processes in shaping bacterial and fungal community structure (Fig.7) and that warming-induced selection pressure was reinforced by significantly reduced SWC, besides the soil pH and available nutrients (TableS1). These results suggested that warming could serve as a deterministic filtering factor to impose significant selection on microbes, potentially allowing only a limited number of species with similar characteristics (e.g., those that can tolerate high temperatures and reduced water availability) to survive [14]. The assembly of microbial communities could be more determined by deterministic mechanisms due to the resultant increased level of environmental filtration. Additionally, a partial CCA-based variation partitioning analysis revealed that the measured environmental variables explained a significant amount of the variance (17.05–37.38%) in the composition and structure of the bacterial and fungal communities (Fig.3a-b), supporting the notion that deterministic processes are more crucial to the assembly of microbial communities. These results were consistent with several previous studies showing that the effects of warming on microbial communities are intertwined with soil properties such as pH, water and nutrient content, and plant variables [11,24].
This study investigated how rhizosphere microbial communities respond to warming in drylands. Warming induced shifts in the composition and structure of bacterial and fungal communities, accompanied by changes in microbial diversity. Given the critical role of microbial interactions for ecosystem functioning, the observed decline in microbial network complexity under warming treatments—particularly pronounced in fungal networks—suggests potential constraints on drylands’ capacity to maintain multiple soil functions. IDENs analysis revealed intensified cross-trophic bacteria-fungi interactions under warming, contributing to the preservation of hierarchical microbial interactions, particularly sustaining bacterial network complexity. Our results demonstrate that considering both fungal and bacterial community characteristics jointly provided a better prediction of multifunctionality in nutrient cycling than considering either kingdom separately. This highlights the significance of multitrophic network complexity for ecosystem multifunctionality. While this study focused exclusively on bacteria and fungi within the microbiome, our findings suggest that incorporating broader trophic interactions (e.g., protists, microfauna) and full food-web complexity might further enhance predictions of dryland multifunctionality. NCM and NST analyses indicated that warming progressively increased the relative importance of deterministic processes in structuring bacterial and fungal communities. This implies that under sustained future warming, dryland rhizosphere microbial communities could transition toward a less stochastic state more rapidly. Consequently, while structural and compositional shifts occurred under experimental warming, our findings suggest that the trajectory of these communities under future climate change might exhibit reduced stochastic variability. However, it remains unclear if these communities will reach a stable, functionally resilient state.
This study is based on controlled warming experiments; extrapolation to natural climate change scenarios requires caution. The focus solely on bacteria and fungi excludes other key microbiome components (e.g., archaea, protists, viruses) potentially critical for multifunctionality. Our assessment of multifunctionality centered on nutrient cycling; other soil functions (e.g., carbon sequestration, pathogen suppression) warrant investigation. The duration of the experiment may not capture long-term adaptation or successional dynamics. Finally, the extent to which the observed increase in deterministic processes applies to non-dryland ecosystems remains untested and requires future research.
