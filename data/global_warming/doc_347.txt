Anastrepha ludens(Mexican fruit-fly) is a highly polyphagous fruit fly species (Tephritidae) attacking wild and commercial fruit from Mexico to Panama. Here we report on a recent habitat and host range expansion asA. ludenslately started to attack apples (Malus domestica) in Mexico, a phenomenon likely influenced by global warming. We document natural infestations in apple-growing regions in the States of Nuevo León and Hidalgo, Mexico whereA. ludenshas started to attack the cultivars ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’. No infestations were found in the apple-growing region of Zacatlán, Puebla. To determine apple cultivar susceptibility to the attack of this emerging pest, we ran forced infestation assays in enclosed fruit-bearing branches in all three apple-growing regions and studied the metabolome of all fruit. A clear pattern emerged indicating that the cultivar ‘Golden Delicious’ was the most susceptible, with ‘Criolla’ exhibiting complete resistance in one location (Puebla). AlthoughA. ludenscan develop in this new host, development rates (egg—adult) and adult emergence were affected when compared with the performance in the natural host ‘Marsh’ grapefruit. Warmer temperatures and specific secondary metabolites of some apple cultivars are likely contributing to the territorial and host expansion ofA. ludens.

Global warming, one of the most important factors involved in global climate change, is causing many, and still little understood alterations in the environment and affecting a myriad of interactions between plants and insects1–3. Among the most worrisome effects, some of the following stand out: expanded distribution of invasive species4, habitat and host range expansion of insect pests5, alterations in population dynamics of beneficial, neutral, and pestiferous insects6,7, alterations in flowering and fruiting patterns of trees, among them commercially grown species8, and local extinctions of key beneficial species9.
Among the most important agricultural pests worldwide, fruit flies (Diptera: Tephritidae) stand out because of their direct damage to fruit (larvae feeding in the pulp causing eventual rotting) and local/international restrictions to trade10,11. In recent years, a series of studies have been published on the potential expansion of their natural distribution range due to climatic alterations, among others, increases in temperature12–21. The phenomenon includes highly polyphagous species such as the Mediterranean fruit fly,Ceratitis capitataWiedemann or the Oriental fruit fly,Bactrocera dorsalis(Hendel)17,22,23, but also stenophagous species such as the South American Cucurbit fly,Anastrepha grandis(Macquart)24,25.
Our study object, the Mexican fruit fly,Anastrepha ludens(Loew), is a potentially invasive polyphagous species distributed from Mexico to Panama26. The three purported ancestral hosts areCasimiroa edulisLa Llave & Lex,C. greggii(S. Watson) F. Chiang andC. pubescensRamírez (all Rutaceae), but the pest also develops well inCitrus×﻿aurantiumL. (bitter orange),C. ×paradisiMacfad. (grapefruit) (also both Rutaceae), andMangifera indicaL. (Anacardiaceae)27,28, all of them growing in tropical and subtropical regions of Mexico. Other natural hosts, albeit ones where the larvae develop less well transforming into small pupae and adults, arePrunus persica(L.) Bastch (peach, Rosaceae) andCapsicum pubescens(L.) (‘Manzano’ pepper, Solanaceae)27. Field infestations byA.ludensin pomegranate (Punica granatumL., Lythraceae), albeit in very small numbers possibly indicating a recent local host shift, have also been reported in a few backyards in isolated semi-arid temperate areas at 1500 masl in NE Mexico29. We have been able to recently (2024) confirmA.ludensinfestations in semicommercial and commercial pomegranate groves (Tasquillo, Hidalgo, Mexico) (MA, unpublished data) and also detected incipient infestations in apples (Malus domestica(Suckow) Borkh., Rosaceae) in isolated commercial orchards 12 years ago which we now here firmly confirm. Based on the later worrisome finding, we first embarked in a study under strict quarantine artificial laboratory conditions in Switzerland addressing the question if there was differential susceptibility to the attack ofA.ludensin highly valued commercial cultivars such as ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Gala’ and ‘Fuji’, and if small apples, of low commercial value, but cherished by old farmers, were resistant to the infestation of this pestiferous fruit fly15. We discovered that indeed some “old” cultivars of low commercial value were totally resistant to the attack ofA.ludensunder artificial laboratory conditions, and that cultivars such as ‘Golden Delicious’ were totally destroyed by the larvae (yield of ca. 1000 pupae in 25 fruit;15). We then embarked in a much more ambitious and recent study in Mexico, applying state-of-the-art-techniques such as niche modeling, population genetics, metabolomics (aimed at finding resistance chemical markers), transcriptomics (aimed at ascertaining if apples exposed to different temperatures and infestation regimes expressed genes related to temperature adaptation), proteomics (to determine the proteins in the antenna that possible relate to the recognition of apple odors), and the study of the apple volatilome in uncut apples, white sapote, bitter orange, and grapefruit to then study the electroantenographic and wind tunnel responses of sexually mature, mated females to certain apple volatiles and synthetics thereof (all work in progress). We note, that the only otherAnastrephaspecies reported attacking apples isA.fraterculus(Wiedemann), which also became a pest of this valuable commodity in Brazil30–33.
Here we report on a in depth study carried out in three apple-growing regions in Mexico (states of Puebla, Hidalgo, and Nuevo León) that are being apparently invaded byA.ludenslikely due to global warming. We note, that on a straight line, lowland (i.e., 100–300 m above sea level, henceforth masl) ‘Marsh’ grapefruit groves and wild patches of bitter orange (both severely infested byA.ludens) are located less than 100 km from highland (i.e., 1800–2300 masl) commercial apple orchards. We further note that continuous records over a 40-year period indicate that temperature has been rising in the apple-growing regions. We collected naturally infested apples in the field, and also conducted forced infestation studies in bagged, fruit-bearing apples, of three locally grown apple cultivars: ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’ to determine natural infestation levels (i.e., pupae/kg of fruit) and signs of tolerance/resistance to the attack of the new invasive pestA.ludens. We also studied the metabolome of the three apple cultivars in an effort to identify potential chemical resistance markers. Based on what we previously learned in Switzerland15, we hypothesized that the intensive breeding process of commercial apple cultivars reduces their resistance to insect attack also exposing them to the effects of global warming that is favoring the establishment of new pest species. We predicted increased levels of secondary metabolites and higher levels of resistance to the infestation ofA. ludensin local cultivars of low commercial value (i.e., ‘Criolla’) than in intensively bred cultivars (e.g., ‘Golden Delicious’).

The locality of Zacatlán, Puebla (PU) exhibited the lowest temperatures but a more stable temperature pattern over time. Eighteen phenolic compounds and one precursor (L-phenylanine) were identified and quantified in apple samples from Hidalgo (Table3), Nuevo León (Table4) and Puebla (Table5). The phenolics identified belong to different chemical subgroups, mainly phenolic acids and flavonoids. According to the one-way ANOVA (Fig.5), procyanidin B2, followed by (−)-epicatechin, (+)-catechin and phloridzin were among the compounds that most influenced the grouping of the PCA, where two PCs explain 81.7% of the variance among apple treatments. Four groups were clearly formed (Fig.6), with procyanidin B2 being the compound that most influenced the formation of the PCA groups. The treatments and groups with the most contrasting phenolic profiles were ‘Rayada’-PU (Group 1) and ‘Criolla’-PU (Group 4) (Figs.6and7). Groups 2 and 3 were closer between them; Group 2 included the treatments ‘Criolla’-HG, ‘Golden’-HG, ‘Golden’-NL and ‘Golden’-PU, and the Group 3 included the treatments ‘Rayada’-HG, ‘Criolla’-NL and ‘Rayada’-NL (Fig.6). Several noteworthy findings emerged from our study: (1) We have now firm evidence thatA.ludenshas indeed expanded its distribution and host range to highland, temperate areas where it now infests the hitherto unknown hostM.domestica. Related to this finding, we highlight the fact thatA. ludenshas also been found infesting the exotic hostP.granatumat 1500 masl29. As noted in theresultssection, the differences in mean temperature between the Durango, Zimapán (Hidalgo), El Cilantrillo, Santiago (Nuevo León) and Zacatlán (Puebla) sites between 1981 and 2021 were 1.37 °C, 0.94 °C, and 1.14 °C, respectively to the states, which coincides with the upward trend (i.e., countrywide increase of 0.31 °C in mean temperature) for Mexico in the period 1991–2020 reported by The World Bank Group34. This has important ecological/behavioral (e.g., a new competitive interaction between the establishedR.pomonellaand the invaderA.ludenswill ensue) and economic implications, and is also coincident with the prediction of Rubenstein et al.3who indicated that several animal species are shifting their spatial distribution in response to rising temperature, in this case towards higher latitudes and greater elevations. Consistent with this, we found natural infestations byA. ludensin apples from Nuevo León and Hidalgo, the two states where the highest mean maximum temperatures were recorded over a 40 year (1981–2021) period (20.7 and 21.9 °C, respectively), compared to Puebla (18.4 °C) where so far no infestations have been recorded. (2) In line with our working hypothesis and derived predictions, and despite the fact that this tephritid fly is now able to infest apples in the field, the new host poses challenges to the pestiferous insect, as our data clearly show that the development of larvae is suboptimal compared to the natural host grapefruit (see end of legend in Fig.4for further details), likely due to the high polyphenol content of certain apple cultivars. That is, we are witnessing an incipient process of adaptation to a new host that offers ample opportunities to study the biochemical processes at play during host expansion phenomena. (3) The cultivar ‘Golden Delicious’ planted worldwide in apple-growing regions is the most susceptible to the attack/infestation of this new pest of apples, elevating the status ofA.ludensas a key quarantine pest of international significance. Related to this, we note that of the three cultivars studied, ‘Golden Delicious’ fruit contained the most sugar content (with the exception of ‘Rayada’ Puebla) but at the same time the lowest levels of most polyphenols determined, particularly those present in ‘Criolla’ Puebla which was totally resistant to the attack ofA.ludensin the forced field infestation tests (e.g., phenylalanine, most flavonoids measured, procyanidin B2; Tables4and5). (4) Our results clearly flushed out site effects on apple characteristics, also showing that at least in the cases of the cultivars locally named ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’ should be renamed as they are not the same. To start, physicochemical characteristics clearly distinguish them (Table2; Fig.8) which was not the case with ‘Golden Delicious’. Furthermore, our metabolomic study also flushed out differences in secondary metabolite contents (and concentrations), pointing to the fact that ‘Golden Delicious’ from the three sites were quite similar, but ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’ exhibited significant variability among sites and in infestation levels. For example, ‘Criolla’ apples from Hidalgo are totally different from the ones in Nuevo León and Puebla measured by the level of infestation (mean number of larvae/fruit; Fig.4a).

We were able to confirm previous experimental studies15indicating thatA.ludenscan indeed infest apples under laboratory conditions, but here we also undoubtedly report natural infestations of apples by this pestiferous tephritid fly in the field. If the later can be completely or partially attributed to the effect of global warming, requires further investigation, but the fact is that between 1981 and 2021 the mean temperature has risen 1.37 °C (from 17.01 to 18.40 °C), 0.94 °C (from 14.22 to 15.16 °C), and 1.14 °C (from 14.13 to 15.27 °C) in Durango, Zimapán (Hidalgo), El Cilantrillo, Santiago (Nuevo León) and Zacatlán (Puebla), respectively. It is now known that one degree difference in temperature can have profound ecological implications35–37,among them a shift in the distribution range of various organisms from lower to higher altitudes3. The rising temperatures became more apparent in the last three years (2022–2024) as maximum overall (the entire state, not only study sites) mean temperatures were 29.9, 30.9, 30.6 °C (Nuevo León), 26.6, 27.6, 29.6 °C (Hidalgo), and 26.4, 26.7 and 28.66 °C (Puebla)(Comisión Nacional del Agua, CONAGUA, Mexico). We note too that commercialization and/or transport by humans (including migrants) ofA. ludenshost fruits to regions where apples are grown could also aid the pest in expanding its distribution range. That is, a synergism between global warming, the susceptibility of some apple cultivars, and the transport of infested fruits from lowland to highland areas could be likely favoring the geographical range and host expansion byA. ludens38,39. Of the three apple-growing regions studied, two (Hidalgo and Nuevo León) have already been invaded byA.ludenswhich is infesting at least three cultivars: mainly ‘Golden Delicious’, followed by ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’, which could also be influenced by the nearby presence of some of the native hosts ofA. ludenssuch asC. edulisand C.pubescens. In the remaining region (Puebla), we were not able to document field infestations. That is, there is an interaction between the site of collection and the infestation rate.
Our forced infestation bioassays in the field confirmed the findings of Aluja and collaborators obtained under artificial laboratory conditions15, in the sense that not all cultivars are equally infested. ‘Golden Delicious’ yielded the greatest number of larvae/pupae per kilogram of fruit in Nuevo León and Hidalgo (in the latter case the high variability in the data obscured the differences) unexpectedly almost as the same level of ‘Criolla’ from Hidalgo (‘Criolla’-HG) (Fig.4a). Notably, in the Puebla study site, the ‘Criolla’ cultivar exhibited total resistance to the attack ofA.ludens. In broad terms, we can surmise that we are witnessing an incipient adaptation process to a new host, and that apples, although viable substrates for larval development, still represent a challenge to the new pest. As noted before, we obtained 4558 larvae/pupae from 25 grapefruit (a natural host) and only 773 larvae from 25 ‘Golden Delicious’-HG apples (the most infested cultivar/site). Related to this, the mean number of larvae per fruit in the three apple cultivars at three different sites was consistently lower than five, while in grapefruit this value was almost 40. Another critical fitness parameter, pupal weight, was also very different when averaging over all apple cultivars/sites and comparing it to grapefruit: 12.2 mg vs 19.7 mg, respectively. This system thus offers a unique opportunity to study in depth the physiological/biochemical adaptation processes at play during the adaptation process to a new host in a pestiferous tephritid fly.
The fact that we have now firmly established thatA.ludensis infesting apples in the field has important implications in the regulatory realm as apples are grown worldwide and apple-producing countries should reinforce quarantine policies to precludeA.ludensfrom entering these countries. As recently shown by Guillén et al.21, adults of this species can survive well over a year under artificial laboratory conditions (growth chamber) at 15 °C, but also manage to survive 40 and 200 days at 5 and 10 °C, respectively. Survival in highland habitats (1500 masl) with low winter temperatures was documented by Vanoye-Eligio et al.29in the municipalities of Miquihuana and Bustamante in the state of Tamaulipas, Mexico. This could mean that adults could easily establish in a single apple-growing season if accidentally brought to some European countries such as Switzerland, France, Italy and Germany21. But there are also interesting ecological/behavioral implications. In North America (Mexico, USA, Canada), apples are infested byR.pomonella, a species that shifted from hawthorns to apples ca. 200 years ago40–43. The fact that we have discovered that in Mexico apples are also being infested byA.ludens, implies that a new competitive interaction between these two species is in progress. We have started a series of experiments to dissect this new phenomenon, and at least for now, and despite the much smaller size ofR.pomonella, this species is holding ground (Guillén et al., unpublished information). But this will likely change asA.ludensexhibits enormous ecological and behavioral plasticity27,44–46.
We note, that even though we selected in each site what was originally thought to be the same cultivars (based on the local name and knowledge), our results indicate that at least in the case of the cultivars ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’, additional studies are necessary to exactly determine the cultivar identity (e.g., studies on the genetic background) of each cultivar in each region. The ‘Golden Delicious’ fruits at the three sites were very similar in physical (i.e., size, shape and color; Table2; Fig.3), and chemical features (phenolic compounds profiles; Tables3,4and5), which together probably influenced the susceptibility of ‘Golden Delicious’ apples toA. ludensattack. For example, the color of the ‘Golden Delicious’ apples is yellow-green, and precisely, these colors are reported as the most attractive forA. ludens, even more than red47. Other factors that may have influenced susceptibility to attack byA. ludensmay have been the fruit firmness and soluble solids content (°Brix), which are inversely related. For example, ‘Criolla’-HG, the second most infested treatment, had the least fruit firmness and at the same time one of the sweetest, which contrasts with ‘Criolla’-NL, which was one of the least infested but had the firmest and the least sweet fruit.Anastrepha ludenscan adjust the number of eggs it lays according to the hardness of the fruit, which can be an indicator of the ripeness of the fruit or more suitable conditions for larval development44.
In addition to the physicochemical characteristics color, fruit firmness, and sugar content, the metabolic profile was different in each cultivar and at each study site. Phenolic compounds present in the fruit peel are synthesized to cope with the biotic and abiotic stress conditions, and their content directly depend on the cultivar and chemical defense mechanisms48–50. In this sense, the different levels of phenolic compounds found in this study may have contributed to the different apple cultivars being more susceptible or tolerant toA. ludensinfestation15,51,52. L-phenylalanine is biosynthesized in the shikimate pathway and converted toρ-coumaroyl-CoA by the action of PAL, C4H and 4CL enzymes, forming the general phenylpropanoid pathway. Subsequently,ρ-coumaroyl-CoA as the initial substrate is eventually converted into flavonoids, some of which are involved in defense mechanisms inhibiting insect development and oviposition to a certain extent53. For example, higher levels of the phenolic precursor phenylalanine, the flavonoids (−)-epicatechin, (+)-catechin, phloridzin and rutin among others, and the procyanidin B2 (a proanthocyanidin) in the ‘Criolla’-PU compared to the other cultivars which had lower levels (Tables3,4and5), probably contributed to increasing the defenses of this cultivar againstA. ludensattack and also inhibiting the development of eggs and larvae by acting as antinutritional or toxic compounds. The protective effects against herbivory that proanthocyanidins (end products of the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway and also called condensed tannins) convey to plants are well known and are mainly due to the astringency they give to fruits, seeds, leaves, and barks of many plants, thus reducing their palatability and acting as feeding deterrents54, but can also affect the insect’s development15. In treatments such as ‘Rayada’-PU, which also exhibited higher concentrations of phenolic compounds but at lower levels than ‘Criolla’-PU,A. ludensoviposited on these fruits, but its offspring performed poorly (low emergence and long development time). In contrast, ‘Criolla’-HG, the treatment with the highest overallA. ludensinfestation, exhibited markedly lower concentrations of phenolic compounds, mainly procyanidin B2 (2.34 ug/g vs. 1654 ug/g of ‘Criolla’-PU), the lowest fruit firmness but high sugar content. Similar results were reported by Sun et al.55working with wild apples resistant to the blue mold produced byPenicillium expansum.They found higher concentrations of (-)-epicatechin, procyanidin B1, procyanidin B2, phloridzin, quercetin 3-O-glucoside and chlorogenic acid in the resistant wild apples compared to susceptible highly bred cultivars, such as ‘Golden Delicious’55.
Although at the beginning (planning phase) of in this study the treatment ‘Criolla’-PU was considered equivalent to ‘Criolla’-HG and therefore possibly resistant toA. ludensattack (based on Aluja et al.15), studies carried out simultaneously to ours on the volatilome56and metabolome (Camacho-Vázquez et al., unpublished data) quickly unraveled the fact that we were dealing with fruit of very different characteristics. Despite the latter, one factor that could have increased the susceptibility of the purported ‘Criolla’ cultivar in Hidalgo could have been ambient temperature. It is known that the biosynthesis of phenolic compounds in plants are dependent of biotic and abiotic factors (e.g. temperature, UV radiation, etc.)57,58. During our study, the ‘Criolla’ apples from Hidalgo experienced high temperatures for a week that may have decreased polyphenol biosynthesis, in contrast to the ‘Criolla’ apples from Puebla which experienced stable temperatures throughout the study. In addition, the cumulative effect of the increase in average minimum and maximum temperatures observed over the last 40 years in Hidalgo, may already be interfering with the biosynthesis of phenolic compounds.
In conclusion, our findings have important practical implications for the management of an emerging pest of apples. Rising temperatures are, on the one hand, likely pushingA.ludensto highland apple-growing regions from its original tropical range, and on the other, have already begun to affect apple-growing areas in northern Mexico, where growers are being forced to use cold compensators to meet the apples’ cold-hour requirements59. That is, apple growers in Mexico (and potentially other apple-growing regions in the world), must prepare fast to deal with rapidly changing scenarios. Barran-Prior56studied the volatilome of the three apple cultivars studied here and of three natural hosts ofA.ludens(White Sapote,C. edulis; Grapefruit,C.×paradisi; and Sour Orange,C.×aurantium, all Rutaceae), in an attempt to ascertain if these hosts share common volatiles, and if esters emitted by apples that are highly attractive toR. pomonella, an established pest of apples in the USA and Canada, are also attractive toA.ludens. The latest with the goal of developing efficient monitoring systems forA.ludensin apple orchards. Our work here and an additional ongoing one on secondary metabolite contents in apples (Camacho-Vázquez et al., unpublished data), might be helpful in selecting tolerant or resistant cultivars for planting in new orchards. It will also aid growers in increasing marketability of their crops as interest in antioxidants (e.g., polyphenols) is rising among consumers. We will also soon know based on our studies on the genetic structure ofA.ludenspopulations infesting apples (Piñero and collaborators, unpublished data), if we are witnessing an incipient speciation process with genetic underpinnings. Should this be true, the quarantine status of this pestiferous fruit fly will be solidified.

These temperature data were obtained from the NASA Langley Research Center (LaRC) POWER Project funded through the NASA Earth Science/Applied Science Program, accessing the POWER Data Access Viewer v2.0.0 on 16/03/2023 for Veracruz´s data and on 17/0723 for Hidalgo and Puebla data60.
To determine if apples grown in commercial orchards were already being infested byA. ludensin Mexico, apples were collected from various locally grown cultivars (among them ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Rayada’ and ‘Criolla’) in different locations in Mexico over an 11 year period (2010–2021), including the sites where “forced infestation bioassays” were run in 2021 (details follow). Complete details on collection site names, coordinates, altitude, and infestation status of fruits are provided in TableS1. In each collection site, we sampled fruit from the tree crown or fruit that had already fallen to the ground, weighed each sample, and placed the fruit in a plastic 30 × 22 × 13 cm (length, width and depth) washbowl which we covered with nylon pantyhoses to precludeDrosophilaand other insects from damaging the fruit. Samples were then transported to the headquarters of the Instituto de Ecología, A.C.—INECOL in Xalapa, Veracruz which entailed driving 1150 km considering the most remote location. There, each washbowl was placed in cement racks in a special laboratory under natural ambient conditions to allow the larvae to complete development and pupate. To facilitate pupation, we placed vermiculite on the base of the washbowl. Inspection in search of pupae occurred every third day. Pupae were placed in 150 mL plastic containers with moistened vermiculite. For some collections, when pupae were 4 days old, they were individually weighed and then returned to the 150 mL plastic containers until adults emerged.
To analyze the differential susceptibility of the three apple cultivars ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Criolla’ and ‘Rayada’ (Fig.8) to the attack ofA. ludens, we carried out forced infestations in different study sites in three states of Mexico (Puebla, Hidalgo and Nuevo León). As a comparative reference (control treatment), the natural host grapefruit ‘Marsh’ was also forcibly infested in San José Acateno, Puebla following the same experimental procedures as in apples (TableS9).
In this study we used apple cultivars that according to the perception of the growers and a previous publication15, could have different degrees of susceptibility to the attack ofA.ludens. The cultivars were identified considering the names that locals in the area use to refer to them and by physically comparing the fruit appearance with pictures published in Mexican books, technical reports, articles and student theses61–63. To determine apple shape and peel color, we used the guide of the Fruit ID Apple Identification (https://www.fruitid.com/#quickid/1) and the Montana Heritage Apple Identification Key from the Montana State University (https://agresearch.montana.edu/warc/guides/apples/heritage_orchard_management_guide/heritage-fruit-id-guide/apple_identification_key.html) which considers three criteria to evaluate the color (the overall appearance, the ground color [primary color], and over color [a secondary color “blush type” on top of the ground color]).
The following are the cultivars used which we grouped according to similarities and the local names used by producers:
Group ‘Golden Delicious’: The cultivar ‘Golden Delicious’ is one of the most widely distributed in the world and was introduced to Mexico during the 20th century61. Due to its great juiciness, sugar content and low degree of acidity, it has great demand in the market, and it is present in all apple-producing areas of Mexico. The fruits used in this study were in appearance very uniform in shape (round conical) and peel color (green but yellow when ripe and with little russeting on the top cavity) but different in size (Table2, Fig.8). In the three places, some producers call it ‘Golden Agua Nueva’ but here we refer to it as as ‘Golden Delicious’.
Group ‘Rayada’: It was one of the first cultivars introduced to Mexico 200 years ago61. The name ‘Rayada’ is due to the presence of strips in the skin (strips means “rayas” in Spanish). This cultivar is apparently derived from ‘Red Delicious’ and it is red striped with yellow ground color (background). The cultivar ‘Rayada’, identified by producers in the three sites, had fruit with some variations in shape and color. For example, in Puebla, the fruit has a flat-round shape and a light red striped color with a pale green color in the ground. But in Hidalgo, the fruit had an oblong shape and red color very vaguely striped with a pale green background and partially russetted on the top. In Nuevo León the fruit shape is round, red color with vague strips and partially russetted on the top (Table2, Fig.8).
Group ‘Criolla’: Producers call ‘Criolla’ any small apples as they do not know the origin and cultivar. The fruit of this cultivar were quite different in appearance among sites. For example in Puebla, the fruit is very small (compared to the locally grown ‘Golden Delicious’ and ‘Rayada’) and its peel has a yellow ground color with pink-red hue flushed colored by sunlight. In Hidalgo, the ‘Criolla’ apples are medium-sized with a red peel color. Finally, in Nuevo León, the ‘Criolla’ cultivar, also named “Perón Saltillense” has a medium size and green/yellow peel color (Table2, Fig.8).
To obtain fruit flies for forced infestation bioassays, fruits of White Sapote (C. edulis), naturally infested withA. ludens, were collected from sites near to the apple orchards in which we worked (TableS9). Collected fruits were taken to the fruit processing laboratory at INECOL, and then placed in a plastic basket (32.5 cm × 26.5 cm × 8.5 cm), inside a plastic tray (36 cm × 30 cm × 14 cm), that contained vermiculite (1 cm deep) as a pupation substrate. Pupae were separated every three days, placed in 150 mL containers with vermiculite and sprayed every third day until adult emergence. Newly emerged flies (100 females and 50 males) were placed in cages (30 cm on each side) at 25 ± 1 °C, 70 ± 10% relative humidity (RH), and a photoperiod of 12:12 h L: D. Flies were fed  ad libitum with sugar: hydrolyzed protein (3:1) and water until sexual maturity (15–25 days old), until used for forced infestation bioassays.
Twenty days before forced infestation bioassays started, groups of 2–5 fruits exhibiting physiological maturity and attached to the tree, were bagged with 15 × 23 cm or 30 × 50 cm white tulle fabric (tricot nylon) bags to avoid damage by fruit flies and other insects, birds, and mammals (following the methods described by Aluja et al.64). After 20 days, the bags were removed, and the apples were carefully washed with distilled water, dried, and re-bagged in groups of 2–5 fruits with the same bags (all fruit remained attached to the branches). Then, fruits were exposed to the fruit flies, in a proportion of two sexually mature, gravid females and one male per fruit. Flies were fedad libitumwith an artificial diet (sugar: hydrolyzed protein 3:1) and water was supplied in a 1.5 mL microtube (food was placed on the lid and water was placed inside the tube with a piece of felt). In five trees of each apple cultivar ‘Golden Delicious’, ‘Criolla’ or ‘Rayada’) and grapefruit ‘Marsh’, 20-23 fruits per tree were exposed toA. ludensduring three days in each site (Puebla, Hidalgo and Nuevo León). After this time of exposure, flies, food, and water were removed from the bags, and fruits were left on the tree for 20 days to allow the natural development ofA. ludenseggs and larvae. At day 21, apples were harvested and transported to INECOL for further processing.
In the laboratory, the fruits used for forced-infestation bioassays were placed individually in plastic containers (11 cm base diameter, 15 cm top diameter, 6.5 cm height), and covered with pantyhose to avoid the attack of other insects. All fruit were kept at 25 ± 1 °C, 70 ± 5% RH, and a photoperiod of 12:12 h L: D. Daily, containers were checked, and the following parameters were registered per fruit: (a) Number of larvae, pupae, and emerged adults (males and females), (b) Pupal weight at four days after pupation, (c) development time from egg to the adult emergence, (d) Longevity of 100 adults since emergence (50 females and 50 males).
On the first day of the forced infestation bioassays, a sample of 20 additional apples per tree (100 fruits in total per cultivar per site) not exposed to flies, were harvested to evaluate the following physicochemical parameters: (1) weight (g) measured with a digital balance (Electronic, Mod. SF-400); (2) size (diameter and height in cm) measured with a manual vernier; (3) sugar content measured with a manual refractometer (Atago, Mod. N-1E); (4) fruit hardness using a portable penetrometer (Wagner Instruments, Mod. FT011 Greenwich, CT) with a 2 mm diameter “needle”; (5) fruit color using a portable colorimeter (Konica Minolta, Inc., Mod. CR-20, Tokyo, Japan), which measured the color space using the luminosity (L*) and the chromatic coordinates red/green (a*) and yellow/blue (b*); and 6) content of phenolics compounds (see next section).
The identification and quantification of phenolic compounds was performed following the same methods as previously reported in Monribot-Villanueva et al.65,66.
Methanolic extracts were prepared with an accelerated solvent extraction system (ASE 350, Thermo Scientific) according to previous reports65,66. Methanolic extracts were dried in rotatory evaporators (Mod.RII, Büchi, Switzerland) and 50 mg were dissolved in methanol at 50 mg/mL, filtered with 0.2 μm PTFE membranes and placed in 2 mL UPLC vials.
Data were obtained with a 1290 infinity Agilent ultrahigh resolution liquid chromatograph coupled to a 6460 Agilent triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. The mobile phases were water with 0.1% formic acid (A) and acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid (B), both in MS grade. The flow was 0.3 mL/min. The injection volume was 2 µL. The column was a Waters, Acquity BEH C18, 2.1 × 50 mm, 1.7 μm and the column temperature was 40 °C. The elution gradient and the mass spectrometer conditions are described in TableS10. The method used was a dynamic MRM (Multiple Reaction Monitoring). The conditions for each compound are described in MS conditions of TableS11.
Natural infestation data were organized and presented in a descriptive table (Tables1,S1). Considering that apples had different appearances in the three study sites, each combination of cultivar and place was considered a treatment. Thus, we had nine treatments (3 sites × 3 cultivars in each one), and the mean values for each tree were considered a replicate (n= 5). The categorical independent variable was the cultivar/site and the dependent variables for physicochemical parameters were weight, longitude, °Brix and firmness. All fruit parameters were analyzed with one-way ANOVA. When the analysis showed statistical significance, we performed Tukey HSD for multiple comparisons of means. Additionally, considering that firmness and °Brix are related to fruit maturity and, therefore, are parameters that can influence the female’s decision to oviposit a fruit, a linear regression was performed with all the data of these two parameters without considering the treatment. In the case of the parameters used to measure the skin color of apples (coordinates of luminosity [L*], chromatic red/green [a*] and yellow/blue [b*]), since they define together the color status, a MANOVA test was used. The Wilks lambda of the model was 0.00012, so the three parameters were kept to search for differences among treatments. The assumptions of normal distribution and equal variance of the residuals were confirmed graphically67.
A linear model was fitted to the data of the mean number of larvae per fruit as a function of the apple cultivar-site treatment (n = five trees per cultivar site each with 20–23 apples subjected to the forced infestation treatment). Data were log (y + 0.01715) transformed to comply with the model assumptions of homoscedasticity. The model was used to estimate the means and 95% confidence intervals (95% CI) of larvae per fruit found in each cultivar site. Mean model estimates and their 95% CI were back transformed to their original scale using an exponential function.
In the case of data on pupation and adult emergence (i.e., proportion data), generalized linear models (GLM) were fitted with quasibinomial error distribution (to account for overdispersion) and logit link function. To weigh the sample size from which the proportions of pupation or adult emergence were estimated, the models were fitted to the data of the response variables in the form of a binary vector containing the number of larvae that pupated or the number of flies that emerged and those that did not. The significance of the effects of the predictor on the response variable was evaluated using an analysis of deviance with an F distribution.
In the cases of data on pupal weight and adult lifespan, linear models failed to find a statistically significant effect of the predictor variable on the response variables (p= 0.2107 in the case of pupal weight, andp= 0.3683 in the case of adult lifespan). As such, null models (i.e., the overall mean) were used to describe the data. To examine if the null models were more suitable for describing the data than the non-significant linear models with the cultivar-site as a predictor variable, we assessed by ANOVA if reducing the model caused a significant reduction in the explanatory power of the model, and computed Akaike’s Information Criterion with a correction for a small design (AICc) to select the best model.
In the case of the development time of flies from egg to adult, data were transformed (y− 3) to comply with the ANOVA assumption of homoscedasticity. Mean model estimates and their 95% CI were back transformed to their original scale by raising them to − 1/3.
In all cases, the assumptions of normal distribution and equal variance of the residuals were checked graphically. When a model detected significant effects of the predictor in the response variable, we used post-hoc Tukey tests to determine the statistical significance of the differences between pairs of means. Analyses were performed with the R software68using the packages MuMIn, multcomp and gtools69–71and the Statistica 10 software72.
The phenolic compounds data of the apples were analyzed in the MetaboAnalyst 5.0 platform (https://www.metaboanalyst.ca/MetaboAnalyst/ModuleView.xhtml). The original data were normalized by Pareto scaling. The metabolomic data analyses included one-way ANOVA to identify the most important compounds and post-hoc analysis (Fisher´s LSD test), both with p value threshold 0.05, to compare each compound between each pair of treatments. Also, a principal component analysis (PCA) was used to check the best separation of treatments and the components that explained most of the variance. Finally, a hierarchical clustering analysis using Euclidean distance for similarity measure of compounds, and the clustering algorithm Ward’s linkage (the ward.D algorithm) for clustering to minimize the sum of squares of any two clusters, was also run.
