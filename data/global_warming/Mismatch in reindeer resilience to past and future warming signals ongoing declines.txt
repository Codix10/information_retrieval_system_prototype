Rangifer tarandus(caribou or reindeer) survived periods of abrupt climatic warming during the last deglaciation but are currently in global decline. Using process-explicit models of likely climate-human-Rangiferinteractions and inferences of demographic change from radiocarbon-dated fossils and ancient DNA, we reconstruct and decipher 21,000 years ofRangiferpopulation dynamics. These high-resolution population reconstructions pinpoint ecological characteristics and life-history traits that most likely enabledRangiferto survive rapid warming events following the Last Glacial Maximum. Projecting these process-driven models into the future reveals that these attributes are unlikely to bufferRangiferagainst wide-scale population declines from expected 21st Century climatic warming. Our findings highlight a need to boost investments in the management and conservation ofRangifer, particularly in North America, where projected losses are expected to exceed 80%. This will not only support the survival of the species and the vital services it renders in Arctic ecosystems, but also help sustain the socioeconomic, cultural, and emotional well-being of manyRangifer-dependent communities.
Rangiferpopulation dynamics over 21,000 years reveal that future climatic warming will cause losses that surpass safe boundaries.
Rangifer tarandus(caribou or reindeer) is the only member of the deer family (Cervidae) adapted to year-round occupancy of the Arctic, exhibiting unique morphological, behavioral, and physiological adaptations to cold environments (1). Its foraging activities regulate the structure and function of climate-sensitive tundra ecosystems, providing important services to nature and people (2,3). From a historical perspective,R. taranduswas important in pushing the frontiers of human settlement of the Arctic and far north (4), where it continues to provide an important cultural and socioeconomic value to humans (5).
Despite being the most abundant terrestrial large herbivore in the Arctic,Rangifer(comprising the many subspecies ofR. tarandus) (6) has lost nearly two-thirds of its global abundance in the past three decades (7,8). These declines are likely the result of direct and indirect effects of Arctic warming (5,8,9), among other synergistic drivers of global change (e.g., land-use change), altering wild and semidomesticatedRangiferpopulations in a diverse array of climatic and environmental conditions (9,10). Consequently, there are now serious and growing concerns for the future conservation of populations ofRangifer(11), the vital ecosystem services they provide to Arctic natural systems (2,3), and the food security and cultural integrity they deliver to Indigenous communities (12,13). However, as a Pleistocene species,Rangiferhas experienced past glacial-interglacial cycles, each including rapid Arctic warming events (14), when local temperatures increased by as much as 10°C in a matter of decades (15,16). Because these temperature changes are comparable in pace and magnitude to 21st century forecasts (17), they offer unparalleled opportunities to anticipate population responses ofRangiferto ongoing changing climates (18).
The ability ofRangiferto survive rapid warming events (19) that alone or in combination with human activities caused the extinction of many other Arctic megafauna (20–23) indicates a level of demographic resilience (Box 1) to climatic warming (24,25). However, precisely which ecological and demographic mechanisms promoted this resilience to past episodes of rapid warming and whether they can reduce vulnerability to extinction from future climate change remain unknown. Where the past climatic change has been considered in estimates of population or geographic range dynamics ofRangiferfollowing the last ice age, these inferences of causation have been based on snapshots (points in time ≥1000 years apart) of projected historical range movement (19,26–28) and analysis of time-binned fossil and archaeological data (29–31) using correlative and not process-explicit modeling methods (32). Consequently, the habitat preferences, spatial population processes, and life-history traits that enabledRangiferto persist during rapid warming events remain unclear, hampering projections of responses ofRangiferto future climatic change.
Demographic resilience:ability of populations to resist and recover from alterations in their demographic structures.
Life-history traits:demographic characteristics that influence an organism’s survival and reproduction.
Niche breadth:measure of the diversity of environments tolerated by a species that shape its geographic distribution.
Niche marginality:measure of niche specialization based on a comparison of the habitat conditions used by a species and those present across the sampling area.
Pattern-oriented modeling:an approach for using empirical patterns for the selection and calibration of models of complex systems.
Population dynamics:study of how and why population numbers change in time and space.
Process-explicit models:causal models that represent the dynamics of a biological system as explicit functions of the events that drive change in that system.
Range dynamics:study of the structure and temporal dynamics of species’ geographical distributions.
Validation:comparison of simulated data with independent data obtained by observation and measurement of the real system.
Using hundreds of thousands of process-explicit simulations of climate-human-Rangiferinteractions that synthesize extensive paleontological and ancient genetic information, we uncover and decipher 21,000 years ofRangiferpopulation and geographic range dynamics and their underlying processes. The unprecedented spatial and temporal resolution and extent of this undertaking reveals past and future population responses ofRangiferto rapid climatic warming, pinpointing ecological characteristics and life-history traits that facilitate megafaunal persistence in changing climates.
Pattern-oriented validation (33) of 10,000 process-explicit simulations of likely climate-human-Rangiferinteractions identified a subset of simulations that closely reconciled independent measures of demographic change over a hierarchy of spatial (circumpolar, regional, and local) scales based on high-quality radiocarbon-dated fossils and ancient DNA (aDNA) (fig. S1 and data S1 and S2). These “best” validated simulations (top 0.5% of all model simulations)—based on approximate Bayesian computation (34)—closely reconstructedRangifertotal population size inferred from aDNA and timings of occurrence at fossil sites, arrival in Greenland, and regional extinctions in Europe (Fig. 1A). Moreover, they projected a geographical distribution at 1500 CE that aligns closely with the current endemic distribution ofRangifer(Fig. 1B) (35). They also projected high population stability (fig. S2) in areas where genetic diversity is high (26,36), as expected by observation and theory (26,37,38). Together, these findings provide additional support and confidence in the capacity of our simulations to closely reconstruct the climate-human-Rangiferinteractions responsible for today’s distribution ofRangifer.

Exploration of the parameters in these “best” simulations revealed thatRangiferis likely to have needed specific ecological niche requirements and life-history traits to have persisted in the face of past climatic change and sustained hunting by humans. Our model-based results indicate that the survival ofRangiferduring rapid changes in local-to-regional temperatures, some occurring on a scale of decades (16), probably required it to have a broad ecological niche breadth and low niche marginality [indicators of ecological versatility (Box 1) (39–41)], a high propensity for dispersal, and weak (population-level) Allee effects (Fig. 1Cand table S1). These attributes most likely allowed the species to contract its distributional range to climatic refugia in response to rapid deglacial increases in temperature, probably persisting where required in low abundances in small isolated refugia, expanding its distribution in response to more gradual rates of warming (Fig. 1Dand movie S1).
Our results show thatRangiferlikely underwent a considerable poleward contraction between 21 and 5 thousand years (kyr) before the present (B.P.) in response to sustained climatic warming (Fig. 1B). Our process-based reconstructions indicate thatRangiferwas all but absent from southern and central Europe before the onset of the Holocene at 11.7 kyr B.P. (table S2).Rangiferprobably survived in some isolated patches of tundra and coniferous forest in the Austrian and Swiss Alps until 7 kyr B.P. (movie S1), where these preferred vegetation types are thought to have persisted longer (42). Our models estimate thatRangiferpopulations were lost from the British Isles at 10.04 ± 2.26 kyr B.P. and in North Central Europe at 5.88 ± 4.41 kyr B.P. (Fig. 1B). This coincides spatiotemporally with warmer temperatures promoting the development of temperate deciduous forests (42). In North America, our models simulateRangiferpopulations persisting south of the Laurentide ice sheet during the last deglaciation, remaining completely separated from Alaskan populations until 11 kyr B.P. (movie S1). These populations are simulated to have fused at ~8 kyr B.P. (movie S1), a finding that is entirely consistent with independent genetic measurements and environmental modeling that show similar timings of contact (26,43).
The steepest declines and increases in our simulation-based reconstructions ofRangiferpopulation abundances occurred during periods of rapid warming (14.6 to 12.8 kyr B.P.) and cooling (17.5 to 14.6 kyr B.P., 12.8 to 11.7 kyr B.P., and 9 to 2 kyr B.P.), respectively (Fig. 2). Population declines are likely to have been most severe in Asia and Europe following the 14.7 kyr B.P. Dansgaard-Oeschger warming event when regional temperatures—based on paleoclimate simulations (44)—increased by as much as 5°C in four centuries (Fig. 2). We estimate that the abundance ofRangiferin Asia declined by >14% in two centuries (and 27% in four centuries) following this rapid warming event, operating at a rate of 0.5% per generation (fig. S3). Our modeled reconstructions indicate that a second major decline in abundance ofRangiferin Asia is likely to have occurred during the late Allerød (12.8 kyr B.P.), after whichRangifergradually rebounded in the Holocene (Fig. 2). This is despite unusually warm regional climates during the Holocene Thermal Maximum (8 to 5 kyr B.P.), which are hypothesized to have caused a northern expansion of taiga forests and permafrost-free peatlands in northern areas of Asia (45). Projected increases inRangiferpopulation abundances in Asia at ~3.5 kyr B.P., before long-term stabilization, coincide with a cooling period that is hypothesized to have caused forests to retreat and the tundra zone to occupy its current extent (45,46).

While our reconstructions indicate that densities ofRangiferin Europe were relatively low through the Late Pleistocene and Holocene compared to other regions (Fig. 2), our simulations suggest that population size changed abruptly over this period (fig. S3).Rangiferin Europe is likely to have experienced large population declines immediately following the 14.7 kyr B.P. warming event, with regional population size potentially having declined by >26% in two centuries (fig. S3). This population loss was most likely followed by a rapid increase in abundance, probably in response to variation in the Atlantic meridional overturning circulation, which caused severe cooling at 13.9 kyr B.P. (47).Rangiferin Europe is also likely to have suffered a second rapid decline in population abundance in the late Allerød (12.8 kyr B.P.), with a gradual partial rebound in population abundance in the Younger Dryas (12.8 to 11.7 kyr B.P.) (Fig. 2and fig. S3).
In North America, we show that population declines during Bølling-Allerød warming events are likely to have been less pronounced than in other regions (Fig. 2and fig. S3). This is probably due toRangifer(outside of Alaska) occurring exclusively south of the Laurentide Ice Sheet (movie S1), where temperature increases are projected to have been less severe (47). Our modeling suggests that climate warming in North America in the Early Holocene probably enabledRangiferto rapidly expand its geographical range following the retreat of the Laurentide Ice Sheet (movie S1). This is likely to have caused rapid increases in the population size of North AmericanRangifer(Fig. 2), which probably accelerated at ~7 kyr B.P. (Fig. 2), perhaps in response to the neoglacial cooling of the Arctic (48).
Projecting these climate-human-Rangiferinteractions forward in time shows that future climatic change is likely to causeRangiferpopulations to decline and the Holarctic distribution of the species to contract by 2100 CE (Fig. 3). These future changes in abundance and distribution will probably operate at rates rarely seen over the past 21,000 years—at least for areas outside of Asia (Fig. 4). We predict that the Holarctic abundance ofRangiferwill decrease by 58% (±13%), and its geographic range area will contract by 46% (±16%) within 12 generations (from 2020 to 2100) under a business-as-usual emission-intensive scenario [RCP8.5; equivalent to SSP5-8.5 (49)] (table S3). This downward trend is likely to be most severe in North America with population abundances predicted to decline by 84% (±11%) and range area by 71% (±17%) by 2100 (Fig. 3and table S3). Even under a more optimistic (“middle of the road”) scenario [RCP4.5; equivalent to SSP2-4.5 (49)], forecasts indicate that population abundances ofRangiferin North America will still decline by 42% (±12%) and range area will shrink by 21% (±12%) this century (Fig. 3and table S3). Such outcomes are particularly concerning given that many populations ofRangiferare already in a state of decline. It is estimated thatRangiferpopulations in North American tundra environments have already declined by 65% since the mid-1990s alone, with particularly notable declines in some of the largest herds (7,8). This means that declining trends in population abundances observed over the past 25 years (5) are likely to continue or even accelerate depending on the region and trajectory of future greenhouse gas emissions.

We forecast thatRangiferpopulations at the southern edge of their current Asian distribution will disappear by the end of the 21st century under both emission scenarios. However, intermodel variation in projected declines in reindeer population size and range area in Asia is higher than for North America (table S3). In the north of Asia, we predict that increases in population abundances will occur in the Taimyr peninsula (Fig. 3)—an area that provided important climatic refugia (46) and vegetational stability (50) during the Late Pleistocene—whereRangiferpopulations are today increasing in size (9). We also forecast geographic range expansions in central Greenland (fig. S4 and movie S2), where the melting of ice sheets (51) is set to open new suitable habitats (52).
Comparing our forecasts to our estimates of past rates of reductions in total and regional population sizes and distributional areas ofRangiferreveals that the magnitude of future rates of declines is likely to have occurred over the past 21,000 years, but only for Asia (Fig. 4). Our modeling shows that rates of future population declines projected under RCP4.5 (≥12% over 12 generations) probably occurred frequently in Asia in the past based on our multimodel-averaged population reconstruction. While more severe rates of decline, mirroring those predicted under RCP8.5 (≥25%), are likely to have occurred, they were rare events, happening in the mid-to-late deglaciation (MLD; 15 to 11 kyr B.P.) (Fig. 4A). We show a similar but perhaps less pronounced relationship for past and future declines in geographic range areas simulated forRangiferin Asia (Fig. 4B).
We found no evidence that forecast rates of decline in population size and geographic range area ofRangiferin North America (≥42 and ≥21%, respectively) and across their Holarctic distribution (≥27 and ≥15%, respectively) have occurred since the Last Glacial Maximum regardless of the future greenhouse gas emission scenario (Fig. 4). However, some individual models did predict analogs under the RCP4.5 scenario.
Absolute temperatures in the Arctic are predicted to soon exceed levels not seen for at least 6000 years (53), with potentially severe consequences for the functioning of Arctic ecosystems and for the livelihoods of Indigenous peoples who rely onRangiferfor food security, cultural identity, and community integrity (54,55). Our extensive computational approach, combining a wide variety of paleontological and genetic (aDNA) information, pinpointed important ecological characteristics and life-history traits that are likely to have enabledRangiferto survive abrupt climatic warming during the last deglaciation. Projecting these models forward in time revealed that these attributes are unlikely to safeguardRangiferagainst large-scale population declines from 21st century climatic warming. We show that in coming decades,Rangiferis expected to experience rates of climate-driven demographic change that have no post–ice age analog, at least for populations outside of Asia.
Our high-resolution process-based reconstructions of 21,000 years ofRangiferpopulation dynamics found that demographic resilience to past warming events was most likely facilitated by ecological versatility (based on ecological niche characteristics), a high propensity for dispersal, high maximum densities (sensu carrying capacity in the models), and weak (population-level) Allee effects. Similar ecological characteristics and life-history traits have recently been found to be important for reconstructing the geographic range dynamics of another survivor of Late Pleistocene megafaunal extinctions, the muskox, which co-occurred withRangifer(56). Conversely, for extinct Late Pleistocene megafauna that co-occurred withRangifer, including the woolly mammoth and the woolly rhinoceros, dispersal is likely to have been more limited and maximum population abundances lower (20,23). This indicates that high population densities and high mobility are key life-history traits that favor resilience and persistence in the face of climatic change, sustained exploitation, and their interaction. Moreover, our reconstructions ofRangiferpopulation abundances at a fine spatiotemporal resolution provide vital empirical evidence supporting ecological contexts and demographic processes theorized to condition extinction risk from future climate change (39,57).
Using process-explicit models tested on independent measurements of past demographic change, we project thatRangiferwill experience a 27 to 58% reduction in total population size and a 15 to 46% reduction in its Holarctic distributional area by 2100. Therefore, it is most likely that observed declines in the abundance ofRangiferin recent decades are set to continue (7,8). In some cases, these recent declines in local population abundances have been dramatic. For instance, the George River caribou herd in North America, which is one of the largestRangiferherds in the world, declined by an estimated 99% in less than 30 years, with declines beginning in the early to mid-1990s (58). Likewise, the MulchatnaRangiferherd, which was once one of the largest in Alaska, declined by ~95% over the same period (59). Accordingly, our forecasts identify North American populations ofRangiferas being most vulnerable to climatic warming, with losses of up to 84 and 71% in region-wide population size and distributional area by 2100, respectively. These declines are likely to have far-reaching ecological implications that will further increase the vulnerability ofRangiferto climatic warming and other stressors (2,3).
The coupled consumptive and nonconsumptive impacts of herbivory byRangifermediate competition among tundra plant species, contributing to plant community diversity and stability (2). Severe future declines in North AmericanRangiferpopulations could therefore reduce species diversity of tundra plants, with cascading effects on carbon uptake, soil nutrient availability, and even surface albedo (2,3,60). This could exacerbate the effects of climatic warming, promoting the greening of the Arctic and potentially opening previously unavailable habitats for invasive species and infectious diseases (60).
Comparisons of past and future magnitudes and rates of demographic change found that only in Asia areRangiferherds likely to have previously survived analogs of future rates of population decline and geographic range contraction—at least since the last ice age. Our modeling established that forecast losses in abundance and distribution ofRangiferare likely to be substantially lower in Asia compared to North America and across its entire Holarctic distribution. By using the past to contextualize the impact of these forecast changes in population and distribution onRangiferpersistence (18), we show that demographic changes projected forRangiferin Asia in coming decades might occur within safe boundaries but only if there are substantial efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Alternatively, for populations outside Asia, and particularly those in North America, projected losses will far outpace even the largest declines that occurred in response to abrupt warming following Earth’s emergence from a glacial climate some 21,000 to 18,000 years ago.
These projections of large-scale population declines ofRangiferfrom 21st century climatic warming thus provide vital mechanistic support for earlier forecasts done using simpler correlative modeling methods (26,61). By using process-explicit and pattern-oriented modeling (POM) methods to directly pinpoint climate-driven changes in population abundances ofRangiferfollowing the last ice age at high spatial and temporal resolutions, we provide greater certainty to the threat future climate change poses to the persistence ofRangiferacross its circumpolar distribution. By directly modeling the population processes that are likely to affect extinction risk, our projections of vulnerability are more robust and useful for conservation management and policy decision-making than current inferences based on changes in potential habitat suitability (62).
While reconstructing inferences of past population and geographic range dynamics required specific ecological characteristics, life-history traits, and anthropogenic threats, the posterior distributions for some parameters in our process-explicit models remained wide despite multiple rounds of pattern-oriented optimization. For these nonidentifiable parameters, it is important to consider whether the empirical targets used for POM best fit the study animal and system (63), which they did. By using diverse types of data to quantify demographic changes ofRangiferacross a hierarchy of spatial scales (see Materials and Methods), our POM method allowed us to characterize the range and population dynamics ofRangifer. Wide posterior distributions for some ecological parameters (including niche marginality and volume) might reflect the differentRangiferecotypes simulated, with subspecies potentially having unique behavioral adaptations to their habitats (6), which were not explicitly captured in our simulations. These include differences in movement (64), where dispersal and home range movement forRangiferare typically lower in forest-dwelling, high-artic, and insularRangiferecotypes (64,65) compared to more migratory-type ecotypes (64). Future modeling could focus on better integration of ecotypes, as well as higher-resolution topographical and temporal processes, including seasonality. Implementation of alternative inferences of spatiotemporal occurrence from sedimentary aDNA (18,66), as well as any new fossil discoveries, could further improve model prediction. Moreover, current and future projections could benefit from broader consideration of the complexity of threats facingRangifertoday and how they are likely to change in space and time. These include parasitic diseases, changing freeze-thaw climatic conditions, increasing frequency and magnitude of forest and tundra fires, declines in sea-ice extent, and changes in plant phenology leading to trophic mismatches (11,60).
Using a process-based simulation approach that directly considers demographic responses to rapid climatic change using fossils and aDNA, we show that the past can help identify safe operating boundaries for the survival of species in a warming and increasingly “anthropocized” world. Our results flag an immediate need to increase investments in the management and conservation of populations ofRangifer, particularly in North America, where losses are forecast to be greatest. This will not only benefit the persistence of this species and the services it provides to Arctic ecosystems but also help ensure the maintenance of the socioeconomic, cultural integrity, and emotional well-being of manyRangifer-dependent communities.
One hundred thousand process-explicit simulation models were developed to reconstruct 21,000 years ofRangiferpopulation and range dynamics, each synthesizing extensive paleoecological, paleontological, and ancient genetic information. POM techniques (33) and approximate Bayesian computation (34) were used to validate models and identify the ecological characteristics and life-history traits that enabledRangiferto survive past periods of rapid climatic change. A subset of “best” models that closely reconstructed inferences of past demographic change was used to determine future demographic responses ofRangiferto climatic change.
Georeferenced radiocarbon-dated fossils and sightings ofRangiferwere sourced from publicly accessible databases and published literature (Supplementary Materials, data S1). The reliability of radiocarbon ages was assessed using a numeric ranking system based on the Mead-Meltzer scale, updated to reflect advances in radiocarbon dating and to better incorporate archaeological evidence (67). All reliable fossils were aged calibrated in OxCal (68) using the IntCal20 calibration curve (69).
Community Climate System Model Version 3 (CCSM3) (70) and the Transient Climate Evolution of the Last 21,000 years (TraCE-21k) simulation (71) were used to generate gridded paleoclimate reconstructions for the Northern Hemisphere for the period of 21,000 B.P. to 1989 CE at a resampled resolution of 1° by 1° (Supplementary Materials). We intersected fossil locations and time periods (calibrated age ± 1 SD) to paleoclimate reconstructions of climatic parameters (temperature, precipitation, and evapotranspiration) that have potential direct and indirect effects onRangiferpopulation dynamics. Sightings ofRangifer(1700 to 2019) were intersected with the CRU TS version 4.03 (72) climate data after its harmonization with CCSM3 (Supplementary Materials). The climatic values associated with fossil and sighting records were used to derive a multitemporaln-dimensional hypervolume of the climatic niche ofRangifer, representing the climatic space where the species can persist and thrive (73). The resulting hypervolume, which represents the full potential climatic niche ofRangifer(23,56), was exhaustively subsampled to derive thousands of plausible realized niches (n= 2500) (20), each with different breadths and marginalities (seeBox 1).
Spatial projections of climatic suitability forRangiferin the Holarctic were generated from 21 kyr B.P. to 1500 CE at 7-year (generational) time steps (74) for all 2500 plausible niches. We used a Lambert azimuthal equal area projection centered on −15° east and 57° north, with a resolution of 100 km by 100 km.
The colonization dynamics of people in Eurasia and North America and their relative abundances were modeled using the Climate Informed Spatial Genetics Model (CISGeM), where genetic history and local demography are informed by paleoclimatic and paleovegetation reconstructions of net primary productivity (75). CISGeM was used to calculate a time series of relative local population change for humans from 21 kyr B.P. to 0 B.P. at 25-year time steps. The arrival, occupancy, and density [modeled as effective population size (Ne)] were forced by spatiotemporal estimates of climate and sea level changes over the past 21,000 years using CCSM3. To account for parameter uncertainty in spatiotemporal projections ofNe, we ran 4950 unique models (each with different parameter settings) using established model parameters (75).Nevalues were then scaled between 0 and 1 using the 95th percentile of maximum values and used in the process-driven ecological model as potential spatiotemporal measures of the relative abundance of humans (56). CISGeM and its application are described in detail in the Supplementary Materials.
Climate suitability projections and CISGeM estimates of human population size were coupled with stochastic (process-driven) demographic models to simulate landscape-scale spatial population processes forRangiferacross its Holarctic distribution. This spatially explicit population model (SEPM) reconstructed spatial abundance at 7-year generational time steps from 21 kyr B.P. to 1500 CE, following a burn-in period of 100 generations. We generated 100,000 model instances with different combinations of values for life-history traits (population growth rate and its variance, dispersal proportion and distance, and Allee effect), ecological characteristics (niche breadth and marginality), and human interactions (human abundance, rates of exploitation, and functional response), sampled from plausible ranges using Latin hypercube sampling, to provide a robust coverage of multidimensional parameter space (76). Each model instance was run for a single replicate (56). Additional information about the structure and parameters of the model can be found in the Supplementary Materials.
POM (33) was used to evaluate the adequacy of model simulations to reconstruct spatiotemporal demographic responses ofRangiferto climatic change and human exploitation (fig. S1). This was done by comparing simulated population and geographic range dynamics against independent measurements of demographic and distributional change from fossil- and DNA-based data using approximate Bayesian computation (34). Measurements used for model testing were quantitatively derived from empirical data (data S1 and S2) (77). Specifically, simulations were validated against a four-parameter multivariate target of (i) occurrence at the fossil site at the correct time (calibrated age ± 1 SD), (ii) timing of regional extirpation [corrected for the Signor-Lipps effect (78)], (iii) arrival time in Greenland, and (iv) temporal changes in population size based on effective population size inferred from aDNA. Together, these metrics cover a hierarchy of demographic and biogeographic responses that would have been pertinent to the population and geographic range dynamics ofRangifer(19). The top 0.5% of the models (i.e., models that most closely reconstructed the validation targets) were identified using approximate Bayesian computation. Similar POM targets have recently been used to reconstruct population change for other arctic megafaunal species (20,23,56).
The SEPM and POM procedures were repeated three times. In each instance, this was done by building 10,000 SEPMs using parameter bounds drawn from the posterior distributions of the previous round of POM (79). Bayes factors and posterior-predictive checks were used to detect when posterior parameters had converged. The “best” model instances (n= 50) from the final round of POM were retained and used to generate weighted ensemble-averaged estimates of spatial abundance, extirpation time, and total population size across the Holarctic and three subregions representing Europe, Asia, and North America. The boundary between Europe and Asia was defined by drawing a line that follows the Volga River and the Ural Mountains, while the boundary between Asia and North America was defined by the Bering Strait. Estimates were weighted by the inverse of the Euclidean distance of the model from the idealized targets, giving higher weights to models that best reproduced our validation targets (23).
Thus, all projections were based on models that could closely reconstruct occurrence in space and time, extirpation and colonization events, and population size trends observed from the fossil record and aDNA. Further validation of the selected models was done using independent estimates of occurrence inferred from sedimentary aDNA (66).
Process-explicit models with parameters that accurately reconstructed inferences of demographic and distributional change from fossils and aDNA (based on POM validation) were used to simulate population responses ofRangiferto future climate change over the period of 2016 to 2100. StableClim (80) was used to generate forecasts of climatic change for a “middle of the road” scenario (RCP4.5, equivalent to SSP2-4.5) and a “business as usual” scenario (RCP8.5, equivalent to SSP5-8.5) based on an ensemble of 19 Atmosphere-Ocean General Circulation Models from the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 5. These forecasts have been harmonized with CCSM3 TraCE-21k projections in StableClim.
Forecasts ofRangiferdistribution and abundance (2016 to 2100) were based on 50 individual models with their parameters defined by POM (table S1), making past and future simulations directly comparable. Carrying capacities were constrained using current-day land use, and abundances were initialized in grid cells within the present-day distribution ofRangifer(35). Models were run at generational time steps (7 years), with a burn-in period matching that used for past simulations. Outputs were used to generate an ensemble-averaged estimate of spatial abundance that accounted for the probability of occurrence (56).
We calculated the percentage of change in total population size and geographic range area (number of occupied cells × area of the cell) forRangiferin Europe, Asia, and North America and across their entire Holarctic distribution over a period of 12 generations (84 years). For future forecasts, we used the period from 2016 and 2100. For past reconstructions, we calculated these measures of demographic change from 21,000 B.P. to 1500 CE using an 84-year moving window, matching the time interval used for the future forecasts. To determine whether past populations ofRangiferhave experienced analogous rates of forecast change, we compared multimodel-averaged estimates of past and future change in total population size and range area. Given that the geographic extent ofRangiferpopulations in Europe is relatively small, covering restricted areas of Norway, Finland, and north-west Russia, the comparative results for this region need to be interpreted carefully. They can be found in the Supplementary Materials.
