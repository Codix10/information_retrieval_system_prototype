As the threat of climate change continues to grow, bolstering individual-level support for climate change initiatives is crucial. More research is needed to better understand how individual difference factors, such as climate change anxiety and intolerance of uncertainty (IU), may shape how people perceive climate change and respond to climate change messaging. To date, the majority of published studies have not taken these individual difference factors into consideration, and IU has been particularly neglected in the climate change literature. This study examined the independent effects of climate change anxiety and IU on three climate change-related outcomes: climate-related distress, support for climate change policies, and behavioural engagement.
Participants were Florida residents (N= 441) who completed an online survey, including measures of climate change anxiety and IU. Participants then watched a video describing climate change consequences and completed three outcome measures: post-video distress, climate change policy support, and behavioural engagement.
Controlling for demographic covariates, both climate change anxiety (β= .43,p< .001) and IU (β= .27,p< .001) were associated with greater post-video distress, but only IU independently predicted greater policy support (β= .10,p= .034) and behavioural engagement (β= .12,p= .017).
Our findings suggest that IU may be an important factor in promoting pro-environmental behaviour and policy support, but climate change anxiety may increase emotional distress without boosting meaningful behaviours or support. Our findings highlight the potential influence of cognitive factors on climate change engagement and suggest that invoking uncertainty rather than anxiety may be more effective in catalysing effective environmental engagement.
Keywords:climate anxiety, climate change, intolerance of uncertainty, pro-environmental behaviour
A large and growing pool of evidence suggests that anthropogenic greenhouse gases are unequivocally influencing the climate system (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), 2022a;Steffen et al., 2018). Although the stance that climate change is a result of human activities is supported by 97% of scientists and the IPCC (Bouman et al., 2020;Cook et al., 2016;IPCC, 2022a;Whitmarsh, 2008), bolstering individual-level support for climate change initiatives has been challenging (Gifford, 2011). For example, only 52% of Americans rate dealing with global climate change as a top priority (Pew Research Center, 2020). Furthermore, even if people hold the opinion that climate change is a critical concern, they still may not engage with climate change initiatives (Leiserowitz et al., 2019) or take action to influence public policies (Doherty & Webler, 2016). A contributing factor to this lack of engagement is that people focus on the inherentuncertaintyof climate change as a global issue, in terms of timeframe, severity of impacts, location, efficacy of action and more (Morton et al., 2011). Because individuals are generally uncertainty-averse, they may respond to the uncertainty-laden issue of climate change with general avoidance and disengagement from any specific climate-change mitigation or adaptation initiatives (Morton et al., 2011). Compounding this problem, many people perceive individual-level contributions to be insufficient in addressing the climate change problem (Bonniface & Henley, 2008;Soliman et al., 2018)—a proverbial ‘drop in the bucket’. Within the context of an ever-increasing threat of climate change (IPCC, 2022a), more research is needed to better understand why people may not engage with climate change initiatives and how to increase individual-level buy-in.
Individual difference factors may interact with affective responses to shape how people perceive climate change and respond to climate change messages (van der Linden, 2014). As the threat of natural hazards has worsened and the detrimental effect that climate change can have on people’s mental health has become more prevalent, there has been a growing focus on the individual difference factor of climate change anxiety, also sometimes referred to as eco-anxiety. Climate change anxiety is defined as a stable and negative affective, cognitive, and behavioural response that a person experiences related to climate change (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020). Climate change anxiety is especially of interest in light of new data from a nationally representative survey, which found that found that global warming is causing psychological distress in a small, but notable proportion of the American population (Leiserowitz et al., 2023). Specifically, the authors found that about 1 in 10 Americans experienced anxiety related to global warming for several or more days out of the last 2 weeks. Another study surveying a large sample of children and young people across ten countries found that climate change anxiety is widespread and impacts daily functioning (Hickman et al., 2021). Climate anxiety also appears to directly affect individuals’ responses to climate change. One study found that climate change anxiety was correlated with strong affective (i.e., symptoms of depression and anxiety) responses to climate change, though not necessarily with behavioural responses (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020). In addition, climate change anxiety is closely related to worry, which was found in one study to be the single strongest predictor of support for climate and energy policies (Smith & Leiserowitz, 2014). Further research is needed to better understand how climate change anxiety may predict affective and behavioural responses to climate change initiatives.
Whereas climate change anxiety is thought to be specific to climate change-related issues, intolerance of uncertainty (IU) is a general dispositional factor reflecting negative beliefs pertaining to uncertainty and regarding a person’s ability to endure uncertain situations and events (Koerner, 2014;Koerner & Dugas, 2008). In situations with high levels of uncertainty and threat, people who perceive themselves to be intolerant to uncertainty experience greater distress, ultimately culminating in greater levels of worry and anxiety (e.g.,Freeston et al., 2020). As climate change is characterized by high levels of uncertainty and threat (de Coninck et al., 2018;IPCC, 2018;Kunreuther et al., 2014;Morton et al., 2011), IU may be an important factor contributing to climate change-related distress. IU also influences and shapes behavioural responses, leading to low problem-solving confidence, low self-efficacy, negative outcome expectancies during problem-solving, and increased avoidance behaviour (Koerner, 2014;Luhmann et al., 2011;Patrick, 2016). Thus, people with high IU may struggle to engage with pro-environmental behaviours and may be more likely to display increased avoidance of the issue. To our knowledge, no study has examined the distinct effects of IU and climate change anxiety on climate change attitudes or behaviours, representing a key gap in the literature.
The overarching aim of the current study was to examine the effect of individual-level cognitive-emotional factors on pro-environmental engagement. The extant literature (e.g.,Bertolotti & Catellani, 2014;Bilandzic et al., 2017;Gifford & Comeau, 2011;Hurlstone et al., 2014;Morton et al., 2011;Spence & Pidgeon, 2010) has been characterized by several limitations, which we sought to address in the current study. First, we controlled for theoretically relevant sociodemographic variables in all analyses, to examine the impact of individual-level cognitive-emotional factors above and beyond demographic factors known to influence climate change engagement. Second, we considered separate dependent variables, which captured engagement with climate change initiatives across three separate domains. Specifically, we consideredemotional distressfollowing climate change messages,attitudinal supportfor climate change policies, andbehavioural engagementwith different initiatives (Doherty & Webler, 2016;Stern, 2000).
Our primary aim was to examine whether climate change anxiety and IU predicted pro-environmental engagement across our three dependent variables. Based on previous literature suggesting that climate change anxiety is a strong predictor of climate policy support (Smith & Leiserowitz, 2014) but uncorrelated with behavioural responses to climate change (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020), we hypothesized that climate change anxiety would be a strong predictor of support for climate policies (Smith & Leiserowitz, 2014) but not behavioural engagement (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020). Similarly, we expected that people with high IU would be more likely to support climate policies, based on their desire to reduce uncertainty and the association between worry and greater policy support (Smith & Leiserowitz, 2014), but less likely to engage in behaviours, due to avoidance and barriers to action. We predicted both climate change anxiety and IU would be associated with greater distress.
Participants (N= 560) were Florida residents recruited through Qualtrics XM (Qualtrics) using their panel system. The Qualtrics panel system is an online platform aggregator of various panels of individuals willing to complete surveys for various incentives. Incentives range based on various factors, including survey length and study quotas, and can include monetary compensation, airline miles and gift cards. Qualtrics randomly selects potential participants and invites them to participate in the survey. No details of the study are specified; however, participants are informed that the survey is for research purposes only, along with expected length and incentive information. Qualtrics will continue sending emails to the respective panels until the sample size target is met. We targeted 560 participants based on allotted funding for data collection, which allowed us to collect more than double the sample size of a similarly designed study (i.e.,Bilandzic et al., 2017,N= 247).
The final sample included 441 participants; we removed 119 participants who failed one of two attention checks or completed the survey in under 15 min. We determined 15 min as the cut-off point based on pilot testing within our research group, suggesting that anything less would be an insufficient amount of time to pay proper attention to all survey materials. Participants in the final sample had a mean age of 47.78 years (SD = 16.20). The majority of participants (80%) identified as White (n= 353); 87 participants self-identified as a person of colour (19.7%), whereas 69 participants identified as Hispanic or Latino/a (15.6%). Study procedures consisted of a survey, which took, on average, 30 minutes to complete. Participants were told that the study was about information-processing related to climate change, and were asked to provide virtual consent, along with a pledge to provide honest answers. Participants first completed demographic measures, including indicating whether they identify as Black, Indigenous or Person of Colour (BIPOC) and reporting their political ideology (from very liberal to very conservative). Participants then completed a pre-manipulation assessment of climate change opinions and a series of questionnaires, in randomized order, assessing psychological individual difference factors and environmental attitudes and beliefs.
After completion of these questionnaires, participants watched a brief video depicting an anthropo-morphic earth delivering a message about climate change. Participants were randomly assigned to one of four climate change messaging video conditions. While the effect of climate change message condition was not the focus of the present report, we have described the conditions in brief below, and the full messages and links to view the videos can be found in theSupplementary Information. In the consensus condition, participants learned the consensus message that 97% of climate scientists agree humans are causing global warming. The other three conditions included the consensus message and additional climate change information: the loss condition included information related to potential negative consequences of climate change; the gain-negative-general condition emphasized that negative consequences can be avoided if the global community takes action against climate change; the gain-negative-personal condition emphasized that the participant themselves can take action to prevent climate change and thus avoid negative consequences.
Following the video, participants reported their emotional response to the information provided. Next, participants responded to the same climate change opinion assessment given prior to the video. Finally, we assessed our three primary dependent variables: post-video distress, policy support and behavioural engagement. Participants first indicated their support for various climate change policies. They were then given the opportunity to actively engage in five climate-related initiatives that they could learn more about and sign up for. This study was reviewed and approved by the Institutional Review Board.
We assessed climate change anxiety using cognitive-emotional impairment and functional impairment factors, based on previous research (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020). Participants rated 13 items on a scale from 1 (never) to 5 (almost always). Clayton and colleagues demonstrated concurrent and discriminant validity between the subscales and environmental identity, emotional response to climate change and depression and anxiety (Clayton & Karazsia, 2020). In the present sample, the internal reliability was found to be excellent (α = .97).
IU was assessed using a 12-item, self-report measure (Carleton et al., 2007). Items are rated on a scale from 1 (a little characteristic of me) to 5 (entirely characteristic of me), with higher scores reflecting greater difficulties tolerating uncertainty. Emblematic items include, ‘Unforeseen events upset me greatly’ and ‘When I am uncertain, I can’t function very well’. The reduced IUS-12 has been shown to have excellent reliability (α = .91) and a total score highly correlated (r= .96) with that of the original 27-item scale (Carleton et al., 2007). Further, a cross-validation of the IUS-12 with related measures indicated significant correlations (Carleton et al., 2007). Internal reliability in the present sample was found to be within acceptable limits (α = .91).
In line with procedures fromBilandzic et al. (2017), we assessed self-reported fear and guilt that participants endorsed directly following the video. Participants rated six items (fear: frightened, afraid, scared; guilt: remorseful, guilty, conscience-stricken) modified and adapted to English from the German Modified Differential Affect Scale (Renaud & Unz, 2006). Each emotion was rated on a Likert scale from 1 (I did not feel this way at all) to 7 (I felt this way very much). We then summed ratings for fear and guilt subscales to form the post-video distress rating; internal reliability for the distress rating in the present sample was found to be acceptable (α = .94).
Participants indicated support or opposition for five emission reduction policies to mitigate global warming, including ‘regulating carbon dioxide as a pollutant’, ‘require electric utilities to produce electricity from renewable energy sources’, ‘require automakers to increase fuel efficiency’, ‘fund more research into renewable energy sources’ and ‘provide tax rebates for people who purchase energy-efficient vehicles or solar panels’ (Zhao et al., 2011). Though, this measure has not been formally validated, it has been used or adapted in prior studies which found evidence of good reliability, including α = .90 (Chu & Yang, 2020) and α = .85 (Rickard et al., 2016). We selected this measure for its brevity. Participants rated each item on a scale from 1 (strongly oppose) to 4 (strongly support). These responses were summed for a total Policy Support index, with good reliability (α = .91).
At the end of the survey, participants were given the opportunity to participate in five different climate change initiatives to derive an index of behavioural climate change engagement. Procedures were adapted from work bySchneider et al. (2017)andZaval et al. (2015). The activities were selected with a Florida sample in mind, with some of the options specifically relevant for Floridians. We presented the items in order of difficulty across passive or active engagement, along with the level of effort required for engagement; rankings were based on pilot testing within our research group. For each behaviour, participants were first given a brief overview of the activity and could then select “Tell me more!” or “No thanks” response options. If they selected “Tell me more!”, they were provided with more details about the climate change initiative and were then given the opportunity to ostensibly sign-up or participate for the activity. Responses across the five climate change initiatives were used to derive a dimensional gradient of engagement. More specifically, each activity for which participants selected “Tell me more!” received one point, and each behavioural sign-up received an additional point. The total number of points ranged from 0 to 10. In reality, participants did not actually sign up or engage in any of these behaviours. However, they were fully debriefed at the end of the survey and provided online links to give them the opportunity to participate in each respective activity.
Data were screened and checked for missing values, outliers, and violations of statistical assumptions. Descriptive statistics were also examined for potential data-entry errors. Internal consistency was examined by calculating Cronbach’s alpha for all self-report measures. Data for each group was screened for assumptions of normality and variance using the Shapiro–Wilk test of normality and Levene’s test of equal variance. Primary study aims were examined using stepwise linear regression models, allowing us to consider the main effects of climate change anxiety and IU while controlling for key demographic variables. Reflecting prior literature on constructs that shape people’s responses to climate change, we took a stepwise regression approach to constructing each model. Three separate models were considered, one for each dependent variable. While the effect of the video messaging condition was not the main focus of the present report, the messaging condition was an important element of our study design, and we therefore controlled for the messaging condition in each model. We conducted a stepwise hierarchical regression for each dependent variable. The first step of the model included the messaging condition (with the loss message as the reference condition), and the second step added demographic covariates. Our third step considered the effects of our main predictors, climate change anxiety and IU. Analyses were performed using SPSS software ver. 26 (IBM) and R (R Core Team, 2020), with ap< .05 cut-off for statistical significance.

Establishing how individual difference factors independently shape climate change engagement is critical for crafting effective pro-engagement messaging. However, the extant literature on how individual difference factors may impact environmental engagement is extremely limited. Additionally, existing work largely fails to consider individual difference factors while controlling for key sociodemographic factors that are known to predict climate change engagement, including age, gender, ethnicity, education, BIPOC status and political ideology (Cottrell, 2003;Diamantopoulos et al., 2003;Dietz et al., 1998;Gregersen et al., 2020;Leiserowitz et al., 2018;Whitmarsh & O’Neill, 2010;Xiao & McCright, 2015;Zelezny et al., 2000). To fill these gaps in the literature, we ran a novel analysis examining how socio-demographic covariates and emotional-cognitive factors impact multiple metrics of pro-environmental engagement. Specifically, we explored how climate change anxiety and IU influenced support for climate change mitigation policies, behavioural engagement, and distress following a climate change video message.
In terms of demographic covariates, we found that political ideology was the only variable that most robustly predicted all outcome variables. More liberal political ideology was associated with greater post-video distress, policy support, and behavioural engagement. These results align with a wealth of previous research: political ideology has been widely established as one of the strongest predictors of climate change engagement (Cottrell, 2003;Dietz et al., 1998;Gregersen et al., 2020;Leiserowitz et al., 2018). We found weak evidence that older age may correspond with higher behavioural engagement, as well. But given that this effect just obtained significance, future work should seek to replicate it. Although prior studies have shown higher levels of education to predict increased environmental policy support (Klineberg et al., 1998;Zahran et al., 2006), we did not replicate these effects after accounting for the effects of IU and climate change anxiety. Additionally, we did not replicate past work that establishes associations between BIPOC status and greater policy support (Dietz et al., 2007;Leiserowitz & Akerlof, 2010). Future work should further explore moderating conditions for the demographic effects that we failed to replicate in our paradigm.
While our results broadly reinforce past work on the predictive power of demographic factors, especially political ideology, in shaping climate change engagement, the theoretical role of individual difference factors should not be underestimated. When considering larger policy and health behaviour changes, identifying individual differences that could shape behaviour is crucial. Interventions designed solely on demographic predictors might not achieve the desired changes if they fail to address the diverse emotional and cognitive landscapes of individuals. By integrating research findings on individual difference factors, we can craft more nuanced, targeted, and effective interventions, such as more tailored messaging. This holistic approach, combining demographic and individual difference insights, can equip policymakers and health professionals with a more comprehensive understanding, enabling them to design strategies with a broader and more profound impact.
Climate change anxiety was a significant, unique predictor of post-video distress but did not significantly predict policy support or behavioural engagement above and beyond demographic covariates and IU. The significant distress stemming from climate change anxiety when individuals are exposed to climate change information is a pressing societal concern. Two ongoing trends converge to highlight the urgency of understanding this dynamic. First, an increasingly large segment of the population is reporting climate change anxiety, or heightened emotional responses to climate issues (Hickman et al., 2021). Concurrently, there is an inundation of media reports on climate change impacts: global media reporting on climate-related topics escalated from approximately 47,000 articles in 2016–2017 to nearly 87,000 in 2020–2021 (IPCC, 2022b). Such coverage makes it nearly unavoidable for individuals, especially those with heightened climate change anxiety, to be confronted with these distressing topics frequently. Understanding the ramifications of this heightened distress is crucial. From a health perspective, we must investigate how persistent exposure to distressing climate news affects individuals mentally and physically (Swim et al., 2009,2011). For example, distress and anxiety stemming from climate change could intensify other mental health issues or magnify everyday stress, impacting overall well-being, life quality and daily operations (Reyes et al., 2021;Stanley et al., 2021). Equally important is understanding the behavioural ramifications: How does this distress shape individual decision-making, especially concerning pro-environmental actions? Given the current progression of climate change and its impact, understanding and addressing the effects of climate change anxiety on individual well-being and behaviour is both a scholarly and societal imperative.
A novel finding was that we found a significant and positive association between IU and all three outcomes of interest: post-video distress, policy support, and behavioural engagement. It is particularly noteworthy that IU predicted higher pro-environmental behavioural engagement. This represents an important clarification to the climate change literature and to theoretical models of IU, such as the model of situational uncertainty distress proposed byFreeston et al. (2020). Applied to our work, theFreeston et al. (2020)model suggests that people higher in IU may experience greater distress in response to potentially threatening climate change information, which may lead them to engage in uncertainty-reducing behaviours, like avoidance behaviour. In line with this theoretical model, past research has suggested that high levels of distress might cause people to employ emotion-focused coping and exhibit avoidance behaviour, a known barrier to action in people intolerant of uncertainty (Koerner, 2014;Luhmann et al., 2011;Patrick, 2016). Our results extend these past findings and suggest that, in the case of climate change, uncertainty-reducing behaviour in those high in IU may also include taking pro-environmental action. Our work therefore underscores the importance of conducting more research to gain a more nuanced understanding of how individual difference factors like IU could be harnessed to increase proactive climate change behaviour. As the realities of climate change become more pronounced, it is critical to understand how uncertainty tied to its consequences influences individuals’ perceptions and actions.
Our findings should be considered in light of several limitations. First, our sample is comprised entirely of Florida residents, which inherently limits the generalizability of our findings. However, we note that we specifically targeted Florida residents to minimize variability in the sample with respect to the day-to-day impact of climate change, based on the assumption that Florida residents might be more personally invested in climate change-related issues by living in a region particularly sensitive to adverse effects of climate change (Wong et al., 2014). An additional limitation concerns the length of our video messages. We opted for brevity to prevent participant fatigue, but it is possible that more comprehensive or emotion-inducing information might have had different effects than those reported here. A final limitation is that due to the COVID-19 pandemic, we were unable to conduct any in-person laboratory or field studies to corroborate our behavioural engagement results. Although participants engaged in actual virtual behaviours rather than simply reporting intentions, this type of research would benefit from in-person testing to assess if these behaviours carry over.
An important tool in mitigating and adapting to climate change is individual action and compliance with sustainable behaviours. Our results suggest that individual difference factors which shape how people process information can influence the effectiveness of climate change messaging. We found that the individual-level cognitive-emotional factor of IU is a potentially motivating factor in promoting pro-environmental engagement. In contrast, climate anxiety may have a distressing and paralysing effect, leading people to disengage and avoid the issue altogether. To date, the majority of published studies have not taken these individual difference factors into consideration, and IU has been particularly neglected in the climate change literature. Our findings highlight that a focus on group-level demographics alone might be too reductive to understand people’s responses to climate change, as it potentially neglects the various internal processes that drive individual thoughts and actions. We argue this represents a key gap in our understanding of how information processing may influence support for climate change policy and behaviours, and we provide initial evidence that both climate change anxiety and IU are important factors to consider. More work is necessary to understand the precise mechanisms explaining increased engagement versus avoidance outcomes, which will ultimately help shape future messaging interventions to improve climate change engagement.
We demonstrated the important role of two individual difference factors, climate change anxiety and intolerance of uncertainty (IU), in influencing emotional and behavioural responses to climate change messaging.
IU and climate anxiety both significantly predicted greater climate change distress, however, only IU significantly predicted policy support and behavioural engagement.
Individual difference factors appear to influence the effectiveness of climate change messaging. IU may be a potentially motivating factor in promoting pro-environmental engagement, while climate anxiety may have a distressing and paralysing effect, leading people to disengage and avoid the issue altogether.
