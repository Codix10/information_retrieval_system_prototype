The rise in surface ocean temperature imposes strong effects on marine organisms. With large population sizes and fast generation times, microorganisms may rapidly adapt to ocean warming. However, long‐term experimental adaptation studies on heterotrophic protists are very limited, hindering our comprehensive understanding of their adaptation capability and the underlying mechanisms. In this study, we conducted long‐term thermal selection experiments on six representative marine protozoa. By assessing their phenotypes, metabolism, thermal performance curves, thermal traits, and carbon allocation status, we found that all investigated heterotrophic protists exhibited a fitness‐improved adaptation response to warming scenarios. After growing in warmer conditions for hundreds of generations (~850 forCafeteria burkhardaeand ~500 for other species), their optimal temperature and maximum rates of both growth and ingestion increased, whereas respiration rates significantly decreased. In addition, the cell size, cellular carbon, and nitrogen content at an elevated environmental temperature also increased after warming adaptation. Mechanisms underlying the increased competitive fitness at high temperatures after warming adaptation might be associated with the alleviated oxidative stress, shown by the remarkable reduction in cellular reactive oxygen species contents. Our study, for the first time, reveals the ability of marine heterotrophic protists to adapt to higher temperatures, providing experimental support for predictive modeling studies that integrate evolutionary potential with short‐term physiological responses, with important implications for marine ecosystem functioning and biogeochemical cycling under global warming.

Our study unraveled profitable adaptations of marine protozoa to long‐term warming. The optimal temperature, maximum growth rates, cell homeostasis, and cell size significantly increased after warming adaptation, suggesting a resilient future for this functional group. It provides important implications for marine ecosystem functioning and biogeochemical cycling under global warming.

The ocean, as the major heat reservoir in Earth's climate system, has warmed significantly since 1900 (Garcia‐Soto et al.2021; Deng2024). The ongoing warming poses risks to marine organisms as temperatures may approach their thermal tolerance limits (Thomas et al.2012). Multiple studies have predicted that warming will lead to a substantial decline in marine primary production and diversity (Thomas et al.2012; Benedetti et al.2021; Pigot et al.2023), disrupting ecosystem functions and services that are vital for human livelihoods and marine health.
However, subsequent experimental research has challenged these predictions, emphasizing the rapid thermal adaptation capabilities of marine microorganisms, such as bacteria (Yung et al.2015), phytoplankton (Reusch and Boyd2013; Schaum et al.2017,2018; O'Donnell et al.2018; Benner et al.2020; Barton et al.2023), and mixotrophs that combine photosynthesis with phagotrophy (Lepori‐Bui et al.2022). Among these works, some explore the thermal adaptation responses using growth maxima (μmax) achieved at the optimal temperature (Topt) derived from the thermal performance curves (TPC) of growth rates (Listmann et al.2016; Schaum et al.2017,2018; O'Donnell et al.2018; Barton et al.2023), which usually exhibit unimodal distributions over a wide temperature range. It has been identified that theToptof phytoplankton growth is strongly linked to local temperature conditions (Thomas et al.2012,2016), indicating an evolutionary adaptation to temperature variations. Similar findings have been observed in specific cyanobacteria, green algae, and diatom species in experimental thermal adaptation studies (Schaum et al.2017; Barton et al.2023), where both growthToptandμmaxincreased after long‐term warming selection. By examining metabolic rates or carbon utilization efficiency at a reference temperature, several studies also found that the fitness (i.e., growth rates or carbon utilization efficiency) of specific phytoplankton and mixotrophs was significantly improved after adapting to warming (Padfield et al.2016; Schaum et al.2018; Barton et al.2020; Lepori‐Bui et al.2022). However, the effect of long‐term warming selection on fitness is not entirely consistent. Some species showed no sign of adaptation or increased thermal tolerance but reduced their fitness (Barton et al.2023).
Overall, the current findings on thermal adaptation scenarios are mostly from phytoplankton. The adaptations of protozoa, such as ciliates and heterotrophic nanoflagellates (HNFs), to rising ocean temperatures are yet to be investigated. These heterotrophic protists are key consumers of bacteria and microalgae, playing crucial roles in nutrient remineralization and carbon transfer within marine ecosystems (Fenchel1986; Jürgens and Massana2008). Their significant functions have been recognized in various environments, including sunlit oceans, deep seas, and hypersaline waters (Pernice et al.2015; Schoenle et al.2021; Obiol et al.2021). Additionally, as the grazing function has been reported to have higher thermal optima and tolerance than the autotrophic process (Wilken et al.2013; Pang et al.2023), heterotrophic protists may exhibit better adaptation regimes, a possibility that remains to be explored.
To address this knowledge gap, we conducted long‐term warming selection experiments on six marine protozoa, including two ciliates (Pseudocohnilembus persalinusandUronema marinum) and four HNFs (Cafeteria burkhardae,Paraphysomonas foraminifera,Paraphysomonas imperforata, andParaphysomonassp.; Table1).P. persalinusandU.marinumare free‐living bacterivorous ciliates, with some species in these two genera capable of parasitism known to cause systemic tissue destruction and high mortality in marine fish (Bassleer1983; Song et al.2009).C. burkhardaein Bicosoecida is a typical representative of HNFs (Massana et al.2021) that has been found to dominate HNFs in the global ocean (Obiol et al.2021; Schoenle et al.2022). The other three species are also cosmopolitan flagellated protozoa that all affiliate with the highly diverse genusParaphysomonasin Chrysophyceae (Lim et al.1999; Scoble and Cavalier‐Smith2014). To test their warming adaptation regimes, we analyzed differences in phenotypes, metabolism, thermal traits, and carbon allocation across various thermal selection conditions and evaluated their fitness and cellular homeostasis at elevated environmental temperatures. This study could provide new insights into the fate of marine protozoa and their roles within the marine microbial food web and biogeochemical cycles in the context of global warming.

We conducted long‐term temperature selection experiments on each species, with the selection temperature setting based on the isolation temperature and local temperature range. An elevated environmental temperature of 31°C was chosen as the warming selection regime (S31) for all the species and applied as the reference temperature to assess the effects of temperature selection on the trait performance, as we are particularly interested in the fate of this functional type in a high‐temperature environment. The isolation temperature of each species was chosen as its control selection condition (i.e., S19 forP. persalinus,U.marinum,P. imperforate, S22 forC. burkhardae, and S25 forP. foraminiferaandParaphysomonassp.; Table1). ForP. persalinus,U.marinum,P. imperforata, andC. burkhardae, in addition to S31, 25°C was designated as the moderate warming selection condition (S25) due to the relatively low isolation temperatures of these species (Table1). During the long‐term thermal selection,P. imperforatadid not survive under S31. Therefore, S25 was the only warming selection condition for this species in later experiments, and 25°C was correspondingly chosen as the reference temperature (i.e., an elevated environmental temperature) for this species. In summary, there were three selection conditions forP. persalinus,U.marinum, andC. burkhardae, and two selection conditions forP. foraminifera,P. imperforata, andParaphysomonassp. (Table1).
We initiated our adaptation experiment with populations that presumably contained inherent genotypic heterogeneity rather than monolithic clones, with the intent to amplify the selective responses and mimic the evolutionary dynamics of natural populations (Reusch and Boyd2013; Padfield et al.2016). During the long‐term adaptation period, cultures were transferred every 6 days to new 40 mL culture flasks (EasYFlask, Thermo Fisher; initial cell density ~1 × 103cells mL−1) with 0.01% yeast extract addition. Compared with rice, yeast extract provides high concentration and readily utilized organic substances to ambient heterotrophic bacteria, enabling them to proliferate rapidly to a higher concentration, thereby satisfying the food requirements of protozoa. The initial and final protozoa density during each transfer enables us to estimate the approximate number of generations during the long‐term adaptation.C. burkhardaestarted adaptation 1 year prior to the others, as it was the first protozoan available in this study at that time. To ensure subsequent experimental approaches are performed at the same time for all species, we extended the adaptation ofC. burkhardaefor one more year while waiting for the other species to pass at least 450 generations. In the end, the thermal selection period was ~2 years forC. burkhardae(~850 generations) and ~1 year for other species (465–530 generations; Table1; TableS1). To track adaptation progress in the long‐term culturing, growth rates during the exponential growth phase were measured in the warmest selection regimes (S25 forP. imperforate; S31 for other species), except forC. burkhardaedue to manpower constraints in the year prior. These growth rate measurements (as described in Section2.3) were made every ~50 days initially, then closer to weekly in the later stages. After long‐term adaptation, cultures from each selection condition were transferred to multiple assay temperatures (Table1) and acclimated for ~12 days (transfer every 4 days). Subsequently, following acclimation, experimental parameters from each assay temperature were measured in triplicate cultures during the exponential growth phase. A schematic diagram of the experimental structure is shown in FigureS1. All the flasks were well‐ventilated and shaken gently several times daily.
The equivalent spherical diameter (ESD) was measured using a Beckman Coulter Multisizer 4e, with a total of one thousand cells analyzed for each treatment. Particulate organic carbon (POC) and nitrogen (PON) were quantified using a CHNS elemental analyzer (FLASH 2000; Thermo Scientific). Specifically, 50 mL of the culture was filtered onto a precombusted (450°C for 5 h) GF/A glass filter (Whatman), dried at 50°C for 48 h, wrapped with a tin capsule, burned in the Prepacked Quartz Reactor (Thermo Scientific), and then analyzed with a CHNS analyzer for protists' POC and PON measurement (Ng and Liu2015). Another 100 mL of culture was prefiltered through a 1‐μm pore size polycarbonate membrane to remove the protists first. Then, the remaining bacterial culture was collected onto a precombusted GF/75 glass filter (ADVANTEC), followed by the aforementioned procedures to analyze the bacterial POC with a CHNS analyzer. Protists were counted using inverted microscopy (Olympus IX51) following staining with 2% Lugol's solution. Bacteria were enumerated under the FITC channel with a flow cytometer (BD FACSCelesta) after being fixed with 1% paraformaldehyde and stained with SYBR Green I (Sigma‐Aldrich, USA) in a 35°C‐water bath. Cellular carbon and nitrogen contents were obtained by POC and PON content divided by cell abundance. Cell‐based physiological rates were normalized to carbon‐based values to account for variations in cell size.

The growth rates ofP. persalinus,U.marinum,P. foraminifera,P. imperforate, andParaphysomonassp. during the exponential growth phase under their warmest selection conditions increased gradually, reaching a plateau after 300 days of adaptation, indicating successful adaptation to the elevated temperature (Figure1b). Following this adaptation period, the growth rates ofP. persalinusandP. imperforateincreased by over 60% compared to their rates prior to warming selection, whereas the growth rates ofU.marinum,P. foraminifera, andParaphysomonassp. increased more than 25%.
The ESD of the six protozoa significantly increased after warming adaptation at an elevated environmental temperature (25°C forP. imperforateand 31°C for other species) (p< 0.001,n= 1000, ANOVA test; Figure2a), followed by their raised cellular carbon and nitrogen contents (Figure2b,c; TablesS2andS4). The most notable changes in ESD, cellular carbon, and nitrogen were observed in ciliates. Specifically, the ESD increased by 16.6% forP. persalinusand 22.9% forU.marinum, whereas their cellular carbon and nitrogen contents rose by over 50% (comparing S31–S19). When normalizing the cellular carbon and nitrogen content to per biovolume, although some species showed significant differences between different selection conditions (i.e.,U.marinum,C. burkhardae, andParaphysomonassp.), there was no consistent pattern among all the protozoa (FigureS2). Besides, the cellular carbon to nitrogen ratio between different selection conditions showed no significant difference (Figure2d).

Investigating functional type‐specific adaptation in responses to warming scenarios allows us to develop pioneering views of ecological feedback under climate change (Cael et al.2023). Our study, by assessing the phenotypes, metabolism, thermal traits, and carbon allocation under different temperature selection regimes, unraveled profitable adaptations of marine protozoa to long‐term warming. The optimal temperature, maximum growth rates, cell homeostasis, and cell size significantly increased after warming adaptation, whereas respiration and its contributed carbon allocation declined vastly. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the warming adaptation capability of marine heterotrophic protists.
The lack of long‐term experimental studies on protozoa beyond a few generations limits our recognition of their adaptation capability in prolonged environmental changes, such as global warming. Warming adaptation experiments integrating hundreds‐generation‐long thermal adaptation and dozens‐generation‐long temperature acclimation enable us to investigate how organismal traits respond to climate warming and forecast population fate near their current tolerance limits (Collins et al.2020). In this study, we investigated the warming adaptation scenario of marine protozoa by assaying six representative species simultaneously. This differs from most previous thermal adaptation studies, which typically focus on a single species. Our findings are thus robust in representing a functional type‐specific response, allowing comparisons across taxa with similar ecological functions, as the same experimental protocol was applied.
The growth rate is typically quantified alongside its corresponding TPC and thermal traits to reflect fitness following warming adaptation. The increased growthToptandμmax, and the competitive fitness at an elevated environmental temperature indicate a fitness‐improved adaptation scenario for our experimental protozoa to long‐term warming selection. This has also been observed in some photoautotrophs, such asThalassiosira pseudonana(O'Donnell et al.2018),Chlamydomonas reinhardtii(Schaum et al.2017),Micromonas polaris(Benner et al.2020), and marineSynechococusssp. (Barton et al.2023). This adaptation regime aligns with the concept that “warmer is (partially) better” (Knies et al.2009; Barton and Yvon‐Durocher2019; Liu et al.2022), suggesting that organisms adapting to warmer environments achieve higher thermal optima and growth maxima.
The improved fitness of the protozoa may be associated with reduced respiration and enhanced ingestion at high assay temperatures, both of which are closely linked to oxidative stress and cellular homeostasis. As a key process contributing to carbon loss, respiration decreased significantly following long‐term warming selection (Figure5a). This finding is consistent with adaptation studies on phytoplankton, bacterivorous mixotrophs, and certain vascular plants (Atkin et al.2015; Padfield et al.2016; Schaum et al.2018; Barton et al.2020; Lepori‐Bui et al.2022), which may be attributed to the reduced cellular stress, as indicated by the lower levels of ROS content (Figure5b). ROS like peroxides (e.g., H2O2) and superoxide (∙O2−) can be formed from O2in oxidative phosphorylation during the cellular respiration process (Nohl et al.2003). Under basal conditions, ROS concentrations remain low due to a balance between their production and removal. However, internal factors (such as aging) or external stressors (like elevated temperatures and hypoxia) can disrupt this balance, resulting in ROS accumulation and subsequent oxidative stress (Betteridge2000). In our study, ROS levels were significantly elevated when strains from the control selection condition were exposed to the high temperature, relative to the warm‐adapted strains (Figure5b), suggesting greater oxidative stress prior to adaptation. Mitochondria, as the primary site of ROS generation, are likely the first to be affected, potentially leading to ROS leakage. This leakage can subsequently harm various cellular components, including organelles, lipids, proteins, and DNA. To maintain cellular homeostasis, investments in autophagy, heat‐shock protein, and repair protein would be increased, at the same time stimulating lysosomes to degrade damaged or depleted materials (Horváth et al.2012; Awan and Deng2014; Tang2016; Leles and Levine2023). These oxidative stress‐related processes are highly energy‐intensive (Plomp et al.1989), necessitating an acceleration of cellular respiration to generate additional ATP. This may explain why respiration and its correspondingEawere significantly elevated in the absence of warming adaptation (Figure5a; FiguresS3andS4). In this scenario, energy is primarily directed toward managing oxidative stress to maintain cellular homeostasis rather than being invested in proliferation‐related processes (such as DNA replication) and predation (Kumar2021; Shore and Albert2022). This is why the control selection regime exhibits low ingestion and growth rates at high assay temperatures, correspondingly low optimal temperatures, and maximum performance (Figures3,4, and7).
Conversely, adaptation to warming may alleviate the oxidative stress caused by elevated temperatures, enabling protozoa to prioritize energy allocation toward proliferation and predation and thus achieve higher growth and ingestion rates. Such a trade‐off of energy allocation between managing oxidative stress and promoting growth has also been observed in other adaptation scenarios, such asThalassiosira pseudonanain response to warming selection (Schaum et al.2018; Leles and Levine2023) and gammaproteobacteria during aerobic respiration (Anand et al.2019). It may represent a crucial adaptive strategy for organisms facing environmental stress over extended timescales. Further investigation using multiomics tools and approaches is warranted to explore this phenomenon in greater depth. Another potential explanation for the changes in TPC and thermal traits following adaptation is the difference in enzyme deactivation, thermal stability, and the thermodynamics of enzyme‐catalyzed reactions across different biological processes. Studies have shown that under prolonged thermal selection, certain enzymes can alter their protein conformation to enhance heat resistance and catalytic efficiency (Sang et al.2020; Chao et al.2020).
At an elevated environmental temperature, the significant decrease in respiration and its corresponding proportion in carbon allocation following warming adaptation suggests that previous forecasts of respiration‐derived carbon dioxide emissions in future warming may be overestimated. The gross growth efficiency, however, showed only a marginal response to warming selection, except forP. persalinus(Figure7), which is not as pronounced as the changes observed in the carbon utilization efficiency in phytoplankton (Padfield et al.2016). For protozoa, respiration is not the sole pathway for carbon loss; excretion must also be taken into account. The increased carbon acquisition from ingestion, alongside significantly decreased respiration‐related carbon loss and only marginal changes in growth‐related carbon accumulation, suggests a rise in both the quantity and proportion of carbon lost through egestion (TableS9). The excretion process not only releases dissolved and particulate organic carbon but also plays a role in nitrogen regeneration (Sherr et al.1983; Pelegri et al.1999). Once released, these substances can nourish bacteria and phytoplankton in the surrounding waters (Verity1985). Overall, our findings regarding ingestion, growth, and inferred excretion suggest that the warming adaptation of marine protozoa may enhance energy transfer and material utilization within the microbial food web, thereby boosting ecosystem primary production through nutrient remineralization.
After adaptation, cell size at an elevated environmental temperature increased markedly (Figure2a). According to general temperature‐size rules, organisms typically reduce their cell size as temperatures rise (Atkinson et al.2003). This reduction is primarily aimed at increasing the surface area‐to‐volume ratio, which enhances oxygen and carbon dioxide diffusion during respiration and improves foraging efficiency to better cope with thermal stress (Verberk et al.2021). However, the observed increase in cell size following warming selection aligns with findings on phytoplankton adaptation from both Schaum et al. (2018) and Barton et al. (2023), suggesting a reduced cell stress after adaptation. Besides, larger cell size is proposed as an important strategy to allow for increased lipid content, where the higher lipid contents were found in many warm‐adapted phytoplankton cases for mitigating oxidative stress (Jin et al.2020; Leles and Levine2023). On the other hand, the enlarged cell size could also be a result of increased digestive vacuoles in both numbers and volume (Siegmund et al.2013) induced by the higher ingestion rates after warming adaptation (Figure7). The increased cellular carbon and nitrogen contents, at the same time, could be a consequence of increased digestive vacuoles and less respirated carbon, although their dense (per biovolume carbon and nitrogen contents) overall did not show a clear pattern among all the protozoa investigated. These findings have important implications for modeling predictions regarding the extent of microorganism size reduction in future warming scenarios.
In contrast to scenarios where fitness improves, some phytoplankton species adapt to warming at the cost of growth (e.g.,Osterococcus tauri) or fail to adapt (e.g.,Phaeodactylum tricornutum; Barton et al.2023). Barton et al. (2023) suggest that the capacity for thermal adaptation is proportional to the cell or genome size of the organisms examined. However, this rule does not seem to apply to our experimental heterotrophs; rather, the larger ciliated protozoa exhibited better adaptation than the smaller flagellated protozoa, evidenced by the higher growth rates (Figure7). This may be attributed to the filter‐feeding behavior and unique binucleate structures of ciliates, which enhance their predation efficiency and reproductive capacity, allowing them to thrive in changing environments (Verni and Gualtieri1997; Pucciarelli et al.2009; Plattner2022). This aspect warrants further investigation in future studies. Additionally, the selection conditions (moderate, extreme, or fluctuating warming) and the reference temperature (sub‐ or supra‐optimal) can significantly influence the assessment of warming adaptation capabilities. In our study, we define “profitable adaptation” based on the S25 and S31 warming selection regimes and an elevated environmental temperature as the reference. Choosing extremely severe warming regimes or a low reference temperature could yield entirely different outcomes (O'Donnell et al.2018; Schaum et al.2018; Leles and Levine2023).
Our findings regarding a profitable warming adaptation scenario for six marine heterotrophic protists suggest a resilient future for this functional group in the context of global warming. These results provide a foundation for refining ecological models by linking adaptation capabilities with physiological traits, which have significant implications for the future of marine protozoa, respiration‐induced carbon dioxide emissions, community size structures, and the efficiency of biogeochemical cycles in a changing climate. It is important to note that althoughC. burkhardaehad a longer adaptation, as we ensured the same experimental approaches across all the species and at least 450 generations passed prior to the postadaptation assays, this difference is not expected to substantially influence the trends observed. Furthermore, the potential difference in prey metabolism and community composition across the treatments may impact prey–predator interactions and protozoa respiration quotients (Saleem et al.2013; Patel et al.2018), deserving further exploration in the future. In general, our conclusions are based on experimental warming, which may not fully capture the complexities of natural scenarios. In natural environments, the adaptation capacities may be constrained by multiple stressors such as resource limitations (Aranguren‐Gassis et al.2019), but could also be accelerated through co‐evolution and genetic diversity (Lawrence et al.2012). Therefore, we advocate for considering co‐evolution and multiple environmental stressors in future works.
