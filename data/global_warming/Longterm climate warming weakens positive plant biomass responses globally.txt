This study is significant because it provides crucial insights into how global warming affects plant biomass, which is a key factor in carbon sequestration. Understanding the diminishing long-term biomass responses to rising temperatures is essential for predicting the effectiveness of plants in mitigating climate change. With global warming projected to continue, this research offers valuable empirical data and improves global models, helping policymakers and scientists design more accurate climate mitigation strategies. The findings also underscore the importance of considering factors like soil properties and land use when evaluating the future role of terrestrial ecosystems in carbon storage.
Keywords:warming duration, warming magnitude, aboveground biomass, belowground biomass, environmental factors
Carbon sequestration through plant biomass responses to global warming plays a vital role in mitigating climate change, but recent evidence suggests that this effect diminishes in the long term. To investigate the effect of warming duration on plant biomass responses, we analyzed a global dataset of 2,291 paired observations, revealing that warming increased overall aboveground plant biomass by 9.4% and belowground biomass by 2.6%. However, as the warming duration increased, the positive plant biomass response shifted to neutral or negative, with great biomass reductions in regions where mean annual temperatures exceeded 10 °C. Global aboveground biomass is projected to decline by 4 to 16% under a 2 °C increase in climate warming. Our study suggests that long-term climate warming will significantly weaken plant carbon storage, even if temperature increases remain within the targets of the Paris Agreement, and offers empirical constraints for global biomass–climate models.
As human activities release more carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O) into the atmosphere, global surface temperatures are expected to rise by 1.4 to 5.8 °C by the end of the century (1). Climate warming is widely believed to stimulate the decomposition of soil organic matter, leading to a climate-carbon feedback loop (2). Terrestrial plants play a key role in regulating global climate by enhancing photosynthesis and promoting biomass accumulation, which can partially or fully offset soil carbon losses (3–5). This forms a negative feedback loop that helps slow the rate of global warming (6–9). Beyond climate regulation, terrestrial plant biomass also serves as a major source of raw materials for fibers, renewable chemicals, and other essential resources for human use (10). However, the impacts of climate warming on ecosystems are not short-lived, as warming exerts long-term influences on plant productivity and biomass accumulation (11). Various approaches have been used to quantify the long-term effects of climate warming, including process-based models and data-driven methods based on remote sensing and experimental observations (12–14). In situ warming experiments conducted across various regions and timeframes have revealed substantial spatial and temporal variation in biomass responses. Nevertheless, the potential impact of warming duration on these responses has not been systematically quantified (11,15,16). This hinders reliable projections of the potential impacts of mitigation strategies and requires quantitative insights into the mechanisms of plant carbon sequestration in future climate scenarios.
Earlier studies based on CMIP5 models estimated that global net primary productivity increases by 4.17 PgC when the Earth warms by 1.5 °C (17). Similar findings have been reported by a recent dynamic global vegetation model from the Chinese Academy of Sciences (18). When the air temperature increases by 2 °C, global total primary productivity is projected to increase by 21% (19). Three recent meta-analyses reveal that a temperature increase of 2 to 3 °C would increase total global plant biomass by 8.9 to 19% (14,20,21). These findings underscore the key role of plant biomass responses to warming and the opportunities to offset carbon emissions.
Although global-scale plant carbon sinks in terrestrial ecosystems are well supported by remote sensing data and synthesis analyses (14,17–23), experimental warming studies have reported positive, neutral, and negative biomass responses, with the underlying mechanisms remaining unclear (3,13,24,25). The inconsistent responses of plant biomass to climate warming may be attributed to factors such as land use (e.g., forests, grasslands, croplands), climatic conditions (e.g., mean annual temperature), warming magnitude, nitrogen addition, warming methods, and warming duration. First, different land-use types vary in their capacity to store carbon and respond to warming. For example, forests can sequester carbon for a longer period than grasslands and croplands due to the longevity and structural stability (7). Second, warming experiments conducted in cold regions with suitable warming magnitudes benefit plant cell division and biomass increase (14,26–28). By contrast, in tropical regions or under high warming magnitudes, temperatures may surpass the optimal threshold for photosynthesis, resulting in limited or negative impacts on biomass production (29). Third, warming can accelerate nitrogen mineralization, leading to greater soil nitrogen availability. This effect is particularly important in nitrogen-limited ecosystems, where plant growth is highly sensitive to increases in available nitrogen (21,30). Fourth, the warming method (e.g., open-top chambers, infrared heaters, heating cables) can influence the observed responses of plant biomass to climate warming. For example, compared to infrared heaters and open-top chambers, warming with heating cables may significantly increase soil nitrogen mineralization rates (22,30), providing plants with more nutrients to support growth. Last, field experiments have demonstrated that warming for less than 5 y does not significantly alter soil carbon and nitrogen pools (31,32) but stimulates plant biomass increase (33). In contrast, warming lasting more than 5 y significantly reduces soil carbon and nitrogen pools (32,34,35), with variable effects on aboveground and belowground plant biomass (3,36). This suggests that long-term warming (>5 y) may weaken the positive effects of warming on plant productivity by reducing nutrient availability. However, the considerable variation in biomass responses and the confounding influence of interacting factors underscore the need for a comprehensive analysis of empirical evidence.
Moreover, uncertainties about the buffering capacity of soils and the pronounced heterogeneity in local soil fertility have been identified as key limitations in process-based models assessing plant biomass responses to long-term warming (17–19,37,38). Given the importance of biomass dynamics for global climate feedbacks, unraveling the long-term impacts of warming—while accounting for site-specific and experimental conditions—is essential for informing effective climate change mitigation strategies.
Our research aims to comprehensively understand the response mechanisms of terrestrial plant biomass to climate warming. We will incorporate plant biomass responses to warming at different field sites and establish links with climate, soil characteristics, and nutrient management. This approach strives for a more accurate quantification of the effect of climate warming on plant biomass. Building upon numerous experimental observations and using meta-regression techniques, we aim to unravel the long-term effect of global warming on plant biomass. This includes correcting for the intrinsic complexity of experimental conditions (i.e., warming duration, warming magnitude, and warming methods), land use (i.e., forest, grassland, cropland), climate (i.e., mean annual temperature, mean annual precipitation), soil properties (i.e., soil organic carbon, soil total nitrogen, soil carbon: nitrogen ratio, soil total phosphorus, soil pH, and soil clay content), and nutrient management (i.e., nitrogen fertilizer) (7,39–44). In addition, the response mechanism to the above environmental factors under climate warming may be influenced by soil moisture loss due to increased temperatures (45). In particular, warming-induced reductions in soil water availability may suppress the positive effects of warming-enhanced soil nitrogen mineralization on plant biomass (46).
Here, we collect 2,291 sets of warming experimental data on aboveground and belowground biomass from 214 studies done in 28 countries (Fig. 1andSI Appendix, Table S1andDataset S1). Building upon existing knowledge, we hypothesize that 1) climate warming promotes plant biomass in the short term (<5 y), particularly in nitrogen-limited soils; however, this positive response diminishes under long-term warming (>5 y), and 2) environmental factors such as land use, climate, soil properties, and nutrient management regulate the response of plant biomass to climate warming. Quantifying the biomass response to warming while accounting for these environmental factors allows us to estimate global changes in biomass production in response to future warming scenarios. Our study provides constraints on the response of biomass to climate warming under experimental conditions and enhances our understanding of the environmental factors controlling the biomass response to warming.

In contrast, greater warming magnitude, higher mean annual precipitation, and lower soil pH were associated with stronger positive biomass responses (Fig. 4B,D, andF). The positive effect of warming on aboveground biomass was stronger in nitrogen-deficient soils, as indicated by the significant correlation with the nitrogen fertilizer rate, carbon:nitrogen ratio, and soil organic carbon level (Fig. 4E,G, andH). We focused on modeling aboveground biomass globally, as sample points for belowground biomass and belowground:aboveground biomass ratio were limited. As warming duration increased, the plant aboveground biomass response to warming in multiple regions showed increased variability (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Globally, the mean predicted increase in aboveground biomass under a future 2 °C warming scenario was 21% (ranging from −2.3 to 49%) after 1 y. This declined to −4.4% (−33 to 37%) after 5 y and −16% (ranging from −44 to 27%) after 10 y (Fig. 5). Under a future 4.8 °C warming scenario, the global mean predicted increase in aboveground biomass was 40% (ranging from −11 to 121%) after 1 y, 22% (−42 to 158%) after 5 y, and 12% (−52 to 168%) after 10 y (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). As warming duration increased, the positive effect of climate warming on plant biomass gradually weakened, particularly in major predictable regions of the Northern Hemisphere (Fig. 6).

Our results showed that under experimental warming, plant aboveground and belowground biomass increased by 9.4% and 2.6%, respectively (Fig. 2), indicating that climate warming enhances global vegetation’s ability to absorb atmospheric CO2(47). Whether this increase results in additional carbon sequestration depends on warming’s effect on microbial metabolism, enzyme activity, and fine root growth and turnover, all of which are influenced by higher temperatures (14,48–50). In addition, nitrogen cycling plays a crucial role in regulating the response of plant biomass to warming. Globally, nitrogen limitation constrains plant carbon fixation (51). Warming can initially stimulate plant growth by accelerating nitrogen mineralization and turnover, thereby increasing nitrogen availability in the soil (42,52). However, under prolonged warming, enhanced nitrogen losses through gaseous emissions and leaching may gradually diminish soil nutrient pools, weakening biomass responses and potentially reducing the long-term carbon sequestration capacity of terrestrial ecosystems (34,35,53). Compared to grasslands and croplands, forests had a greater capacity for CO2fixation, with both aboveground and belowground biomass showing significantly larger positive responses to warming (Fig. 3). This aligned with our hypothesis that land use changed the response of plant biomass to warming. In forests, complex and deep root systems facilitate nutrient acquisition (7), contributing to the observed increases in both aboveground and belowground biomass under experimental warming. Moreover, compared to the rapid biomass turnover in grasslands and croplands, forests are generally considered more effective at storing sequestered CO2over longer periods (54). However, this apparent advantage in long-term forest carbon storage warrants careful consideration. As Büntgen et al. (55) noted, warming-induced tree growth does not necessarily lead to sustained carbon sequestration, owing to the trade-off between tree growth rate and lifespan. Fast-growing species often exhibit shorter life cycles, reducing carbon residence time and thereby limiting the long-term sink capacity of forests under warming.
Environmental factors such as warming magnitude, mean annual temperature, precipitation, soil carbon:nitrogen ratio, and nitrogen addition rate significantly influenced plant biomass (Fig. 4). These findings supported our hypothesis that climate, soil properties, and nutrient management regulated the response of plant biomass to climate warming. An increase in warming magnitude led to higher plant aboveground biomass (Fig. 4B), further supporting that climate warming stimulates plant carbon fixation (47). This effect is likely attributable to enhanced cell division at elevated temperatures, resulting in increased plant growth rates (56). However, in regions with high mean annual temperature (>10 °C), the negative response of plant aboveground biomass to climate warming became more pronounced (Fig. 4A). This is consistent with prior synthesis studies (57), which showed that forest productivity declined with increasing baseline temperature. Our study reinforces this pattern with experimental data from broader geographic contexts, underscoring the vulnerability of warmer regions. This may be because, in hot regions, warming exceeds the optimal temperature range for plant growth, thereby limiting photosynthesis and cell division, while enhancing plant respiration (7). However, high rainfall can mitigate warming-induced drought stress (58), supporting increases in aboveground biomass (Fig. 4D). In addition, warming enhances nutrient mineralization, increasing plant biomass, especially in nitrogen-deficient areas (29). Thus, under nutrient-limited conditions (e.g., high soil carbon:nitrogen ratio indicating low nitrogen availability), warming was particularly favorable for boosting aboveground biomass (Fig. 4G). We also found that nitrogen fertilization significantly decreased the positive response of plant aboveground biomass to warming (Fig. 4E), supporting our hypothesis that warming would boost plant biomass under nitrogen-limited conditions. However, this effect may diminish over time as warming-induced nutrient losses intensify (53).
Environmental factors also mediated the response of the plant belowground:aboveground biomass ratio to climate warming. Increased warming magnitudes raised aboveground biomass but did slightly affect belowground biomass, reducing the plant belowground:aboveground biomass ratio (Fig. 4andSI Appendix, Fig. S2B). This may be due to increased competition for aboveground resources (such as light) under warming (59,60). Under high soil carbon:nitrogen conditions, warming increases nitrogen mineralization, leading to more carbon being allocated to aboveground biomass for photosynthesis rather than to belowground biomass (21). This shifted investment toward aboveground biomass, decreasing the belowground:aboveground biomass ratio (SI Appendix, Fig. S2F). The effects of this mechanism are particularly visible in the northern hemisphere, where soils with high organic carbon levels and nitrogen deficiency are more common (51).
Globally, the overall effect of warming on aboveground biomass shifted from positive to neutral or negative under 2 °C warming scenarios, particularly when warming persisted for more than 5 y (Figs. 5and6). These findings supported our hypothesis that long-term warming reduced biomass response across ecosystems, weakening the potential of biomass to offset CO2emissions. Long-term warming leads to the gradual loss of soil carbon and nitrogen through gas emissions or leaching (48,61), deteriorating soil fertility and limiting aboveground and belowground plant growth (53). Warming also reduces the temporal stability of plant biomass by decreasing species asynchrony (62), which may lead to biomass loss under long-term warming. In regions with calcareous soils (pH > 7), there were considerable negative effects of long-term warming on plant aboveground biomass (Fig. 4FandSI Appendix, Fig. S7), possibly through suppressed photosynthesis (25,63) or increased moisture evaporation, leading to soil salinization (30), which jeopardizes plant growth. This suggests that under future warming scenarios, plant carbon storage in alkaline soils will be further threatened.
While previous studies suggest that plant communities act as a carbon sink under warming (20,22,23), our findings showed that long-term warming weakened this effect. The total carbon stock in global aboveground forests is 662 PgC (64). Assuming carbon loss reflects biomass loss, 5 and 10 y of 2 °C global warming are projected to reduce forest carbon uptake by 29 PgC and 105 PgC, respectively, corresponding to average declines of 4.4 and 16% in aboveground biomass (Fig. 5). Aboveground biomass response was closely correlated with belowground biomass response (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Long-term warming intensified negative effects on belowground biomass (Fig. 4andSI Appendix, Fig. S5), posing a significant threat to plant carbon sequestration. Global warming also decreased net carbon inputs to the soil, reducing the soil carbon pool (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). This suggests that future climate warming will reduce the carbon storage capacity of both soil and plants, weakening the resilience of natural and agricultural ecosystems. Our study highlights the crucial role of long-term research and environmental factors in mediating the effect of climate warming on plant biomass. Future studies can build on our findings by enhancing models to incorporate long-term effects and integrate factors such as land use, climate, and soil properties. Moreover, as warming duration increased, both the spatial extent and confidence of our projections declined (SI Appendix, Fig. S8), underscoring the need for more long-term experiments to improve our understanding of warming impacts on carbon sinks and to inform effective mitigation strategies.
Tropical and temperate rainforests, which play a crucial role in the global carbon cycle, were poorly represented in global predictions due to a lack of sufficient experimental warming studies (Fig. 5). These ecosystems are highly sensitive to climate warming, with tropical forests experiencing reduced photosynthetic rates and overall declines in carbon assimilation under warming conditions (65). Process-based forest growth models project a future productivity loss of approximately 10% for tropical humid rainforests due to climate warming (66). Consistently, our findings indicated that plant biomass losses intensified when mean annual temperatures exceeded 10 °C, suggesting a potentially decreased trend in tropical areas. This implies that our results might underestimate the negative effects of future climate warming on plant carbon fixation, highlighting the urgent need for regional studies. Therefore, conducting more experimental warming studies in tropical regions will be essential for enhancing the robustness of predictions on the response of plant biomass to global warming.
Our analysis focused on topsoil roots due to data constraints, but warming-induced drought and heat stress may drive plants to allocate biomass to deeper roots for nutrient acquisition (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). This focus on the upper soil layer may have weakened the expected positive response of root biomass to warming. However, in most grasslands and croplands, biomass is primarily distributed aboveground and within the top 30 cm of soil (67), and prolonged warming diminishes their positive response (Fig. 4). Different warming methods, such as soil heating cables versus canopy air warming, may lead to distinct responses in plant biomass and microbial processes (Fig. 3), as heating cables tend to simulate more localized and direct soil temperature increases (22). Given the variations in warming methods and their potential impacts, future research should systematically compare the results from warming techniques to better understand the underlying mechanisms. In addition, the mediating effect of soil moisture needs further exploration since beneficial effects of warming are likely more pronounced under conditions where soil moisture levels are within the optimum range for plant growth, while detrimental effects are likely more pronounced when soil moisture decreases below this range (45,58). Given the data limitations of the collected experimental observations, this hypothesis could neither be confirmed nor denied (SI Appendix, Fig. S12).
In future climates, the rise in temperature will be accompanied by an increase in global CO2concentrations. Elevated CO2may theoretically mitigate warming-induced water deficits by reducing transpiration via suppressed stomatal conductance (68). However, the overall effects on plant transpiration and soil moisture are complex and context-dependent. Specifically, elevated CO2can reduce stomatal conductance by lowering leaf hydraulic conductance and increasing canopy shading. However, this effect may be offset by concurrent increases in leaf area index (LAI), thereby maintaining overall canopy transpiration (69,70). Under conditions of low soil moisture, the suppression of transpiration by elevated CO2tends to be more pronounced; yet, the resulting increase in leaf temperature can elevate vapor pressure deficit, accelerating leaf senescence and abscission (71). Moreover, elevated CO2influences ecosystem water cycling by increasing LAI and litter accumulation, which enhance rainfall interception and reduce soil evaporation, respectively, leading to complex effects on soil moisture dynamics (71,72). These physiological and structural responses are generally more pronounced in forests, where CO2-induced changes in transpiration and carbon exchange are greater, whereas grasslands buffer such impacts through canopy-level microclimate regulation (73). Plant biomass responses to warming and elevated CO2are shaped by interacting physiological and structural feedbacks that differ across ecosystems. To improve future projections, research and models must better account for this complexity in predicting biomass and water cycle responses. Furthermore, field-based warming experiments simulate rapid temperature increases, which influence plant biomass by rapidly altering soil nutrient cycling (31) at a much faster speed than the projected rate of climate warming over the next 80 y. This implies that our results are indicative for the expected changes in plant biomass under future warming conditions. Nevertheless, it provides empirical-based constraints on the response of plant biomass to climate warming and offers a basic understanding of its potential drivers (74), insights relevant for calibration of global biomass models. Future studies should integrate in-situ observations of decadal-scale warming trends with manipulative experiments using ramped temperature treatments (e.g., a gradual increase of +2 °C over 10 y) to better capture ecosystem acclimation processes.
Our study demonstrates that the positive effects of warming on biomass tend to diminish over the long term (>5 y), and this effect varies in response to local site conditions such as temperature, precipitation, and nitrogen availability. This may further exacerbate the climate-carbon positive feedback loop, increasing the urgency for appropriate and well-designed mitigation measures to enhance carbon sequestration in plant biomass and soils.
We conducted an extensive literature search using Web of Science, Google Scholar, and China National Knowledge Infrastructure for articles published up until August 3, 2023. Our search terms included “(experimental warming or temperature increase or simulated warming or temperature rise) and (biomass or aboveground biomass or belowground biomass or shoot or root).” We selected publications that met specific criteria: 1) investigating the effects of warming on plant aboveground or belowground biomass; 2) comparing warming treatments with unwarming treatments, keeping other initial conditions unchanged; 3) employing artificial warming methods such as open-top chambers, infrared radiation, and cable heating for warming treatments. Considering the high turnover rate of fine roots, which cannot fully represent the entire root system, we excluded data on their biomass. The search followed the “Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses” (PRISMA) protocol (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). WebPlotDigitizer was employed for data extraction, as revealed in published figures depicting aboveground and belowground biomass. The belowground:aboveground biomass ratio was collected, an essential measure of plant response to warming (75). Additionally, factors such as soil organic carbon, soil moisture, nitrate and ammonium nitrogen, total soil nitrogen, and dissolved organic carbon were extracted for both warming and control treatments. Furthermore, we gathered information on the warming magnitude (the increased warming degree in the air), warming methods, warming duration, land use, mean annual temperature, annual precipitation, latitude, longitude, initial soil organic carbon, initial soil total nitrogen, initial soil carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, initial soil pH, initial soil total phosphorus, initial soil clay content, and nitrogen fertilizer application. The assessment of plant belowground biomass was primarily conducted for the 0 to 30 cm soil depth. In the absence of available data on mean annual temperature and precipitation, we obtained these metrics from the WorldClim2 dataset (76). Missing data regarding initial organic carbon, initial total nitrogen, initial soil carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, initial total phosphorus, clay content, and soil pH were obtained from the SoilGrids250m database (77). A comprehensive collection of metadata from 214 studies was compiled, encompassing a total of 2,291 observation pairs (Dataset S1).

A prior analysis indicated that the direct soil warming effect under heating cables (n = 19 observations) had a stronger effect on root growth and the mineralization of soil carbon and nitrogen in comparison to canopy air warming (n = 302 observations) (Fig. 3). To minimize the effect of the warming method on the observed responses (given the limited number of observations for the heating cables), we omitted the data from Heating cables for further analysis of the response of belowground biomass to warming.
Although one study found that different fungal types mediated plant biomass responses to climate warming, this significant intergroup differential response was more pronounced in fine roots rather than overall biomass (78). Additionally, considering that the fungal type and abundance are directly influenced by soil properties and climatic context (74), and the number of observations regarding mycorrhizal fungi is rather limited (Dataset S1), we did not explicitly analyze the effect of mycorrhizal fungi on the response of plant biomass to warming. Several observational studies consider the effect of climate warming on plant biomass, encompassing factors such as elevated CO2levels. Given the limitations of the databases associated with these investigations (e.g., elevated CO2levels), we excluded these specific treatments from our linear regression analyses. Subsequently, all continuous variables were standardized to unit variance to improve comparability and facilitate the identification of key variables influencing microbial responses to warming.

The mixed-effects regression model was implemented using the nlme package, fitting the model with restricted maximum likelihood estimation and setting the error variance structure to 1 (80). To eliminate spatial autocorrelation among different samples, we set the correlation to corGaus(form = ~ longitude + latitude). To avoid overfitting in the mixed-effects regression model, we used the “dredge” function from the muMIn package to select a parsimonious model based on AIC values (81). The presence of collinearity in the model may introduce bias in the results; therefore, we used the car package to compute variance inflation factors (VIF) and removed the factors exhibiting the highest collinearity (82). We iterated until all factors had VIF values below 3, resulting in the most parsimonious models for plant aboveground biomass, plant belowground biomass, and plant belowground:aboveground biomass ratio (SI Appendix, Table S3).
We used the Shapiro–Wilk test to assess the assumption of normality for the residuals in the parsimonious model, revealing violations of the normality assumption in all residual distributions (SI Appendix, Table S3). Consequently, to address this issue, we conducted 1,000 iterations of coefficient estimation using the lmeresampler package (83). We converted lnRR to percentage change using the formula(elnRR-1)×100%.
We used accumulated local effects (ALE) plots to visualize the relationships between each predictor variable and the response of plant biomass to climate warming with the DALEX package (84). ALE functions by decomposing the model’s predictive effect to observe how each feature influences the prediction on average across different local regions. Initially, ALE divides the feature space into distinct intervals and then computes the cumulative effects of feature value changes on the model output within each interval. ALE effectively handles interactions between variables, offering explanations that are a faster and unbiased alternative to partial dependence plots (85).

To obtain an equation solely regarding the effect of warming duration on plant aboveground biomass in relation to mean annual temperature, we assigned median values for the other predicted variables (i.e., pH, soil organic carbon, and warming magnitude) (Dataset S1).
We used the calibrated linear mixed-effects model to predict the response of plant biomass to climate warming on a global scale using available global datasets for soil and climatic data as explanatory variables (SI Appendix, Table S3). Global soil properties, such as soil organic carbon, pH, and C:N ratio were obtained from SoilGrids250m (77). Global mean annual temperature and mean annual precipitation layers were obtained from WorldClim2 (76). Global land use type was obtained from ESA CCI (86). The N rate in the forests and grasslands was set equal to the global N deposition rate (87), and set to 180 kg N/ha for cropland (the median N rate for croplands observed inDataset S1). The warming magnitude was assumed to be 2 or 4.8 °C, and the warming duration was assumed 1, 5, and 10 y. 2 °C represents the threshold set by the Paris Agreement to limit global temperature rise (88), and 4.8 °C is an approximate value at the end of the 21st century based on IPCC reports (89). The derived spatial maps of plant biomass responses to global warming were used to illustrate and unravel the spatial arrangement of global-scale plant biomass responses to future warming.
To avoid extrapolations beyond the variation present in the dataset used to calibrate the linear mixed-effects model, we excluded spatial locations whose soil and climate properties are outside the observed variation in the collected dataset from warming experiments. To do so, we applied the Mahalanobis algorithm, a method often used to measure the distance between a single data point and the mean of all measured covariates while controlling for multicollinearity; a site above the threshold set at chisq = 0.975 was considered unpredictable. Additionally, due to the lack of data points in tropical and temperate rainforest regions, we excluded these areas from the prediction space.
All statistical analyses were performed using R version 4.1.1 (90).
