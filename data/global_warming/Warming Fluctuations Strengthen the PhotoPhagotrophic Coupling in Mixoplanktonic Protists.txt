Mixoplankton, a major trophic group in aquatic ecosystems, are being affected by global warming. However, most studies on temperature effects use constant mean conditions, overlooking how short-term thermal fluctuations could deviate from climate projections and impact this group. We experimentally quantified how increasing amplitudes of warming fluctuation (± 1, 3, and 5 °C) alter carbon-specific electron transport (ETRc), net photosynthesis (Pc), respiration (Rc), phagotrophy (Phc), carbon use efficiency (CUE), and growth (µ) in four protist species (three mixoplanktonic and one strict phototroph). We observed a consistent positive link between photosynthetic efficiency (Pc:ETRcratio) and Phc, and a shift towards a strengthening of the phagotrophy (Pc:ETRc/ Phcratio) with greater thermal fluctuation. A potential explanation is a selective behavior aimed to increase phagotrophy to obtain inorganic nutrients through ingested prey internal re-cycling rather than relying on the environment, to support an enhanced photosynthetic efficiency and growth. An enhanced, coupled photo-phagotrophy activity could boost mixoplankton competitiveness compared to phytoplankton. Our findings underscore the need to incorporate trophic flexibility and its interaction with environmental variability into trait-based models to better predict community dynamics, biogeochemical cycling, and food web structure in aquatic ecosystems.
Keywords:Carbon use efficiency, Electron transport rates, Jensen inequality, Phago-mixotrophy, Net photosynthesis, Respiration
Phago-mixotrophy, the trophic mode through which an organism use photo- and phagotrophy simultaneously for growth, is one of the widespread trophic strategies on Earth [1,2]. By combining both trophic strategies, mixoplankton play a pivotal role in carbon and nutrient cycling because they boost the trophic transfer efficiency in food webs and the carbon exportation via the biological pump [3,4]. Mixoplanktonic algae account for 80% of the total chlorophyll in the open ocean [5], and drive carbon remineralization in ocean gyres as consumers [6]. Phago-mixotrophy has been proposed as a dominant strategy that provide non-substitutable resources [7] and carbon [8] in microbial plankton food webs. Therefore, a better understanding of mixoplankton responses to the ongoing global change is needed to obtain accurate predictions about how it could alter biomass accumulation in other trophic levels or carbon export via the biological pump [9].
Rising mean temperatures associated with climate change are projected to impact aquatic ecosystems by altering the metabolism of aquatic organisms [10]. The Metabolic Theory of Ecology posits that auto- and heterotrophic metabolism scales exponentially with temperature to meet increasingly cellular metabolic demands and potential physiological stress [11]. Within this framework, heterotrophic processes (e.g. phagotrophy, respiration) are thought to be more sensitive to temperature than autotrophic (e.g. photosynthesis) ones, thus mixoplankton is expected to become more phagotrophic under warming conditions [12]. Additionally, thermal stress promotes that mixoplanktonic systems switch between alternative carbon states over evolutionary scales, favoring their transition from a carbon sink to a carbon source state [13]. The higher thermal dependence of hetero- vs. autotrophic organisms has been recently questioned by empirical evidence showing that autotrophic growth can be as sensitive to temperature, or even more so than heterotrophic one [14,15]. In fact, certain investigations have reported a predominance of photoautotrophic metabolism in acute responses of mixoplankton exposed to warming [16,17], but it depends on the prey abundance [18]. As in many biological systems, the relationship between phagotrophy and temperature follows a Gaussian distribution, constrained by the organism’s thermal tolerance [19]. Several studies have reported elevated ingestion rates in various mixoplankton species in response to warming [12,19,20] supporting the relevance of Metabolic Theory of Ecology to mixoplanktonic protists.
Although the thermal dependence of metabolism is a well-stated idea in the global change ecology, previous studies have mostly focused on quantifying the effects of mean increasing temperatures [21]. In this context, Jensen’s inequality (also termed the fallacy of the average) predicts that the response of a given system to average conditions is seldom equal to the mean response to variable ones [22]. In addition, climate change models predict not only an increased mean temperature but also a higher thermal variability [23]. In line with this theoretical framework and predictions, experimental results show that fluctuating environments promote major genomic and phenotypic changes which allow cells to develop a rapid tolerance to temperature [24,25]. Both factors, increased mean temperatures and thermal variability, may explain that the response of systems to environmental drivers is usually not linear [26]. These changes observed in a thermally varying environment are also translated into a reduction of the stimulatory effect of warming on cyanobacterial biomass production and recruitment in freshwater phytoplankton [27]. However, in estuarine phytoplankton [28] and different marine diatom species [29], they might result in an attenuation of the negative effects of warming on primary productivity. Other studies have shown that temperature fluctuations reduced the calcification in the coccolitophoridEmiliania huxleyi[30] and the specific-nitrogen and carbon fixation rates in the nitrogen fixerTrichodesmium[31] or growth rates [32,33] and species richness and diversity in natural phytoplankton communities [34]. It has also been shown that thermal fluctuations, respect to constant temperature, stimulated the growth of the mixoplanktonic speciesChromulinasp. over that of the phototrophicMonoraphidium minutumin mixed culture conditions, likely due to the latter was more impaired to a physiological level (e.g. reduced photosynthesis) [35]. From the available information and considering the current global climate change context, two interlinked questions arise: Will increasing temperature fluctuations enhance the effects of warming on heterotrophic processes more than on autotrophic ones? If so, could trophic flexibility of mixotrophy enhance photosynthetic efficiency through phagotrophy in protists, thereby enabling them to outcompete with strict phototrophs (i.e. phytoplankton)?
The goal of this study is to disentangle how changes in temperature alter the phototrophic and phagotrophic metabolism and how mixoplankton deal with increasing amplitude of thermal fluctuations mediated by global climate change (including extreme events), and if such changes could potentially confer a competitive advantage to mixoplankton over phytoplankton due to this dual trophic mode in a changing environment. To achieve this goal, we experimentally quantified the photo-physiological activity, photosynthesis, respiration and phagotrophy rates, carbon use efficiency and growth in four protist species (three of mixoplankton and one of phytoplankton) belonging to different evolutionary groups (Cryptophyta, Haptophyta, and Bacillariophyta). All species were exposed to a constant control temperature (T constant, 19 °C) and three different diel warming-fluctuating temperature regimes (T ± 1, T ± 3, and T ± 5 °C) for seven days.
Semi-continuous, non-axenic cultures ofCryptomonas ovataEhrenberg, 1832 (Cryptophyceae; mixoplankton; 900 µm3),Isochrysis galbanaParke 1949 (Coccolitophyceae; mixoplankton; 70 µm3),Rhodomonas lensPascher & Ruttner 1913 (Cryptophyceae; mixoplankton; 17 µm3) andPhaeodactylum tricornutumBohlin 1897 (Bacillariophyceae; phytoplankton; 30 µm3) were grown in non-aerated 1-L Erlenmeyer flasks at 19 °C and a 12 h:12 h light: darkness (7 am to 19 pm, light; 19 pm to 7 am of the following day, darkness) constant photoperiod (100 µmol photons m− 2s− 1), and maintained in exponential growth by diluting them daily with fresh medium i.e. f/2 media [36] forI. galbana,P. tricornutum(enriched with sodium silicate), andR. lens, and COMBO [37] media forC. ovata. Both growth media are the most commonly used for the chosen species and their nutrient content is high enough to avoid any limitation over short-term exposures. Both media were prepared using Tris (pH = 8.2 for the f/2 medium) and HEPES (pH = 7 for the COMBO medium) buffer solutions to ensure that the pH of the cultures remained constant. Daily pH measurements indicated no changes in this variable over time for any of the four species tested.C. ovatawas isolated by our group in a pond close to our research center,I. galbanacame from Estación de Fotobiología Playa Unión culture collection (Argentina;www.efpu.org.ar) andR. lensandP. tricornutumfrom Centro de Investigación Mariña – Universidade de Vigo culture collection (Spain;https://cim.uvigo.gal/en/ecimat/services-provided/supply-of-marine-biological-resources/). We chose these mixoplankton species because they have been previously reported as phago-mixotrophs [38,39] and because Cryptophyta and Haptophyte are major contributors to the total grazing impact on bacteria in aquatic ecosystems [40,41]. Finally, the phytoplankton species was selected this species because it is one of the best-studied representatives of diatoms to any biological organization level (including its genome; [42], and diatoms are major contributors to the global carbon fixation (20–25% of the total [43].
All species were acclimated to the growth mean temperature (i.e. 19 °C) and light conditions for at least two weeks before being used in the experimentation. We consider that we were working with a fully acclimated phenotype since, during this period, populations went through multiple generations (between 4 (i.e.R. lens) and ~ 7 generations (P. tricornutum)). To evaluate the effects of increasing warming-fluctuating regimes, species were placed separately in 250 mL quartz flasks and exposed (in quadruplicate and without dilution) to four temperature treatments: control (constant temperature of 19 °C) and three warming-fluctuating regimes i.e. ± 1 (19–20–19 °C), ± 3 (19–22–19 °C), and ± 5 °C (19–24–19 °C), respectively, over 24-h cycles (with maximum temperature at noon, and minimum at mid-night) for 7 days (Fig. S1). Therefore, during the exposure period, cultures underwent seven complete fluctuation cycles. We used 19 °C as our control treatment because it is the temperature at which the strains has been maintained in our culture collection over the last decade. Additionally, we performed a literature compilation of the interest species, finding that its optima thermal breadth ranged between 18 and 22 °C (Fig. S2). The three fluctuating temperature treatments used followed a sinusoidal pattern and mimicked natural daily variations registered in surface coastal waters [44] and freshwater ecosystems [45]. The exposure period selected was based on previous growth curves of the interest species during the acclimation phase which showed that they needed between 8 and 12 (i.e.C. ovataandR. lens) and 16 days or more (i.e.I. galbanaandP. tricornutum) to reach the stationary phase. For comparison purposes and to be sure that all species experienced the experimental conditions for the same period of time, we sampled them (and measured the response variables associated; see below) the same days. All vessels were incubated in a temperature-controlled (± 0.1 °C) aquaria system equipped with automatized refrigerators (Teco®TK2000, EU) and a continuous water recirculation system and the same light source used for growth conditions (Nano LED light v.2.0, BLAU Aquaristic, EU).
5 mL sub-samples were taken from each flask and fixed with 2.5% alkaline lugol. Sub-samples of 10 µL were placed in a Neubauer chamber (American Optical, Buffalo, NY, USA) and counted at 400× using an inverted microscope (Carl Zeiss AX10, LCC, USA). The biovolume of the protists was calculated from suitable geometric formulas to the cell shape and the mean dimensions of 20 individuals of each species according to Hillebrand et al. [46] and then converted into carbon content (i.e., biomass) using the coefficients of protists with < 3000 µm3cell biovolume forC. ovataandR. lens, Prymnesiophyceae forI. galbana, and diatoms forP. tricornutumfrom Menden-Deuer & Lessard [47]. Biomass-based growth rates were calculated as the slope of the linear regression (R2> 0.89 for all treatments/species) of the natural logarithm of C biomass vs. time during the experimental period.

Oxygen rates were converted into carbon units assuming a respiratory quotient [54] of 1, and the C-specific net photosynthesis (Pc) and C-specific respiration (Rc) rates (in mg C fixed or respired mg C− 1d− 1) were calculated by normalizing each rate by the C biomass. From Pcand ETRc, we calculated the photosynthetic efficiency (Pc:ETRcratio), as a proxy of the effectiveness of the photosynthetic process, by considering how much carbon was potentially fixed during the dark phase of the photosynthesis in relation to the electrons transported through the photosystem II during the light phase of such process.
The C ingestion (Phc) was measured following a standardized methodology [55]. Sub-samples of 10 mL from flasks were placed in 25-mL quartz vessels (four replicates and one blank per temperature treatment), the radiotracer3H-thymidine (20 nM f.c.) was added to each sub-sample and then incubated for 1 h on days 1, 2, 4, and 7 under the same experimental conditions. Incubations were done only during the light period due to a previous study by our group found similar phagotrophy rates after 12-h of a light or a dark exposure period [56], hence we assumed they were the same over a day-night cycle. The consequences of this decision will be revisited in the discussion.
After the incubation, phagotrophic activity was halted by adding neutralized formaldehyde (2.5%, w/v final concentration). Blanks were formaldehyde-killed before the incubation, and radio-traced once the incubation ended. All samples were stored at 4 °C until analyzed following the method by González-Olalla et al. [35]. The blanks provided a measure of the adsorption and passive uptake of3H-thymidine by phytoplankton and bacteria > 1-µm cell size that could be retained on the filter, and their value was subtracted from that of each individual replicate. Previous bioassays have demonstrated no significant differences between blanks and axenic cultures of phytoplankton species, hence a3H-thymidine uptake by them could be excluded [57]. We used a conversion factor of 1.5 × 1018cell mol− 1to estimate the number of growing bacteria consumed per mole of incorporated3H-thymidine [58], and the factor 20 fg C cell− 1to convert the number of consumed bacteria in C terms [59]. C-specific phagotrophy (Phc, in mg C ingested mg C− 1d− 1) rates were obtained by normalizing phagotrophy by C biomass.

Because we worked with different species, which have different generation times, and a day-to-day variability existed over time (Fig. S3-S5), we considered more appropriate to estimate time-integrated values for each response variable. To do that, we calculated the cumulative values of ETRc, Pc, Rcand Phc, as the sum of the rates measured in each sampling day and standardized them by the duration of the experiment (7 days) to obtain rates instead cumulative measures. These time-integrated rates provide an ecologically meaningful measure of total energy and carbon fluxes over the experimental period, reducing noise from transient daily variations, and giving a more comprehensive view about how the thermal fluctuations regime affected their responses. Through this approach, we considered the total C fixed, ingested or respired and electrons transported. For CUE andµ, we calculated mean values from all thermal fluctuation regimes to be able to compare both interlinked rates. We reported mean values for the latter two variables due to they are steady-state parameters, while the rates are dynamic processes better represented by time-integrated values.
A two-way analysis of the variance (ANOVA) was used to test the effect of warming-fluctuating regimes on ETRc, Pc, Rc, Pc:ETRc, Phc, CUE,µand the Pc:ETRc/Phc ratio. The species were included as a factor in our analysis to test not only differences between the mixoplanktonic ones but also to compare them with the phototrophic species. A one-way ANOVA was used to test significant differences between species on the mean CUE andµ. Normality of residuals by Shapiro-Wilk’s test and homogeneity of the variance by Levene’s and Cochran’s C tests were used to check that ANOVA assumptions were met. When significant temperature×species interactions were detected a Least Significant Differencespost hoctest was performed to evaluate significant differences among and within treatments. A linear regression model was used to assess the relationship between Pc:ETRcand Phcfor all mixoplankton species considered. Assumptions of residuals independence, homogeneity and normality were verified through Durbin-Watson, Breusch-Pagan and Shapiro-Wilk tests, respectively. All statistical analysis were done using Rstudio v. 2025.05.0 [60].

Our work demonstrate that fluctuating temperature regimes exert a significant impact on the physiology and ecological functioning of mixoplankton. Thus, whileP. tricornutum, a strict phototroph, maintained stable performance across thermally fluctuating regimes, exhibiting the highest photosynthetic efficiency but the lowest electron transport rates, the three mixoplanktonic species used displayed more complex, variable responses, including significant increases photosynthetic efficiency and phagotrophy rates as the thermal variability accentuated. These variable responses could have been influenced by the different thermal breadths that the phylogenetic groups considered have (e.g. > Haptophytes > Cryptophytes > Bacillariophyta) [61], despite species were growing inside their thermal optima range. Across all fluctuating temperature regimes,R. lensexhibited consistently high carbon-specific electron transport rates, photosynthesis, respiration and phagotrophy. The higher phagotrophy inR. lenscould further explain their differential behavior respect to the other two mixoplankton species considered. This physiological-metabolic dominance points to a broad thermal niche which could confer an enhanced adaptability to variable environmental conditions. This proposal would entail a potential ecological success in increasingly dynamic systems [62]. While the study of functional traits has gained much attention in phyto- and mixoplankton ecology recently [63–65], only a few studies have considered an integrated multi-trait and multi-species approach in a global change context [33,66]. Thus, our results highlight the need to integrate multiple functional traits, in particular the mixotrophic potential, into predictive frameworks for organismal responses to environmental change.
The positive association between phagotrophy and photosynthetic efficiency found is striking and supports the significant metabolic plasticity (ability to alter their investment into different metabolic processes) of these organisms in response to changes in environmental conditions [67,68]. Our findings suggest a facilitative interaction between both trophic modes, phototrophy and heterotrophy, in mixoplanktonic taxa, potentially reinforcing each other and enhancing inorganic nutrients and energy acquisition and buffering against environmental instability. This synergism between trophic modes and its ecological consequences for cell populations are consistent with previous findings that showed an increased internal phosphorus (and iron) concentration, growth and cell fitness in a constitute mixotroph [57]. The nutrients gathered through phagotrophy may also relieve physiological and metabolic bottlenecks in carbon fixation, in particular under thermally varying conditions where enzyme function and nutrient demand rise [69]. This supports an emerging view of mixotrophy as a flexible, integrative strategy in fluctuating environments rather than a compromise between nutritional modes [2,70].
Our results are also consistent with the proposal by Mitra’s et al. (1) that mixoplankton do not necessarily need to have a larger cell size than phytoplankton to have a comparable photosynthetic efficiency i.e. housing two nutritional systems do not result in larger cell sizes compared to cells with only one system (photosynthesis). In fact,C. ovataandI. galbanahave larger cell sizes thanP. tricornutum(and evenR. lens) (~ 900 and 70 vs. ~30 and 17 µm3cell− 1, respectively) but they exhibited lower photosynthetic efficiency. This is likely due to a higher energetic cost associated with respiration (and photo-reductant, for photosynthesis as well) to sustain both metabolic pathways, photosynthesis and phagotrophy, functioning [71].
Carbon use efficiency and growth responses further reinforced the multi-trait-based patterns presented above.R. lensandP. tricornutum—despite having divergent trophic modes—shared similar CUEs, especially under moderate thermal variability. Although we found similar mean CUEs and growth rates in the strict phototrophic species andR. lens, the other mixoplankton species showed lower CUE which were translated intoµtwo- (C. ovata) and fourth (I. galbana) times lower. An underlying mechanism behind the differential response observed could be a narrower thermal niche breadth [31,72] for these species. Previous results have also shown that faster-growing species (asP. tricornutumandR. lens) tend to have wider thermal tolerance [61]. Chen hypothesized that a faster growth might be due to a higher concentration of rate-limiting enzymes (e.g. RubisCO), which allow a positive growth even when these enzymes are partially denatured at extreme temperatures. We cannot rule out that cells spending less time within their optimal temperature range under a fluctuating temperature regime, as opposed to constant conditions, require greater energetic costs to maintain performance [73]. In line with this proposal, Walworth et al. [74] showed that under equal physical dynamics conditions (i.e. fluctuating temperatures in the ocean surface), slower growing populations (asC. ovataandI. galbanain our study) would experience a more variable environment than those growing faster. This is because a lower number of generations elapsed experiencing a given environment, henceC. ovataandI. galbanawere more sensitive to the effects of thermal fluctuations thanP. tricornutumandR. lens. The contrasting cell size of the species tested (17–900 µm3) likely also affected their physiology and metabolism since this trait correlates with multiple other ones including stoichiometry, growth, etc [75].
Although phagotrophy contribution to the total carbon pool was low (and we assumed that it was the same in light and darkness periods based on previous findings) [56], probably because the mixoplankton species here studied are (or act) constitutive mixoplankton [76], we consider that this trophic mode was not limited by prey availability. The prey: predator ratios measured (in terms of C biomass) were 3- (forI. galbana), 6- (forR. lens) and 14-fold (forC. ovata) higher than the one recommended (10:1) to avoid such limitation [77] or by nutrients. A potential nutrient limitation can be discarded because all species grew in a highly nutrients-enriched media (between 800 and 1000 µM for nitrate and 33–50 µM phosphate) and previous assays by our group evidenced that these species reached the stationary phase after longer incubation periods than considered here (between 8 and 16 days). Finally, mixoplankton presents a selective behavior in high-nutrient availability environments (i.e. they obtain nutrients rather than organic carbon preferentially from prey ingestion), and vice versa [78]. This potential selective behavior is consistent with the increased phagotrophy measured in mixoplankton and would support the idea that the provision of nitrogen and phosphorus through internal recycling of ingested prey-nitrogen and phosphorus can stimulate photosynthetic efficiency (as seen inI. galbanaandR. lens) [79]. Additionally, it agrees with Metabolic Theory of Ecology predictions that traits/processes have a differential thermal sensitivity (i.e. heterotrophic processes > autotrophic processes [11]) and thus, support Wilken’s et al.. hypothesis [12] and recent results with in-lab cultures and global ocean communities [17,18,80] that mixoplankton become more phagotrophic rising temperature. Therefore, an enhanced, coupled photo-phagotrophy activity could boost competitiveness of mixoplankton compared to that of phytoplankton. Nevertheless, mixoplankton and phytoplankton responses to abrupt (or sustained) temperature changes are also dependent of the temporal scale of exposure [17].
Altogether, these results reveal that mixotrophy is not a monolithic trait but comprises distinct functional expressions within this broad, diverse group that vary in sensitivity to environmental variability. The ecological implications of these responses are that shifts in the amplitude of temperature fluctuations, as occur during heatwave events, in a warming world may favor phagotrophy and ultimately, faster growing mixoplankton species (e.g. asR. lens). Thus, knowledge of mean patterns within a population may not be enough to accurately predict the future dynamics [26], hence the need for quantifying how organisms experience the ‘aquatic weather’ i.e. the environmental fluctuations, and what are their consequences for individuals or communities [81,82]. We also found a considerable inter-trait and inter-specific variability which, in some cases, exceeded variations due to warming-fluctuating conditions. This suggests that traits become more variable under enhanced metabolic activity [this study, 33].
Our study conducted to a population level and under nutrient replete conditions, precluded us from measuring interspecific interactions (i.e. between phytoplankton and mixoplankton) and how they respond to thermal fluctuations when are competing for nutrients, but also how their metabolism and performance is altered when they are experiencing a nutrient-limited phycosphere. Recent studies with strict phototrophs have showed that nutrients limitation expands the range of mean temperatures at which a species is a better competitor [83] but reduces the thermal fluctuation amplitude regime, relative to mean temperature, under which two species can coexist [84]. Finally, we focused on acclimation level and optimal temperatures; thus, mixoplankton responses to thermal fluctuations over evolutionary timescales and beyond optimal thermal ranges remain to be explored. Despite the limitations outlined above, our findings unveil a pressing need to incorporate trophic flexibility and the environmental variability to improve trait-based models of community dynamics, biogeochemical cycling, and food web structure in warming and increasingly unpredictable aquatic ecosystems. These mechanistic insights set new goals to guide upcoming research efforts in incorporating mixoplankton ecology and physiology in global-change models.
