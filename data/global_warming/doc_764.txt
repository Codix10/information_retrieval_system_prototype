The rapid increase in population, industrialization, and the rise in fossil fuel consumption have led to issues such as the depletion of natural resources, loss of biodiversity, and climate change, bringing the concept of sustainability to the forefront. This study aimed to examine the relationship between sustainable food literacy, organic food consumption, climate change awareness and worry.
This cross-sectional study included 1660 adults aged 18–65 years. Data were collected via a web-based survey that included demographic information, self-reported anthropometric measurements, and validated scales: Climate Change Awareness Scale, Climate Change Worry Scale, Organic Food Consumption Scale, and Sustainable Food Literacy Scale.
The mean age of the participants was 29.3 ± 12.07 years. A strong positive relationship was observed between the total score of the Climate Change Awareness Scale and the scores of the Climate Change Worry Scale, Organic Food Consumption Scale, and Sustainable Food Literacy Scale (r= 0.230,p< 0.001;r= 0.149,p< 0.001;r= 0.173,p< 0.001, respectively). According to linear regression analysis, it was determined that gender, education level, Climate Change Awareness Scale total score, Climate Change Worry Scale score, and Sustainable Food Literacy Scale score were correlated with the Organic Food Consumption Scale score (R²=0.527;p< 0.001). On the other hand, it was determined that gender, education level, income status, Climate Change Awareness Scale total score, Climate Change Anxiety Scale score and Organic Food Consumption Scale score were correlated with the Sustainable Food Literacy Scale score (R²=0.505;p< 0.001). It was determined that Climate Change Anxiety Scale score and the Sustainable Food Literacy Scale score were associated with the Climate Change Awareness Scale total score (R²=0.246;p< 0.001). In addition, it was determined that gender, marital status, Climate Change Awareness Scale total score, and Sustainable Food Literacy Scale score were associated with the Climate Change Worry Scale score (R²=0.286;p< 0.001).
As a result, to combat global climate change, it is necessary to conduct further research in this field by considering these relationships, to identify and improve the factors influencing sustainable behaviors, to promote organic food consumption, and to assess awareness and anxiety regarding climate change.
Keywords:Sustainable food literacy, Organic food consumption, Climate change awareness, Climate change worry
In recent years, developments such as the rapidly increasing population, the industrial revolution, the use of fossil fuels, and the increase in greenhouse gas emissions have led to negative global outcomes, including the depletion of natural resources, loss of biodiversity, global warming, and climate change. It is thought that the concept of sustainability can be an effective factor in the search for a solution [1]. The term “sustainable,” originally derived from the World Environment Commission’s 1987 definition of “sustainable development,” has more recently been defined in academic discourse as “the ability to be maintained over the long term while fulfilling present needs without jeopardizing the capacity of future generations to meet their own needs.” [2,3]. In this context, sustainability necessitates a multidimensional approach, ranging from individual behaviors to system-level policies.
One of the key elements of sustainability, food systems, has the potential to affect both the environment and health. The processes of food production and consumption heavily utilize natural resources such as soil, water, and energy, and can generate environmental impacts. Ensuring sustainability in these processes can both reduce environmental harm and enhance food security, thereby supporting human health [4,5]. As articulated by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations within the context of sustainable food systems, the fundamental sustainability imperatives of both the present and the future encompass the eradication of hunger and the enhancement of food security and nutrition [6]. These recommendations are also in line with the United Nations’ 2030 Sustainable Development Goals [7]. At this point, the concept of sustainable food literacy has emerged, emphasizing the ability to make food choices that consider society, larger communities, the environment, and individual health. Sustainable food literacy is recognized as a critical tool for enhancing individuals’ knowledge and awareness of sustainable food systems, encouraging conscious consumption behaviors, and enabling choices that minimize environmental impacts [8–10].
The FAO has highlighted that the food system must be addressed in the context of rapid global population growth, urbanization, changes in consumption habits, globalization, and climate change, underscoring the increasing importance of literacy about sustainable food systems [6]. A systematic review from 2025 on sustainable food literacy noted that existing studies on this subject in the literature are neither comprehensive nor sufficient. For both individual and societal benefit, it has been suggested to conduct studies focusing on levels of attitudes toward sustainable food literacy, the factors influencing these attitudes, and strategies to encourange awareness [11].
In recent years, the increase in sustainable food literacy has led to a growing emphasis on organic food consumption. Organic food refers to products obtained from an agricultural system that minimizes the use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides, preserves biodiversity, and reduces environmental damage [12]. Studies have shown that organic farming can lead to a 20–30% reduction in energy use and lower greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 40% compared to conventional farming methods [13,14]. Additionally, soil quality and biodiversity are improved in organic agricultural systems due to the absence of synthetic chemicals [15]. Food purchasing behaviors can be influenced by various factors, including sustainability awareness, health concerns, and perceived environmental benefits [12]. Research indicates that organic food consumers are more likely to prioritize pesticide-free and eco-friendly products, which aligns with broader sustainability goals [16]. However, the environmental advantages of organic farming can vary based on factors such as local production vs. importation and transportation emissions. In some cases, the carbon footprint of imported organic food can offset its environmental benefits [17]. Therefore, the sustainability of organic food consumption depends on multiple variables, including supply chain efficiency and production location. In this context, organic food consumption is considered not merely a matter of individual preferences but an element that serves broader ecological balance and sustainability goals [18–20]. Increasing concerns about health and the environment are becoming increasingly significant in consumers’ decision-making processes, and it is noted that this can influence the demand for organic food [21].
In addition, climate change stands out as one of the most significant global challenges at the core of sustainability efforts. In recent years, the negative impacts of human life on our planet have led to a profound transformation known as climate change. This transformation has resulted in an increase in other abiotic stress conditions, such as drought, the frequency and intensity of heatwaves, floods, hurricanes, and freezing events [1]. Due to the concern raised by awareness of global climate change, the WHO has stated that climate change can have adverse effects not only on physical health but also on mental health [22,23]. However, this concern does not necessarily have to be pathological. It is suggested that concern about climate change may contribute to individuals gravitating toward more environmentally friendly and sustainable products, and that the level of awareness plays a critical role in aligning these processes with sustainability goals [24].
This study aims to evaluate the relationship between sustainable food literacy, organic food consumption, climate change awareness, and worry among adults. More specifically, this study aims to:
Identify factors associated with organic food consumption.
Identify factors associated with sustainable food literacy.
Identify factors associated with climate change awareness and worry.
Considering the growing importance of the concepts of sustainability and climate change in recent years, this study is expected to fill a gap in the literature and guide future research in this area. The findings obtained from this study are intended to shed light and contribute to future longitudinal research and intervention studies.
This cross-sectional study was conducted among 1660 adults aged 18 to 65 years between September 2024 and January 2025. Research data were collected through a web-based survey (Google Forms) developed by the researchers and administered in Ankara Province, Türkiye, using the convenience sampling method. The survey was disseminated via social media platforms, including Twitter, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Instagram. The inclusion criteria for participation in the study were as follows: individuals aged between 19 and 65 years with internet access who voluntarily agreed to participate by selecting the “I agree to participate in this study voluntarily” option at the beginning of the online survey and who completed the questionnaire in its entirety. Prior to the study, ethical approval was obtained from the University of Health Sciences Gülhane Scientific Research Ethics Committee (Decision No: 2024/543). All study procedures adhered to the principles outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki. The survey collected data on participants’ demographic characteristics (gender, age, education level, marital status, income level, and working status) as well as anthropometric measurements (body weight and height). Additionally, the study examined the relationships between climate change awareness, climate change worry, organic food consumption, and sustainable food literacy using Turkish validated scales.
Anthropometric measurements, including body weight and height, were obtained through self-reports provided by the participants. Instructions on how to accurately measure and report anthropometric data were included in the questionnaire. Body Mass Index (BMI) was calculated by dividing body weight (kg) by the square of height (m²). A BMI of less than 18.50 kg/m² was classified as underweight, 18.50–24.99 kg/m² as normal weight, 25.00–29.99 kg/m² as overweight, and 30.00 kg/m² or higher as obese [25].
The Climate Change Awareness Scale was employed to assess participants’ awareness of climate change. The Turkish validity and reliability study of the scale was conducted by Ataklı and Kuran [26] in 2022. The scale comprises five factors and 52 items, measured on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from “strongly disagree” [1] to “strongly agree” [2]. A total score is obtained by summing the scores of all items, with higher scores indicating a greater level of climate change awareness. The five factors of the scale include climate change awareness, perception of the problem, knowledge of the causes of climate change, climate change anxiety, and expectations from behaviors and policies. The maximum possible scores for each subfactor are 45, 25, 45, 55, and 90 points, respectively. The internal consistency of the total scale, as measured by Cronbach’s alpha, was 0.92. The reliability coefficients for the subscales were as follows: expectations from behaviors and policies (0.93), climate change anxiety (0.91), knowledge of the causes of climate change (0.87), climate change awareness (0.80), and perception of the problem (0.81).
The Climate Worry Scale, originally developed by Stewart [27] in 2021, was adapted into Turkish by Gezer and İlhan [28] in the same year. The scale consists of 10 items, each rated on a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 to 5. The total score is obtained by summing the ratings of all items, yielding a possible range of 10 to 50 points. In the study, evaluation was made based on the total score and the total score was obtained by summing the item scores. Higher scores indicating greater levels of concern about climate change.
The 18-item Organic Food Consumption Scale developed by İçli et al. [29] was used to assess organic food consumption orientation. The Cronbach alpha internal consistency value of the scale is 0.771. The scale is scored on a 5-point Likert-type scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 2 = Disagree, 3 = Undecided, 4 = Agree, 5 = Strongly Agree). There are no questions with reverse coding in the scale scoring. In the study, the evaluation was conducted based on the total score, which was derived by summing the individual item scores. Low scores from the scale indicate a decreasing trend in organic food consumption, while high scores indicate an increasing trend in organic food consumption.
The Turkish validity and reliability study was conducted by Yüksel and Kubilay [30]. The scale consists of 26 items. Scale items are scored using a 7-point Likert type with scores ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 7 (strongly agree). In the study, the evaluation was conducted using the total score, which was calculated by summing the scores of the individual items. Increasing scores indicate an increasing level of sustainable food literacy. Cronbach’s Alpha values of all sub-dimensions were found to be above the recommended value of 0.70.
The G*Power software (Version 3.1.9.6) was used to analyze the sample’s size. The effect size of the study was aimed to be a poor or medium correlation between organic food consumption and climate change worry. Based on the effect size|ρ|=0.20, correlation: point biserial model, according to the with two tail, alpha of 5%, power (1-β) = 0.95, the sample size was calculated 526. The post-doc analysis was performed after the study, based on the analysis (r= 0.482) the statistical power (1-β) was found to be 99% for the statistical significance of 2-sided alpha of 5%.
All analyses were performed using the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS) software, version 22.0. Descriptive statistics, including mean, standard deviation, frequency, and percentage, were utilized to summarize the data. Whether the data were normally distributed or not was evaluated by histogram, coefficient of variation, skewness, kurtosis and Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests. The relationships between variables were examined using the Pearson correlation coefficient. This study examined the relationship between sustainable food literacy, organic food consumption, and climate change awareness and worry. The findings indicate significant relationships among these variables. It was found that organic food consumption is influenced by gender, education level, sustainable food literacy, and climate change awareness and worry. On the other hand, gender, education level, income status, organic food consumption, and climate change awareness and worry were found to influence sustainable food literacy. Climate change worry and sustainable food literacy were identified as factors associated with climate change awareness. Additionally, climate change worry is influenced by gender, marital status, climate change awareness, and sustainable food literacy.
The rapidly growing population, industrialization, and increased use of fossil fuels have led to issues such as the depletion of natural resources, loss of biodiversity, and climate change, making the concept of sustainability a priority [1]. Sustainable behaviors encompass many components. Identifying and enhancing literacy levels regarding sustainable behaviors, determining the factors influencing these behaviors, and thereby achieving sustainability can reduce environmental harm and support human health by increasing food security [4,5]. Traditional methods have started to fail in providing the necessary food production to meet the demands of the rapidly growing global population, leading farmers to adopt technological interventions to address the increasing need for food. Unfortunately, this increase in food production has also led to a rise in the use of chemical pesticides and fertilizers, contributing to climate change and health problems. For this reason, environmentally sustainable food consumption is a crucial component of the fight against climate change [31].
Sustainable nutrition encompasses the secure provision of sufficient food and nutrients for everyone while also protecting the environment, biodiversity, and local resources. The term sustainable diet has been shown to have a variety of meanings for consumers. These include consuming a healthy, balanced diet; consuming organic foods; and higher-quality diets in general. While protecting the environment serves as a motivation for sustainable diets, it is also noted that health concerns play a significant role in the development of sustainable behaviors [32]. Despite the growing interest in food literacy, there is a limited number of studies in the literature addressing sustainable food literacy and the factors influencing it.
Studies have shown that consumers’ knowledge, attitudes, and behaviors toward sustainability components are influenced by various sociodemographic variables. Women are reported to be more interested in the concept of sustainable diets, with higher levels of anxiety about climate change and a greater likelihood of acting accordingly [33–35]. However, while women may be more open to taking action, research highlights that married women’s interest may be hindered by less willing male partners and children. Conversely, there is evidence that men can adopt sustainable dietary behaviors, such as organic food consumption, under the influence of a vegetarian partner [36–38]. Another study found that reducing meat consumption for a more sustainable diet was more common among women, while being single also increased these behaviors [16]. On the other hand, studies indicate that married individuals may experience increased concern about climate change due to the sense of responsibility associated with being a current or future parent [39,40]. A positive relationship has been observed between higher education levels and sustainable food literacy, as well as more sustainable dietary behaviors [41,42]. A relationship has been observed between higher education levels and more sustainable dietary practices. Studies suggest that individuals with higher education levels are more likely to consume fewer foods they believe to be unsustainable and to purchase products accordingly [35,43–45]. However, some studies have found no significant relationship between sustainable food literacy and gender [41,42]. In this study, it was found that women had higher levels of sustainable food literacy, organic food consumption, and climate change worry. Sustainable food literacy was observed to be positively associated with education and income levels.
Consumers were noted to perceive organic foods as a sustainable alternative in food choices [46]. The prominent reasons for preferring organic foods include the use of fewer pesticides in production and concerns related to climate change [47]. Organic food production is sustainable and has the potential to promote health. It has been reported that increased awareness of climate change leads to greater interest in sustainable consumption and the eco-friendly characteristics of products, which, in turn, encourages the purchase of organic products [16]. Various studies also support the idea that high levels of knowledge and literacy about nutrition, as well as environmental awareness and concern about climate change, can significantly increase organic food consumption [48–50]. Organic food consumption is influenced not only by awareness levels but also by various demographic characteristics. In a study involving 68,946 consumers, higher organic food consumption scores were positively associated with female gender, high occupational status or monthly income, and post-secondary education levels [51]. Another study also shows that women have higher environmental concerns and a greater tendency to prefer organic foods, and that organic food consumption increases with higher education levels [52]. Similarly, this study found that women had higher levels of organic food consumption, and there was a positive relationship between organic food consumption and sustainable food literacy. Organic food consumption was also positively associated with education level, climate change awareness, and worry.
Climate change constitutes one of the most significant threats to current and future species and humans, threatening people’s living conditions, livelihoods, and health. An important question about climate change worry is its relationship to climate change awareness and whether it can activate behavioral responses to climate change by motivating sustainable behavior. From an evolutionary psychology perspective, the global and slow-moving nature of climate change does not provoke an immediate self-preservation response and reduces the likelihood of humans adapting their behaviors. However, informative actions aimed at raising awareness of climate change have been suggested to enhance the effectiveness of environmental communications and overcome this misalignment [53]. Studies show that increasing climate change awareness leads to greater worry, and also increases the willingness to take action [54,55]. Several studies have identified a positive relationship between climate change worry and sustainable behaviors [56,57]. The level of climate change worry increases with climate change awareness; it has been found to be higher in women and married individuals [58]. However, there are also studies that suggest an increase in climate change worry does not necessarily correlate with sustainable behaviors [59,60]. In studies conducted in Turkey, similar to this research, it has been found that having a high school or university education and a great interest in environmental issues increases climate change worry, and a positive relationship has been found between climate change awareness and sustainable behaviors [61,62]. The results of this study show that increasing climate change awareness is strongly associated with the increase in climate change worry and sustainable food literacy. It was found that climate change worry is higher in women and married individuals, and this concern is also strongly related to climate change awareness and sustainable food literacy. According to the results of this study, increasing sustainable food literacy is associated with increased organic food consumption, climate change awareness, and worry. Although there are few studies on sustainable food literacy [4,5,11], the findings of this study parallel existing research [33–35,43–45]. The relationship between sustainable food literacy, climate change, and organic food consumption and its contribution to the literature with a new perspective is a strong aspect of the study. Moreover, the large sample size strengthens the statistical significance of the study’s results. On the other hand, there are some limitations to the study. Firstly, being cross-sectional prevents causal inferences between variables. Therefore, longitudinal studies are needed to determine the direction of observed relationships. Secondly, self-reported data from participants may lead to bias in reporting and result in unreliable outcomes. The study data were collected via a web-based survey. Web-based surveys tend to include younger, more educated, and more digitally active individuals, and may not be fully representative of the general population. Therefore, the study sample may overrepresent individuals who are more familiar with sustainability concepts and organic food consumption. However, this sampling method allowed for the collection of a large data set. It is recommended that future studies use more diverse data collection methods, especially alternative strategies such as offline surveys, to obtain a more representative sample and increase the generalizability of the findings. Another limitation concerns the economic aspect of organic food consumption. While we acknowledge that organic products tend to have higher prices compared to conventional alternatives, our study did not include a detailed economic analysis of affordability and purchasing power. Future research could integrate more comprehensive pricing data and examine the role of financial constraints in organic food choices more explicitly.
In conclusion, this study highlights the significant relationships between sustainable food literacy, organic food consumption, climate change awareness, and worry. It is important to raise awareness about climate change among individuals to increase organic food consumption and food literacy. Additionally, by emphasizing sustainability in food literacy, steps should be taken at the societal level to encourage individuals to adopt healthy and environmentally beneficial dietary habits, and to raise awareness about the effects of climate change. More attention should be given to the topics of climate change and sustainable food literacy, as this will lead to positive outcomes for both health and the environment. This study is expected to shed light on future research on climate change, sustainable food literacy, and organic food consumption.
