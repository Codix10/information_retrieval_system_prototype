Adolescents are increasingly recognized as important stakeholders in responding to the challenges of climate change, with their psychological responses shaping both mental health outcomes and behavioral choices. However, the intricate relationship between climate change awareness and climate change anxiety among adolescents, as significant manifestations of psychological reactions to climate change, has not yet been thoroughly investigated. Grounded in the Stress and Coping Theory, this study aimed to empirically investigate the bidirectional relationship between climate change awareness and climate change anxiety among Chinese adolescents.
Data were collected through a three-wave longitudinal survey (2022–2024) from 426 Chinese adolescents. We employed repeated measures ANOVA to examine developmental patterns and gender differences in climate change awareness and climate change anxiety, and constructed cross-lagged panel models, along with the calculation of feedback effects, to investigate their reciprocal relationships across time points.
Results revealed significant increases in both climate change awareness and climate change anxiety over time, with females consistently exhibiting higher levels. Cross-lagged analyses demonstrated that climate change awareness significantly predicted an increase in climate change anxiety, and climate change anxiety, in turn, significantly enhanced climate change awareness. Furthermore, the feedback effect between climate change awareness and climate change anxiety was significant at both T1-T2 and T2-T3 intervals.
This study demonstrates a bidirectional relationship between climate change awareness and anxiety among adolescents, providing a theoretical framework and empirical evidence for understanding adolescents’ complex psychological responses to climate change. It also presents valuable suggestions for implementing targeted mental health interventions, and climate change education.
Keywords:Climate change awareness, Climate change anxiety, Cross-lagged analyses, Stress and coping theory
The “often-unseen” impacts of climate change on human health, particularly the mental health consequences, have received widespread attention [1]. A large-scale survey in China revealed that extreme heat induced by climate change significantly increased depressive symptoms and the risk of disorders [2]. Similarly, a large-scale multinational study demonstrated that climate change exerts a widespread and profound effect on mental health, contributing to prevalent anxiety and negative emotions [3]. Adolescents are crucial participants in climate change governance, with their ideas and actions exerting a significant influence on addressing future climate challenges [4]. Studies have indicated that, as a vulnerable group, adolescents are susceptible to heightened emotional distress, including feelings of unease, frustration, sorrow and anxiety, in response to climate change [5]. Furthermore, it has also been found that climate change can not only provoke emotional distress but also serve as a source of motivation and optimism for adolescents through meaning-focused coping strategies, such as positive reappraisal and trust in societal actors [6,7]. These constructive psychological responses are positively associated with life satisfaction, environmental efficacy, and pro-environmental behavior [8]. Therefore, the complex psychological reactions of adolescents to climate change merit in-depth research.
Recently, as the primary psychological reactions to climate change, adolescents’ climate change awareness (CCAw) and climate change anxiety (CCAn) have garnered widespread attention. CCAw refers to individuals’ recognition of the reality of climate change, encompassing cognitive, emotional, and behavioral aspects. This awareness not only drives the pursuit of pro-climate behaviors and cleaner production methods [9], but is also correlated with negative emotions such as frustration and anxiety [10]. Understanding adolescents’ CCAw helps assess their readiness to tackle environmental challenges and reveals their emotional responses and adaptability to growing climate impacts [11]. Furthermore, CCAn, defined by the APA as “a chronic fear of environmental doom”, is a key component of adolescents’ psychological reactions to climate change [12]. Recent studies show most adolescents experience moderate to high levels of CCAn, often leading to chronic worry and sleep disturbances [13]. However, personal responsibility and climate mitigation behaviors have been found to be unexpectedly promoted by elevated CCAn levels [14].
The relationship between CCAw and CCAn plays a critical role in shaping adolescents’ psychological reactions to climate change [15]. Previous studies have suggested that CCAw can alleviate CCAn [16]. However, according to Construal Level Theory (CLT) [17], CCAw makes the abstract concept of climate change more tangible, which may reduce psychological distance but increase anxiety due to perceived threats [18]. Meanwhile, the impact of CCAn on CCAw is also debated. Some argue that CCAn leads to “information avoidance”, reducing awareness [19], while others suggest it can serve as a “green motivation” [20]. Despite the widespread attention to the relationship between the two, a robust theoretical foundation and empirical evidence are still needed.
Stress and Coping Theory provides a theoretical framework to understand the interplay between CCAw and CCAn [21]. When individuals encounter environmental threats like climate change, they experience a stress process that involves cognitive appraisal and coping responses. In the primary appraisal, they assess the threat to themselves and their environment, forming the foundation for CCAw [22]. During the secondary appraisal, they evaluate their ability to cope, guiding their coping strategies. As the perceived threat grows, emotional responses such as anxiety, stress, and fear increase, contributing to CCAn. This rise in CCAn can increase attention to climate threats and further enhance CCAw, which leads to a reappraisal of the stressor, creating a feedback loop where anxiety drives greater awareness and engagement [23].
In summary, existing research and theoretical frameworks provide a reference for understanding the interaction between CCAw and CCAn. However, longitudinal evidence clarifying the relationship between CCAw and CCAn remains scarce, particularly among adolescents. Grounded in Stress and Coping Theory, this study employs a longitudinal survey with a three-wave cross-lagged model to explore the relationship between CCAw and CCAn in Chinese adolescents. The findings advance our understanding of the interaction between these variables and offer insights for developing targeted interventions to enhance adolescents’ proactive and effective coping with climate change.
Stress and Coping Theory, formulated by Lazarus and Folkman, provides a framework for understanding the cognitive processes involved in stress perception and management [21]. This theory suggests that potentially stressful situations trigger an initial appraisal process, during which individuals assess the degree of threat to their well-being. Subsequently, a secondary appraisal assesses the individual’s coping resources and their capacity to manage the threat. When individuals assess those situational demands exceed their available resources, coping responses are triggered to alleviate the stressor. These coping responses can be categorized into two primary strategies: problem-focused coping, which entails directly addressing the problem in an attempt to resolve it, and emotion-focused coping, which involves strategies aimed at regulating or alleviating negative emotions associated with the stressor [24]. During coping, reappraisal of new information can reshape an individual’s situation assessment. This is crucial for emotion-focused coping, as it may shift threat perception and adjust emotional responses to stressors [25]. This appraisal-reappraisal cycle emphasizes the dynamic nature of stress management, underscoring cognitive flexibility’s role in adaptive coping [26].
The application of this theory within the psychology of climate change has attracted significant scholarly attention. On one hand, cognitive appraisal of climate change significantly affects individuals’ emotions and coping strategies. It was demonstrated that climate change exposure influences individuals’ primary coping and worry emotional responses through risk appraisal. Notably, during the reappraisal stage, collective efficacy was shown to further positively modulate worry emotions, particularly among women [27]. However, some studies have found that the assessment of climate change stressors serves as a more robust predictor of distress related to climate change. Participants who perceived these stressors as highly problematic experienced greater distress [28]. On the other hand, research has begun to explore coping strategies employed in response to the stress associated with contemplating climate change threats. Different coping mechanisms, such as avoidance, denial, and cognitive restructuring, have been found to function at various stages of stress exposure, resulting in distinct behavioral outcomes [29]. Moreover, the impact of different coping strategies on psychological well-being has been found to vary. An empirical study focused on Swedish children and examined the use of three coping strategies: problem-focused coping, emotion-focused coping, and meaning-focused coping, in response to the threat of climate change. It was found that positive reappraisal, a meaning-focused coping strategy, more effectively regulates emotional responses and enhances individual satisfaction and psychological well-being [30].
The United Nations International Strategy for Disaster Reduction (UNISDR) conceptualizes climate change awareness as the process of disseminating information to the general public, enhancing risk perception, and promoting hazard mitigation behaviors [31]. This definition has been expanded to describe CCAw as the outcome of three interacting mechanisms: knowledge, experience and attention [32]. Some researchers have broadened CCAw to include the creation of knowledge, development of values, changes in attitudes, and the cultivation of skills related to climate change in individuals and groups [33]. In this study, CCAw refers to an individual’s ability to cognitively engage with climate change information and convert it into psychological awareness and behavioral intentions, encompassing cognitive, emotional, and conative dimensions [11].
Existing research on CCAw predominantly focuses on adults rather than adolescents [34]. However, compared to adults, adolescents exhibit significant differences in terms of information sources, behavioral engagement, and emotional responses [35]. Adolescents are especially susceptible to the effects of climate change, and their awareness regarding climate change willingness to take action evolves with age [36]. Other research has identified gender differences as an important predictor of CCAw, with females generally exhibiting higher levels of CCAw than males [37]. Meanwhile, a significant change in CCAw affects both public perception and adolescents’ behavior. While increased awareness of climate change’s harmful impacts may elicit negative emotions in adolescents [38], social cognitive theory suggests that human factors and environmental influences interactively shape behavior [39]. CCAw enhances the recognition of collective norms, which boosts self-efficacy and increases adolescents’ willingness to engage in climate action [40], particularly when associated costs are low, promoting “green” behaviors like energy conservation [41].
Current measures of CCAw predominantly classify dimensions based on cognitive, emotional, and conative aspects of attitudes [42]. For instance, CCAw is often divided into five aspects: concern about climate change, climate change knowledge, beliefs and issue involvement, climate change–related behavior, and policy preferences [43]. In recent years, a significant contribution is the Climate Change Awareness Scale developed by Kuthe [11], which includes five dimensions: multiplicative actions, knowledge, personal concern, attitude, and climate-friendly behavior. This scale effectively differentiates between groups based on their knowledge, attitudes, and behaviors concerning climate change, and has gained widespread recognition and application [44].
CCAn was first broadly defined as “a psychological response to the negative changes in the state of the Earth”, classifying it as a “psychoterratic” syndrome [45]. Based on this definition, the American Psychological Association explains CCAn as “a chronic fear of environmental doom” [12], and others characterize it in young individuals as the diverse array of emotions and cognitive responses triggered by distressing news regarding our planet and the environment [46]. Recently, CCAn is considered as a clinically significant form of anxiety related to climate change that has the potential to undermine human well-being and functioning [47]. Within this study, CCAn refers to the persistent psychological stress experienced by individuals due to the recognition of the irreversible impacts of climate change and its extensive threats to both human and natural environments [48].
Adolescence is a critical developmental period for interest in global environmental issues, during which adolescents are especially vulnerable to the negative impacts of CCAn, more so than adults [49]. This chronic stress can lead to permanent changes in brain structure, increasing the risk of psychiatric disorders such as depression, anxiety, and substance use later in life [50]. Therefore, it is essential to focus on CCAn among adolescents to mitigate these long-term mental health risks. Existing research indicates a direct correlation between age and the degree of CCAn [51], with older adolescents exhibiting more CCAn than younger children [52]. Recent studies have highlighted the factors influencing CCAn, identifying climate concern, information seeking, nature relatedness, experience and age as the most influential [53]. Another study highlighted the significance of pro-environmental intentions, denial of guilt, satisfaction with capability, and satisfaction with relatedness in understanding differences in CCAn [19]. Meanwhile, CCAn significantly affects adolescents’ mental health and behavior. Quantitative analyses have revealed a moderate negative correlation between CCAn and mental well-being [54]. Numerous studies have linked CCAn to mental health issues such as depression, stress, anxiety and insomnia [55]. While CCAn can lead to cognitive and functional impairments, it may also trigger adaptive responses. Mediation models have shown that CCAn-induced cognitive impairment can directly promote pro-environmental behavior [56].
CCAn has garnered significant research attention, with numerous studies dedicated to exploring how to measure its levels. Some measurement items focused on anxiety about the negative impacts of climate change on various groups [8,30]. Another approach measured CCAn across three dimensions: frequent worry, anxiety about the future, and difficulty sleeping [52]. Notably, Clayton and Karazsia developed the Climate Change Anxiety Scale and identified a four-factor structure through exploratory factor analysis: behavioral engagement, personal experience, cognitive-emotional impairment and functional impairment [47]. The scale focuses on CCAn itself and provided the first operationalization of CCAn, which has since been widely adopted and recognized [57].
Current research indicates that CCAw can help alleviate CCAn. For instance, a significant negative correlation between CCAw and CCAn was observed in a large-scale German study, controlling for demographic and personality variables [16]. This finding aligns with research on American college students, which revealed that higher CCAw correlates with increased pro-environmental behaviors aimed at reducing CCAn [58]. Conversely, Norwegian research identified a subset of students who downplayed climate change severity to alleviate anxiety [59]. Furthermore, regarding the impact of CCAn on CCAw, research indicates that moderate CCAn can paradoxically reduce CCAw when individuals perceive their actions as inadequate. This perceived inefficacy may lead to helplessness, potentially escalating into severe anxiety that further diminishes CCAw and impedes pro-environmental behavior [60]. Currently, more research has found that CCAn is no longer an impediment to CCAw but a “facilitator” of it. Adaptive CCAn has been shown in numerous studies to be positively correlated with CCAw [61] and can further motivate climate action, including group efforts and personal mitigation strategies [62]. Nevertheless, while the relationship between CCAw and CCAn has received attention, its bidirectional interaction, particularly among adolescents, remains underexplored. Considering the crucial role of adolescents’ psychological reactions in addressing the climate crisis, further research on this interaction is essential.
Stress and Coping Theory provides a useful framework for understanding the relationship between CCAw and CCAn (Fig.1). According to this theory, the direct or indirect impacts of climate change serve as ongoing stressors. When encountering these stressors, individuals undergo primary appraisal by recognizing or perceiving the potential threats posed by climate change to themselves and their environment, thus forming an initial CCAw. Subsequently, individuals evaluate their resources and capabilities to cope with climate change threats and refine their cognition regarding climate change. After undergoing cognitive appraisal, there remain individual differences in climate change awareness. Those who believe in their own or collective institutions’ ability to effectively tackle climate change challenges through specific actions are more inclined to adopt problem-focused coping [63]. However, for adolescents, climate change represents a challenge of such overwhelming scale that individual action often feels futile. Consequently, an exclusive reliance on problem-focused coping strategies may result in reduced well-being and heightened anxiety. In the absence of clear solutions, emotional coping mechanisms are likely to prevail [63,64]. Research indicates that cognitive appraisal of climate change stressors is a significant predictor of CCAn, which subsequently drives the adoption of various emotion-focused coping mechanisms, such as “disengagement” [28]. To mitigate CCAn, individuals, particularly adolescents, often engage in proactive climate information-seeking, enhancing their awareness and understanding [57]. A UK survey demonstrated that youth with higher climate anxiety levels are more prone to seek climate change information [53]. This process further elevates individuals’ CCAw, initiating a new cycle of climate stressor appraisal. Therefore, based on the Stress and Coping Theory, this study hypothesizes that the relationship between CCAw and CCAn forms a positive feedback loop, whereby adolescents’ climate change awareness and climate change anxiety mutually reinforce each other in a positive cyclical relationship.

The conceptual model of the research
Based on the literature review, we have identified several areas in previous studies that require further exploration. On one hand, existing research often lacks focus on the variability of CCAw and CCAn, as well as the developmental differences brought about by gender differences [65,66]. On the other hand, most existing studies use cross-sectional designs to infer the unidirectional relationship between CCAw and CCAn [67], with little attention given to their reciprocal influence. Moreover, the limited longitudinal studies primarily focus on adults, with insufficient research on adolescents [53,68]. Therefore, the purpose of the present study is to examine the directional relationship between CCAw and CCAn using a three-wave cross-lagged model among Chinese adolescents. In addition, we will explore the developmental differences in CCAw and CCAn while controlling for gender. Based on literature review, theoretical frameworks, and research objectives, we propose the following hypotheses: (1) CCAw significantly positively influences CCAn; (2) CCAn significantly positively influences CCAw.
We used the cluster convenient sampling method to select adolescents (ages 16–18) from eastern China. The study was conducted at three time points: April 2022 (Time 1, T1), April 2023 (Time 2, T2) and April 2024 (Time 3, T3). Through the longitudinal study, a cross-lagged model was developed to explore the reciprocal predictive relationship between CCAn and CCAw among Chinese adolescents. At Time 1, a total of 1,368 participants were involved, including 644 males (47.1%) and 724 females (52.9%). At Time 2, there were 1,353 participants, with 634 males (46.9%) and 719 females (53.1%). At Time 3, there were 1,228 participants, with 561 males (45.7%) and 667 females (54.3%). By matching student ID across all three waves, we identified 140 participants who failed to complete all three survey waves due to various reasons, and 564 participants who had data matching issues due to missing or incorrectly recorded student ID. Furthermore, during data cleaning, we excluded 238 participants who failed validity check items embedded within the questionnaires. Finally, we obtained 426 valid paired questionnaires were obtained, with 189 completed by males (attrition rate = 70.7%) and 237 completed by females (attrition rate = 67.2%).
Three waves of surveys were conducted using paper-based questionnaires, adhering to the principles of the Research Ethics Committee of Zhejiang Normal University, and with the consent of the adolescents themselves, their parents, and their schools. Prior to the commencement of the tests, participants were informed about the study’s purpose, the voluntary nature of their involvement, and the process for withdrawing from the survey.
We applied the Climate Change Awareness Scale designed by Alina Kuth, which comprises five dimensions: Multiplicative Action, Climate-friendly Behavior, Knowledge, Attitude, and Personal Concern, with a total of 21 items [11]. Examples of items include “It is my responsibility to act in a climate-friendly manner”. This scale employs a six-point Likert scale ranging from 1 to 6, with higher scores indicating greater CCAw. In this study, the Cronbach’s α for the scale was 0.885 (T1), 0.874 (T2) and 0.849 (T3). The structural validity of the first measurement was χ2/df = 2.512, CFI = 0.947, TLI = 0.920, RMSEA = 0.035, SRMR = 0.013; the structural validity of the second measurement was χ2/df = 2.745, CFI = 0.954, TLI = 0.942, RMSEA = 0.042, SRMR = 0.022; the structural validity of the third measurement was χ2/df = 1.953, CFI = 0.918, TLI = 0.936, RMSEA = 0.037, SRMR = 0.032. These results confirm that the scale exhibits good reliability and validity among adolescent populations [69].
The Climate Change Anxiety Scale designed by Clayton and Karazsia was used in this study, which is divided into two dimensions: cognitive-emotional impairment and functional impairment, comprising a total of 7 items [47]. Examples of items include “I find myself crying because of climate change”. This scale is scored on a range from 1 (never) to 7 (always), with higher scores indicating greater CCAn. In this study, the Cronbach’s α for the scale was 0.902 (T1), 0.887 (T2) and 0.891 (T3). The structural validity of the first measurement was χ2/df = 2.008, CFI = 0.985, TLI = 0.973, RMSEA = 0.048, SRMR = 0.414; the structural validity of the second measurement was χ2/df = 1.712, CFI = 0.915, TLI = 0.901, RMSEA = 0.040, SRMR = 0.044; the structural validity of the third measurement was χ2/df = 2.052, CFI = 0.932, TLI = 0.961, RMSEA = 0.045, SRMR = 0.028. Studies on Chinese samples have confirmed that the scale exhibits good reliability and validity among Chinese adolescent populations [67].
First, common method biases in our data were assessed using one-way analysis of variance. To ensure the stability of the data measured at three time points, we examined the measurement invariance of the CCAw and CCAn scales, specifically considering invariance across time points and gender. Secondly, we examined CCAn and CCAw at T1, T2, and T3 using descriptive statistical analysis and Pearson correlation coefficients. SPSS 27.0 was employed to analyze stability and gender differences in CCAn and CCAw using repeated measures ANOVA. Third, a cross-lagged model was developed for the two variables using Mplus 7.4 to further validate their relationships. Additionally, to precisely identify the bidirectional dynamic mechanisms, we computed feedback effects by multiplying the corresponding cross-lagged path coefficients at each stage (T1 to T2, and T2 to T3), quantifying the dynamic interplay between CCAn and CCAw over time [70]. This approach provides a more reliable dynamic model to support the design of future climate change psychology interventions for adolescents.
Harman’s single-factor test was employed to thoroughly examine the inherent common method bias in the threedata sets, ensuring the validity of the study [71]. The results indicated that across the three measurements, the first factor accounted for only 26.234%, 28.342%, and 25.875% of the variability, all below the critical threshold of 40%, suggesting that no significant biases were present in our data.
First, we conducted an analysis of the measurement invariance of the CCAw and CCAn scales across different time points. Based on previous studies, ΔCFI < 0.02 and ΔRMSEA < 0.03 indicated that comparable measurement structures across time were present for the variables [72]. According to the fit indices, configural, metric, and scalar invariance were all established (Table1).

In ensuring that gender differences do not affect the accuracy of measurement tools, a 3*2 repeated measures ANOVA was conducted with CCAn as the dependent variable, measurement time (T1, T2 and T3) as the within-subjects variable, and gender (male, female) as the between-subjects variable (Fig.2). The results indicated a significant main effect of measurement time (F(1, 424)= 8.820,P< 0.05), suggesting that adolescents’ CCAn is unstable, with significantly higher anxiety levels at both T3 to T2 and T2 compared to T1. There was also a significant main effect of gender (F(1, 424)= 4.196,P< 0.05), with female adolescents showing notably greater levels of CCAn than male adolescents. Furthermore, the interaction between measurement time and gender was significant (F(1, 424)= 4.382,P< 0.05), indicating that both male and female adolescents showed an increase in CCAn from T1 to T2, with the increase being more pronounced in female adolescents. When considering CCAw as the dependent variable (under the same conditions as above), a 3*2 repeated measures ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of measurement time (F(1, 424)= 16.769P< 0.05). This result indicates that adolescents’ CCAw is not stable, with significantly higher levels of awareness at both T3 to T2 and T2 compared to T1. Additionally, there was a significant main effect of gender (F(1, 424)= 8.651,P< 0.05), with female adolescents exhibiting significantly higher levels of CCAw than their male counterparts. However, the interaction between measurement time and gender was not significant (F(1, 424) = 0.001,P> 0.05), indicating that the change in awareness over time did not differ between genders.

We used Mplus 7.4 to construct a cross-lagged model and analyze the reciprocal relationship between CCAn and CCAw across three time points (Fig.3). After controlling gender, the model with all cross-lagged paths, auto-regressive paths, and concurrent covariance demonstrated an acceptable fit to the data, χ²/df = 2.671(2 < χ²/df < 5), CFI = 0.915(≥ 0.8), TLI = 0.921(≥ 0.8), RMSEA = 0.042(< 0.05), SRMR = 0.035(< 0.05). From T1 to T2, both CCAn and CCAw were highly stable, with standardized autoregressive path coefficients of 0.344 (p< 0.001) and 0.243 (p< 0.001), respectively. Similarly, from T2 to T3, CCAn and CCAw continued to exhibit high stability, with standardized autoregressive path coefficients of 0.169 (p< 0.01) and 0.217 (p< 0.001), respectively. In the cross-lagged paths, (T1) CCAn significantly positively predicted (T2) CCAw (β = 0.208,p< 0.001), and (T1) CCAw significantly positively predicted (T2) CCAn (β = 0.225,p< 0.001). Most previous studies on the relationship between CCAw and CCAn have adopted a cross-sectional design and failed to draw conclusions regarding the trends and reciprocal relationships between the two. Grounded in Stress and Coping Theory, this study utilized three waves of longitudinal data on Chinese adolescents to examine the trends and reciprocal relationship between CCAw and CCAn, providing deeper insights into climate change psychology and climate change education.
This study found that adolescent CCAw exhibits developmental characteristics, consistent with previous research [37]. Multiple studies have shown that as adolescents mature, they acquire more climate change knowledge and experience, leading to increased beliefs, concerns, and action tendencies regarding climate change [73,74]. This developmental trend is closely linked to the increasing incorporation of climate change education into Chinese education systems, where both national initiatives and school-based curricula have progressively enhanced adolescents’ awareness of climate change [75]. The results also indicated a significant gender effect on CCAw, with females showing significantly higher awareness than males, which aligns with existing research findings [37]. Females generally tend to seek and accept more information, possess greater scientific knowledge, and express higher concern about climate change [76]. Moreover, they are disproportionately affected by environmental risks, such as droughts, floods, and heatwaves, and are more vulnerable to climate impacts [77]. This study also confirmed the developmental trajectory of adolescent CCAn, consistent with previous findings [13]. The ongoing rise in anxiety can be explained by the stress-vulnerability health model, where climate change functions as a chronic environmental stressor, exacerbating adolescents’ biological vulnerability and limited psychosocial resources, resulting in a significant increase in CCAn [78]. In recent decades, China has experienced a consistent rise in temperatures, accompanied by an increased likelihood of extreme weather events [79]. These climatic shifts likely amplify adolescents’ perception of climate change risks. And media coverage of climate change may also further intensify adolescents’ emotional responses [80]. Furthermore, a notable interaction between time and gender was identified, with females exhibiting higher anxiety at three time points, corroborating results from other regions [81,82]. This gender disparity may arise from females’ heightened perception of climate change’s negative impacts, highlighting the need for targeted psychological interventions [66].
Cross-lagged panel analyses revealed a bidirectional reinforcing relationship between CCAw and CCAn, further corroborating the study’s hypothesis and aligning with the Stress and Coping Theory. According to this theory, climate change, as a continuous source of stress, triggers two processes in adolescents: cognitive appraisal and coping responses, which in turn lead to the formation of specific coping strategies and psychological reactions [21].
Specifically, this study demonstrated that CCAw significantly predicts CCAn in a positive direction, corroborating earlier findings [59,83]. According to the Stress and Coping Theory, in the primary appraisal stage, the clearer adolescents perceive the potential threats posed by climate change, the more likely they are to experience anxiety corresponding to these threats at a psychological level [16]. This aligns with the “motivated attention framework”, which posits that CCAw is shaped by an attention selection mechanism modulated by individuals’ motivations and beliefs. When individuals recognize the reality of climate change, they actively direct their visual attention toward “more threatening” climate evidence, which in turn triggers stronger negative emotions such as anxiety [84]. Furthermore, the process further substantiates the role of CLT in shaping psychological reactions to climate change. According to CLT, when climate change is perceived as a distant threat, it is represented abstractly, and individuals may not experience urgent anxiety as its impacts seem unrelated to their current lives [85]. A community survey indicated that some individuals perceive climate change as a distant issue, both geographically and temporally, thereby reducing their CCAn [83]. However, once the impacts of climate change are perceived as psychologically proximal, individuals’ cognitive representations of the issue become more concrete. They are more likely to perceive it as a personal and immediate threat, which intensifies CCAn and strengthens their pro-environmental intentions [86]. Yet, some studies suggest that CLT may oversimplify the psychological processes underlying climate change. When the direct effect of distance on personal relevance is assumed, or when perceived distance is conceptualized as a relatively stable personal belief, reducing psychological distance does not necessarily increase engagement [87]. This further underscore the complexity involved in the generation and development of psychological responses to climate change.
Meanwhile, this study also found that CCAn significantly and positively predicts CCAw, which is consistent with previous research findings [61]. In the secondary appraisal stage of the Stress and Coping Theory, adolescents assess whether they have sufficient resources to cope with climate-related threats and choose appropriate coping strategies. From the perspective of perceptual neuroscience, CCAn enhances adolescents’ sensitivity to “threat-related cues” in the environment, creating a perceptual amplification effect [88]. When experiencing moderate CCAn, they tend to actively seek relevant information to alleviate the stress caused by uncertainty. This aligns with the principles of information-seeking theory [89]. Prior research suggests that such adaptive anxiety responses can strengthen adolescents’ green self-identity, motivating them to increase their knowledge and engagement with climate change issues [90]. However, when anxiety exceeds a certain threshold, it may develop into excessive or unhelpful forms, placing an emotional burden on adolescents that surpasses their available coping resources and strategies. This can lead to negative emotional responses or avoidant behaviors, ultimately weakening their cognitive engagement and proactive concern for climate change [91].These dynamics highlight the complexity and fluidity inherent in the “stress–appraisal–coping” mechanism emphasized by Stress and Coping Theory. They suggest that the strength of the relationship between anxiety and awareness may be moderated by the availability and effectiveness of coping resources during the secondary appraisal stage, warranting close attention to its evolving trajectory [24].
Furthermore, this study incorporated the estimation of feedback effects within the framework of a cross-lagged panel model, providing deeper insight into the reciprocal dynamics between CCAw and CCAn across time points [70]. The results revealed that the feedback effects between CCAw and CCAn were significant during both the T1–T2 and T2–T3 intervals, indicating a sustained, bidirectional psychological feedback mechanism. This enduring interaction further substantiates the progressive structure proposed in the Stress and Coping Theory: moderate anxiety stimulates information-seeking and engagement behaviors that elevate awareness; in turn, heightened awareness amplifies threat perception, intensifying emotional responses and promoting pro-environmental coping strategies [26]. This process illustrates a dynamic trajectory of “appraisal–emotion–reappraisal–coping” [24]. Compared to merely reporting unidirectional predictive paths between variables, the introduction of feedback effects in cross-lagged models allows for the precise identification of bidirectional dynamic mechanisms, while controlling for the stability of the variables themselves. This methodological advantage not only enhances the explanatory power of the relationships between variables in this study but also provides more reliable dynamic model support for the design of future climate psychology intervention strategies for adolescents [87].
This study not only deepens the understanding of adolescents’ psychological reactions to climate change, but also provides practical guidance for public health researchers and climate change educators. Firstly, gender differences were observed in the study of CCAw and CCAn, with female adolescents exhibiting significantly higher levels of both compared to male adolescents. Therefore, researchers and educators should develop differentiated educational and psychological support strategies tailored to different genders to address gender-specific psychological reactions to climate change [92]. Additionally, the study indicates that the bidirectional reinforcement between CCAw and CCAn creates a positive feedback loop. To effectively manage this cycle, educators should apply the framework of the Stress and Coping Theory by guiding adolescents through accurate cognitive appraisal. This involves primary appraisal, where adolescents assess the perceived threats posed by climate change, and secondary appraisal, where they evaluate their personal resources and ability to cope with these threats. Also, mental health researchers should collaborate with educators to design assessment tools that accurately measure adolescents’ psychological condition. Once this foundation is established, a balanced approach should be promoted to coping strategies. Problem-focused coping could be encouraged by engaging adolescents in local environmental projects, which not only provides them with a tangible sense of agency but also fosters a sense of efficacy in addressing climate challenges [7]. Simultaneously, emotion-focused coping should be cultivated through mindfulness practices and emotional regulation techniques, enabling adolescents to manage their anxiety constructively [93]. This integrated approach ensures that climate anxiety is transformed into constructive motivation, allowing adolescents to engage proactively with climate issues while developing psychological resilience and maintaining emotional well-being [94]. Throughout this process, adolescents’ CCAw is continually reinforced, further enhancing their capacity to address environmental challenges effectively.
This study advances understanding of adolescent CCAw and CCAn, but certain limitations should be noted. Firstly, the reliance on self-reported data makes it vulnerable to social desirability and participant biases [95,96]. Subsequent studies could verify these findings by gathering data from alternative sources, such as guardians and teachers, and by employing operational experiments to assess adolescents’ psychological responses to climate change. Secondly, this study involved only three waves of longitudinal data, which constrains our capacity to assess the temporal stability and underlying mechanisms of CCAw and CCAn over time. Therefore, further research is necessary to collect more waves of data to better understand the intrinsic mechanisms of climate change psychology. Thirdly, potential mediating and moderating variables may influence the relationship between awareness and anxiety in this study. Future research should broaden its scope to consider individual factors such as psychological resilience, environmental factors like current weather conditions, and societal factors such as climate change policies. Fourthly, it is challenging to control for adolescents’ access to climate change information under the current study conditions, and we acknowledge that as adolescents’ levels of reading and comprehension increase, it can affect their judgments and responses to climate change, which may influence the findings of the study. However, the main purpose of this study was to explore the complex relationship between CCAw and CCAn and was not concerned with causal inference, and more powerful research tools will be considered in the future to eliminate interference. Fifthly, this study was conducted with only a relatively small sample of Chinese adolescents. Subsequent research could increase the sample size and perform cross-cultural comparisons to enhance the generalizability of the findings [97]. Furthermore, an additional limitation of this study is due to the lack of pre-registration. Although the research protocol and data collection procedures were reviewed and approved by the institutional ethics committee prior to implementation, the analytic plan was not registered in a public repository. This lack of pre-registration may raise concerns regarding analytical flexibility and potential researcher degrees of freedom [98]. In light of the increasing emphasis on open science practices, future longitudinal studies should incorporate pre-registration to enhance transparency, mitigate bias, and improve the robustness of scientific claims.
Guided by Stress and Coping Theory, the longitudinal study employed a three-wave cross-lagged panel model to examine the reciprocal relationship between CCAw and CCA among Chinese adolescents. The findings revealed significant developmental trends and gender differences in both constructs and confirmed a bidirectional facilitative association: a heightened perception of threat increased CCAn, which in turn activated adaptive coping responses that further strengthened CCAw. This cyclical feedback loop, supported by significant cross-lagged effects, illustrates the dynamic interplay of cognition and emotion as outlined in Stress and Coping Theory. It offers both a robust theoretical foundation and empirical support for understanding the complex psychological responses adolescents exhibit in the face of climate change. From an applied perspective, the findings highlight the value of integrative interventions, such as implementing gender-sensitive climate change education programs and involving adolescents in local environmental initiatives. These strategies enhance climate literacy while facilitating emotional regulation, thereby alleviating negative emotions induced by climate change and strengthening adolescents’ psychological resilience to long-term environmental stress.
