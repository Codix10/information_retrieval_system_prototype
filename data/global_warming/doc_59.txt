The current consensus predicts warming trends with more pronounced temperature changes in winter than in summer (i.e., asymmetric winter warming), and such warming implications on microbial physiological processes (e.g., growth) and soil carbon storage remain elusive. Microbial carbon use efficiency (CUE), the proportion of metabolized organic carbon allocated to microbial biomass, is an important driver of soil carbon storage. Using stable isotope techniques, we investigated changes in microbial growth and CUE under different warming conditions. We found that both warming manipulations reduced microbial growth rate and CUE, with asymmetric winter warming exerting a greater impact than symmetric year-round warming. This indicated that simulations of year-round equal warming may underestimate the consequences of global climate change.
Keywords:winter warming, year-round warming, microbial CUE, qSIP, bacterial growth
Asymmetric seasonal warming trends are evident across terrestrial ecosystems, with winter temperatures rising more than summer ones. Yet, the impact of such asymmetric seasonal warming on soil microbial carbon metabolism and growth remains poorly understood. Using18O isotope labeling, we examined the effects of a decade-long experimental seasonal warming on microbial carbon use efficiency (CUE) and growth in alpine grassland ecosystems. Moreover, the quantitative stable isotope probing with18O-H2O was employed to evaluate taxon-specific bacterial growth in these ecosystems. Results show that symmetric year-round warming decreased microbial growth rate by 31% and CUE by 22%. Asymmetric winter warming resulted in a further decrease in microbial growth rate of 27% and microbial CUE of 59% compared to symmetric year-round warming. Long-term warming increased microbial carbon limitations, especially under asymmetric winter warming. Long-term warming suppressed the growth rates of most bacterial genera, with asymmetric winter warming having a stronger inhibition on the growth rates of specific genera (e.g.,Gp10,Actinomarinicola,Bosea,Acidibacter, andGemmata) compared to symmetric year-round warming. Bacterial growth was phylogenetically conserved, but this conservation diminished under warming conditions, primarily due to shifts in bacterial physiological states rather than the number of bacterial species and community composition. Overall, long-term warming escalated microbial carbon limitations, decreased microbial growth and CUE, with asymmetric winter warming having a more pronounced effect. Understanding these impacts is crucial for predicting soil carbon cycling as global warming progresses.
Climate change results in asymmetric seasonal warming, notably greater winter temperature increases compared to summer in high-altitude regions (1,2). The Tibetan Plateau, a prime example, has experienced significant winter warming that exceeds summer temperature increases (1,3). This region holds approximately 30 to 40 Gt of soil organic carbon (SOC), which constitutes 2 to 3% of the global SOC reserve (4). Evidence from soil warming experiments and global datasets indicates increased SOC decomposition rates under warming conditions (5,6). Soil microbial activity plays a crucial role in the terrestrial carbon (C) cycle (7), as soil microorganisms drive global element cycles through their metabolic activities (8). Most studies, however, have focused on symmetric year-round warming (9–11), leaving the specific effects of asymmetric winter warming on soil microbial activity underexplored.
The acquisition, storage, and release of C and nutrients by soil microorganisms are essential for the functioning of terrestrial ecosystems (12,13). Microbial carbon use efficiency (CUE) is a critical determinant of SOC accumulation (14). Changes in abiotic and biotic factors induced by warming can alter the allocation of metabolized C between microbial growth and respiration, thereby affecting microbial CUE (10,15). Long-term warming has been shown to increase soil enzyme activity (16), accelerate SOC turnover, and decrease soil C availability (17–19). Additionally, long-term warming disrupts plant life cycles, leading to earlier plant development, reduced above-ground biomass, and lower seed production (2,20,21). These changes collectively reduce the input of plant-derived C into the soil, potentially limiting microbial growth rates due to decreased substrate availability. Moreover, higher winter temperatures enhance microbial activity (22), deplete soil nutrients (9,10), and reduce nutrient availability for plants in subsequent growing seasons (15,23). Under such nutrient constraints, microbes prioritize C allocation for nutrient acquisition over growth, thus lowering CUE (9,24). Asymmetric winter warming, characterized by greater increases in soil temperatures during winter compared to symmetric year-round warming, has a more pronounced impact on microbial activity during winter and advances plant phenology to a greater extent (2). This exacerbates resource limitations for soil microbes in the subsequent growing season, potentially resulting in even lower CUE. Consequently, we hypothesize that microbial C and nutrient limitations are most acute under asymmetric winter warming, leading to the lowest microbial CUE, followed by symmetric year-round warming, and are least severe under ambient conditions.
Soil microorganisms adapt to survival, growth, and reproduction in response to their environment, and environmental changes, developing life history strategies (25). Given their high degree of diversity, closely related microbial taxa often display a wide range of ecological traits related to habitat preferences and metabolic activities (25,26). Growth, closely linked to ecological and evolutionary processes like resource utilization and metabolism, is a key indicator of microbial ecological strategies (27,28). The growth rate, influenced by many genes and genomic features, shows phylogenetic conservation (7,29). Microbial CUE varies with growth rate, suggesting phylogenetic constraints on this important physiological trait (30). However, due to the plasticity of microbial physiological and ecological traits (e.g., growth and metabolism), current or previous environmental exposures can modify these traits, influencing the observed phylogenetic conservation (31,32). For instance, extended exposure to drought or elevated atmospheric CO2levels can reduce the growth of certain soil microbes, altering their ecological responses and diminishing phylogenetic conservation (11,31). Emerging evidence indicates that prolonged warming can lead to some microbial environmental acclimation and adaptation, thereby reducing the phylogenetic conservation of microbial growth rates (32). However, the differential impacts of distinct warming patterns, such as symmetric year-round warming and asymmetric winter warming, on microbial growth and its phylogenetic characteristics remain unclear.
The Qinghai–Tibetan Plateau is undergoing asymmetric seasonal warming, notably in winter, due to global climate change (1,3). To understand the impact of climate change on soil ecosystems, it’s vital to study the effects of asymmetric winter warming on microbial growth and CUE. We utilized18O isotope labeling to assess microbial CUE and growth by tracking the integration of18O into newly synthesized DNA (30). Additionally, we employed quantitative stable isotope probing (qSIP) with18O-labeled water to quantify taxon-specific bacterial growth (33). We investigated how asymmetric winter warming affects microbial CUE and taxon-specific bacterial growth rates in soils from alpine grasslands exposed to a decade of symmetric year-round warming (2 °C above ambient), asymmetric winter warming (2.5 to 2.8 °C above ambient in the nongrowing season and 0.5 to 0.8 °C above ambient in the growing season), and normal temperatures. Considering that enzymatic activities indicate microbial metabolic needs under varying resource conditions (24,34), we used ecoenzymatic stoichiometry to study C and nutrient limitations caused by long-term warming, including both symmetric and asymmetric patterns. Our study aims to understand how different warming conditions affect phylogenetic signals in microbial communities and to identify specific populations impacting microbial CUE. This will enhance predictions of future responses of grassland soil ecosystems to global warming.
Our experimental warming treatment effectively increased soil temperatures as anticipated. Compared to ambient treatment, the symmetric year-round warming treatment elevated soil temperature by 1.4 °C and the asymmetric winter warming treatment increased it by 0.3 °C during the sampling month (SI Appendix, Table S1). Long-term warming reduced soil water content by 23% and decreased plant aboveground biomass by 19 to 20% relative to ambient conditions. No significant differences were observed in soil respiration, which includes both heterotrophic respiration from soil microbes and autotrophic respiration from roots, under field conditions (SI Appendix, Table S1).
Asymmetric winter warming significantly increased microbial respiration rate by 117% compared with ambient conditions (Fig. 1A). By contrast, symmetric year-round warming had no substantial impact on this rate. Long-term warming decreased microbial growth rate and CUE (Fig. 1BandC). Asymmetric winter warming had a more pronounced effect on these parameters than symmetric year-round warming. Specifically, symmetric year-round warming decreased the microbial growth rate by 31% and reduced microbial CUE by 22% compared to ambient conditions. Furthermore, asymmetric winter warming further decreased microbial growth rates by 27% and reduced microbial CUE by 59% compared to symmetric year-round warming. Symmetric year-round warming decreased microbial biomass C (MBC) by 17% compared to ambient conditions, while asymmetric winter warming had no substantial impact on MBC (SI Appendix, Table S1). The turnover time for MBC in asymmetric winter-warmed soils was approximately 198 d, significantly longer than the 108 d observed in ambient soils (Fig. 1D). Conversely, the turnover time for MBC in symmetric year-round warmed soils was similar to that of the ambient soils.

To elucidate the factors influencing microbial CUE, we performed correlation analyses between microbial CUE and various factors, including soil physicochemical properties, soil respiration, aboveground biomass, and microbial metabolic limitations (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Our results indicated that soil total P and aboveground biomass were positively correlated with microbial CUE, while microbial C limitation was negatively correlated with microbial CUE. Using the piecewiseSEM to further evaluate the drivers of microbial CUE (SI Appendix, Fig. S2), we found that soil C availability (soil total C and dissolved organic C) and microbial C limitation were the primary drivers of microbial CUE, followed by microbial P limitation. Water limitation was found to have a comparatively minor impact.
Based on qSIP results, we identified zero-radius Operational Taxonomic Units (zOTUs) that effectively incorporated18O into DNA [lower 95% CI of18O excess atom fractions (EAF-18O) > 0] as growing bacterial species (i.e., species in a state of reproduction) (Fig. 3). It is important to note that this method may not accurately detect slow-growing bacteria, as these taxa may have 95% CIs overlapping zero. Warming treatments resulted in a decline in both the proportion and number of growing bacterial species, with asymmetric winter warming causing a more significant reduction than symmetric year-round warming. In the ambient samples, out of 3172 zOTUs, 2821 (89%) were identified as growing species (Fig. 3A). Under symmetric year-round warming, 3233 zOTUs were found, with 796 (25%) growing species (Fig. 3B). Under asymmetric winter warming, 3151 zOTUs were identified, with 508 (16%) growing species (Fig. 3C). All (sub)phyla, except for Bacteroidetes, showed a consistent decline in both the proportion and number of growing bacterial species in response to warming. During asymmetric winter warming, Bacteroidetes represented a higher proportion and number of growing bacterial species compared to both ambient and symmetric year-round warming treatments.

We calculated cumulative absolute population growth rates by summing the growth rates of zOTUs identified as growing. It is noted that this way may exclude the slow-growing zOTUs, but it is more accurate to get the active growing zOTUs involved in the C utilization. Warming treatments led to a decrease in the cumulative growth rates of bacterial communities compared to ambient conditions (Fig. 4). Compared to ambient conditions, symmetric year-round warming decreased the absolute population growth rates (log-transformed) by 10%, and asymmetric winter warming decreased by 12%. The cumulative growth rates of all (sub)phyla declined after warming, except for the phylum Bacteroidetes.

Phylogenetic signal tests for growing taxa were conducted to assess the level of phylogenetic conservation in bacterial growth rates under ambient conditions, symmetric year-round warming, and asymmetric winter warming treatments (Fig. 4). Both Pagel’s λ and Blomberg’s K metrics indicated significant phylogenetic signal across all treatments (P< 0.05). Pagel’s λ values decreased from 0.997 under ambient conditions to 0.648 under asymmetric winter warming, suggesting decreasing phylogenetic conservation. Blomberg’s K values were consistently lower than 1, indicating that the similarity in growth rates among phylogenetically related taxa was less than expected by chance. The decline in phylogenetic signal with warming indicates that closely related microbes diverge in their growth responses as thermal regimes change.
Nested taxonomic linear mixed-effect models effectively fit growing bacterial growth rates under ambient conditions (P< 0.001), symmetric year-round warming (P< 0.001), and asymmetric winter warming (P< 0.001) conditions (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). In ambient soils, the genus was the most explanatory taxonomic level, accounting for 41% of the total variation in growth rates. However, under asymmetric winter warming, taxonomy as a whole had minimal explanatory power (25% of variation), while symmetric year-round warming represented an intermediate effect, with the genus still substantially influencing growth rates (explaining 38% of variation).
Growing bacterial growth responses to experimental warming were evaluated at the genus level, identified as the most informative taxonomic rank (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Overall, warming suppressed the growth rates of the vast majority of bacterial genera, whether it was asymmetric winter warming or symmetric year-round warming. Across various phyla, the majority of genera showed decreased growth rates, ranging from −5.5 to −0.5 log1016S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene copies g−1soil day−1under symmetric year-round warming, and from −5.9 to −0.4 under asymmetric winter warming. Specifically, asymmetric winter warming had a stronger inhibition on the growth rates of certain genera within Acidobacteria (6 out of 13 genera; namelyGp10,Gp17,Gp3,Gp5,Gp6,Gp7), Actinobacteria (8 out of 39 genera; namelyActinomarinicola,Agromyces,Blastococcus,Cellulomonas,Ilumatobacter,Mycobacterium,Pseudonocardia,Streptosporangium), Alphaproteobacteria (8 out of 27 genera; namelyBosea,Dongia,Mesorhizobium,Microvirga,Pedomicrobium,Phyllobacterium,Pseudorhodoplanes,Skermanella), Gammaproteobacteria (1 out of 9 genera; namelyAcidibacter), and Planctomycetes (2 out of 5 genera; namelyGemmataandPirellula), compared to symmetric year-round warming. However, noteworthy exceptions were observed in specific genera, such asGp25(Acidobacteria),Arthrobacter(Actinobacteria),Ohtaekwangia(Bacteroidetes), andParaflavitalea(Bacteroidetes). Symmetric year-round or asymmetric winter warming promotes the growth rates of these genera. Particularly,Arthrobacterdisplayed increased growth rates under both symmetric year-round and asymmetric winter warming.
We conducted an analysis to correlating per-capita growth rates with initial population density, derived from unfractionated DNA samples of zOTUs, to determine the density dependence of bacterial growth (Fig. 5). This sheds light on the role of ecological processes in shaping microbial communities. With increasing population densities, resource competition intensifies, potentially constraining population growth and influencing the ecological processes in microbial communities. We observed across bacterial taxa that per capita population growth rates decreased as population sizes grew, indicating a negative density dependence, a common phenomenon in population ecology. The relationship between per-capita growth rates and initial population density was markedly steeper in symmetric year-round warming (slope = 0.005) and asymmetric winter warming soils (slope = 0.006) than in ambient soils (slope = 0.004; the difference in slope:P< 0.05). This suggests that in warmer conditions, growing species face greater resource limitations.

The integration of18O isotope labeling, the ecoenzyme vector model, and H218O-DNA-qSIP analysis allowed us to explore the effects of soil warming on microbial C metabolism and bacterial growth in alpine grasslands. Experiments in an alpine meadow, with both symmetric year-round and asymmetric winter warming, showed that warming—particularly asymmetric winter warming—leads to microbial metabolic limitations, reducing microbial growth rates and CUE. Understanding the differential impacts of symmetric versus asymmetric soil warming is crucial for predicting future soil C cycling, as winters are expected to warm disproportionately.
Long-term warming decreased microbial growth rate and CUE (Fig. 1). Microbial C limitation was intensified by warming was the primary effect factor in microbial CUE (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). The enhanced microbial capacity to decompose recalcitrant C under warming conditions (35–37), which could threaten soil C stability and increase ecosystem respiration. Warming alleviates low-temperature constraints on enzymatic activity, accelerating soil microbial metabolism, stimulating SOC mineralization (10,38), depleting the soil labile C pool, and diminishing SOC quality (39,40). Although soil total C content showed no significant changes among ambient conditions, symmetric year-round warming, and asymmetric winter warming conditions (SI Appendix, Table S1), warming can alter the composition and configuration of SOC pools (41). The proportion of plant-derived SOC likely decreased, while microbially derived SOC increased (41), potentially reducing the availability of SOC substrates for microbes, despite unchanged total C stocks. It is worth noting that although soil dissolved organic C increased under warming conditions, at the same time warming reduced soil water content (SI Appendix, Table S1). Reduced soil moisture can limit microbial utilization of dissolved C and limit microbial utilization of this C. Furthermore, asymmetric winter warming had more pronounced effects on microbial C limitation and CUE than symmetric year-round warming (Fig. 1). Long-term warming accelerated the onset of spring phenology in alpine plants (2), increasing their vulnerability to frost damage and potential mismatches with pollinators, thereby reducing plant reproductive success (42). Asymmetric winter warming, in particular, demonstrated a greater capacity to advance the onset of spring phenology in alpine plants compared to symmetric year-round warming (2). This led to a reduction in aboveground biomass and a decline in plant-derived soil C inputs.
Microbial C limitation often coincides with pronounced P limitation (43). P is a critical constraint on microbial growth (44), stemming from soil P’s nonrenewable nature (45). Our results show that soil total P is positively correlated with microbial CUE (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Asymmetric winter warming negatively affected soil total P concentration more than symmetric year-round warming (SI Appendix, Table S1), imposing greater P limitations on microbes (Fig. 2). Consistent with a previous study, changes in total P were generally more significant under asymmetric winter warming than symmetric year-round warming (46). In P-limited conditions, microbial communities may divert more C resources to produce and excrete enzymes for P metabolism (47). Consequently, microbes allocate more C to maintenance rather than biomass production (9,24). Microbial CUE decreases as nutrient availability drops and substrate recalcitrance increases (48,49).
Under warming conditions, microbes experience more severe water limitation stress compared to ambient conditions (50). This increased water limitation restricts enzyme and substrate diffusion (51), ultimately constraining microbial growth and reproduction (52). Additionally, we observed stronger negative density dependence in warming soil compared to ambient conditions (Fig. 5). Negative density-dependent phenomena indicate that microbial growth is limited by resource (32). We further conclude that warming exacerbates resource limitations on microbial growth.
Warming acts as a deterministic factor, selecting environmental microorganisms and causing shifts in soil microbial community composition (53,54). Although the number of bacterial species and the composition of the bacterial community remained largely unchanged, the growth rate (as represented by the EAF-18O) of bacterial species was significantly influenced by warming (SI Appendix, Fig. S5), suggesting that warming primarily affected the bacterial physiological state rather than the number of bacterial species and community composition. Warming leads to a decrease in the number and proportion of growing bacteria, with more significant reductions observed during asymmetric winter warming compared to symmetric year-round warming (Fig. 3). Previous research delineates that short-term warming results in an increase in the number of growing bacteria, while long-term warming conditions lead to a decrease (55). This reduction in the number of growing cells under long-term warming conditions may be attributed to decreased soil connectivity resulting from water limitation (56). Long-term warming-induced decreases in C availability (Fig. 2) may have reduced the number of growing bacteria (57). Additionally, warming may hasten the senescence of microbial cells via increased protein turnover (58) while enhancing virus activity, thereby increasing the incidence of cellular demise (59), and possibly decreasing the number of growing microbes.
Most microbial traits typically exhibit phylogenetic conservatism, stemming from the prevalence of vertical gene inheritance in microbial evolution (60,61). Our findings indicate that similar microbial growth rates are phylogenetically clustered, indicating a conservation of genomic traits shaping these rates (60). However, this relationship weakens with increasing warming, especially under asymmetric winter warming conditions (Fig. 4). This suggests that the predictive power of phylogeny in microbial growth rates diminishes for soil bacterial communities experiencing warming. Due to the plasticity of microbial physiological traits, microorganisms experiencing distinct historical environmental pressures may alter their original physiological traits (31,32), leading to random distribution of these traits across the microbial phylogeny (11). In addition, our findings suggest that microbial C and P constraints are exacerbated under warming conditions (Fig. 2). During microbial metabolic limitations, closely related organisms might experience intensified competitive exclusion, further disrupting the expected pattern of phylogenetic conservatism (62).
Consistent with previous research (37), our results showed that the magnitude of the response to warming varied among bacterial genera, but the direction of the response—reduced growth—was universal (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). However, some genera, such asGp25(Acidobacteria),Arthrobacter(Actinobacteria),Ohtaekwangia(Bacteroidetes), andParaflavitalea(Bacteroidetes), displayed significant deviations, indicating a potential shift in microbial role in ecosystem functions (63). Remarkably, both warming conditions enhancedArthrobactergrowth rates, reflecting its adaptability (SI Appendix, Fig. S4).Arthrobacter, a member of the Actinobacteria phylum, has frequently been observed in high-altitude frozen regions including the Tibetan Plateau (64,65). Its diverse metabolic capabilities and resistance to various environmental stressors, such as extended cold exposure, oxidative stress, osmotic fluctuations, nutrient deprivation, and desiccation, contribute to its widespread presence in extreme environments (64,65). Warming led to an increased abundance ofArthrobacter(66), a key player in SOC decomposition and degradation of recalcitrant C (67,68), thereby significantly impacting biomass production and CO2emissions (69).
Previous study has suggested that during the nongrowing season, asymmetric winter warming significantly increases soil temperatures compared to symmetric year-round warming (2), thereby potentially mitigating cold stress and stimulating microbial growth, activity, and soil C decomposition (70). The efflux of CO2from soils to the atmosphere persists throughout the nongrowing season (e.g., winter), with recent measurements indicating that a significant portion, 60% or more, of C uptake during the growing season can be lost during this period (71,72). It is noteworthy that our study exclusively focused on soil C sequestration and microbial growth dynamics during the growing season. Future investigations are warranted to elucidate the impacts of diverse temperature regimes on microbial growth and C sequestration across distinct growing seasons. Additionally, while our study primarily focused on bacterial contributions to microbial CUE, the broader community-level CUE is influenced by various microorganisms, notably fungi. Given the distinct CUE dynamics between bacteria and fungi (73), future investigations should comprehensively explore fungal contributions to enhance our understanding of soil-atmosphere C exchange dynamics.
In conclusion, our study reveals that both symmetric year-round and asymmetric winter warming result in C limitations for soil microbes in alpine grasslands, decreasing microbial growth rates and CUE (Fig. 6). Asymmetric winter warming has a more pronounced effect than symmetric year-round warming in reducing the number and proportion of growing bacteria, decreasing growth rates across most major bacterial taxa, and intensifying negative-density dependence on microbial growth. This indicates that asymmetric winter warming imposes the greatest microbial C limitations. Additionally, there’s a noticeable decline in the phylogenetic conservation of microbial growth rates with increasing warming. These findings suggest that while soil warming generally reduces microbial CUE by increasing microbial metabolic limitations and constraining activity, asymmetric winter warming has the most substantial effect on slowing microbial growth rates and reducing CUE. Understanding these effects of asymmetric winter warming is vital for predicting soil C cycling as winters continue to warm. Future research should focus on strategies to mitigate nutrient constraints and maintain microbial CUE for sustained soil functioning.

The field experiment began in 2011 at the Qinghai Haibei National Field Research Station of the Alpine Grassland Ecosystem (101°19’E, 37°37’N, 3200 m.a.s.l.), characterized by a continental monsoon climate, an average annual temperature of −1.7 °C, and annual precipitation of 485 mm. The soil type is Mat–Gryic Cambisols with a clay loam texture.
The experiment involved three warming levels: ambient climate, symmetric year-round warming (2 °C above ambient), and asymmetric winter warming (2.5 to 2.8 °C above ambient in nongrowing season and 0.5 to 0.8 °C above ambient during the growing season). The nongrowing seasons commence when the 7 d smoothed average daily air temperature stays below 0 °C for five consecutive days. Conversely, growing seasons start when the temperature remains above 0 °C for the same duration (2). Experimental plots were established in an area of 50 × 110 m, using a randomized block design (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). The study included ambient temperature controls and a symmetric year-round warming treatment each with six replicate plots (each plot was 2.2 × 1.8 m), and an asymmetric winter warming treatment with five replicate plots. Each warming plot was equipped with two medium-wave heaters (220 V, 1,200 W, 1.0 m long, 0.22 m wide), while the ambient climate plot had two nonfunctional heaters. Soil cores (0 to 10 cm depth) were collected in triplicate from each plot at the end of the growing season (August 2020) and combined. As a result of higher soil temperatures, vigorous plant growth, and more growing microorganisms during the growing season compared to the nongrowing season (2,74,75), sampling during the growing season may be more convenient to capture differences in microbial growth and CUE under different warming patterns. The average soil temperatures (10 cm) in August for the three treatments were as follows: 13.3 °C for the ambient plots, 14.7 °C for the symmetric year-round warmed plots, and 13.6 °C for the asymmetric winter warmed plots. These fresh soil samples were promptly transported to the laboratory, where they were sieved through a 2-mm mesh. The soil properties are detailed inSI Appendix, Table S1. In situ soil respiration, which includes heterotrophic respiration from soil microbes and autotrophic respiration from soil roots, was measured using a Li-8100 automated soil CO2flux system (Li-Cor Inc.). The soil temperature in situ was measured using a thermocouple probe (LI-8100-203; Li-Cor Inc.), and soil moisture levels were measured using an automatic moisture sensor (Decagon Devices Inc.). The data collection was conducted every hour.
Soil microcosms were designed and subjected to incubation with18O-labeled and regular (unlabeled) H2O. Initially, ambient soils underwent a preincubation phase where their soil moisture was adjusted to 60% of their water-holding capacity and then maintained at 16 °C (the average temperature for the growth season) in the dark for a period of 7 d. By contrast, soils designated for year-round and winter warming experiments were preincubated at the same moisture level but at a slightly higher temperature of 18 °C (2 °C above the ambient soils) in the dark for the same duration. Following this, the ambient soils were air-dried on sterile trays for 24 h at 16 °C, while the warming soils underwent drying at 18 °C. This method of controlled drying at respective incubation temperatures was implemented to minimize the impact of the natural abundance of water (76). After drying, 2 g samples of each soil type were placed into 25 mL screw cap vials. These samples were then incubated for 48 h in the dark with either 400 μL of 98 atom%18O-H2O or natural abundance water, at their specific treatment temperatures (16 °C for ambient and 18 °C for warming soils). To obtain air control samples, three empty vials were sealed at each incubation temperature (16 °C and 18 °C). In total, 51 soil samples were collected, which included 17 soil samples at the 0 h mark (6 from ambient climate, 6 from symmetric year-round warming, and 5 from asymmetric winter warming) and 34 soil samples after a 48 h incubation period (six ambient climate, six symmetric year-round warming, and five asymmetric winter warming × two types of H2O addition).
Based on an estimation of microbial respiration and biomass growth, microbial CUE was determined by calculating the ratio of biomass growth to substrate uptake (77). Respiration flux was assessed based on the amount of CO2produced (Crespiration, µg C g−1soil h−1) over the incubation period. To collect headspace gas samples, 15 mL of gas was extracted from the closed vials every 2 h using a syringe. This was followed by a 15 min period of opening the vials to vent CO2and allow for re-equilibration, a process that was repeated throughout the 48 h incubation. The gas samples were then analyzed using a gas chromatograph (Agilent 7890, Santa Clara, CA, USA).
To calculate microbial biomass growth (Cgrowth, µg C g−1soil h−1), the amount of MBC produced during incubation was divided by the incubation period (30). A conversion factor was used to correlate soil DNA concentration with total soil MBC, facilitating the conversion of DNA production during incubation to MBC production. DNA was extracted from soils both before incubation and after 48 h using a FastDNA™ SPIN Kit (MP Biomedicals, Cleveland, OH, USA). DNA concentration was measured fluorometrically using a Qubit 4 fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific™, Waltham, MA, USA). After measurement, DNA samples were dried in silver capsules at 60 °C overnight. The samples were then analyzed for18O abundance and total oxygen content using elemental analyzer/isotope ratio mass spectrometry (Delta V Advantage, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Bremen, Germany) coupled with a Conflo III open-split system. Total MBC in the soils was quantified after preincubation using the chloroform fumigation extraction method, which involved a 24 h fumigation period. Organic C was extracted from both fumigated and nonfumigated soil samples using 0.5 M K2SO4and measured using an elemental analyzer (Multi N/C 3100 TOC, Analytik Jena, Germany). The total MBC was calculated employing a conversion factor of 2.22 (78).
Under the steady-state assumption, the amount of C taken up by the microbial biomass (Cuptake) was calculated as the sum ofCrespirationandCgrowth. Microbial CUE was then computed using the following formula: CUE =Cgrowth/Cuptake. Microbial biomass turnover time was calculated as MBC turnover time = MBC/Cgrowth.
Microbial metabolic limitation was assessed using vector lengths and angles of enzymatic activities (34). We measured activities of C (β-1,4-glucosidase), N (leucine aminopeptidase; β-1,4-N-acetylglucosaminidase), and P (phosphatase) acquisition enzymes fluorometrically (79). Specifically, 0.5 g of fresh soil was combined with 50 mL of 50 mM sodium acetate buffer, adjusted to the soil samples’ pH, and shaken at 200 rpm for 1 h at 25 °C. We then prepared 96-well plates with 200 μL of soil extract and 50 μL of a 200 μM specific enzyme substrate for sample wells, 50 μL of acetate buffer and 200 μL of extract for controls, and 50 μL of enzyme substrate in 200 μL of acetate buffer for blanks. Plates were incubated at 25 °C for 4 h before fluorescence was measured (365 nm excitation, 450 nm emission) using a Tecan Spark 10 M plate reader. Enzyme activity was expressed as nmol g−1soil h−1. We calculated ecoenzymatic vector length (unitless) and angle (degree) from the proportional enzymatic C: N and C: P ratios to determine relative microbial resource acquisition strategies (34). A longer vector length indicates greater C limitation, and vector angles of <45° indicate N limitation and angles of >45° indicate P limitation (34).
For DNA analysis, we selected three replicates from each treatment’s incubated soil for density gradient centrifugation and fractionation, as previously described (33), with minor modifications. Briefly, 3 μg DNA was added to CsCl gradient buffer (0.1 M Tris-HCl, 0.1 M KCl, 1mM EDTA, pH 8.0, initial density 1.718 g mL−1) in 5.1 mL Quick‐Seal polyallomer ultracentrifugation tubes (Beckman Coulter, Palo Alto, CA, USA). Tubes were centrifuged at 177,000 g for 72 h at 18 °C (VTi 65.2 Rotor, Beckman Coulter). After centrifugation, we partitioned the gradients into 20 equal fractions, each approximately 250 μL, by displacing the gradient medium with sterile water from the top of the tube using a syringe pump (Longer Pump, LSP01-1A, China). We measured the refractive index of 30 μL aliquots from each fraction using an AR200 digital handheld refractometer (Reichert, Inc., Buffalo, NY, USA), determining the buoyant density of each collected fraction. The CsCl-precipitated DNA from each fraction was precipitated by adding 500 μL of a 30% polyethylene glycol 6000 and 1.6 M NaCl solution, followed by a 1 h incubation at 37 °C and two washes with 70% ethanol. The DNA in each fraction was then reconstituted in 30 μL of Tris-EDTA buffer.
qPCR was employed to quantify bacterial 16S rRNA gene copies across all density gradient fractions and unfractionated 0 h soil DNA samples. Standard curves for absolute quantification were constructed through 10-fold serial dilutions of 16S rRNA gene amplicons produced via PCR using universal bacterial primers 515F (5′-GTG CCA GCM GCC GCG G-3′) and 907R (5′-CCG TCA ATT CMT TTR AGT TT-3′). qPCRs were carried out in 25 μL volumes containing the following components: 12.5 μL SYBR Premix Ex Taq 2× reaction buffer (TaKaRa Biotechnology, Otsu, Shiga, Japan), 0.5 μL each of 10 μM forward and reverse primers, 0.5 μL 50× ROX Reference Dye II, 1 μL template DNA (2 to 20 ng), and 10.5 μL nuclease-free water. Thermocycling conditions were as follows: 95 °C for 5 min, 40 cycles of 15 s at 95 °C, 34 s at 64 °C and a final temperature increase to 95 °C for 15 s. Data were collected after each annealing step. The average PCR efficiency was 103% and the average slope was −3.25, with all standard curves having R2≥ 0.98 (SI Appendix, Fig. S7).
Using the same primers as qPCR (515F/907R), DNA from 0 h samples (unfractionated) and fractionated DNA (densities between 1.703 and 1.727 g mL−1) that cumulatively contained more than 99% of the gene copy numbers of each sample were selected for high-throughput sequencing analysis. The DNA sequencing was performed using an Illumina MiSeq (2 × 250 bp) platform (Genesky Biotechnologies, Shanghai, China). Sequence data were analyzed by USEARCH (80). Briefly, the forward and reverse reads were merged, and the merged sequences were truncated to 370 bp and then low-quality sequences (quality score < 30) were filtered out. Quality reads were clustered into zOTUs at a 100% similarity threshold using UNOISE3. In this step, reads with sequencing errors were identified and corrected, and chimeras were removed. 16S rRNA gene reads were classified using the RDP classifier (V18) with a threshold of 0.8. As detection of archaeal zOTUs was negligible in these soils, we focused our analyses on bacterial zOTUs. We discarded any zOTUs that accounted for <0.005% of the total sequences and those that were classified as Archaea.
We estimated EAF-18O enrichment for each zOTU using calculations described previously (33). Briefly, the buoyant density of each zOTU’s 16S sequences in a CsCl buffer within a given replicate was weighted based on its relative abundance in each fraction. The molecular weights of the DNA were then calculated for each zOTU from both unlabeled and labeled samples, which were used to estimate the per-zOTU shift in buoyant density due to isotope incorporation. For all calculations, bootstrap resampling (999 iterations) of replicates within each treatment was used to generate uncertainty estimates of 95% CIs around the per-taxon EAF values. An zOTU was considered to have isotopic incorporation (growing microbe) if the lower 95% CIs of its EAF value were >0.
We determined the per-capita gross growth rates (gi, day−1) for each replicate through the application of an exponential growth model (32). Per-capita gross growth rates were used to estimate the production of new 16S rRNA gene copies per gram dry soil per day for each zOTU (Ni, absolute growth rate, 16S rRNA genes copies g−1soil day−1) (32,69). Cumulative absolute population growth rates for growing bacterial taxa (with 95% CIs of EAF-18O above zero) were calculated by summing the taxon-specific growth rates for zOTUs identified as growing within each bacterial taxon. All calculations were performed using the “qSIP” package (https://github.com/bramstone/qsip) in R (Version 4.1.2).
Cumulative growing absolute growth rates at phylum and genus levels were determined by summing taxon-specific growth rates for zOTUs within each taxon. Phylogenetic trees were created using PyNAST Alignment (81) and FastTree (82) in Galaxy/DengLab, and visualized with iTOL. We assessed phylogenetic clustering to examine variations in growing absolute growth rate among microbial species and phylogenetic groups, using Pagel’s λ (83) and Blomberg’s K (84) phylogenetic signal tests (using the “phytools” and “picante” packages). Linear mixed-effects models, with taxonomic levels as nested random effects, were employed to estimate the variance in growth rate explained by each taxonomic level. Variance components of each level were estimated using the “ape” package (85).
The Shapiro–Wilk test was used to assess data normality, and Bartlett’s test was used to assess homoscedasticity. Nonnormal or heteroscedastic data were log-transformed for analysis. Comparisons between climate conditions were conducted using LSD post hoc tests. Unpaired t-tests, adjusted by false discovery rate correction (86), were used to compare warming versus ambient conditions. Effects were considered significant atP< 0.05. To investigate density-dependent growth among growing microbes, we performed a robust analysis using simple linear regression, designating population density as the independent variable and the per capita growing absolute growth rate as the dependent variable. We compared slopes using the “diffslope” function (“simba” package) (87), which uses permutation testing (1,000 iterations) to computePvalues as the ratio of slope differences exceeding the initial slope difference over the number of permutations. The relationships between CUE and various factors, including soil physicochemical properties, soil respiration, above-ground biomass, and microbial metabolic limitations were analyzed by univariate linear regression (functionlm). We used piecewiseSEM to evaluate the drivers of microbial CUE, incorporating factors such as C availability (soil total C and dissolved organic C), P availability (Soil total P and available P), soil water content, microbial C limitation (vector length), and microbial P limitation (vector angle). All measured variables were consolidated into a “composite variable” before inclusion in the SEM. These analyses were performed using the “piecewiseSEM”, “nlme”, and “lme4” packages (88,89). We employed Fisher’sC-test (with 0.05 <P< 1.00) to validate the goodness of fit for the models. Subsequently, we refined our models based on significance (P< 0.05) and overall model fit. All analyses were conducted using R statistical software v.4.1.2.
