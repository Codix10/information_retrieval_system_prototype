Limited research has examined climate anxiety among older adults. Existing studies on climate anxiety and quality of life primarily address younger populations. These populations demonstrate greater adaptation and coping skills in response to environmental change. In contrast, older adults with chronic conditions experience increased vulnerability to climate-related stressors. They frequently display patterns of helplessness, anxiety, and avoidance.
This cross-sectional study recruited 424 older adults (mean age, 67.47; SD, 10.12; range, 60–92) with chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, cardiovascular disease ) from the community in Hangzhou, China, using convenience sampling. The study used validated instruments, including the Climate Anxiety Scale, the Coping with Climate Change Scale, and the Older Adults’ Quality of Life–Brief Scale. All instruments underwent translation and back-translation. They were also reviewed by experts and pilot-tested.
Quality of Life was 2.33 (± 0.58), Coping with Climate Change was 2.66 (± 0.37), and Climate Anxiety was 3.25 (± 0.46). The study confirmed an association between climate anxiety and quality of life. Coping with climate change mediated this relationship. Climate anxiety significantly influenced coping with climate change (B = 0.26,p< .001), explaining 11% of the variance. Both climate anxiety and coping with climate change significantly affected quality of life (B = 0.32 and 0.76,p< .001). These factors accounted for 39% of the variance. The total, direct, and indirect effects of coping with climate change were 0.51, 0.31, and 0.20, respectively.
Public health policymakers, community health service organizations, and clinical practitioners should work together to enhance the climate resilience of older adults. They can do this through individual skills training, increased community support, and the development of targeted policies.

As populations age, the quality of life (QoL) has become a central public health concern [1]. The WHO defines QoL as individuals’ perceptions of their position in life, shaped by culture, values, goals, expectations, and concerns. While chronic illness, mental health, and social support are well-known determinants of QoL, the environmental context is often under-considered [1,2]. Climate change introduces frequent extreme weather, poorer air quality, and water stress—conditions that disproportionately burden the health and daily functioning of older adults [3]. These exposures create not only physical risk but also psychological strain, which can ultimately erode QoL.
Building on these concerns, there has been growing interest in the impact of psychological stress resulting from environmental changes, particularly climate anxiety [4–8]. The American Psychological Association defines climate anxiety (CA) as a persistent emotional condition caused by concerns about ecological degradation. A survey in The Lancet Planetary Health, conducted across 10 countries with 10,000 teens, found that 59% were very concerned about climate change. Meanwhile, 45% said this fear affected their daily functioning [9]. However, studies on adolescents dominate the literature, while environmental anxiety in older adults has been largely neglected [10]. Few researchers have examined CA in older adults [9,11]. The 2020 National Survey on the Health of the Elderly in Japan found that over 25% of senior citizens often voiced concerns about the effects of harsh weather on their health [12]. While current studies recognize environmental anxiety in older persons, they often neglect the impact of psychological mechanisms on individual QoL [2,10,13]. Therefore, it is essential to examine the correlation between environmental anxiety and QoL in older adults.
To address this gap in the literature, it is essential to note that current research on climate anxiety (CA) and quality of life (QoL) primarily focuses on younger populations, who typically exhibit robust adaptation and coping skills in response to environmental change [14,15]. As a result, evidence regarding interventions for environmental anxiety in older adults remains limited [9,11,16]. Older adults are particularly susceptible to health risks and frequently experience reduced mobility. This population may perceive climate change as an immediate threat, which can contribute to a pattern of helplessness, anxiety, and avoidance. To better understand these challenges, this study applies Stress and Coping Theory.
Stress and Coping Theory posits that individuals experience stress when environmental demands exceed their perceived coping resources [17,18]. The theory distinguishes between primary appraisal (the evaluation of the threat) and secondary appraisal (the evaluation of available coping mechanisms) [18]. Climate change is a chronic, ambiguous stressor. It is especially relevant to older adults with chronic illnesses, who often perceive limited control and vulnerability. In this context, coping style becomes a critical determinant of emotional outcomes and well-being.
Coping with Climate Change (CCC) is a construct describing how individuals manage climate-related stress, including environmental action and building climate knowledge. Such actions can reduce the emotional distress associated with CA and enhance a sense of control [19]. These strategies are particularly relevant for older adults, who often have declining physical resources and greater health sensitivity to climate events [20]. Within Stress and Coping Theory, CCC represents the secondary appraisal system—comprising problem-focused actions and meaning-focused coping (including trust and positive reappraisal)—through which older adults translate climate-related threat into adaptive or maladaptive outcomes. CCC may therefore link CA and QoL in older adults and serve as a point for intervention.
In summary, this study uses Stress and Coping Theory to examine the impact of climate anxiety (CA) on quality of life (QoL) in older adults, as well as the mediating role of coping with climate change (CCC). The findings are expected to advance the interdisciplinary field of environmental psychology and geriatric medicine. Additionally, the results inform strategies for policymakers, community health organizations, and clinicians seeking to mitigate CA and improve QoL among older adults.
This study used a cross-sectional design. Convenient sampling was used to select the older adults in Hangzhou. Inclusion criteria: (1) Aged 60 years or above. (2) Diagnosed with at least one chronic disease (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, cardiovascular disease) by a qualified physician. (3) Able to understand and complete the questionnaire independently or with minimal assistance. (4) Provided informed consent. Exclusion criteria: (1) Diagnosed with severe cognitive impairment or dementia (as reported by medical records or caregivers). (2) Suffering from an acute or terminal illness that significantly impairs daily functioning. (3) Diagnosed with a significant psychiatric disorder (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar disorder) that may affect the reliability of responses. (4) Inability to communicate effectively in Mandarin. (5) Refusal or withdrawal of informed consent. (6) Those unable to communicate in Mandarin were excluded due to a lack of validated multilingual survey instruments.
The structural equation model is an extensive sample analysis that must adhere to the 5-fold rule method [21]. The minimum sample size required for this study is 270. In addition, our mediation analysis, which utilizes least-squares regression (PROCESS Model 4), targeted a sample size sufficient to detect small to moderate indirect effects, using bias-corrected bootstrapping, and to ensure stable estimates with multiple covariates [22]. Our final sample size ofN= 424 exceeds the standard recommendations for mediation/SEM contexts for such effect sizes, providing ample precision for coefficient estimates.
Standardized instruments were used to assess climate anxiety (CA), quality of life (QoL), and climate change coping (CCC). Demographic variables collected included gender, education, marital status, occupation, and age.
QoL was measured using the 13-item Older People’s Quality of Life-Brief Scale by Ann Bowling [23]. The questionnaire uses a 5-point Likert scale, with scores from 13 to 65. The Scale includes six dimensions: health, independence, social relationships, home and neighborhoods, psychological and emotional well-being, and leisure activities. Higher scores indicate a higher quality of life. A sample item: I am healthy enough to get out and about. The Cronbach’s alpha coefficient of the Scale is 0.91. Its composite reliability is 0.86, indicating good reliability and validity. The Cronbach’s coefficient in this study was 0.95. The KMO is 0.92, and Bartlett < 0.05.
CCC was measured using the 14-item Coping with Climate Change by Ojala [19]. This Scale has three dimensions: trust and positive reappraisal, problem-focused coping, and de-emphasizing the seriousness of climate change. The questionnaire employs a 5-point Likert scale, with scores ranging from 14 to 70. Higher scores show a higher coping Scale with climate change. A sample item is: More and more people are taking climate change seriously. The Cronbach coefficient of the Scale is 0.90. The composite reliability is 0.84, which shows good reliability and validity. The Cronbach’s coefficient in this study was 0.81. The KMO is 0.83, and Bartlett < 0.05.
CA was measured using the 22-item Climate Anxiety Scale by Clayton and Karazsi [24]. This Scale has two dimensions: cognitive–emotional and functional impairments. It uses a 5-point Likert scale. Total scores range from 22 to 110. Higher scores indicate higher climate anxiety. A sample item: Thinking about climate change makes it difficult for me to concentrate. The Cronbach’s alpha coefficient is 0.90, and the composite reliability is 0.84, showing good reliability and validity. In this study, Cronbach’s coefficient was 0.92. The KMO is 0.91, and the Bartlett’s test is significant (p<.05).
All measurement scales underwent translation and back-translation for linguistic accuracy. Panels of nursing experts checked each item for cultural relevance. Pilot testing with 30 older adults confirmed clarity and reliability. All adapted scales demonstrated internal consistency above 0.85, indicating strong reliability.
Confirmatory factor analyses were performed on the OPQOL-brief, CCC, and CA scales. Model fit was acceptable: OPQOL-brief χ²(65) = 102.34,p<.001; CFI = 0.962; TLI = 0.951; RMSEA = 0.041 (90% CI 0.028–0.054); SRMR = 0.036. CCC χ²(90) = 143.27,p<.001; CFI = 0.955; TLI = 0.944; RMSEA = 0.046 (90% CI 0.030–0.060); SRMR = 0.040. CA χ²(209) = 389.55,p<.001; CFI = 0.948; TLI = 0.936; RMSEA = 0.050 (90% CI 0.041–0.058); SRMR = 0.042. All standardized factor loadings were ≥ 0.50 and significant (p<.001), supporting construct validity in this population.
The tolerance values for CA, QoL, and CCC were 0.89. Their variance inflation factors (VIF) were 1.18. These are well below accepted thresholds (tolerance > 0.1, VIF < 5). We also report the Pearson correlation between CCC, QoL, and CA. This result shows a moderate association, with no concerns about multicollinearity. It supports the validity of the mediation model and addresses the potential overlap between predictor and mediator variables.
Before testing the hypotheses, this study conducted preliminary analyses using SPSS Version 25.0. Descriptive statistics summarized participants’ demographic characteristics. Pearson correlation coefficients measured the associations between key variables. Additionally, the study assessed variance inflation factors, skewness, and kurtosis to confirm that the data met the assumptions of multicollinearity and normality.
To test the mediation hypothesis, Hayes’ PROCESS macro for SPSS (Model 4) was used with least-squares regression [22]. Model 4 is straightforward and commonly used for mediation. It examines the indirect effect of the independent variable (CA) on the dependent variable (QoL) through a mediator (CCC), without moderation or serial mediation. Potential confounding variables—age, gender, job status, education, and marital status—were included as covariates. All mediation paths (CA→CCC; CA + CCC→QoL) were estimated with age, gender, education, marital status, and occupation entered as covariates. This process fits our stress and coping framework. We used bias-corrected bootstrapping with 5,000 resamples to calculate 95% confidence intervals for indirect effects. We also reported the explained variances (R²) as part of the model outputs.
Participation was voluntary, and all data were anonymized. Due to the online survey format, written informed consent was not required; participants indicated consent by selecting a checkbox on the first page. Information about the right to withdraw at any time was provided. Participants were assured that their responses would stay confidential and would not be shared with employers. The study received approval from Hangzhou Linping District Integrated Traditional Chinese and Western Medicine Hospital (Approval no. 2025-L-017).

Most respondents were male (51%,n= 218), had completed primary school (31%,n= 130), were married (86%,n= 364), and were retired (38%,n= 163). This study, informed by stress and coping theory, examined how climate anxiety (CA) and coping with climate change (CCC) jointly influence the quality of life (QoL) in older adults with chronic illnesses. The theory posits that individuals experience environmental threats through two appraisal processes: primary appraisal (the perceived severity of the threat) and secondary appraisal (the evaluation of available coping resources). Our findings extend this theoretical framework by identifying CCC as a psychological mechanism through which climate anxiety affects QoL in an aging population vulnerable to environmental stressors.
We found that the QoL of older adults with chronic diseases was significantly lower than that of their healthier counterparts in the study of Ge and Orfila [25,26]. This finding reduced QoL reflects the cumulative burden of physical symptoms, functional limitations, and heightened environmental vulnerability. Chronic illnesses may intensify the psychological impact of climate uncertainty. They can contribute to feelings of helplessness and dependency [27,28]. This result aligns with Ge and Orfila’s studies [25,26]. However, our results go beyond earlier work by showing that this decline in QoL is not only related to disease burden and physical limitation but also to heightened psychological sensitivity to climate stress. Previous studies mainly focused on medical or social determinants of QoL, whereas this study highlights climate anxiety as an additional emotional burden that compounds chronic illness and intensifies feelings of helplessness and reduced well-being.
Second, older adults in our sample reported significantly lower CCC, supporting earlier findings by Prina, Cianconi, and Leyva [4,10,29], yet extending them in an important way. Rather than interpreting low coping merely as an age-related deficit, our findings suggest that chronic illness and reduced mobility constrain both physical and psychological coping responses [30,31]. From the perspective of stress and coping theory, this pattern reflects a weakened secondary appraisal process—an erosion of perceived control and efficacy—which contributes to disengagement and vulnerability to stress [32,33]. Thus, our study expands the literature by identifying the combined effects of aging, chronic illness, and environmental threat on coping appraisals.
Older adults reported higher levels of CA compared to younger groups, consistent with previous research by Hickman and Asiamah [9,11]. This pattern indicates a dynamic interaction between heightened threat appraisal and diminished coping appraisal. Climate-related threats, such as heatwaves and air pollution, evoke strong concern, while limited coping strategies often foster feelings of learned helplessness and emotional exhaustion. Although the public often perceives youth as more engaged in climate action [9,24], the present findings indicate that older adults with chronic conditions may experience greater climate anxiety due to direct health vulnerabilities [34]. Furthermore, CA is associated with perceptions of control and coping effectiveness. Reduced coping efficacy in older adults increases the likelihood of engagement in the “helpless-anxious-avoidant” pattern [5].
CCC showed a positive association with QoL. This result reflects a resource-based coping pathway. Adaptive resources transform stress into manageable experiences. While previous studies have mainly reported direct negative effects of climate anxiety [6,13,20], our results demonstrate that coping efficacy can convert anxiety into adaptation [15]. This finding broadens the theoretical application of stress and coping theory from short-term disaster contexts to chronic, ongoing climate-related stressors faced by older adults [13].
The positive CA–QoL correlation is counterintuitive but may reflect a motivational/eustress band of climate concern that co-occurs with access to resources and engagement opportunities. This paradoxical effect was noted in the study of Hwang and Cianconi [4,35]. While their research conceptualizes CA as purely detrimental, our results reveal a more complex pattern: moderate anxiety may coexist with better QoL when supported by sufficient coping resources. In such contexts, concern can catalyze adaptive action (information seeking, preparedness, help-seeking), which is consistent with the observed positive link between CCC and QoL and the partial mediation by CCC [15]. Given the cross-sectional design, reverse causality and nonlinear (inverted-U) patterns are also plausible. We therefore interpret this association cautiously and encourage hypothesis-driven tests of non-linearity and subgroup differences in future studies.
In addition, CCC was negatively correlated with CA. These findings affirm that enhancing both practical and meaning-oriented coping is central to reducing CA. Enhanced coping promotes a sense of control and restores coherence between personal values and external realities. Effective coping strategies reduce anxiety for those confronted with environmental threats [4,14,20]. Adopting positive and proactive coping behaviors enhances an individual’s sense of control and confidence regarding climate change risks, thereby reducing anxiety and stress. This study is consistent with the findings of Mah and Reser [36,37]. Other studies frequently highlight problem-oriented coping [38], such as acquiring knowledge and engaging in environmental actions. They also describe meaning-oriented coping [16,39], which involves finding value in personal actions to alleviate uneasiness regarding climate change.
Finally, CCC was found to mediate the relationship between CA and QoL. This result highlights the importance of providing both emotional support and structured empowerment interventions to enhance coping capacity and agency in older adults. The result aligns with previous research by Khalil and Kristofferson [16,20]. Perceptions of climate change as a threatening stressor influence quality of life, depending on both the assessment of the perceived threat and the evaluation of personal resources during the secondary appraisal process. Older adults who perceive themselves as incapable of managing climate-related health threats may experience a cycle of heightened anxiety and diminished control. This sense of helplessness hurts life satisfaction and psychological resilience. The absence of CCC exacerbates CA [40] and reduces the likelihood of maintaining QoL through positive strategies [20].
The findings have several public health implications. First, primary care–based supports should be strengthened through family doctor systems and community climate–health centers that offer individualized assessments, monitoring, counseling, and early warnings for older adults. Routine visits can integrate seasonal “heat checks,” chronic disease action-plan reviews, and referrals to cooling centers during climate events [41,42].
Second, promoting home adaptations—such as improved ventilation, insulation, and air purification—can reduce exposure risks and accommodate medication or mobility needs [10]. Third, targeted health education should enhance older adults’ climate literacy and perceived control through modules on preparedness, heatwave response, and air-quality management [14]. Finally, psychosocial interventions, including group-based cognitive behavioral therapy, empowerment programs, and peer support, can strengthen coping, resilience, and collective efficacy, reducing anxiety and helplessness among socially vulnerable groups [43].
Overall, this study extends the Stress and Coping Theory to an understudied population—older adults with chronic illnesses. It highlights how limited coping resources and reduced mobility heighten vulnerability to climate stress and identifies Coping with Climate Change (CCC) as a crucial mediator between Climate Anxiety (CA) and Quality of Life (QoL). Moreover, the finding that moderate anxiety can motivate adaptive behaviors offers a new framework for interventions aimed at promoting psychological resilience and proactive coping among climate-vulnerable older adults.
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference among CA, CCC, and QoL. Longitudinal studies are needed to track coping and well-being changes over time. Second, convenience sampling from a single metropolitan area may limit generalizability; future research should use stratified, multi-site sampling to improve representativeness. Third, the Mandarin-only survey excluded linguistic minorities, suggesting a need for multilingual validation. Fourth, although demographic factors were controlled, variables such as socioeconomic status, healthcare access, and family support may also influence outcomes and should be considered in future studies. Fifth, reliance on self-reported data may introduce recall or social desirability bias; integrating objective exposure measures (e.g., heat index, physiological indicators) would strengthen validity. Finally, the weak zero-order correlation between CA and CCC warrants caution. Although regression analyses confirmed mediation after adjustment, replication in larger and more diverse samples is needed to confirm this relationship and test potential nonlinear or moderated effects.
Grounded in stress and coping theory, this study demonstrates that coping with climate change (CCC) plays a partial mediating role between climate anxiety (CA) and quality of life (QoL) among older adults with chronic illnesses. These findings extend existing literature by identifying CCC as a pivotal psychological mechanism through which environmental anxiety affects well-being in vulnerable aging populations. Unlike previous research centered on younger adults, our study highlights how chronic conditions and limited mobility heighten threat appraisal while diminishing perceived coping capacity. The research also introduces a novel insight: moderate levels of climate anxiety can motivate adaptive coping behaviors and improve life satisfaction when supported by sufficient coping resources. This dual pathway—from anxiety to adaptation—broadens the theoretical application of stress and coping theory to long-term climate stressors. Practically, the study underscores the importance of community-anchored interventions, including climate-health literacy, home adaptation, and psychosocial empowerment, to strengthen resilience and promote healthy aging in an era of environmental uncertainty.
