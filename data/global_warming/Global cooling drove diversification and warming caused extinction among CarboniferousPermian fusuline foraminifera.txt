The fossil record provides the only direct evidence of changes in biodiversity over time. Patterns in more inclusive taxonomic levels (e.g., families and orders) often become more complex because of interactions between biological traits and environmental conditions across different evolutionary lineages. Using supercomputing and artificial intelligence algorithms, we analyzed a high-resolution global dataset of fusuline foraminifera—the most diverse marine fossil group from the Carboniferous to the Permian (~340 to 252 million years ago)—at an unprecedented temporal resolution of <45 thousand years. Our unbinned diversity reconstruction reveals unexpectedly simple diversity dynamics in this exceptionally well-preserved clade. We identify two (and likely a third) truncated exponential diversifications and four major diversity declines. During this interval, long-term cooling consistently promoted biodiversification, whereas warming events were closely linked to extinctions. These findings imply that the current rapid global warming, driven by anthropogenic CO2emissions, represents a critical threat to modern ecosystems.
High-resolution data for fusuline foraminifera show that global cooling drove diversification while warming triggered extinction.
Concerns about the impact of rising atmospheric partial pressure of CO2(Pco2) and temperatures have renewed interest in how environmental factors regulate biodiversity. An apparent exponential increase in the current extinction rate of terrestrial vertebrates has been projected by some to soon reach the level of the Big Five mass extinctions documented in the fossil record (1–3). The short time span of the current increase in global temperatures and the conflation of short- and long-term records make such claims uncertain, particularly with nonlinear diversity responses.
During the Carboniferous and Permian periods, ~340 to 252 million years ago (Ma), the Earth experienced the Late Paleozoic ice age (LPIA), along with major tectonic plate reorganization, changing global sea levels, and pronounced changes in global atmospheric and oceanic circulation and climate. Atmospheric CO2concentrations and the average sea surface temperature decreased to near-modern levels (4–7) before shifting into a warmhouse (5° to 10°C warmer than today) (8) in the Early Permian followed by a hothouse (>10°C warmer than today) (8) stage in the Early Triassic (9–11). These transitions from the Carboniferous through the Permian provide an ideal analog to interrogate biotic and/or climatic controls on biodiversity for today.
Foraminifera, with a large carbonate biomass, rapid evolution, and high sensitivity to oceanic redox conditions, are an excellent case for unraveling the diversity response of marine organisms to changing climatic conditions in the Cenozoic (12–17). Here, we extend this approach by using a deeper time analog: the Fusulinida, the first larger benthic foraminifera group to evolve, flourishing in the Late Paleozoic (18,19). Fusuline foraminifera have a history spanning ~91.8 million years (Myr), originated in the early Visean (~344 Ma), diversifying during the LPIA, to become extinct in the latest Permian (20–23). This clade has one of the best fossil records in the Paleozoic. Abundantly preserved in Carboniferous and Permian limestones, they are renowned for their rapid morphological change and the association between morphological forms and depositional environments. Similar to modern larger benthic foraminifera, they constructed multichambered shells (referred to as tests) throughout their lifespan. Their tests commonly exceed 3 mm3in volume and exhibit fusiform, spherical, or lenticular shapes with dozens to hundreds of involute and planispiral chambers (24). Fusulines are primarily found in tropical and subtropical shallow marine limestones and have long been suspected of harboring algal symbionts (25,26), although direct fossil evidence for such symbioses remains lacking. These traits have firmly established fusulines as key index fossils for Late Paleozoic biostratigraphic and palaeoecological studies. Their diversification through the LPIA provides a great opportunity to further examine the foraminiferal response to changing climate.
Quantifying past diversity patterns and testing macroevolutionary hypotheses during such icehouse-warmhouse transitions require high-resolution reconstructions of diversity change. Previous studies either were based on regional data (18,27) or had coarser temporal resolution, for example, using stages or biozones (usually 8 to 10 Myr for each time bin) (28,29). The use of coarse time bins for biodiversity analysis creates artificial data discretization. For instance, in the Carboniferous System, the longest time bin spans ~16 Myr, substantially limiting the resolution for evolutionary studies. The development of new supercomputer techniques and artificial intelligence algorithms permits the generation of deep-time diversity patterns with much greater temporal resolution (18,27). These techniques have great potential to elucidate paleobiodiversity patterns at temporal resolutions approaching ecologically meaningful timescales.
Here, we capitalize on the potential of this approach to assess how fusuline foraminifera responded to long-term shifts from icehouse to warmhouse and hothouse climate extremes and to identify the primary driving factors. This analysis relies on a high-quality global database for foraminifera and a high-resolution global fusuline biodiversity curve using constrained optimization (CONOP) (Fig. 1), an unbinned approach with a mean imputed temporal resolution over our study interval of <45 thousand years (kyr).

The fusuline diversity pattern exhibits a generally bell-shaped trajectory from initiation in the early Visean with high diversity by the Bashkirian and a peak at the midpoint in the early Cisuralian before the clade finally became extinct during the end-Permian mass extinction (Fig. 1A). With a mean temporal resolution of <45 kyr, this diversity pattern captures nearly all steps of actual diversity fluctuations, thereby enabling the rigorous analysis of true biodiversification and diversity decline processes.
During this interval, two major biodiversification episodes and possibly a third biodiversification episode and four major losses of diversity are evident (Fig. 1B). The first diversification of fusuline species occurred from late Serpukhovian to the Bashkirian/Moscovian boundary (~343 to 315 Ma) (Fig. 1A). Then, a major decline in species diversity (~45%) occurred during the late Moscovian with minimum diversity at 303.56 Ma. This was followed by rapid biodiversification to the highest peak between 295.24 and 293.57 Ma. Species richness increased by around three times, mainly from the rapid evolution of large and keriotheca-containing Schwagerinidae, and as a result, the dominant group of fusuline shifted from families Fusulinidae and Ozawainellidae to the family Schwagerinidae (Fig. 1B).
Immediately afterward, fusuline diversity declined. The largest and most abrupt drop eliminated 59.5% of species between 293.57 and 283.58 Ma with an average extinction rate of 15.8 species/Myr. After a gentle recovery between 283.81 and 268.61 Ma, the species diversity of all fusuline families decreased simultaneously (Fig. 1A). This diversity loss persisted for more than ~9.3 Myr in the Guadalupian (Fig. 1). Species diversity remained relatively low during the Lopingian before the clade disappeared at the end of the Permian (Fig. 1). The generic pattern generally mirrors the species-level pattern. Rarefaction analyses support the two biodiversification events and four major diversity losses (Fig. 1B). The raw richness and numbers of first and last occurrences (FOs and LOs, respectively) calculated in 1-, 2-, and 5-Myr sliding time series also (fig. S1) support the diversity pattern shown inFig. 1.
The calculated proportional origination, extinction, diversification, and turnover rates (Fig. 2, A and B) were high from the Serpukhovian/Bashkirian boundary (~323 Ma) until the Sakmarian/Artinskian boundary (~290 Ma). The high rates in the end-Changhsingian reflect the extinction of a small number of remaining species. The intervals with high origination generally corresponded with high extinction rates and low species longevity, which accounts for the biodiversification events. The end-Permian mass extinction event (EPME) is characterized by both high extinction and extremely low origination rates.

Several previous studies have investigated patterns of fusuline diversity change, recognizing their importance as a major component of Late Paleozoic marine ecosystems (18–21). These studies either are based on regional datasets [e.g., (18,19)] or used much coarser temporal resolution than used here [e.g., (20,21)]. Despite these limitations, they all generally agree on the overall patterns of fusuline diversification and extinction.
The notably higher temporal resolution of our analysis—on the order of tens of thousands of years—allows us to resolve more detailed trends and events not apparent in previous work. Key findings include the following: slow initial diversification during the Visean-Serpukhovian (~343 to 323 Ma), with near-stasis in the Serpukhovian; exponential radiation beginning in the early Bashkirian (~321 Ma); a temporary Kasimovian diversity decline followed by recovery, culminating in an Asselian peak (~295 Ma); and prolonged Kungurian-Guadalupian (~278 to 266 Ma) diversification, interrupted by an end-Guadalupian decline before final end-Permian extinction. These patterns show notable correlations with contemporaneous climate proxy records, suggesting strong climatic controls on fusuline diversity dynamics.
Our analysis reveals two key faunal transitions: (i) In Kasimovian-Gzhelian/Sakmarian Turnover, the dominant small-sized Fusulinidae and Ozawainellidae were replaced by keriotheca-bearing Schwagerinidae. The honeycomb-like wall structure of schwagerinids (32) resembles the “pore cups” of modern symbiont-bearing foraminifera (22,33), suggesting similar endosymbiotic relationships. The keriotheca may have facilitated schwagerinid dominance during the latest Carboniferous-earliest Permian icehouse conditions, particularly in regressive shallow shelf environments. (ii) In Sakmarian-Artinskian/Kungurian-Wordian Transition, Schwagerinid dominance gave way to a mixed fauna including the first evolved Verbeekinidae and Neoschwagerinidae (Fig. 1B). While verbeekinids also had keriotheca, neoschwagerinids developed novel septula that further subdivided their tests—potentially enhancing symbiont hosting capacity. This transition occurred during a warmhouse-to-hothouse climate phase marked by high sea levels and frequent volcanism-induced anoxia, which likely constrained diversification rates despite the morphological innovations.
It is generally recognized that the controls on biodiversification are complex, with a rich interplay between biotic and abiotic factors controlling time-varying species richness. However, our results show substantial simple diversity dynamics, which are closely coupled to changes in climate. The initial appearance of small, opportunistic species of Fusulinida in the early Visean and the pronounced diversification from the Gzhelian to the middle Asselian both corresponded with progressive cooling (Figs. 1and4, A and B). The first episode occurred during the expansion of the shallow marine habitats and regression during the widespread Variscan and South Tianshan orogens (19). The later diversification coincided with the waxing of the LPIA (Figs. 1and4, A and B).
In contrast, each of the fusuline biodiversity declines occurred during episodes of rapid climatic warming and profound anoxia related to volcanic CO2injections into the atmosphere-ocean system (Fig. 4, A, B, D, and F). Fusuline diversity declined to the first minimum at 303.56 Ma associated with the Kasimovian/Gzhelian (K/G) warming event, a distinct interglacial phase associated with a major carbon perturbation (Fig. 4E) (34). Enhanced thermocline stratification and increased nutrient fluxes during the K/G warming event led to widespread anoxia (Fig. 4D) and ~45% species loss. Our analysis of the diversity pattern suggests that this interglacial event probably started in the late Kasimovian, which is earlier than previously documented.
The largest decline with a 59.5% species loss corresponded to a shift from the LPIA to a warmhouse stage over ~10 Myr from 293.57 to 283.58 Ma (Figs. 1Band4B). A long-term global sea surface temperature increase (Fig. 4F) associated with distinct oceanic acidification has been recorded by conodont δ18Oapatitedata coupled with distinct δ13Ccarbonexcursions (Fig. 4E) in South China (6,7,35,36). A persistent decrease in the87Sr/86Sr ratio (Fig. 4G) (37,38) was likely caused by the increased intensity of volcanism such as Tarim large igneous province (LIP), Panjal Traps, and Maduo-Zaduo LIP and associated with the northward movement of Pangea and active rifting in the Tethys Oceans (10). These volcanic events have been strongly linked to long-term global warming, as supported by extensive evidence from carbon isotope excursions and paleotemperature reconstructions. Sustained global warming can damage or eliminate established ecological habitats, resulting in extinctions of benthic organisms that cannot adapt to rapidly changing environmental conditions (2,5,39,40). This warming may also disrupt the ability of equatorial regions to serve as key areas for biological recovery and renewal (16,41).
The middle to late Guadalupian (~266 to 260 Ma) diversity decline affected all fusuline taxa with large test sizes including verbeekinids, neoschwagerinids, and a large portion of schwagerinids (Fig. 1B); follows the enhanced early to middle Guadalupian warmhouse; and has been widely recognized as the end-Guadalupian extinction (20,42). The higher temporal resolution available in this study shows that the fusuline decline began by the late Roadian and extended through the Wordian and Capitanian (Fig. 1B). This decline can probably be linked to the Emeishan volcanism, which likely began in the Early Permian and intensified in the middle and late Guadalupian (43). Last, the fusuline clade was eliminated by rapid warming at the EPME associated with the Siberian Traps LIP (44) and widespread felsic volcanism (45,46). Therefore, each fusuline diversity drop was associated with warming periods with distinct δ13Ccarbonexcursions and anoxic conditions (Fig. 4, B, E, and F). These correlations suggest that the shifts of the paleoclimatic regime were a first-order control on macroevolution of the clade from the Carboniferous throughout the Permian. The persistent cooling during the Carboniferous and Permian fostered biodiversification, while the warming events caused biodiversity declines and mass extinctions (Fig. 4, B, E, and F).
Milankovitch cyclicity shows a limited influence on fusuline diversity changes. Million-year cycles in the Late Paleozoic have been linked to long-term Milankovitch forcing (47). The 2.1-Myr cycle identified here (Fig. 3and fig. S2) is close to the modern-day eccentricity grand cycle (~2.4 Myr; fig. S3) and may correspond with the (g4−g3) term whereg3andg4are related to the precession of the perihelion of the Earth and Mars. The ~1.0-Myr cycle is close to the modern-day obliquity grand cycle (~1.2 Myr) and might correspond to the (s4−s3) term wheres3ands4are related to the precession of the node of the Earth and Mars (48,49). Long-term Milankovitch cycles potentially modulate changes in seasonality during both icehouse and warmhouse worlds (fig. S4). During the minima of long-term Milankovitch cycles, the variability of the amplitude of orbital cycles is rather small, which leads to damped seasonal contrasts (50,51). Under an icehouse condition, reduced seasonal contrasts could facilitate glacial expansion and relative sea-level fall, increasing terrestrial input and flux in relevant nutrients that may have increased fusuline speciation. In a warmhouse period, continental aquifer drawdown and sea-level transgression were associated with reduced seasonal contrasts, with marine anoxia suppressing fusuline biodiversity (50). In contrast, the maxima of the long-term Milankovitch cycles would be characterized by extreme seasonal contrasts and lead to the opposite effect on fusuline biodiversity.
How biotic and abiotic factors interact in controlling biodiversification has long been contentious (52–54). With Cenozoic foraminifers, both biotic and abiotic drivers can generate diversity (53,55–58). To examine the long-term species diversification process, the diversity change that can be attributed to the short-term Milankovitch cycles (≤2.1 Myr) was removed to emphasize the two diversification episodes. Results show that the two episodes follow an identical exponential function (y= 10.854e0.0859x, both withR2≥ 0.95; fig. S5), and there is no evidence for biotic constraints associated with these episodes.
The exponential increase terminated during the major climate regime shift in the late Sakmarian to Artinskian (Fig. 1Band fig. S5). In the aftermath, species diversity was relatively flat through the Kungurian-early Guadalupian interval with the detrended origination rate correlating with the detrended extinction rate with an increasing time lag from 0.5 to 1.2 Myr (r= 0.50 and 0.55,P< 0.001; fig. S6). This indicates an equilibrium state when speciation is balanced by the extinction events, although with a decreasing power. This interval was still dominated by schwagerinids, but the first evolved verbeekinid and neoschwagerinid genera quickly radiated to become important components (Fig. 1B). We hypothesize that this multiclade eco-space might be the reason for the diversity equilibrium, and the slowing compensation of speciation might be related to the volcanic activities in the Paleotethys at ~280 Ma (59). The subsequent Guadalupian crisis severely affected fusuline species diversity, and the species diversity in the Lopingian is so low that no patterns are evident.
The exponential increases in fusuline species richness (fig. S5) could represent logistic growth where the carrying capacity is sufficiently high that the diversity dependence was not evident even at the maximum observed species richness, or it can be explained by logistic growth where the species diversification rate is sufficiently high that it tracked the carrying capacity that was increasing exponentially (60) or some combination thereof. The subsequent decline can be interpreted as a reduction in carrying capacity.
To test for possible linear relationships between the overall diversity and environmental proxies, Pearson correlation coefficients between species richness and environmental proxies includingPco2,87Sr/86Sr, partial pressure of oxygen (Po2), δ13Ccarbon, sea level, δ18Oapatite, and continental fragmentation index were computed (table S1). Among the seven indices,87Sr/86Sr, δ13Ccarbon, and δ18Oapatitehave relatively larger coefficient values, but there is no strong correlation between any single environmental proxy and the species richness profile (allR2< 0.5,P≤ 0.001;Fig. 4and table S1). However, analyses containing interactions among pairs of these variables are more substantial. Species richness has a high correlation with four environmental proxies,Pco2,87Sr/86Sr,Po2, and δ13Ccarbontogether (R2= 0.81,P≤ 0.001) (Fig. 4and table S1), after removing proxies with multicollinearity, and they contributed 80.8% of the diversity changes (table S1). Among these four variables,Pco2usually manifested by δ13C in deep time is most directly related to the Earth’s surface temperature. Oxygen in seawater is essential for foraminifera (61). The bottom-water dissolved oxygen levels are significantly correlated with the diversity of living benthic foraminifera (62–64). Previous studies suggested that foraminiferal diversity is lower in dysoxic environments (61,65,66). Meanwhile, trends toward gigantism in fusulines and foraminifera have been positively correlated withPo2because of constraints of oxygen transportation in aerobic organisms (61,67,68). The87Sr/86Sr ratio tracks shifts in continental weathering as well as high- and low-temperature hydrothermal inputs. The long-term decline in87Sr/86Sr values from the latest Carboniferous to late Guadalupian was probably caused by the increasing hydrothermal flux associated with the mid-ocean spreading and volcanism (37,38). Thus, the high correlation between these environmental indices and diversity patterns strongly suggests that climate change probably induced by continental reconfiguration and intensive volcanism played critical roles in fusuline macroevolution.
Our multivariate linear analysis also reveals significant correlation between fusuline species richness and shifts in abiotic environmental proxies associated with global climate changes. The four environmental proxies show more significant relationships with the diversity change during the warmhouse stage (284 to 252 Ma) (R2= 0.90,P≤ 0.001) than in the icehouse stage (343 to 285 Ma) (R2= 0.79,P≤ 0.001) (table S1). These relationships suggest that persistent icehouse conditions with lowPco2and highPo2resulted in increased biodiversity during the Carboniferous-Permian periods. By contrast, climate warming–associated episodic anoxia and acidification correlate with biodiversity loss, which seriously interrupted fusuline macroevolution (Fig. 4D). Thus, while there was a strong relationship with climate under both icehouse and warmhouse regimes, the impact was greater during the latter.
The climatic tumult of the Late Paleozoic was substantially similar to the Cenozoic, encouraging the comparison of this high-resolution global fusuline composite sequence to Cenozoic records. Although no equivalent high-resolution Cenozoic global compilation of benthic foraminiferal diversity is available, some assessment is possible. The diversity of planktonic foraminifera increased greatly over the past 120 Myr (fig. S7) (69–71). During the Cenozoic Era, species diversity increased fourfold via pulses in the Early Eocene, Oligocene, and Miocene (71), coincident with the general cooling trend (fig. S7). Especially in the coolhouse and icehouse intervals (~34 to 0 Ma) (8), planktonic foraminiferal diversity increased steadily (13,71). Following the Early Pliocene, the maximum diversity declined at around 10%/Myr toward the recent (fig. S7) (8). This is consistent with the shift to larger and more frequent oscillations in benthic foraminiferal δ13C and δ18O, which probably reflects the shift in the climatic regime from the Late Cenozoic icehouse to the current warmhouse regime (8,51,72). Similarly, later foraminifera suffered diversity loss during the Cretaceous-Paleogene, the Paleocene-Eocene thermal maximum, the Late Eocene-Oligocene warming climate, and mid-Pliocene warm period (8,12,13,51,71).
A similar multivariate linear analysis also shows significant correlation between planktonic foraminiferal species richness and abiotic environmental proxies with temperature, inferred from δ18O from deep-sea benthic foraminifera (8). The four most influential environmental proxies for the Cenozoic planktonic foraminiferal diversity change arePco2,87Sr/86Sr, δ18O, and δ13C. The relationships are more significant during the warmhouse stage (65 to 35 Ma) (R2= 0.68,P≤ 0.001) than the icehouse stage (34 to 0 Ma) (R2= 0.37,P≤ 0.01) (table S2), a result consistent with the results for the Carboniferous-Permian periods. These similarities suggest that climatic regime shifts have been critical for changes in foraminiferal biodiversity during both the LPIA and Cenozoic.
While global diversity patterns are traditionally used to reveal notable bioevents (27,29,73), caution is still needed. Geographic migration could easily lead to pseudoextinction, and the divergence between contemporary biogeographic regions can be substantial (74). This type of systematic bias can distort interpretations of biodiversity patterns across both specific time (Cenozoic/Paleozoic) and deep time more broadly. To accurately identify the biotic and abiotic drivers of biodiversity change, analyses of local- and regional-scale datasets remain as essential as global-scale data (74).
In summary, high-resolution fusuline diversity reveals two major episodes of biodiversification and four episodes of major diversity loss. Correlation with environmental variables suggests that fusuline macroevolution was primarily controlled by climatic shifts between icehouse and warmhouse conditions. Fusuline diversifications were interrupted by major climatic warming. These abrupt diversity losses might be linked to global warming derived from long-term massive degassing from increasingly intensified volcanism related to the continental reconfiguration during the Carboniferous and Permian (Fig. 4A). Thus, the diversity changes of the largest benthic foraminifera in marine ecosystems over 91.8 Myr provide insight into the evolution of modern marine ecosystems and their substantial simple dynamics under major climate shifts between icehouse and warmhouse stages. The current global warming from anthropogenic CO2emissions has surpassed the amount input during the massive end-Permian mass extinction (75,76) and is likely a great threat to modern marine ecosystems.
Fusuline specimens are typically well preserved in Carboniferous and Permian shallow-marine limestones. Because of their planispiral internal chamber structure, thin sectioning is essential for taxonomic identification. Unlike Cenozoic larger benthic foraminifera, which can sometimes be studied as whole specimens from disaggregated sediment, fusulines generally require preparation by grinding and sectioning to reveal their internal morphology. As such, most taxonomic studies of fusulines rely heavily on petrographic thin sections, often accompanied by systematic descriptions and annotated images.
In this study, we compiled data from published geological cross sections that contain fusuline-bearing strata from around the world. These published records—comprising fossil occurrences, lithologic descriptions, and stratigraphic context—were integrated into the OneStratigraphy database (https://onestratigraphy.ddeworld.org/). From there, fusuline occurrence data were extracted and subjected to a rigorous data cleaning process. Taxonomic data cleaning includes the following steps: All nonfusuline taxa were removed. Species in open nomenclature, such as undetermined species (?), compared species (cf.), affinitive species (aff.), unnamed species (sp.), and unnamed new species (sp. nov.), were excluded to avoid ambiguity and misleading correlations. Taxa only recorded above species level were also removed. Fusuline identifications were carefully verified by the authors (Y.K.S., Y.C.Z., and Y.W.), who are specialists in the taxonomy of this group. The subspecies were either verified to the assigned species or reassigned to other species. Species with a single occurrence at a single locality were not included to reduce randomness in the analysis.
Primarily, 945 geological cross sections containing 12,888 taxa and 25,657 occurrences of marine fossil groups were extracted. After the taxonomic and stratigraphic data cleaning procedure, the final dataset includes 1391 fusuline species with 5517 occurrences from 299 worldwide-distributed Carboniferous and Permian geological cross sections (fig. S8; details in tables S3 and S4) and covers the entire interval of fusuline evolution from the middle Mississippian to the end-Permian (343.70 to 251.88 Ma) (Fig. 1). This is the most geographically widespread and complete compendia of fusuline foraminifera within this time interval.
CONOP compositing (27,77) generated the best fit composite sequence of FOs and LOs of each species by a simulated annealing algorithm. This method implements a deterministic temporal model (78) to optimally reconstruct sequences of the FOs and LOs to establish a precise chronological framework for biodiversity analysis. By fully using all available fossil data without constraining taxonomic occurrences into predefined intervals, this approach represents the most robust methodology for diversity studies. To increase the speed of calculation, higher weight was applied to the FOs of index fossils (table S5). During the studied interval, the supercontinent of Pangea had created high biotic provinciality, so we developed three virtual sequences from three typical paleogeographic realms. Three virtual sequences based on regional index fossils were constructed for South China, North America, and Russia (table S5) to reconcile extensive provincialism. FO and LO events were used as the basic unit of adjustment to avoid bias from varying sediment-accumulation rates and sampling effects. On the basis of the position of zonal fossils in the composite sequence and their corresponding geological ages (table S6), all FOs and LOs of the composite sequences were ranked by their age as estimated by linear interpolation. The resulting age rank composed the high-resolution timeline. Key associated conodont species were included to further refine the time resolution, but they are not included in the diversity curves. In this study, the composite sequence is composed of 2298 discrete temporal levels ranging from 343.70 to 251.88 Ma, with average temporal resolutions of 44.6 kyr in the Carboniferous and 36.8 kyr in the Permian.
Multiple supercomputer runs were conducted with incremental changes in parameters until the output composite sequences became consistent. Fusuline species and genus richness were then calculated with an unbinned approach (27). We applied rarefaction to test the effect of uneven sampling across the composite sequence with resample sizes of 100, 200, and 300 local ranges (Fig. 1B). With nearly 85% fusuline species in this study ranging over 0.5 Myr, we chose 0.5 Myr as the basic time unit and calculated a mean time series of per-taxon origination, extinction, and turnover rates (Fig. 2) and 1-, 2-, 5-Myr sliding time series for raw richness, the number of FOs per million years, and the number of LOs per million years (fig. S1). To assess the possibility of uneven sampling through time, we examined the correlations between the number of rock units/fossil collections and diversity using both Paleobiolology Database (https://paleobiodb.org/) and Geobiodiversity Database (GBDB;www.geobiodiversity.com/) data; no significant correlations were found (fig. S9).
Rarefaction was applied to test for richness bias caused by the uneven sample numbers at different stratigraphic levels of the composite sequence (Fig. 1C). Here, we followed the algorithm of Hsieh and Li (79) using the number of raw local ranges, i.e., the occurrences before the extension of CONOP, for resampling number for each age rank in the composite sequences. The outcome of species richness is the estimate of species number with ±95% confidence interval. Rarefaction was processed with resample sizes of 100, 200, and 300 local ranges, respectively (Fig. 1C).
To examine the possibility that there was astronomical forcing of fusuline biodiversity, the raw data series were interpolated to an even interval of 0.1 Myr and were detrended by subtracting the >10-Myr trend (fig. S2), which otherwise may have masked any higher frequency cycles that might be present (80). Multitaper method power spectra of the detrended data series were analyzed for the dominant frequency peaks against classic first-order autoregressive “AR (1)” red noise models (81). Evolutionary fast Fourier transform was used to detect the evolution of the power of cycles through time (80). One well-known phenomenon of orbital parameters is that long period cycles modulate short-term cycles. For example, short eccentricity (~100 kyr) is modulated by the longer eccentricity (405 kyr and 2.4 Myr). To explore the amplitude modulation of interpreted 405-kyr eccentricity, the envelope curves were obtained from the filtering of eccentricity cycle signals (fig. S3). Amplitude modulation of ~0.4-Myr eccentricity cycles shows a significant ~2.1-Myr period in four fusuline series (fig. S3), derived from direct modulation in the short-term orbital eccentricity.
All analyses for astronomical cycles were performed using Acycle version 2.6 software (82). The evaluation of band-limited variance in fusuline series probability focused on candidate long-term Milankovitch cycles, and this process was conducted in “R” using the code from Cramptonet al.(30).
Linear regression has been used to assess pairwise correlation between diversity changes and environmental proxies includingPco2,87Sr/86Sr,Po2, δ13Ccarbon, sea level, δ18Oapatite, and continental fragmentation index. Multivariate linear regression was then applied to examine potential relationships between diversity changes and combined environmental factors. Before the multivariate linear analysis, variance inflation factors (VIFs) were calculated for each independent variable to evaluate multicollinearity among environmental proxies. The results show that87Sr/86Sr and continental fragmentation index have VIF >10 when all seven proxies are included; thus, strong multicollinearity is present in either one or both indices. After the continental fragmentation index is removed, VIFs of all variables are <6. The trajectory of ice area produced by van der Meeret al.(83) shows relatively high correlation with our fusuline diversity pattern, but it was based on87Sr/86Sr values, so only87Sr/86Sr was used for analyses. Our results show thatPco2,87Sr/86Sr,Po2, and δ13Ccarboncontributed 81.6% of the diversity changes (table S1).
