Plant roots and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) form a ubiquitous symbiosis in terrestrial ecosystems and critically affect soil organic carbon (SOC) dynamics. However, how roots and AMF mediate the impact of reactive nitrogen (Nr) and climate warming on SOC remains unclear. Using a multi-year Nr addition and simulated warming experiment in a semi-arid grassland, we show that Nr input and warming alter SOC by reshaping plant communities and inducing multidimensional tradeoffs among fine-root traits and AMF communities. Stable isotope (13C) tracing revealed that Nr- and warming-induced changes in roots and AMF reduced C input belowground, and mineral-associated organic C and microbial necromass in soil, while stimulating organic C decomposition. Nr input also increased soil N:P ratios and shifted AMF communities toward taxa with finer extraradical hyphae, weakening SOC protection. Together, these findings highlight root-AMF interactions as critical regulators and improve predictions of long-term SOC dynamics under future climate change.

N input and warming alter plant traits and mycorrhizal communities, reducing belowground C inputs and stable soil organic C while accelerating decomposition, highlighting root–mycorrhizal interactions as key regulators of soil C under global change.
The net balance of soil organic carbon (SOC) accrual and loss can profoundly affect the atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) concentration and climate stability1,2. Over 80% of SOC originates from roots and root-associated microbes3–5, with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) representing an important component of these belowground inputs. Besides functioning as the primary source of plant-derived organic C entering soil, roots and AMF also promote soil C protection by facilitating soil aggregation6,7. On the other hand, they may enhance SOC loss through stimulating the growth of saprotrophic microbes and their production of exoenzymes responsible for organic C degradation7,8. The mycorrhizosphere, particularly the regions surrounding roots and AMF, is a hotspot for organic C transformations that play a crucial role in determining the fate of soil C. However, knowledge gaps in our understanding of root- and AMF-responses to climate change drivers and the resulting impact on microbial transformations of organic C critically hinder our capacity to predict and manage SOC in a changing climate.
Reactive nitrogen (Nr) input and climate warming are two major global change drivers that affect both organic C input (via plant production) to and C output (via microbial decomposition) from soil9,10. Annual global Nr input has doubled due to human activities via synthetic N fertilization, worldwide legume cultivation and industry-induced N deposition11. Simultaneously, global surface temperature has increased by 1.09 °C since the Industrial Revolution, and is predicted to further increase by up to 2.6–4.8 °C by the end of this century12. Because plant growth is N-limited in most terrestrial ecosystems, Nr input generally enhances plant productivity13,14and subsequent input of organic C (e.g., litter and root exudates) to soil, favoring SOC accrual14. However, the impact of N input on organic C decomposition is less certain15–17, as increased soil N generally stimulates decomposition of relatively labile organic C15,18, while suppressing degradation of recalcitrant organic compounds such as lignin19. Consequently, N input often increases decomposition in grasslands15but suppresses it in forests10. Warming, on the one hand, often increases organic C input to soil via enhancing plant growth, particularly in areas where water is not a limiting factor and temperature is low20. However, warming generally enhances microbial decomposition of SOC, inducing soil C losses into the atmosphere9,21. While the individual impacts of Nr input and warming on SOC dynamics have been extensively studied9,16, the roles of roots and AMF in mediating SOC responses to combined Nr and warming influence have received comparatively less attention. In particular, interactions among climate change drivers, root traits, AMF, and soil C transformations remain poorly understood, constraining our conceptual understanding of SOC stability in terrestrial ecosystems.
Root traits are key drivers of plant and ecosystem functioning that may profoundly affect SOC dynamics21–23. Plants adopt different strategies for nutrient acquisition, ranging from a “do-it-yourself” strategy with thin roots to an “outsourcing strategy” with thick roots that facilitate symbiosis with AMF24. In other words, plants can either rely on their own root system for nutrient acquisition through constructing long, narrow-diameter roots or outsource nutrient acquisition via investing in mycorrhizal associations25,26. This trade-off between investing in intrinsic root structures or in mycorrhizal partnerships constitutes a critical axis in root economics and is key to understanding root trait variation and the resulting impact on SOC22,24. However, most studies have so far examined responses of root traits and AMF communities to climate change drivers and their roles in plant resource acquisition, and the resulting impact on SOC dynamics remains largely unexplored22,27,28.
Nr input can alter traits of plant roots and their associated AMF29,30, through multiple mechanisms. First, Nr input often increases plant productivity in grassland and shifts the plant community, favoring the growth of gramineous grasses over forbs, altering C allocation belowground and/or suppressing root growth and biomass31,32. Second, Nr input increases soil N availability and alters the C economy of plants, leading to changes in root morphological traits (i.e., diameter and length) and a decrease in root C:N ratio29. Third, N-induced changes in plant C allocation may also alter the AMF community composition and external hyphal networks30, as AMF species often vary widely in their C requirements. In addition, Nr input alters soil nutrient stoichiometry, which may impact AMF community composition33,34. In phosphorus (P)-limiting soils, it would increase the soil N:P ratio, and promote plant C allocation to AMF for P exploration35.
In contrast, warming reduces soil moisture content, which can exacerbate drought stress and further constrain root proliferation in water-limited ecosystems36. While warming may enhance root cell elongation and metabolic activity37, the concurrent decline in soil moisture can counteract these benefits by limiting root expansion and function36,38. The effects of warming on AMF are similarly variable, with some studies reporting significant impacts and others finding neutral effects39,40. These inconsistencies likely arise from context-dependent factors such as different ecosystems, host plant identity, and initial AMF community composition. For example, elevated temperatures may reduce fungal hyphal growth in arid ecosystems but stimulate symbiotic efficiency in cooler regions where warming alleviates microbial metabolic constraints39,40. Yet, how the interplay between Nr input and warming affects root traits, and AMF, remains largely unexplored.
Changes in root traits and AMF communities induced by Nr input and warming are likely to cascade to SOC dynamics, influencing both organic C input and stabilization21,41. For example, Nr addition favored roots with high specific root length (SRL) and low root diameter or root tissue density (RTD)29. The resulting changes in root trait profiles are often associated with high root turnover or short life span42, limiting their contribution to soil aggregation and subsequent SOC formation43,44. Similarly, AMF species differ in their production of extraradical hyphae and extracellular metabolites, potentially affecting soil C formation and stabilization7,45,46. AMF communities dominated by species with extensive but finer extraradical hyphae (e.g.,ParaglomeraceaeandGigasporaceae) may contribute greater C input to the soil, potentially stimulating microbial decomposition of organic matter21. In contrast, AMF communities with less extensive but thicker hyphae (e.g.,Glomeraceae) may produce organic compounds that are conducive to the formation of mineral-associated organic carbon (MAOC), thereby increasing SOC stability46,47. Yet, few have explored how changes in root traits and AMF composition induced by concurring Nr input and warming may modulate soil C dynamics (both C input and output) within the mycorrhizosphere.
We initiated a long-term field study of Nr input and warming in a semi-arid grassland in 2015 to disentangle the mechanisms underpinning their impact on plant-microbial interactions, and soil C and N dynamics. To generate a comprehensive picture of changes in root-AMF traits, we quantified root production, root traits and root- or AMF-derived C, using root- and hyphae-ingrowth techniques and stable isotope tracing, and characterized AMF with the MiSeq sequencing. We hypothesized that (1) Nr input shifts plant community and alters root traits and AMF composition, increasing labile C input, stimulating microbial growth and enzyme production, and thereby stimulating soil organic C decomposition, (2) warming induces drought stress to plants, and suppresses root growth and AMF, reducing plant-derived C input to soil, and (3) over the long term, Nr and warming reduce soil C stability (i.e., MAOC and microbial necromass C).
Field treatments of Nr input and air warming were initiated in a semi-arid grassland on China’s Yellow Loess Plateau in 2015, using experimental plots of 4 m × 4 m. Nr input (12.0 g urea-N m−2y−1) was carried out through two equal amounts of applications, and warming was simulated via open-top chambers (OTCs; each with 60 cm top width, 75 cm bottom width, and 51.76 cm height) (Supplementary Fig.1)48,49. Nr input did not significantly affect either air or soil temperature or soil moisture (Supplementary Tables1and3). In contrast, warming significantly increased the averaged growing-season air temperature (at 20 cm aboveground) by 2.0, 2.6, and 2.1 °C in 2018, 2019, and 2021, respectively (Supplementary Table1and Supplementary Fig.3a–c). Warming also tended to increase soil temperature in surface layers (Supplementary Table3), although mean soil temperature at 10 cm depth was not significantly elevated in 2018 or 2019 (Supplementary Fig.3d,e), it increased significantly by 0.5 °C in 2021 (Supplementary Table1and Supplementary Fig.3f). In comparison, warming consistently and significantly reduced mean soil moisture by 15.1, 16.8, and 13.5% in 2018, 2019, and 2021, respectively (Supplementary Table1and Supplementary Fig.3g–i).
Nr input significantly increased soil available N (mainly NO3--N), whereas warming alone had no effect on it across the 3 years (Supplementary Tables2,3). However, warming amplified the positive Nr effect on soil available N in 2021 (Supplementary Tables2and3), likely through enhancing N mineralization, nitrification and/or reducing N losses via leaching or denitrification50. Although Nr input did not consistently affect soil available P, it consistently increased the available N:P ratio across the 3 years (Supplementary Tables2and3), suggesting that high N may have induced a relative P limitation for plants and/or microbes.
To assess how Nr input and warming affect plant growth, we characterized plant community composition and measured aboveground and belowground (root) biomass in 2018, 2019, and 2021 (extensive measurements were not conducted in 2020 because the COVID pandemic limited field access). Across the 3 years, no significant interaction was detected between Nr input and warming on plant biomass (Supplementary Table4). Nr input significantly and consistently increased aboveground net primary productivity (ANPP) by ca. 19.9 g C m−2yr−1(Fig.1cand Supplementary Table4; see also ref.51). Concurrently, Nr input shifted plant community from forb dominance (primarilyCarex spp) to grass dominance (Stipa grandis) (Fig.1a,b), increasing the grass-to-forb biomass ratio by 290% in 2021 (Supplementary Fig.4). In contrast, Nr input reduced mean root biomass by 26.1%, whereas warming increased it by 22.7% (bothp< 0.001; Supplementary Table4).

Fine-root growth was quantified using root-ingrowth cores to estimate fine-root production and turnover in 2018, 2019, and 2021 (Supplementary Fig.2). Neither Nr input nor warming significantly affected root production over the 3 years (Fig.1e). In contrast, both factors had significant but opposing main effects on root turnover with no significant interaction: Nr input increased root turnover by 54.6%, whereas warming reduced it by 30.8% (Fig.1fand Supplementary Table4). As for fine-root functional traits, repeated-measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) revealed no significant interaction between Nr input and warming, but significant variation among years (Supplementary Table5and Supplementary Figs.5and6). Over the 3 years, Nr input significantly increased root N concentration by 22.0%, SRL by 17.4% and specific root surface area (SRSA) by 20.8%, but reduced root C:N ratio by 18.5% and RTD by 16.0% (Fig.2a, b, d–f). In contrast, warming significantly reduced SRL by 25.1% and SRSA by 13.4%, but increased root diameter by 17.0% (Fig.2c–e). These tradeoffs in root morphology reflect a fundamental shift in plant C allocation strategy mediated by changes in community composition. Specifically, Nr input promotes communities that invest in long, low-density roots, while warming favors communities that construct thicker, shorter roots.

To evaluate the effect of Nr input and warming, alone or in combination, on root-AMF interactions, we quantified AMF colonization of plant roots and extra-radical AMF biomass in soil. No significant interaction between Nr input and warming was detected for either parameter (Supplementary Table5). Warming did not significantly affect AMF colonization but reduced extra-radical biomass in soil (Fig.2g,h). In comparison, Nr input significantly reduced both parameters (Fig.2g,h, Supplementary Fig.7, and Supplementary Table6), despite concurrent increases in SRL and SRSA (Fig.2d,e). Pairwise correlations revealed that root N was significantly negatively related with RTD (Fig.2iand Supplementary Fig.8), reflecting a “conservation” gradient, that is, the classical fast-slow tradeoffs in root economics, where roots with low construction costs and rapid turnover contrast with more structurally dense, conservative root24,26. In addition, AMF colonization was significantly negatively related with SRL and SRSA, but positively related with root diameter and RTD (Fig.2iand Supplementary Fig.8). This pattern represents a “collaboration” gradient, spanning a continuum from a “do-it-yourself” nutrient acquisition strategy, characterized by thin, high-SRL roots with low mycorrhizal dependence to an “outsourcing” strategy in which plants invest in thicker roots and greater C allocation to AMF to acquire nutrients24,25. Principal component analysis (PCA) further showed that the first two components explained 72.1% of the variation in mycorrhizal colonization and root traits (Fig.2jand Supplementary Table10). The first axis (RTD-RNC) captures variation in RTD and N concentration, reflecting differences in root construction costs and nutrient investment strategies. The second axis (RD-AMF%-SRL) describes a gradient from thin roots with high SRL and low mycorrhizal colonization to thick roots with greater mycorrhizal dependence, corresponding to a cooperative root-AMF economic strategy24.
Alterations in soil nutrient stoichiometry and root traits under Nr input and warming may influence AMF community composition because various AMF species have different life strategies and C requirements21,30,45. We sequenced the small subunit ribosomal RNA (SSU rRNA) gene amplicons for genomic DNA extracted from both plant roots and soil in 2018, 2019, and 2021. Over the 3 years, root AMF communities were dominated byGlomeraceae(ca. 53.7%) andParaglomeraceae(ca. 29.9%) (Fig.3aand Supplementary Fig.9a, c, e), but soil AMF were primarily byGlomeraceae(ca. 58.0%) andGigasporaceae(ca. 25.9%) (Fig.3band Supplementary Fig.9b, d, f). Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) ordination and permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) revealed that plant and soil AMF communities under Nr input were distinct from those under the ambient N control (Fig.3c,d). Nr input consistently reducedGlomeraceaein roots and soils, but increasedParaglomeraceaein roots (Fig.3a,band Supplementary Fig.9). In comparison, warming altered the taxonomic composition of AMF communities primarily in roots, with a significant decrease inGlomeraceaeacross the 3 years (Fig.3), whereasParaglomeraceaeshowed a significant increase only in 2021 (Supplementary Table7). A significant interaction between Nr input and warming was detected solely for soilGigasporaceae(Supplementary Table7). Soil available N:P ratio correlated positively with abundances ofGigasporaceaeandParaglomeraceae, but negatively withGlomeraceaeabundance (Supplementary Fig.10). Compared toGlomeraceae,GigasporaceaeandParaglomeraceaeoften form extensive extra-radical hyphae to facilitate plant uptake of less mobile nutrients such as P52. In P-limiting soils, plants are likely to favor AMF taxa that can effectively acquire P nutrient53. In addition, most gramineous grasses have highly branched fibrous root systems54and are less dependent on AMF hyphae than forbs to explore and absorb nutrients from soil55. Together, altered plant community composition, accompanied by a decrease in photosynthate allocation belowground under Nr input, may promote plants to construct roots with high SRL, low root diameter and/or low RTD, while reducing AMF colonization of roots and/or favoring AMF species with lower C requirement.

To determine whether changes in roots and AMF induced by Nr input and warming alter plant-derived organic C in soil, we first quantified ROOT-derived C (here ROOT refers to roots with AMF) and AMF-derived C inputs to soil, utilizing a dual ingrowth-core isotopic technique8,21. Because AMF are obligately biotrophic, three mesh sizes were used to isolate AMF effects from their host roots (see Supplementary Fig.2). The plant community at our field site is dominated by C3plants51,56and the background soil exhibits a depleted13C signature (δ13C = −23.49‰). In May 2019, ROOT- and hyphae-ingrowth cores filled with maize field soil (δ13C = −14.35‰) were installed (see Methods for more details). After retrieval in 2021,13C enrichment in the cores was quantified to determine AMF- and ROOT-derived C inputs. Results showed that annual ROOT-derived C ranged from 79.6 to 152.5 g C m−2after 2-yr incubation (Fig.4b), generally exceeding aboveground litter mass C in their respective treatments (Fig.1c). A significant interaction between Nr input and warming was detected for ROOT-derived C, but not for AMF-derived C (Fig.4a,band Supplementary Table8). Both Nr input and warming independently reduced AMF-derived C inputs to soil (Fig.4a). Relative to the control, Nr input alone, warming alone and their combination significantly reduced ROOT-derived C by 38.0, 40.3, and 41.2, respectively (Fig.4b).

For the MAOC, no significant interaction was detected for either ROOT- or AMF-derived MAOC (Fig.4c,d). Instead, Nr input had a significant negative main effect on both ROOT- and AMF-derived MAOC, while warming exerted a significant negative main effect only on AMF-derived MAOC (Fig.4c,d). However, a significant interaction between Nr input and AMF presence was detected for total soil MAOC (Supplementary Fig.11b) and Ca-associated organic C (Ca-OC) (Fig.5aand Supplementary Table9). Specifically, in the presence of AMF alone, Nr input significantly reduced total MAOC (Supplementary Fig.12b) and Ca-OC (Fig.5a). Warming also significantly decreased Ca-OC (Fig.5a) but had no effect on Fe-associated organic C (Fe-OC) (Fig.5b). Additionally, compared with the control, Nr input, warming and their interaction significantly reduced microbial necromass C by 11.4, 10.5, and 15.0%, respectively (Fig.5c). In contrast, the presence of AMF alone or roots with AMF had no effect on microbial necromass C (Fig.5c). Regression analysis further showed that AMF colonization, AMF extra-radical biomass, andGlomeraceaeabundance in roots and/or soil were positively correlated with AMF-derived C and MAOC (Supplementary Figs.12and13). Moreover, RTD positively correlated with ROOT-derived C and MAOC accumulation, whereas root diameter, rootParaglomeraceae, and soilGigasporaceaeabundance negatively correlated with them (Supplementary Figs.13and14). Together, these results indicate that shifts in root traits and AMF community induced by Nr input and warming had affected the contribution of ROOT- and/or AMF-derived C to not only the labile C pool but also to the stable MAOC and microbial necromass C.

We further examined how changes in roots and/or AMF under Nr input and warming affected decomposition of organic residues. ROOT- and hyphae-ingrowth cores (see Supplementary Fig.2), each containing a mixture of 1.8 g C4plant materials (13C-enriched C4maize residues; δ13C = −11.58‰) and 150 g of13C-depleted C3soil (δ13C = −23.49‰), were installed in the field in September 2018. After 1-yr incubation, Nr input significantly promoted decomposition of newly added organic residues (i.e., reduced13C remaining) in the presence of AMF alone and in ROOT-ingrowth cores (Fig.5d). In contrast, warming had no significant effect on residue C decomposition in the presence of either live roots or AMF alone (Fig.5d), despite increasing root biomass (Fig.1d), likely because warming-induced water stress on soil microbes offset the accelerated effect of increased root C on microbes20,49.
Alteration in organic C decomposition under Nr input was closely related to root traits and AMF community composition. Residue-derived C remained in the ROOT-ingrowth cores was negatively correlated with SRL and SRSA, but positively with root diameter and RTD (Supplementary Fig.17), suggesting that thinner, longer roots enhance organic C decomposition through microbial priming21,57,58. Additionally, residue-derived C remaining in the AMF-ingrowth cores was positively correlated with AMF colonization rate (Supplementary Fig.15) andGlomeraceaeabundance (Supplementary Fig.16a), but negatively withParaglomeraceaeabundance in plant roots (Supplementary Fig.16b). These results suggest that increased root turnover, reduced root structural integrity, and AMF community shifts collectively enhance microbial decomposition of SOC by increasing substrate availability and reducing physiochemical protection of soil organic matter.
Results from our multi-year study showed that Nr input and warming significantly modified root traits and shifted plant and AMF community composition, inducing multidimensional tradeoffs among roots and AMF (Fig.6). First, Nr input often favors gramineous grass species over forbs in grasslands59,60, which were also observed in our study (Fig.1a, b). This induced plant community alteration elicited a subsequent tradeoff between coarse and fine roots at the community level (Fig.2). Grasses, which typically possess highly branched, fibrous root systems, differ from forbs in root dynamics and C allocation. Specifically, fibrous roots have short lifespans and exhibit faster turnover rates compared to the taproots of forbs22,54. This alteration in root architecture and turnover dynamics has implications for ROOT-derived C inputs into the soil. Second, Nr input increased root N concentration while reducing RTD (Fig.2a,f), reflecting a root tissue conservation gradient that represents the classical tradeoff between fast and slow return for plant C investment24. Third, Nr input and warming altered root traits, such as root diameter, which directly affected mycorrhizal colonization rate (Figs.2and6). For example, Nr input increased SRL and SRSA, while reducing mycorrhizal colonization of roots (Fig.2d, e, g). This indicates that plants experiencing Nr additions may optimize their C allocation belowground for resource acquisition through increasing root length and surface area over investment in AMF24–26. Finally, Nr input and warming induced a shift in the AMF community towardParaglomeraceaeand/orGigasporaceaewith more extensive extra-radical hyphae than commonly dominantGlomeraceaespecies (Fig.3a,b). This shift may represent a response mechanism through which plants adjust their photosynthate allocation in response to altered resource limitations61,62, such as reduced water and P availability. Together, the observed alterations in root traits and AMF communities under Nr input and warming directly affect the C exchange and turnover at the root-soil interface and cascade to affect soil C dynamics.

Our study provides direct evidence illustrating that changes in root and AMF traits induced by Nr input and warming play pivotal roles in mediating soil C dynamics through multiple mechanisms (Figs.2,4, and6). First, alterations in root traits and AMF communities can directly affect organic C input from plants. Root life span often negatively correlates with SRL and positively correlates with RTD41,42, as thinner, less dense roots with high SRL and low RTD are characterized by higher rates of mortality and turn over faster. Increased fine-root turnover under Nr input may increase ROOT-derived C input63, but decreased AMF extra-radical mycelial biomass likely reduces plant-derived C input. Second, altered C input may alter microbial decomposition of organic C. As soil saprophytic microbes are generally more C-limited rather than N and P-limited in temperate grasslands, this labile C from root turnover likely to enhance growth of saprotrophic microbes and production of exoenzymes for degrading residues, stimulating the decomposition of soil organic C64,65, as evidenced by the significant negative correlation between root turnover and residue-derived C remaining in ROOT-ingrowth cores (Supplementary Fig.17e). Third, shifts in root traits and AMF community likely alter abiotic and biotic protection of organic C against microbial attacks66. Because plant roots and AMF hyphae are major binding agents that enmesh microaggregates into macroaggregates43,67, decreased RTD and/or AMF under Nr input (Fig.2f–h) may reduce the formation and stability of macroaggregates, diminishing soil C protection. At the micron and molecular scales, chemical bindings between organic C compounds and reactive minerals are key in maintaining the C persistence in soil66,68. While the Ca-OC binding primarily confers soil C stability in Ca-rich calcareous soils, Fe-OC linkage is believed to be essential for C stability in acidic soils69,70. Roots with AMF reduced Fe- and Ca-OC under Nr input and warming, respectively (Fig.5a,b), suggesting that root-AMF associations may promote the turnover of mineral-associated C and reduce the stability of SOC under future Nr input and warming scenarios. Finally, AMF species differ in their capacity to generate secondary metabolites that contribute to the MAOC pool46,47.Glomeraceaespecies, but notParaglomeraceaeorGigasporaceaespecies, are believed to produce glycoprotein glomalins46, which function as a strong binding agent for microaggregates71. Additionally,Glomeraceaespecies are often associated with faster growth rate and higher turnover, leading to a great contribution of their necromass to the MAOC pool27. The positive correlations betweenGlomeraceaeabundance and AMF-derived C or MAOC, but the negative correlations betweenParaglomeraceaeand/orGigasporaceaeabundance and AMF-derived C or MAOC (Supplementary Fig.13), provide indirect evidence that different AMF lineages have distinct functional impacts on soil C dynamics, and that the observed community shift toward taxa with finer and less dense hyphae may reduce AMF capacity in enmeshing soil aggregates and protecting organic C.
Taken together, our results demonstrate that Nr input and warming induce multidimensional tradeoffs among different plant species, roots and AMF, and/or different AMF species (Fig.6). These tradeoffs enhance root and AMF turnovers and C availability for saprophytic microbes, but may reduce root- and AMF-mediated protection of soil organic compounds. This could promote SOC turnover and thus compromise the potential of soil C sequestration. These findings offer insights into plausible mechanisms underlying the impact of reactive N and warming on plant-microbial interactions and SOC dynamics, which is necessary for improving predictions of soil C balance and terrestrial C-climate feedbacks under future climate change scenarios.
The study was conducted in a grassland on the Loess Plateau at the Yunwu Mountains Natural Preserve (36°13’−36°19’N, 106°24’−106°28’E; ~2000 m a.s.l), northeast of Guyuan City, Ningxia Province, China. The site has a semi-arid climate with a mean annual precipitation (MAP) of ~455 mm, 60–70% of which occurs during the plant growing season (June to September). The mean annual air temperature is 6–7 °C, with the coldest in January (ca.−7 °C) and the hottest in July (ca. 23 °C). The soil is classified as a montane gray-cinnamon, Calci-Orthic Aridisol (Chinese classification) or Haplic Calcisol (FAO classification). The topsoil layer (0–10 cm) contained 40.2 g C and 4.0 g N kg−1soil, and had a pH(H2O)of ca. 8.048,49. The grassland is dominated byStipa grandis, Stipa przewalskyi, Carex spp, Artemisia sacrorum, Chrysanthemus lavandulifolium,andSaussurea alata51,56. Prior to 1986, the grassland was intensively managed, and farmers harvested all the aboveground biomass for fuel or animal forage over nearly 30 years. This long-term removal prevented the accumulation of plant residues on the soil surface.
A long-term climate manipulation experiment was initiated in June 2015 on a largely flat hilltop (Supplementary Fig.1). The experiment employed a full-factorial randomized complete block design with two treatment factors: N input (0 vs. 12 g N m−2yr−1) and temperature (ambient vs. warming). The four different treatments were then formed: (a) ambient temperature and no N addition (Control, Con), (b) N addition (N), (c) warming (W), and (d) combined N addition and warming (N + W). Each of the four replicate blocks contained four plots (each at 4 m × 4 m), separated by a 1.5-m buffer strip. Reactive N (12.0 g N m−2yr−1) was added as commercial, granular urea dissolved in water twice annually (6.0 g N m−2in mid-May and late-June) to simulate fertilization practices, which is common in many areas of the Loess Plateau to increase the yield of forage grass60,72. The N rate was chosen based on our previous study60showing that high N input but not low N input significantly altered plant community composition. The warming treatment was achieved using OTCs (Supplementary Fig.1) because electricity is not accessible in the remote Preserve. A shaded thermocouple and a portable temperature meter were used manually to measure air temperature (15 cm above the ground) and soil temperature (10 cm depth) during the growing seasons of 2018 and 2019. In 2021, air and soil temperatures were continuously monitored using the S20A5 probe (Xuzhou Fala Co., Ltd., Xuzhou, China). Soil moisture was measured using a TDR-100 probe (Spectrum Technologies Inc., Plainfield, IL, USA) during the growing seasons of 2018, 2019, and 2021.
Plant aboveground biomass and community composition were collected in mid-August 2018, 2019, and 202151,73. Two randomly placed 0.5 × 0.5 m quadrats per plot were harvested, and all living vascular plants were sorted to species, oven dried at 65 °C for 48 h, and weighed. Plants were classified into three functional groups: gramineous grasses, forbs and subshrubs. After biomass harvesting, three soil cores (5 cm in diameter, 0–10 cm depth) were collected and composited into one sample per plot. All the composite samples were stored in a cool box and transported by express mails within 2–4 days to the laboratory in Nanjing for processing. Aboveground biomass in all plots was clipped annually in early September after soil sampling, and clippings were removed from each plot to simulate animal grazing.
Soil samples were sieved (2 mm) to remove rocks and collect roots. Subsamples of soil and roots were immediately frozen at −20 °C for molecular analysis, while the remainder was kept at 4 °C for chemical and microbial analyses. All the microbial analyses were either completed or initiated within 2 to 4 weeks after the sieving. Roots separated from the soil were thoroughly washed, dried at 65 °C, and weighed to determine biomass. Root C and N were determined using an elemental analyzer (Vario micro cube, Elementar, Germany).
Soil inorganic-N (NH4+-N and NO3--N) and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) were extracted from 20 g (dry soil equivalent) of soil using 0.5 M K2SO4. Inorganic-N and DOC concentrations were quantified with a flow injection auto analyzer (Skalar SAN++, Skalar Inc., Breda, The Netherlands) and a total organic C analyzer (Vario TOC cube, Elementar, Germany), respectively. Soil available P was determined using the molybdate blue colorimetric method following the extraction with 0.5 M NaHCO374.
To assess how Nr input and warming affect fine-root dynamics, we employed ROOT ingrowth cores (10 cm tall × 5 cm i.d.; see Supplementary Fig.2for details) to determine root production, turnover and traits. Cores were filled with root-free field soil and installed in May 2018, September 2018, and September 2019. Soils in the ingrowth cores were collected from the study area (0–10 cm depth), sieved through a 2 mm mesh to remove roots and stones, and compacted to match the original soil bulk density. A total of 200 g of soil (dry weight equivalent) was added to each core. Cores were installed in May 2018, September 2018, and September 2019, and retrieved in September 2018, 2019, and 2021, respectively. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, cores installed in 2019 were retrieved in 2021. Newly grown roots were carefully separated from soil, washed with tap water and analyzed for root traits (see below for details). SRL, SRSA and RTD of fine roots were then calculated as ratios of root total length to dry mass (L/M), root surface area to dry mass (A/M), and fine root dry mass to its volume (M/V), respectively.
AMF colonization of roots was quantified using the gridline-intersect method76. Briefly, dried roots from each plot were cut into ~1 cm segments, and ~100 segments were randomly chosen and rehydrated in deionized water for ca. 10 h. The rehydrated root samples were then cleared in 10% (w/v) KOH at 90 °C for 30 min, and acidified in 1% (v/v) HCl for 1 h. The root segments were subsequently stained in an acidic glycerol-trypan blue solution (50% glycerol (v/v), 1% hydrochloric acid (v/v), and 0.05% trypan blue (w/v)) and heated for 30 min in a steamer. Stained roots were fully washed with water and spread on a Petri dish with gridlines, and examined for AMF colonization under ×40 magnification by counting ca. 100 intersects per sample, using a stereomicroscope (SZN71, Sunny Optical Technology Co., Ltd, China).
AM fungal biomass in soil was measured using the phospholipid fatty acid (PLFA) method77. Briefly, freeze-dried soil samples (5.0 g) were weighed to extract total lipids in a chloroform-methanol-citrate buffer (1:2:0.8, v/v/v) after shaking for 1 h. The extracted lipids were fractionated into neutral, glyco- and phospholipids on a silicic-acid column by eluting chloroform, acetone, and methanol, respectively. The separated fatty acid methyl esters were analyzed by a gas chromatograph (GC) (7890A, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) coupled with a MIDI peak-identification system (MIDI, Inc., Newark, DE, USA). It is worth mentioning that the PLFA 16:1ω5 biomarker can also be present in Gram-negative bacteria78. However, since the neutral lipid fatty acid (NLFA)/PLFA 16:1ω5 ratio measured in the present study was larger than one (see Supplementary Methods and Supplementary Fig.7g,hfor details), we consider the PLFA 16:1ω5 to be a good proxy for AMF biomass in this study.
Composition of AMF communities in roots and soil were analyzed through Illumina MiSeq sequencing of the SSU rRNA gene in the Glomeromycota79,80. DNA was extracted from 0.05 g of frozen root and 0.3 g of soil using FastDNA Spin and PowerSoil DNA Isolation Kits, respectively (MoBio Laboratories Inc, CA, USA), following the manufacturer’s procedure. A two-step PCR amplification of all root and soil DNA extracts was performed using primer sets of GeoA2/AML2 (first round; CCAGTAGTCATATGCTTGTCTC/GAACCCAAACACTTTGGTTTCC) and NS31F/AMDGR (second round; TTGGAGGGCAAGTCTGGTGCC/CCCAACTATCCCTATTAATCAT). In the first PCR reaction, primers were connected to partial MiSeq sequencing adapters and barcodes. In the second one, the full adapters served as primers. Each 25 µL PCR reaction was performed with 12.5 µL 2×Taq Plus Master Mix, 2 µL bovine serum albumin (BSA), 7.5 µL ddH2O, 1 µL of each primer, and 1 µL of the template DNA. The thermal cycling conditions were run at 94 °C for 5 min; 30 cycles of 94 °C for 30 s, 59 °C for 30 s, and 72 °C for 1 min; final extension at 72 °C for 7 min. In the second reaction, diluted product (10×) of the first PCR reaction served as the template DNA. The PCR products were separated by 1.5% agarose gel electrophoresis, and purified with an Agencourt AMPure XP Kit (Beckman Coulter, Inc., USA). DNA concentration was quantified using a Nanodrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, Wilmington, DE, USA). The purified PCR products were then normalized to equimolar amounts, and sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform (Illumina Inc., San Diego, CA, USA) at Allwegene Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China).
Quantitative Insights Into Microbial Ecology 2 (QIIME 2) toolkit was used to process raw high-throughput sequencing data81. Briefly, the “demux” plugin was used to demultiplex the raw sequences based on their unique barcodes of each sample. Primers were then trimmed from the raw reads using Cutadapt v. 1.1082. Low-quality reads (the quality score below 30, fewer than 200 bp in length, and/or containing ambiguous nucleotides) were filtered and removed. Potential chimeric reads were also detected and removed using Vsearch v. 2.7.083. The remaining sequences were clustered into different operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at a 97% identity cutoff using the UPARSE algorithm84. Finally, the MaarjAM database (http://www.maarjam.botany.ut.ee)85of published Glomeromycotina sequences was used to assign taxonomic information. Each AMF sequence was grouped into a corresponding virtual taxon (VT) based on the following parameters: the sequence identity (≥97%), query coverage (≥97%), e-value (<1e-50) and BLAST bit score (>200)85. Each sample was rarefied to the lowest number of sequences using R package “vegan” with “rarefy” function86to standardize sampling effort.
To assess the effects of roots and AMF on soil C dynamics, we quantified ROOT- and AMF-derived C inputs to soil, evaluated soil C stability by measuring MAOC, mineral-associated C fractions, and microbial necromass C, and assessed residue organic C decomposition using ingrowth-cores with three different mesh sizes (Supplementary Fig.2).
To quantify the contributions of plant roots and associated AMF to soil C inputs, we employed a dual ingrowth-core isotopic technique63,87, taking advantage of the difference in13C signature between C3and C4plants. Because AMF are obligate biotrophs, we used three types of ingrowth cores to isolate the effects of roots and AMF. Three different mesh sizes were used to either completely exclude AMF hyphae (no-hyphae core, 0.45 µm pore size), allow AMF hyphal penetration (hyphae-ingrowth core, 25 µm pore size), or permit penetration of both AMF and fine roots (ROOT-ingrowth core, 1.8 mm pore size) (Supplementary Fig.2). The C4soil, obtained from a long-term corn field nearby, had a similar texture to our field soil. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the cores remained in situ for 2 years before retrieval in 2021. To quantify how Nr input and warming affected the contribution of roots and AMF to soil C stabilization via Ca and Fe mineral associations, we determined Ca-associated organic C (Ca-OC) and Fe-associated organic C (Fe-OC) using a modified sequential extraction method70. Briefly, 0.25 g of the heavy fraction soil samples collected above were first extracted with 40 mL of 0.5 M sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) for 2 h. Organic C released during Na2SO4extraction was considered as Ca-OC. Then, the remaining residue was extracted with dithionite-citrate-bicarbonate (DCB, containing 0.27 M trisodium citrate, 0.11 M sodium bicarbonate and 0.1 M sodium dithionite) at a 1:50 dry soil-to-solution ratio. The suspension was vortexed for 1 min, shaken for 16 h, and centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min. Dissolved organic C in the supernatant suspension was measured as Fe-OC. Several blanks only containing DCB and deionized water were determined to correct C concentrations in the samples to eliminate the influence of C in DCB.
To assess the influence of roots and AMF on microbial necromass within the SOC pool, we analyzed amino sugars as biomarkers of microbial residues. Soil samples from each ingrowth core were air-dried and sieved (<0.25 mm). Amino sugars (glucosamine, galactosamine, mannosamine, and muramic acid) were extracted and quantified following the standard protocol by ref.89. Briefly, samples were hydrolyzed with 6 M HCl at 105 °C for 8 h, withmyo-inositol added as an internal standard. The organic phase was evaporated at 45 °C with N2, and then resuspended in a 1:1 ethyl acetate-hexane solution. The amino sugars were separated and quantified using a GC (7890A, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) equipped with an HP-5 column and a flame ionization detector.

All analyses were performed using R 4.1.290. The linear mixed model was used to examine the main effects of Nr input, warming and their interaction, and whether these changed over time. To account for repeated measurements, a first-order autoregressive function (corCAR1) in the “nlme” package91was included in the model. Then, in each year, two-way ANOVAs were conducted to further analyze the effects of Nr input and warming on root, AMF and soil variables, including Nr input and warming as fixed factors and block as a random effect (“lmer” function from the lme4 package)92. Tukey’s multiple comparisons test was performed for comparisons among the four different treatments. Pearson correlations were used to examine the relationships of AMF and root with soil/root variables, and with the dynamics of AMF- and ROOT-derived C. PCA was performed to visualize the root trait and AMF colonization coordination using the “prcomp” function with scaling option in the “stats” package93.
To determine the effects of Nr input and warming on the community composition of AMF in soil and plant roots, PERMANOVA with 9999 permutations was carried out using the “adonis2” function in the package “vegan” in R86. To illustrate the differences in AMF communities in soil and roots between the four treatments, nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) with Bray–Curtis distances was performed using the “metaMDS” function in the “vegan” package86. The significant differences were determined at the 95% probability level.
