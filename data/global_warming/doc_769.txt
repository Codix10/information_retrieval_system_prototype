Climate change is affecting weather and climate extremes globally. It has been a subject of debate and controversy leading to the emergence of climate deniers and skeptics. It is a subject of great relevance because of its wide-ranging impacts on socioeconomic and natural systems. This necessitates long-term strategic decisions and response measures. There is a gap between the general public and the scientific community in terms of their awareness, understanding, and perception of climate change. Responding to the global climate crisis requires different actions at various levels, including individual. However, the manner in which the public and societies at large act in response to climate change is dependent on their perceptions and beliefs of climate change. This makes understanding the common opinion on climate change salient. There is an overrepresentation of climate change public opinion research in western nations in comparison with developing countries. This work thus contributes to filling this gap by engaging with members of the public who experience flooding in Nigeria to understand their perceptions and opinions on climate change. Qualitative research was carried out with focus group interviews and semistructured one-on-one interviews as primary data collection tools. The research findings indicate that there is a consensus that climate change is occurring, as evidenced by changes in weather patterns over the years. However, there were differences in opinion among the participants on how it was presenting. This work thus yields key insights on the level of awareness of the climate phenomena in a developing African city. Knowledge of climate change can encourage the public to engage more with the climate crisis, act in their own way, and even mobilize to influence and support government policies towards mitigating climate change.

Climate change is affecting weather and climate extremes all over the world (IPCC, 2021). It is a complex phenomenon and a function of multiple causes. It presents different physical characteristics and consequences that can lead to a varying range of risks (Shi et al., 2016), one of which is flooding. The changing global climate has been a topic of great public debate and controversy in contemporary times, leading to the emergence of climate deniers, contrarians, and skeptics (Bain et al., 2012;O’Neill & Boykoff, 2010). One subject of this debate is whether climate change is occurring or not and how it is being observed. This means there are proponents and opponents in the climate change discourse. Flooding, droughts, bushfires, heat waves, and other extreme weather events like hurricanes and tornadoes are some of the ways climate impacts are being felt around the world (Fatorić & Seekamp, 2017;Rankoana, 2016;Toan et al., 2014). The occurrence of such extreme weather events is expected to rise in the future. In Nigeria, such events are already being observed in the intensity and frequency of rainfall and occurrence of more frequent flooding events (Echendu & Georgeou, 2021;Echendu, 2020a). In the literature, concern about climate change has been found to be highest in countries that are most vulnerable to its negative impacts (Corner et al., 2014).
The reality and degree of climate change is a subject of great relevance to climate researchers, global organizations, and countries. This is because of its wide-ranging impacts on socioeconomic and natural systems that necessitate long-term strategic decisions and response measures (Kriegler et al., 2012;van Ginkel et al., 2020). There is also a gap between the general public and the scientific community in terms of their awareness, understanding, and perception of climate change-associated risks (Etkin & Ho, 2007).
Public opinion climate change studies can reveal factors that influence individual behaviors, which makes it easier to design tailored intervention and communication strategies that can foster sustainable changes and actions on various scales (individual, community, regional, and international levels) as well as directly influence climate policies. Public support is thus critical for government policies to succeed. A congruence between public views and government actions fosters the effective implementation of policies (Drews & van den Bergh, 2016). Responding to the impacts of climate change, therefore, requires good understanding of the phenomenon, active public participation, and engagement (Few et al., 2007;Hügel & Davies, 2020).
Climate is a term that describes the typical weather range or mean weather conditions for a region. It is a statistical phenomenon (Weber, 2010). In the meteorological sense, climate change refers to the systematic and usually gradual variations in average conditions of a region which determines the weather (Weber, 2010). It is, therefore, a reference to mean climate conditions over an extended period and not what is observed on a daily or seasonal basis. This implies that climate change itself is not directly observable by individuals (Spence et al., 2011). Nevertheless, weather and seasonal events are the primary ways people observe and experience the climate making it rational that it is a means to judge climate change (Spence et al., 2011).
There is an imbalance in the literature on studies of public perceptions of climate change on a global scale, with majority of the work focusing on western countries (Capstick et al., 2015;Vignola et al., 2013). This is not entirely unexpected due to greater research resources in developed countries versus developing countries. The result is that there are many unknowns about the public discernment of the phenomenon, perceptions, and awareness in African countries (Simpson et al., 2021). Available research on the subject in Africa specifically has overly focused on rural areas and farmers (Amaechina et al., 2022;Asiyanbi, 2015;Njoya et al., 2022). Changing dynamics and increased exposure to climate risks in cities of developing countries necessitate that climate change engagement deepens with urban residents where climate risks and exposure are most concentrated and rising (Asiyanbi, 2015;Jongman, 2018;Simon, 2010).
Also, in certain parts of the world, there has been a rise in public skepticism regarding climate change from the late 2000s (Brechin & Bhandari, 2011;Whitmarsh & Capstick, 2018). Findings from many developed countries show a mixed picture as to the public perception around climate change (Taylor et al., 2014). In developing countries as well, there are varying results on climate change perception among the public. For example, research with middle class Nigerians and the Costa Rican public revealed a high level of climate change awareness and concern (Asiyanbi, 2015;Vignola et al., 2013) whereas Filipinos self-reported low levels of climate change knowledge (Bollettino et al., 2020). There was also a low level of climate change awareness in research conducted in Pakistan (Ashraf et al., 2016).
Nevertheless, there is still a significant gap in knowledge on how public understanding is evolving or has evolved in the developing parts of the world although they remain the most susceptible to climate risks and impacts (Codjoe et al., 2014;Elshirbiny & Abrahamse, 2020).
The rising experience of weather conditions outside historical ranges underscores the growing need for researchers, policymakers, and communicators to be aware of how these changes influence and are influenced by public perceptions and opinions (Howe et al., 2019). As climate impacts continue to be felt across the globe, it is critical to have better knowledge on the temporal shifts and progressions of public perceptions on a more global scale (Capstick et al., 2015). A large body of research on understanding public attitudes and perceptions of climate change has mainly used surveys and statistical analyses (Bergquist et al., 2022;Bouman et al., 2020;Khan et al., 2020;Paudel et al., 2020). However, a growing body of work is beginning to use qualitative methods, given the deeper insight they yield and opportunities for deeper engagement on the subject, which can positively inform communication and action (Bruine de Bruin et al., 2021). The need for mixed methods studies has also been highlighted and recognized, with a growing body of work now also using mixed methodologies (Chapman et al., 2016;Elshirbiny & Abrahamse, 2020;Furberg et al., 2018).
Using qualitative methods, this research engaged with members of the general public in Port Harcourt, a major Nigerian city, to gauge their perceptions and opinion on climate change. This research is part of a larger project with residents with experience of flooding in Port Harcourt city, Nigeria. This larger project sought to incorporate both expert stakeholder knowledge and the public in flood risk management (Echendu, 2023). In recent years, Port Harcourt city has experienced increased annual flooding. The guiding research question was what are the perceptions on climate change and how is climate change manifesting? This article reports on the findings of this public engagement. Following this introduction section is an overview of the research location. This is followed by the research method. The next section presents the findings followed by the application of the research. The conclusion section summarizes the research.
Fieldwork and data collection took place in Nigeria. Engagement with members of the public took place in Port Harcourt (seeFigure 1), a key Nigerian city and its fourth largest, with a population of about 2 million (Nlerum & Wechie, 2018). Port Harcourt (PH) is the capital of Rivers state, one of Nigeria’s 36 states. It is a coastal city in the heart of Nigeria’s Niger Delta region. It is an economically important city that houses a significant portion of Nigeria’s crude oil operations. The city lies at N 4°42'–4°47' and E 6°55'–7°08' in the tropics and about 20 km inland from the Bonny River (Dan-Jumbo, 2017). The city is experiencing climate variability as evidenced in the increase in mean annual rainfall as tracked over the years from 1950 to 2015 as well as an increase in average temperature for the same period (Efe & Weli, 2015). Nigeria has rainy and dry seasons. However, PH has a longer rainy season duration, with the heaviest rainfall occurring in September (Wizor & Mpigi, 2020). The city, like many others around the globe, is already experiencing climate change impacts (Echendu & Georgeou, 2021;Greenwalt et al., 2020), with many residents exposed to flood risk. Given the connection between flooding and climate change, the city was an ideal location to understand local perceptions of climate change.

This was a qualitative study that adopted a constructivist/interpretivist approach and worldview. Qualitative research views knowledge as a product of individual experiences and allows for the understanding of a given topic from the standpoint of the study participants. Constructivists contend that perception of environmental issues is more or less socially constructed and a product of the politics of knowledge (Asiyanbi, 2015;Bird, 1987). An interpretivist approach, on the other hand, allows for the examination in detail, of how people conceptualize phenomena and situates and interprets their understanding. It examines the specifics and nuances of their experiences and emphasizes the points of convergence and divergence (Echendu, 2020b). This approach thus allows for a more nuanced exploration of the research participants’ perspectives and how they construct meaning from a particular phenomenon (Echendu, 2020b;Ochieng, 2009;Teherani et al., 2015), in this instance, climate change. This approach enables a deep understanding of how the participants perceived climate change according to their individual experiences and observations.
A total of 31 participants took part in this research. They were directly recruited during a previous stage of the larger project during which they took part in surveys that sought to collect data on their flood experiences and engagement in environmental planning processes. The data collection areas/locations were purposively selected based on parts of the city prone to flooding in both the formal (Wizor & Mpigi, 2020) and informal settlements of the city. After the purposive selection of the research locations, these flood prone areas were divided into clusters and probability cluster sampling was deployed for the surveys. Cluster sampling saves time and money, and the random nature of this sampling ensures that any part of the clusters has an equal chance of being selected. This allows for generalization (Taherdoost, 2016).
Figures 2and3show the specific locations participants were recruited from. During the survey data collection, where every third house was surveyed, the participants were asked if they would be interested in participating in the subsequent qualitative part of the project, and some of them indicated interest. They were later contacted by the research team and a convenient time chosen by both the interviewers and interviewees. The 31 participants in this study are a subset of the total/initial survey participants where 401 valid questionnaire responses were received. The youngest participant was aged 22 years and the oldest was 68. They were all given pseudonyms to protect their identities. The participants in this research were from different walks of life (predominantly lower and middle class) with occupations in trading, civil service, clergy, and the financial sector. Their educational level ranged from only primary level education to postgraduate level education. They also lived in the different locations in the city, including the formal and informal settlements also known as Waterside. The heterogeneity of the participants allowed for diverse voices to be part of the research.

A mix of focus group discussions (FGD) and semistructured interviews were used in data collection. Focus group discussions were chosen as a data collection method of choice because it enables for collection of rich data, with the moderator able to interact with participants and pose follow-up questions or clarify questions to the participants. It also allows for quick collection of data in comparison to individual interviews and is cost effective (Leung & Savithiri, 2009). However, some downsides of FGD include the possibility of participants responses to be influenced by that of others and the likelihood of some participants dominating the conversation (Leung & Savithiri, 2009). The original plan was to conduct FGD with all the participants, but due to scheduling issues, one-on-one interviews had to be conducted for those participants who could not participate in the group discussions. This approach further enriched the findings, as the bias of groupthink, where one person’s idea could influence that of others as is likely in a FGD, was eliminated with the one-on-one interviews. One-on-one interviews also have some advantages, including flexibility. This method also allows for a topic to be discussed in a more in-depth manner. However, it is time-consuming and less time efficient than FGD. Four focus group discussions were thus held with 21 participants and 10 one-on-one semistructured interviews were conducted. This took place from April to May 2022 in PH. There were five participants in three of the focus groups discussions and six persons participated in one of them. The male participants were 11 in number and there were 20 female participants. This research was purposefully designed to spotlight and elevate female voices, given that Nigeria is a patriarchal society where women’s voices are largely relegated to the background (Bako & Syed, 2018;Jaiyeola, 2020). As more women than men are rendered vulnerable by flooding due gender ascribed roles (Akintoye et al., 2016), this work also sought to incorporate more of their perspectives by involving more of them in this work.Tables 1and2are lists of focus group and semistructured interview participants.

In qualitative studies of this nature, the goal is to elicit rich information from a small group of participants (Bazen et al., 2021) until a saturation point is reached (Guest et al., 2020). The number of participants in this research provided rich responses and a saturation point was reached where the participants did not provide any new information.
The interviews were recorded and transcribed verbatim in Microsoft Word. This was later imported into QSR NVIVO, a qualitative data analysis software. The data were analyzed inductively. Induction is a systematic data analysis approach commonly used in qualitative research. The researcher uses abstraction to arrange and condense data to be able to respond to the research questions using concepts, categories, or themes (Kyngäs, 2020). The interview transcripts were read many times to ascertain key emerging themes and an initial coding frame was developed. This was guided by both the research questions and participants’ responses. Coding is the process of organizing and sorting the often voluminous raw texts into clearer themes and categories (Seers, 2012). QSR NVIVO was used to carry out the final thematic coding, which simplified the process. Ethics clearance for this research was obtained from the graduate research ethics board of Queen’s University, Kingston, Ontario.Figures 2and3are maps highlighting the locations in the city where the participants lived.
Understanding public perceptions of climate change is important, as this can influence the implementation of suitable and effective action and policy measures to combat climate risks (Shi et al., 2015). Within the local research parlance, one of these rising climate threats is annual flooding whereby the connection between climate change and extreme weather events, including flooding, has been established (Echendu, 2022;Sloggy et al., 2021). Experiences with extreme weather events can shape the perceptions of climate change (Haq & Ahmed, 2020;Ruiz et al., 2020). The overarching public view on the causes of extreme events, including climate change, can inhibit or motivate local-level action as well as adoption of policies aimed to mitigate climatic risks (Albright & Crow, 2019). Conversations with residents on their views on climate change revealed a wide range of opinions. Participants were asked whether they believe there have been changes over the years in relation to climate patterns in terms of frequency of rain, intensity of rain, heat, and longer or shorter seasons and given opportunities to expatiate on their responses. They were also asked about changes in flooding patterns in terms of frequency, duration, etc.
The female participants in this research mostly believed there have been changes in climate patterns and had varying opinions on how this presents. Some of them stated that the rains are less now, whereas others believed that the rains have increased in intensity and frequency. Joy was one of those who believed the rain intensity has reduced but the sun’s intensity is higher nowadays. In her words, “In my area, I have observed that the sun’s intensity has increased. It is now very high. Very, very high. The rain does not fall as much, but the heat has really intensified.”
Another participant (an older woman in her sixties) was of the view that the rains are also less now than when she was a child. By her account, “it used to rain all day from 5 a.m. to 5 p.m. on a frequent basis. I would say there has been a bit of change as we don’t have that type of rain anymore. It falls less now. What we have now is it will rain in the morning, stop a bit before raining again.” She told an adage that says, ‘if you follow the rainy season, you will go hungry,’ implying that even when it is pouring all day like it used to in those days, according to her, one still must go about their business to be able to put food on the table. The longer duration of rain she experienced as a child in comparison to these days when rain falls, then pauses before starting again, was her own barometer for judging that there is less rainfall these days. For Bunmi as well, the rain does not fall as much anymore. She says, “The heat is too much these days, the rain is not that much unlike before.” Associations between climate change and “heat” or temperature increase have also been frequently reported in research in other parts of the world (Smith & Leiserowitz, 2012;Taylor et al., 2014;Whitmarsh, 2008b). This implies that certain perceptions of climate change like heat are widely experienced or perceived across the world and not unique to the study location.
Other participants expressed their views:
“I don’t think I take record of the consistency [of weather patterns]. I know from my experience since I came to Port Harcourt that the effect of the season varies …. the rainy season affects us in different ways. Some people say it’s usually worse every 5 years, but I have not taken time to study that or record how true that statement is. But I know sometimes it’s quite heavy and sometimes it’s not as heavy as it’s supposed to be. Like last year I know that we didn’t have much rain….” Amara
“During the rainy season, rain falls continuously. I will say in September, we are not supposed to have much rain but these days in November, it is still raining. However, the rains we are seeing now does not compare to before when we used to have more rains…” Ego
“For a few years now there has been some kind of climatic change. The rain starts earlier and that increases the amount of water coming down to this side [the neighbourhood] and constitutes flooding. Some years too, the rain starts later but is much heavier. You then see that even though rain started later in the year, it is so intense that we have floods in spite of the fact that it didn’t start on time. Climatic changes affect the rainfall….” Uju
It is not out of place that the participants have differing opinions on how climate change has presented over the years given the difficulty in following weather patterns by mere observation. Research on public perception of climate change in different parts of the world has also yielded varying results on how it is being experienced (Bollettino et al., 2020;Haq & Ahmed, 2017;Thomas & Benjamin, 2018;Vignola et al., 2013) even where there is a general consensus that there are climatic changes, such as in this study.
The unreliability of the seasons was another factor highlighted by the participants. Nigeria has two seasons, rainy and dry, lasting for an average of 6 months each. However, certain parts of the country are known to experience longer rainy or drier seasons due to geographical factors and features. Our research location, PH, particularly experiences longer and heavier rainy season (Nkwocha et al., 2017). Currently, however, as was highlighted by the research participants, it is common to experience rainfalls all year long. Even tidal flooding, according to some of the residents of the informal settlements, had a season, but this is no longer the case. Season variability is one of the manifestations of climate change (Kogo et al., 2021).
In the words of some of the participants:
“I would say for the rainy seasons, certain times rain falls continuously. I would say from September, it isn’t supposed to rain that much but these days, by November it still rains…” Ego
“The flooding does not have any season anymore. We used to have time for the big one and the small one. The big one usually comes in October but it is no longer the case these days….” Nengi
“Actually, ….progressively, it has gotten worse… I know this because I started living here in 2011 that is when I got married. So, I know that rainfall has been very somehow [unpredictable]. Sometimes, it comes as if it wants to destroy everything. It has been happening more and more in recent years and for rainfall I know that rain patterns have changed, and rain falls erratically unlike before. We used to know that in April, rain will fall but it’s no more like that. In December, rain can be falling, in January rain can be falling, there’s no particular time. Just like the increase in heat, heat is on another level and another scale so those are like the three things I’ve observed…”IJ
“Yes, there have been changes in the climate, there have been changes in the climate. In the past we experience both dry and rainy season equally but in recent times, it has been raining heavily. For example, from January to November, we experience heavy rainfall even in December. But in those days, it doesn’t fall heavily in December period. This time around we also have hot weather to the extent that most of us spend most of our time outside to receive natural air.” Connie
As highlighted by these interview excerpts, some of the research participants also connected their experiences with the changing climatic conditions to the flooding they experience. This was not out of place, as personalized experiences of environmental phenomena influence perceptions of weather conditions and weather-related extreme events like floods and heat waves (Howe et al., 2019). Lagos residents have also linked their recent experiences of severe floods with climate change (Asiyanbi, 2015).
Among the male participants, there was a consensus that there are changes in climatic patterns, with most of them also complaining of the increase in hot weather patterns. There are also references to both rainfall and heat increasing in intensity and frequency by the participants. Only one participant stated the heat is less these days, but the rainy season is now longer than in previous years. In his words, “it has not always been like this. We have been having longer rainy season but not much that heat …” Chuks
Chidi provided a more detailed account of his experience with the climate patterns and relates it to flooding, saying
“What I want to say about the flooding is that for the past 4-5 years now, we have had a serious flooding in this area and the frequency of the rainfall has been high compared to this very year. And what I have observed during the early parts of this year in January and late February is that the environment was very hot, but there was not much rainfall. Not until the ending of February to the start of May before we started experiencing rainfall. That March, the rainfall was not that heavy and the middle part of March we observed heat as well too. The environment was kind of hot, not until late March and April until the environment became a bit calm. But this time, for rain, the frequency of rain has been very poor. The rainfall has not been that high compared to other years that’s my own observation…” Chidi
This interview with Chidi took place in early May 2022, which is the reason that his account stopped in April. Other participants also expressed their views:
“The seasons are no longer stable like before. I know some 7 years back during the dry season, rain hardly falls. But these days, you can’t differentiate the dry season from the rainy season. Sometimes in the rainy season, the sun will shine for three straight weeks. I think this is because of climate change…” Peter
“For over 20 years now things are getting worse, too much heat during the night. Some of us here don’t even sleep….” John
“It’s a general thing all over the world that climate change is imminent, but it depends on what climate change you are talking about. Is it positive or negative? But we are looking at the negative aspect. There’s quite a difference between the early 70s and now. There’s a huge climate change ….you can see that the rain now falls heavily which now causes a lot of flooding everywhere…” Amakiri
Overall, the participants were of the opinion that there has been a change in climatic conditions over the years. Certainly, climate change cannot be directly perceived, but weather-related extremes (floods, wildfires, etc.) and changing local weather conditions are some of the ways humans can perceive climate change (Howe et al., 2019). The participants in this study expressed and experienced these changes in terms of high heat conditions, seasonal variabilities, and erratic rainfall patterns. The overall opinion aligned with the views of participants inAdelekan and Gbadegesin (2005)’s research in Ibadan, Nigeria, where 92% of their surveyed respondents were aware of the dynamics of local climate change. Some other studies on public perception of climate change in Nigeria found that the majority of the public believe that climate change was happening and associate it with variations in rainfall pattern, heat, etc. (Akpan et al., 2020;Amos et al., 2015;Asekun-Olarinmoye et al., 2014). Some other research, however, found low levels of climate change awareness (Asifat Dr, 2019;Egbue et al., 2014;Oluwafemi, 2019).
Indeed, across Nigeria, variations on climate change awareness have been found to range from 5% to 71% among the different states (Simpson et al., 2021). This points to varying levels of climate change awareness among the different states on a national level. The findings of this research do not support previous research findings that 65% of survey respondents in some countries, including Nigeria, have never heard of climate change (Lee et al., 2015). The differing findings could also be attributed to the differences in quantitative and qualitative research. In qualitative research, there are opportunities for participants to seek clarifications on research questions. This enables a better understanding, in contrast to survey based quantitative research that was administered online as was reported in the work ofLee et al. (2015).
There is some evidence that experience of ecological disasters can affect environmental views of local communities affected (Spence et al., 2011). However, there is no consensus on the link between experience of disaster to positive climate change perceptions (Carlton et al., 2016;Osaka & Bellamy, 2020). This implies that experience of extreme events and changing climate beliefs is not straightforward and that experience of lone extreme events might not be enough to influence climate change perceptions (Carlton et al., 2016). These contradictory findings highlight the overall dissonance in climate perception studies.
There is also the view that personal experience with climate-related events may not be a cause for change of opinion on climate change (Weber, 2010). There have been varying results on the connection of personal experience to perceptions and beliefs of climate change (Carlton et al., 2016;Reser & Bradley, 2020). With regard to flooding disasters, there is some evidence of heightened awareness of flood risk after flood experience. However, participants still viewed flooding as a distinct issue from climate change, identifying instead local more direct causes of flooding like poor maintenance of water pathways (Whitmarsh, 2008a). For example, participants inWhitmarsh (2008a) research viewed flooding as a distinct issue from climate change, identifying instead local more direct causes of flooding such as poor maintenance of water pathways.
The participants in this research believed climate change was happening and narrated their personal observations regarding weather patterns. Perceptions of climate change by communities have centered mainly around observations of variations in temperature and rainfall patterns (Rankoana, 2016). This research yielded interesting results whereby the participants were all flood victims and had a positive perception that climate change is manifesting as evidenced by the changing weather patterns over the years. Yet there was no agreement in how, as seen in the different accounts of changes in the rainfall pattern and heat. Some of the participants believed there were more rains, whereas others believed there were fewer rains. Action that follows from climate change perceptions can be informed by the different schools of thought on the phenomena. In this study, all the participants believed that climate change was real and supported their views with their differing personal observations and experiences.
Understanding local perspectives on climate change is important (Jurt et al., 2015), especially given the rising climate threats that necessitate urgent action across scales. There is evidence that in places where climate change is acknowledged, there is more support for relevant policies (Bromley-Trujillo & Poe, 2020). Moreover, climate change-linked anomalies, such as seasonal temperature deviations and heat waves, have been found to be linked to increased support for environmental policies (Hoffmann et al., 2022).
The participants in this research all experienced flooding; climate change has been linked to the rising incidences of flood disasters. This causative link is due to a warmer atmosphere causing more intense precipitation, as the amount of water evaporating into the air is increased by warmer oceans caused by climate change (Trenberth, 2012). When the moisture-filled air converges into a storm system or moves over land, it yields more precipitation (Kim et al., 2020;Trenberth, 2012). In recent years, the occurrence of extreme single-day precipitation events has increased substantially (Fowler et al., 2021). The overarching public view on the causes of extreme events, including climate change, can inhibit or motivate local-level action as well as adoption of policies aimed to mitigate climatic risks (Albright & Crow, 2019). Engagement with people with lived and living experience of flooding in PH city provided new insights on how people who experience disaster perceive climate change in their local environments. This is key, given that for developing countries, climate perception studies provide important sociological insights that can guide local climate action as well provide insights for channeling effective communication strategies. Such research is pertinent for sustainable and inclusive climate solutions that can work in specific local contexts.
Most research on public perception and opinions of climate change has been in the form of surveys and polls (Brody et al., 2007;Capstick et al., 2015;Mildenberger & Leiserowitz, 2017;Sloggy et al., 2021;Taylor et al., 2014). This style of engagement does not accord the opportunity to engage on a personal and more interactive manner with the public. This study has provided new and richer knowledge on public perception of climate change by conducting qualitative research with members of the public in an under-researched location. This adds to the global body of knowledge on climate change perception in Africa and adds to the global climate perception literature. Drawing on personal experiences on phenomena like the weather can help steer the theoretical nature of climate change from the abstract to the more concrete and familiar with relatable observations (Smith & Joffe, 2012). Such personal experiences of climate change can lead to genuine concern as higher trust is placed on personal direct experiences (Capstick & Pidgeon, 2014). The participants in this research were unanimous in their beliefs of climate change although there was no consensus as to how this is being experienced. Some of them stated there was more rain while some said the rain was less nowadays. Others believed that the heat has increased. Such more personal engagement reveals nuances that cannot be obtained in quantitative research. This highlights the need for more qualitative work in this regard.
Elsewhere, there has been lack of consensus on climate change observations with even decline on the acceptance reported in some western countries (Capstick & Pidgeon, 2014;Leiserowitz et al., 2015). Indeed, politicians, the general public, media, and scientists are known to disagree on climate change for both political and strategic reasons (Weber, 2010). There have also been calls to use words that actually depict urgency in communication of the climate emergency, as the use of euphemisms can undermine the goal of increasing climate change awareness and needed action (Grolleau et al., 2022).
For sub-Saharan African countries, however, there has been a growing recognition of climate change for reasons that are not yet very clear and that deserve more profound research attention (S.Capstick et al., 2015). All the participants in this study have experienced flooding. Individual experience with extreme weather events and disasters is likely to influence beliefs about climate change (Albright & Crow, 2019;Sloggy et al., 2021).Konisky et al. (2016)found a positive relationship between experiences of extreme weather events and concern about climate change whereby the effect is present only for recent experience, in contrast to events that occurred over longer periods of time. In this work, the same inference cannot be made, given that the participants observed the impacts of climate change differently. If they had all reported increase in rainfall patterns that cause flooding, then such an inference could be made. However, there was a unanimous acceptance that climate change is happening. It is also likely there is a relationship between participants’ experience of flooding and their positive view and beliefs on the manifestations of climate change, but this cannot be proven from this work. This is due to the lack of consensus on climate change and varying beliefs of the public in Nigeria and globally among the public. This necessitates further research.
Another key finding from this research centers around the mix of participants who were involved. They belonged to different social classes, with education levels ranging from only primary level education to postgraduate education. Yet, they all reported having positive experience and perceptions with climate change. This reveals a high level of awareness of climate change among a more diverse pool of participants. This adds more depth to previous work byAsiyanbi (2015)that only engaged with postsecondary educated middle-class Nigerians, which also found a positive perception of climate change.
Indeed, perceived changes in both wet weather-related events such as rainfall and hot weather-related events such as heat have been positively linked with climate change beliefs (Taylor et al., 2014). It has been found that even where some lower class and rural populations of developing countries do not have a similar western perception of climate change and associated risk, it does not imply less concern about climate changes (Asiyanbi, 2015). This study broadly focused on perceptions and experiences of climate change among the participants and not the causes of climate change. It is very likely that engagement on the causes may not yield a similar consensus, as was observed in this research. Understanding perceptions on the causes of climate change would yield key insights, because the extent of individuals’ understanding of the causes and impacts of climate may influence lifestyle decisions and political choices (Brody et al., 2007). The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change recognizes that climate change literacy and awareness positively correlate to climate action, especially as people who experience the impacts of climate change are more likely to consider climate change as a serious threat (Pörtner et al., 2019). The high awareness of climate change in this work has positive policy implications, as climate policies can garner wider public support, especially if the wider public is meaningfully involved in the process. On a global scale as well, increased understanding of the patterns of climate change awareness is crucial for rolling out better informed and tailored responses.
This work sought the opinion of members of the public who have experienced flooding to understand their perceptions of climate change. This was particularly pertinent as climate change has been linked to the flooding they experience. The participants in this research, despite their varying level of education and social class, believed there was climate change. They narrated their experiences with regard to changing weather patterns, rainfall, and increased heat. A common characteristic among the participants was that they all had lived and living experience of flooding. However, it is not certain from this work whether their common experience with a climate-related disaster influenced their perception, and this necessitates further research.
The manner in which the general public and societies act in response to climate change is dependent on their perceptions and beliefs about climate change. This makes understanding the common opinion on climate change salient. This can guide the adoption and implementation of policies that can help manage the impacts that are manifesting in costly extreme weather events. This knowledge is even more critical in countries of the developing world where research is lagging, although they are the worst hit by the impacts of climate change. Spotlighting the connection between local events like flooding and climate change can encourage the public to engage more with the climate problem, act in their own way, and even mobilize to influence the government to act to ameliorate likely impacts.
In terms of research on perceptions of climate change all over the world, quantitative methods have been the most used. Yet the use of qualitative methodologies has the potential to yield a better and more in-depth knowledge of the different contexts and on the evolution and beliefs of climate change and how it is manifesting. Expanding this research to include members of the public who do not have direct experience of flooding will provide better insight into overall perception of climate change. This research was also conducted in a southern geographical region of Nigeria with a wetter climate; expanding the research to the drier northern parts of the country would yield more knowledge on the manifestation and perception of climate change across both the wet and dry regions. Understanding what actions that could follow from these perceptions and what other factors influence Nigerians’ opinions aside from weather observations is also an area worthy of further research.
