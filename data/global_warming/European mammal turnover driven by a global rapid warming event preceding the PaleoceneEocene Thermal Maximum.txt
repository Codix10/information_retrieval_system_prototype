The Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM, 56 Mya) was a brief period of intense global warming, often considered a paleo-analogue of present-day climate changes. This event, which triggered a major mammalian turnover in the Northern Hemisphere, was preceded by a precursor warming event, the Pre-Onset Excursion (POE), whose influence on terrestrial biota remains unknown. In this study, we present a mammalian fauna from France, likely dated to the POE/PETM interval. Our findings indicate that taxa, previously thought to have migrated to Europe during the PETM, actually appeared shortly before, probably during the POE. Our findings suggest that the fluctuating latest Paleocene warming, although less intense than the PETM, was a major driver of an unexpected mammalian turnover in Europe.
Keywords:hyperthermals, intercontinental dispersal, Eutheria, Metatheria, carbon isotope excursions
A brief global warming event known as the Pre-Onset Excursion (POE) occurred just before the Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM, 56 Mya). The deconvolution of the evolutionary consequences of these two hyperthermal events is puzzling because of their close temporal proximity and the lack of comprehensive, well-calibrated paleontological records, especially in terrestrial environments. As a consequence, the impact of the POE on mammalian evolution and its role in shaping PETM faunas remains unclear. Here, we report from France a mammalian fauna, named Albas, which is interpreted to postdate the POE and predate the PETM. The absence of artiodactyls, perissodactyls, and euprimates at Albas lends support to the controversial hypothesis that these “modern” mammal groups appeared in the European fossil record during the PETM. In contrast, Albas yielded the European first definitive Paleocene record of metatherians, paromomyid primates, “creodonts,” and rodents, challenging the assumption that these groups migrated into Europe during the PETM. Because the majority of them originated from North American pre-POE species, we tentatively suggest that these “precursor” dispersers entered Europe during the POE. Similar to the modern orders during the PETM, these “precursor” dispersers likely entered Europe through corridors in the continuous evergreen forest belt at high latitudes. Our findings highlight how a brief warming event in the Arctic during the latest Paleocene, such as the POE (which could result in a release of carbon into the atmosphere similar to cumulative ongoing anthropogenic emissions), significantly influenced the evolutionary dynamics of European mammals.
The direct relationship between past warming events and biotic change is complex and challenging to ascertain. Notwithstanding, considerable progress has been made in this field of inquiry over the past decade, both in terms of methodology and field discoveries. The development of multidisciplinary approaches has facilitated the enhancement of integrated climatic, chronological, and paleontological datasets, which could be employed to anticipate the consequences of current climate changes on biodiversity degradation (1,2). The study of the Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), which occurred at the very beginning of the Eocene epoch (56 Mya), is of great importance in this respect (3,4). This brief period of global warming is indeed attributed to rapid and massive injections of13C-depleted carbon into the ocean–atmosphere system (5,6). Accordingly, the PETM is typically regarded as a paleo-analogue of present-day climate changes. It is therefore worth drawing parallels between biotic changes that occurred across the Paleocene/Eocene transition and those observed in modern ecosystems, in order to gain insight into the biodiversity dynamics and its potential future evolutionary paths. In this context, extensive research has demonstrated that the PETM was a significant driver of biotic turnover in terrestrial and marine ecosystems (3,7). In the terrestrial realm, the PETM triggered rapid mammalian dispersals, resulting in the sudden first appearance of three “modern” lineages of mammals (i.e., Artiodactyla, Perissodactyla, and Euprimates) in the fossil record of the Northern Hemisphere (8–10). This biotic event had a profound impact on the Eocene faunal assemblages, which during the Paleocene epoch were primarily endemic and included a wide array of “archaic” mammals (11–13).
Despite compelling evidence for the impact of the PETM on local mammalian communities, a global understanding remains elusive due to the incompleteness of the fossil record in many regions spanning the PETM interval. Indeed, only the North American fossil record is sufficiently well documented, in well-dated continuous sedimentary strata, to ensure the accuracy of evolutionary scenarios (8,14). In contrast, the European record of this interval is documented by a significantly smaller number of fossil-bearing localities. In addition, the majority of the species-rich faunas, which are from the London, Belgian, and Paris Basins, are derived from discontinuous sedimentary successions, thereby representing a major challenge in terms of correlation with the PETM (12,15). This contrasts sharply with the situation in northern Spain and southern France, where the PETM was recorded in expanded and well-exposed sedimentary strata linked to the Pyrenean orogeny (16–19). Unfortunately, the fossil record of mammals from these southern European deposits is scarce, with only a few specimens documented from the late (not the latest) Paleocene or the early (not the earliest) Eocene (20–22). As a result, the impact of the PETM on European mammals remains obscure. Here, we describe a pre-PETM mammal assemblage identified in a continuous Paleocene–Eocene sedimentary section from southern France. This section, which also yielded two diverse post-PETM mammal faunas (22), provides a unique succession for a detailed reinterpretation of the climate-calibrated macroevolution of European mammals across the PETM interval.
The studied stratigraphic section, designated “Les Espeyrols/Le Clot” (Occitanie region, southern France) encompasses a total of ~900 m thick of sedimentary rocks. The section is situated within the Corbières basin, which is a retroforeland basin located in the eastern North Pyrenean area (23). The basin was opened westward to the North Atlantic during the late Paleocene–early Eocene interval (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). The specific tectonic and paleogeographic context of this well-exposed sedimentary section offers a comprehensive and detailed sedimentological record, as well as a crucial fossil record (Fig. 1A). The Paleocene sedimentary succession, which is 510 m thick, comprises continental alluvial and palustrine deposits that conformably overly the Cretaceous/Paleogene transition. The terrestrial series are interrupted by two thin (<1 m thick) beds recording marine deposits, estimated to be late Paleocene in age (Fig. 1A) (24,25) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). At the regional scale, recent carbon isotope chemostratigraphic analyses conducted in the upper part of this terrestrial succession have identified a notable negative excursion in δ13C, which has been interpreted as the PETM (18–19). This terrestrial succession, which thus documents the Paleocene–Eocene boundary, is overlain by a third extended marl to limestone lagoonal/marine unit, attributed to the regional “Ilerdian” stage, i.e., earliest to early Eocene in modern terminology. This unit is exposed between 568.8 m and the top of the section studied (Fig. 1A). The lowest level of the Ilerdian deposits yielded the benthic foraminiferAlveolina avellana, which serves as a biostratigraphic marker for the earliest Eocene benthic foraminiferal zone SBZ5 (25). Finally, at the uppermost top of the stratigraphic sequence, a brief regressive level corresponding to terrestrial deposits, situated at 895.7 m, yielded the early Eocene (= ~MP7, following the European Mammal Paleogene scale) mammal-bearing locality of Le Clot (Fig. 1A) (22). This vertebrate locality lies within the upper part of zone SBZ8 (occurrence of the benthic foraminiferal taxaNummulites globulus nanus,Nummuliteslaxiformis, andAssilina leymerieiat 896.7 m) and within the lower part of the calcareous nannofossil zone NP11 (occurrence ofTribrachiatus orthostylusand absence ofT. contortusat 833.7 m). Moreover, previous δ13Corgchemostratigraphical data indicate that Le Clot predates the ETM2 hyperthermal (~54.1 Mya) (18). This biochemostratigraphic evidence thus indicates that Le Clot must be dated between ~54.3 (First Appearance Datum ofTribrachiatus orthostylus) and ~54.1 Mya. This locality is thus slightly younger than previously estimated (18). In the same subbasin, situated 7 km to the west, similar continental deposits, also situated at the top of the same marine unit, yielded the coeval mammalian locality of Fordones (SI Appendix, Fig. S1) (26). The mammalian fauna of Le Clot/Fordones comprises 22 species, including archaic mammals (“condylarths” and multituberculates) and some representatives of modern lineages, among which euprimate (Donrussellia magna), artiodactyl (Diacodexisaff.antunesi), and perissodactyl species (Cymbalophus hookeri,SI Appendix,Supporting Information Text, Table S1 and Fig. S2http://www.pnas.org/lookup/doi/10.1073/pnas.2505795122#supplementary-materials), which collectively indicate an early Eocene age, postdating the PETM (Fig. 1A).

The studied sedimentary section is therefore of primary importance for having recorded the PETM and early Eocene faunas in stratigraphic superposition. In fact, this section is a unique landmark because our recent extensive fieldwork in the uppermost part of the late Paleocene terrestrial succession has allowed us to identify a mammalian locality (called Albas) at 537.4 m, in a level just prior to the PETM (Fig. 1A). In order to exactly calibrate this fauna with the PETM, we conducted a comprehensive, high-resolution δ13Corgchemostratigraphical investigation, which updates our initial study in the same sedimentary section (18). We extended the chemostratigraphical study to the entire late Paleocene beds and a systematic sampling approach was employed at each lithological variation near the PETM (see Methods).
The δ13Corgvalues along the late Paleocene sediments show a long-term positive trend (from ~100 m to ~265 m) followed by a long-term negative trend (from ~265 m to ~430 m) (Fig. 1A). When compared with standard carbon isotope values from ocean drilling core sites spanning the Paleocene (e.g., ODP Site 1,209) (27), these two long-term trends respectively suggest a Selandian?/Thanetian and then a Thanetian age for the late Paleocene series from Albas. These dates are consistent with local biostratigraphic data from benthic foraminifers and charophytes (SI Appendix, Fig. S1).

The δ13Corgvalues exhibit a first negative carbon isotopic excursion, including a substantial shift from −22.4 ‰ at 516.1 m to −24.1 at 528.5 m (1.7 ‰), followed by an abrupt recovery to background values of −22.1 ‰ at 529.7 m. A second negative carbon isotopic excursion is observed slightly higher up, including a prominent shift from −21.9 ‰ at 537.8 m to −26.0 ‰ at 549 m (4.1 ‰) and a subsequent more stepwise recovery to background values at 567.9 m. These two negative excursions are recorded in the terrestrial pre-Ilerdian deposits, during which a nearly quiescent tectonic period was proposed for the Pyrenean orogeny (23).
The second isotopic excursion, which is thus more prominent and longer than the first one, onsets with a basal prominent negative inflection (−4.1 ‰) of the carbon isotope values (Fig. 1C). It has been assumed that such a basal prominent carbon isotope excursion (3 to 5 ‰) is the geochemical signature of the PETM worldwide, in both marine (deep and shallow) and terrestrial settings (3,5,8,28). The CIE of the PETM is actually the primary criterion of the Global Standard Stratotype-section and Point for the base of the early Eocene (29). In the studied area, the stratigraphic position of the PETM located in the upper part of the terrestrial succession, just below the first marine Ilerdian deposits, is also supported by other recent δ13Corgcorrelations acquired on the nearby “Lairière” section (19). The base of the PETM onset in the studied section starts with a thin gray limestone bed (marked III inFig. 1CandSI Appendix, Fig. S3) corresponding to a lacustrine environment. The sedimentary succession below this limestone bed is characterized by siliciclastic-dominated alluvial deposits. The transition to the onset of the PETM is thus marked by a cessation of the terrigenous input, which is replaced by lacustrine carbonate production.
Despite this variability in facies, the composition of the organic matter remains remarkably stable within the interval between the two isotopic excursions, as testified by the palynofacies study, which suggests an important organic terrestrial input (see details in Palynofacies Analysis section inSI Appendix, Table S3 and Fig. S4http://www.pnas.org/lookup/doi/10.1073/pnas.2505795122#supplementary-materials). This strongly shows that the fluctuating carbon isotopic values in this interval were not controlled by fluctuating, local organic sources, but rather by the regional/global carbon cycle.
With regard to the first isotopic excursion, a comparable pre-PETM hyperthermal event was also recorded at the regional level in some other Pyrenean foreland basins where similar high sediment-supply also provide a quite comprehensive carbon isotopic record. In particular, this event was recorded in the terrestrial sections of Serraduy and Esplugafreda of the Tremp-Graus basin, northern Spain, but also in the marginal marine section of Lussagnet of the Aquitaine basin, southern France (30) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). This excursion was attributed to the “Pre-Onset Excursion” (POE) of the PETM. At the global scale, this event was initially identified in the terrestrial deposits of the Bighorn Basin, Wyoming, USA (31). Additionally, pre-PETM excursions have been documented in a number of marginal marine deposits (Atlantic Coastal Plain, USA; Meghalaya area, India) (32–34) and rare bathyal deposits (Forada area, Italy) (35). The driver, timing, and duration of these pre-PETM excursions remain subjects of debate, and the question of whether they represent a single isochronous global event (i.e., the POE) remains unresolved (33,36). A negative response to this question implies that the latest Paleocene climate could be defined as a ramp up of hyperthermal conditions prior to the PETM onset (36). In any case, the growing body of evidence indicates that the long-term gradual warming of the latest Paleocene climate was less stable than previously thought.
As interpreted in the North American Atlantic Coastal Plain, the POE is concomitant with a rise in sea surface temperature of at least 2 °C and carbon released during this event is estimated more similar to ongoing anthropogenic releases in mass and rate than the PETM (32). The POE is estimated to have a duration of 22 to 45 ky, and it would be separated from the PETM onset by 80 to 100 ky (33). Assuming that the POE is a global event and is actually captured by the latest Paleocene sediments of the studied sedimentary section, this precise chronostratigraphic framework allows for time calibration of the Albas mammal-bearing level. This is particularly relevant given that the fossiliferous level is stratigraphically situated between the POE and the PETM events. Specifically, it is situated 7.7 m above the end of the POE and only 40 cm below the onset of the PETM (Fig. 1CandSI Appendix, Fig. S3). These data allow us to tentatively estimate the fauna to be approximately 2.5 ky older than the onset of the PETM, assuming a local sedimentation rate of ~17 cm/ky (based on the ~170 ky duration of the PETM, corresponding to 30.1 m in the studied section). Given the unlikelihood of constant sedimentation rates, the accuracy of these estimates is open to question. Nevertheless, the data are sufficiently robust to demonstrate that the mammal-bearing locality is very close in time with, but older than, the onset of the PETM. Furthermore, its stratigraphic position, situated subsequent to the POE and just below the PETM onset, allows for direct long-range correlations with other faunas that possess a comparable high-resolution chemostratigraphic framework.
Albas is therefore correlated with the Clarkforkian North American Land Mammal Age (LMA), which also recorded both the POE and the onset of the PETM in the Clarkforkian 2 zone (Cf-2) [(37), note that we follow the recent revised biozonation of the Clarkforkian LMA (38)]. As a result, Albas can be considered as older than the North American Wasatchian Wa-M, Wa-0, and Wa-R faunas, which document the PETM. Similarly, Albas correlates with the late Gashatan Asian LMA (late Paleocene) and is older than the Bumbanian strata (early Eocene), which recorded the PETM (39). In a European context, Albas is of younger age than the late Paleocene Spanish localities of Campo, Tendruy, and Claret from the Tremp-Graus basin (16,21). Claret is the reference fauna for the MP6b level of the mammalian biochronological scale for the Paleogene. The coeval locality of Tendruy occurs stratigraphically below the POE and has been dated to a range of 30 to 55 ky before the PETM (40). Finally, Albas may be contemporaneous with the faunas of Rivecourt (Paris Basin) (12) and Jibou (Romania) (41) for which neither the POE nor the PETM have been recorded. However, a latest Paleocene age has been proposed for these faunas based on the absence of artiodactyls, perissodactyls, and euprimates. The earliest appearance of these groups in Europe is at the MP7 level, which is of a controversial age (15; see below). The Dormaal fauna (Belgium) is the reference locality for this pivotal level. This temporal framework suggests that Albas is the only latest Paleocene European locality that can be confidently dated to the POE/PETM interval.
Albas documents 15 species illustrated by a total of 161 mammalian dental remains. Of these, 75 are taxonomically diagnostic cheek teeth (for a detailed description and comparisons of these specimens, see Systematic Paleontology section inSI Appendix, Table S1 and Fig. S2http://www.pnas.org/lookup/doi/10.1073/pnas.2505795122#supplementary-materials). The most abundant species is the louisinidPaschatherium marianae, which constitutes over 65% of the fossil assemblage (Fig. 2A–F). Louisinids are endemic to Europe and represent a group of archaic mammals (i.e., a group that appeared during the Cretaceous-Paleocene period, without living representatives). In addition toPaschatherium, the louisinids are documented in the assemblage byTeilhardimyscf.reisi(Fig. 2G–I). The high prevalence ofPaschatheriumin the fauna is consistent with the currently known fossil record from the Paleocene–Eocene transition in Europe. For example,Paschatheriumconstitutes over 85 and 63% of the fossil assemblage in Claret and Dormaal, respectively (42).

Archaic mammals are also documented in Albas by several “insectivore” taxa. The adapisoriculidBustylussp. (Fig. 2J) was identified, as was a possible pseudorhyncocyonid genus (Fig. 2K). Additionally, three nyctitheriid soricomorphans were identified, including the taxaPlagioctenodoncf.dormaalensisandWyonycterissp. (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). The ?pseudorhyncocyonid genus is related to a group restricted to the middle Paleocene−late Eocene of Europe. Of particular interest are the specimens identified asBustylussp.,Plagioctenodoncf.dormaalensis, andWyonycterissp., which represent typical families from the European and North American late Paleocene−early Eocene faunas.Bustylusis endemic to Europe, with its earliest occurrence documented in the early Paleocene of Hainin (Belgium) (43). The species from Albas bridges the temporal and morphological gaps between the late Paleocene MP6a species from the French localities of Cernay and Berru (B. cernaysiandB. germanicus) and the last known representative of the genus, which is known from the MP7 of Dormaal (Bustylussp.) (44). Similarly, the taxonPlagioctenodoncf.dormaalensisidentified in Albas exhibits a high degree of similarity toP.dormaalensisfrom Dormaal (45). Other species ofPlagioctenodonare primarily documented from North America, ranging from the Clarkforkian-2 (e.g.,P. rosei) to the early Wasatchian (46). Given the absence ofPlagioctenodonin European Paleocene faunas, with the exception of Albas, and the morphological similarities betweenP. roseiandPlagioctenodoncf.dormaalensis(SI Appendix,Supporting Information Text), we infer a North American dispersal toward Europe for this genus, which likely occurred during the Clarkforkian. The second nyctitheriid identified at Albas is attributed toWyonycterissp. It shows greater similarity to bothW. richardifrom the MP7 (Dormaal and Abbey Wood) andW. chalixfrom the Cf-2 than to earlier North American species from the Tiffanian (47). In light of these data and in line with the proposed hypothesis forPlagioctenodon, it is likely that a North American dispersal ofWyonycteristoward Europe occurred during the Clarkforkian. However, this biogeographic inference is uncertain due to the fragmentary nature of the AlbasWyonycterisspecimens, which prevents us from drawing any definitive conclusions.
The undoubted record of a paromomyid primate at Albas provides more robust interpretations. The species, attributed toArciuscf.rougieri, documents the first Paleocene occurrence of the paromomyid primate family in Europe (Fig. 2L). These archaic primates were not previously known in Europe before the early Eocene [~MP7 localities of Palette and Sotteville-sur-Mer (France) and Silveirinha (Portugal)] (48). It is noteworthy that paromomyids are absent from the MP7 reference level of Dormaal. All European paromomyids belong to the genusArcius. In North America, however, paromomyids are more diversified throughout the Paleocene–Eocene; they first appear during the Torrejonian 1 (early Paleocene) (49). In Asia, paromomyids are only documented from the Bumbanian of China (Wutu locality) by a taxon as yet unnamed, with possible affinities to the Paleocene–Eocene North American genusIgnacius(50). Recent phylogenetic analyses of paromomyids in a paleogeographic context support a North American origin of the European genusArcius, considering its close relationships withPhenacolemur, a long-lived North American genus known from the middle Tiffanian (mid-late Paleocene) to the latest Wasatchian (late early Eocene) (51). In that time-calibrated phylogenetic context, the absence of paromomyids in all Paleocene localities known from Europe, except Albas, implies a latest Paleocene dispersal from North America, possibly from a species close to the Clarkforkian representatives ofPhenacolemur pageiorP. simonsi.
Another unexpected finding at Albas is the identification of a herpetotheriid metatherian, representing a species of the genusPeratherium(Fig. 2M–O). This species documents the first Paleocene occurrence of a metatherian in Europe. The oldest European Cenozoic herpetotheriid was so farP. constans, a species documented from MP7 (Dormaal) and ~MP7 faunas (Le Clot, Fordones, Palette, and possibly Silveirinha) (26). Similarly, in North America, no herpetotheriid has been reported from the Clarkforkian (11,13). The first undoubted North American species ofPeratheriumare earliest Eocene in age; e.g.,P. innominatumhas been reported from the Wa-M and Wa-0 (PETM faunas). However, a dentary of a possible species ofPeratheriumhas been mentioned -but never described- in the late Tiffanian, and several putative stem herpetotheriids inhabited North America during the late Maastrichtian-late Paleocene interval (Nortedelphys,Swaindelphys, andGolerdelphys) (11,52), consistently supporting an origin for the family on this continent. Therefore, given the absence of any metatherians in the rich European Paleocene localities of Hainin, Walbeck, Cernay, and Berru, the Albas species ofPeratheriumis probably the result of a latest Paleocene dispersal of herpetotheriids from North America to Europe.
We also identified two rodent species in Albas that belong to the ischyromyids, among the basalmost rodents. The first species, ?Corbarimyssp., shows similarities with European (~MP7) and North American (early Wasatchian) species (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). The second rodent,Acritoparamysaff.atavus, closely resemblesA.atavus, a species which was proposed as the oldest known rodent from North America (?Cf-1, Cf-2) (53) (Fig. 2QandR). In Asia, ischyromyids appeared later, in the Bumbanian of Wutu with the generaTaishanomysand ?Acritoparamys(54). However, it is well established that glires (rodents and lagomorphs) and stem rodentiaforms originated in Asia during the early-middle Paleocene (55). Given this evolutionary scenario, it has been proposed that the rodents from the European late Paleocene localities of Jibou and Rivecourt -although documented by fragmentary incisors of no alpha taxonomic value- are Asian migrant taxa (12). The presence at Albas of the oldest undoubted occurrence of ischyromyid rodents in Europe, including a species close to the ClarkforkianAcritoparamys atavus, provides a more robust evidence for a rodent dispersal from North America rather than Asia to Europe during the late Paleocene.
Finally, with regard to carnivorous mammals, we have identified a hyaenodontid “creodont” species at Albas, which represents the only hyaenodont mammal discovered so far in the Paleocene of Europe. This probable new species also documents the unique Paleocene occurrence of the genusArfia(Fig. 2P). Until now, the oldest representatives of this genus were the earliest EoceneA. junneifrom Wa-0 in North America,A. gingerichifrom MP7 in Europe (Dormaal and Le Quesnoy localities), andA. langebadreaeplusA. palustrisfrom the Bumbanian in Asia (Tsagan-Khushu and Wutu localities, respectively) (SI Appendix,Supporting Information Text). Considering the hyaenodont fossil record as a whole, the oldest attested genera are from the middle to late Paleocene of Morocco (LahimiaandTinerhodon) and the late Paleocene of China (Prolimnocyon). Thus, while most taxa from Albas have North American affinities,Arfiais more likely to be either an African or Asian immigrant. This hypothesis is supported by rare other examples of mammalian exchanges during the late Paleocene, between Europe and Africa (for archaic insectivorous mammals) and between Europe and Asia (for the earliest carnivoraformVassacyon) (56).
By integrating high-resolution δ13Corgchemostratigraphy and fossil evidence, our data demonstrate that the European mammal fauna during the POE-PETM interval was essentially composed of endemic taxa (e.g., louisinids and pseudorhyncocyonids) and species with North American affinities (nyctitheriid insectivores, paromomyid primates, herpetotheriid metatherians, and ischyromyid rodents). This conclusion has significant implications for both the impact of the POE and the PETM on Holarctic mammals.
The prevailing hypothesis is that Europe, as well as Asia and North America, experienced a major biotic turnover during the PETM. This scenario is based on the absence of some modern lineages of mammals (i.e., euprimates, artiodactyls, and perissodactyls) in the latest Paleocene MP6b localities and the high diversity of such mammal taxa in the earliest Eocene MP7 localities (Fig. 3) (9,12,20–22,26). The correlation of Dormaal (the MP7 reference level) within the PETM has served to reinforce this conclusion (9). However, this consensus view has recently been challenged by a second, new hypothesis advocating a pre-PETM age for Dormaal and other MP7 localities (Le Quesnoy and Suffolk Pebble Beds) (15). The primary consequence of this novel hypothesis is that the first appearance of euprimates, artiodactyls, and perissodactyls in Europe would be dated to the latest Paleocene, i.e., before the PETM. If confirmed, this hypothesis would significantly weaken the role of the PETM in mammalian evolution. The data presented here challenge both proposed hypotheses.

European and North American mammalian fossil records and proposed mammalian dispersal events during the Paleocene–Eocene transition. (A) A first dispersal episode (black arrows) likely happened during the POE, involving the “precursor dispersers” herpetotheriid metatherians, paromomyid primates, rodents, and hyaenodontid “creodonts” (this event also concerns some nyctitheriid “insectivores”; see text). To the exception of the hyaenodontids, these groups entered Europe from North America probably via the Thulean route, a land bridge connecting North America and Europe via Greenland (55,56). A second dispersal episode happened during the PETM, involving the three modern lineages artiodactyls, perissodactyls, and euprimates (9,12). (B) Fossil records of species closely related to those of Albas among herpetotheriid metatherians (11,13,26,52), paromomyid primates (48,49,51), and rodents (11,53), illustrating the inferred dispersal event between Europe and North America during the POE. Green and blue lines correspond to the European and North American fossil records, respectively. Green circles represent the earliest occurrences of each group in Europe. The position of the POE within the Clarkforkian 2 (Cf-2) follows the recent revised biozonation of this North American LMA (38).
First, the chronostratigraphic framework proposed here, in conjunction with the identified taxa, demonstrates that the first occurrences of euprimates, artiodactyls, and perissodactyls in the studied section are post-PETM, in early Eocene strata (Le Clot and Fordones faunas) (Fig. 3). In contrast, these index taxa are absent from the latest Paleocene fauna of Albas. In light of the proposed temporal proximity between this fauna and the onset of the PETM (~2.5 ky), the hypothesis that the modern lineages first appeared in the European fossil record only during the earliest Eocene is therefore strongly supported. This leads to the conclusion that the MP7 European faunas (which are younger than Albas but older than Le Clot and Fordones) are not of the latest Paleocene epoch, but rather can be confidently dated to the earliest Eocene, coeval with the PETM. These findings provide substantial support for the hypothesis that the PETM was a significant driver in the dispersal and evolution of modern mammals (8–10).
Second, the presence of nyctitheriids, paromomyids, and herpetotheriids at Albas indicates that these taxa occurred earlier in Europe than was previously assumed. If it was indeed widely accepted that these taxa arrived in Europe during or shortly after the PETM, the evidence from Albas indicates that these taxa appeared in that continent as early as the latest Paleocene (Fig. 3). In light of these findings, we propose using the term “precursor” dispersers to define these taxa and rodents, which also made their first appearance in Europe during the latest Paleocene. The hypothesis that the various “precursor” dispersers migrated from North America to Europe during the latest Paleocene is supported by the close morphological similarities of the Albas species with their North American Clarkforkian (or older for herpetotheriids) counterparts (Fig. 3). A review of the available plate tectonics and sea level data for the late Paleocene epoch suggests that the North American “precursor” dispersers likely entered Europe via the Thulean route, an Arctic land bridge connecting North America and Europe via Greenland (Fig. 3) (57). This dispersal event appears to have been unidirectional, yet probably selective, as some typical North American late Paleocene archaic taxa (tillodonts, apatotherians, cimolestids, and pantodontans) remain thus far unknown in the latest Paleocene of Europe, including Albas. It seems likely that these groups, along with the “modern mammals,” arrived in Europe only during the PETM.
In conclusion, the fossil evidence indicates that the “precursor” dispersers (nyctitheriids, paromomyids, herpetotheriids, and rodents) were present in various pre- and syn-POE late Clarkforkian faunas in North America. In Europe, these groups were absent from the pre-POE European faunas (MP6b localities of Campo, Tendruy, and Claret) but present in the post-POE fauna of Albas, which is proposed here to represent the reference level MP6c. This evidence suggests that the POE, like the PETM, which occurred 80 to 100 ky later, was a substantial driver of paleobiogeography, triggering a rapid high-latitude dispersal event of mammals from North America to Europe under favorable warming conditions. Obviously, this assumption is only valid if the POE, as currently interpreted and recorded in North America and Europe, is a synchronous global event. Otherwise, the presence of the “precursor” dispersers in Europe at the MP6c biochron implies a mammalian dispersal during the fluctuating long-term gradual warming of the latest Paleocene, before the PETM. In any case, the key finding of this study is that a brief favorable warming condition in high latitude during the latest Paleocene (possibly the POE), although of lower amplitude than the PETM, was the driver of an unsuspected major mammalian turnover in Europe. Because the carbon released during the POE is estimated to be quite similar to ongoing anthropogenic emissions (32), this conclusion prompts further studies of the nature of the enigmatic POE. Indeed, the evolutionary consequences of this event could provide guidance for the predictive models of the impact of the current climate changes on mammalian biodiversity (58,59).
The mammalian assemblage from Albas was collected via bulk-sampling (~500 kg), careful acid etching, and screen-washing techniques. Illustrated specimens were scanned using a μ-CT-scanning station EasyTom 150/Rx Solutions (Montpellier RIO Imaging, ISEM, Montpellier, France) with a resolution of 4.97 μm. Images of these specimens were achieved using the “snapshot” tool of Avizo® 9.3 based on volume renderings of the specimens (Fig. 2andSI Appendix, Fig. S2). The 3D digital models of all illustrated specimens inFig. 2andSI Appendix, Fig. S2are available on the online open-access platform MorphoMuseuM (60).
Ten samples, collected in marine Ilerdian deposits across the “Le Clot” and Albas “Windmill” sedimentary sections (SI Appendix, Fig. S1) (18), were restudied for calcareous nannofossil investigation, following the standard procedure described in the literature (61).
Some palynofacies slides, originally prepared for a previous study (18), were reexamined here, in order to quantitatively assess the origin and distribution of the organic matter components. Selected samples, from the Le Clot and Albas Windmill sedimentary sections (SI Appendix, Fig. S1), are labeled 10AL19, 10AL30BIS, 10AL33, 10AL 36, and 10AL40 and correspond to the crucial POE to PETM interval. A total slide, showing all organic components, and a sieved slide, showing only the ≥10 µm organic fraction, were observed for each sample. In our previous study (18), only a qualitative examination of those slides had been made. Here, in order to precisely analyze the organic components distribution from our samples, we performed a quantitative assessment of the slides. The standard procedure for sample handling, slides preparation, and quantitative microscope analysis used here follows the procedure described in the literature (62,63).
The chemostratigraphy for the composite “Les Espeyrols/Le Clot” section (SI Appendix, Fig. S1) was constructed from 314 carbon-isotope ratios of bulk organic matter analyses (designated No 10 AL-XX and No 19 AL-XX). Fifty-eight samples are from the PETM interval recorded in the ~70 m thick Albas Windmill section (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Sampling is strongest around the Albas fossil-bearing locality (i.e., between the POE and the PETM) with seven samples from 535.2 m to 537.8 m. This represents one or two samples at each stratigraphic level. This sampling is consistent with the significant local sedimentation rate of ~17 cm/ky (see text). Organic matter of the sediments was isolated, following the procedure described in the literature (64,65). The bulk organic carbon isotope analyses (δ13Corg) are based on powdered rock samples of about 1 to 10 g, acidified in 25% HCl solution during two hours in order to remove carbonate. Soluble salts were removed by repetitive (1–10) centrifuging (4,000 RPM) with deionized water until a neutral sediment was obtained. Finally, residues were dried at 35 °C and powdered again. Fractions of each resulting powder were measured with a standard LECO carbon analyzer (CS-200) to determine total organic carbon. Only 58 samples with sufficient organic matter (> 0.05%) were considered for isotopic analysis (SI Appendix, Table S2). Carbon isotope analysis of organic carbon was performed with an elemental analyzer (Carlo-Erba 1110) connected online to a ThermoFinnigan Delta V Plus mass spectrometer at the University of Erlangen (Germany). Organic13C/12C values were normalized to the international VPDB standard (Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite). Each sample was analyzed one to three times; accuracy and reproducibility of the analyses were checked by replicate analyses of international standards USGS40 and USGS41. The reproducibility of analyses is within 0.2‰ (1σ).
