Sub-Antarctic marine ecosystems are highly vulnerable to climate change, with rising ocean temperatures threatening key benthic species.Abatus cordatus, an endemic sea urchin of the Kerguelen Islands with limited dispersal capacity, has been hypothesised to possess a narrow thermal niche, which would render it particularly susceptible to environmental shifts. However, microbiome-mediated acclimation may provide a potential mechanism of resilience to ocean warming. To test these hypotheses, this study evaluates host survival and gut microbiome responses ofA. cordatusto medium-term seawater warming under near-future temperature scenarios using 16S rRNA gene sequencing to compare these changes with those observed in sediment microbiomes.
Host mortality remained relatively low across all temperatures, showing no association with warming intensity and thereby suggesting thermal tolerance. While gut microbiome alpha-diversity remained stable, its composition shifted and variability increased with experiment duration and temperature, leading to greater inter-individual divergence and a decline in both the richness and abundance of core taxa. In contrast, sediment microbiomes remained more stable, exhibiting more deterministic assembly and increased core stability over time. At the taxonomic level, specific gut bacterial ASVs showed temperature-dependent abundance shifts, with greater flexibility at moderate thermal stress. Notably, the depleted and enriched ASVs were affiliated to known sulphate-reducing and fermentative taxa, respectively, suggesting a possible functional shift.
Overall, our findings suggest thatA. cordatuscan tolerate medium-term warming, with gut microbiome plasticity representing a potential mechanism supporting host resilience.

Sub-Antarctic islands are remote, ecologically valuable regions with pristine coastal marine ecosystems [1,2]. Due to their location at the transition zone between subtropical and Antarctic waters, these regions are particularly at risk from the impacts of the southward shift of marine fronts [3], and are currently facing rapid warming [4]. The high endemicity and cold-temperature adaptations of sub-Antarctic marine species make these islands among the most sensitive ecoregions of the Southern Ocean [5]. As natural laboratories, sub-Antarctic islands provide unique insights into the resilience and adaptability of marine communities in the face of climate change [6].
Benthic organisms in the coastal zones of sub-Antarctic islands are particularly sensitive to environmental perturbations, especially to shifts in temperature regime, due to their slow growth, reproduction rates, and limited dispersal capabilities [7–9]. Sea urchins (echinoids class) are key components of these benthic ecosystems, playing a substantial role in trophic networks, promoting benthic diversity, and maintaining ecosystem functions [10–12]. Previous studies predict major changes in echinoid assemblages in the sub-Antarctic region, including the Kerguelen Plateau, with high extinction rates driven by the southward migration of the Antarctic Circumpolar Current and associated shifts in marine fronts [13,14].
Abatus cordatusis a common echinoid species in the nearshore benthic communities in soft bottom environments of the Kerguelen islands [15]. While this species is endemic to the Kerguelen Plateau, the genus is broadly distributed throughout the sub-Antarctic and Antarctic regions, with closely related species inhabiting similar environments [16]. It forms patchy yet high-density populations, reaching up to 70 individuals per 0.5 m2in shallow (0–3 m depth) sheltered bays of the Kerguelen archipelago [15]. It is an infaunal, burrowing species feeding from the organic matter in the ingested sediment (also called “deposit-feeder”) [17], which has been suggested to facilitate horizontal acquisition of microbes from the sediment, thereby influencing gut microbiome composition and function [18].Abatus cordatusplays a key ecological role in sub-Antarctic soft-sediment ecosystems as a dominant bioturbator, contributing to sediment oxygenation, nutrient cycling, and the redistribution of organic matter [19]. Its ecological niche is narrow, restricted to places with mean seafloor temperatures ranging from 3 to 4 °C [13,15]. As a brooding species with no larval stage,A. cordatushas limited dispersal capacity, making populations vulnerable to environmental perturbations [12]. Ecological niche modelling revealed thatA. cordatusalready occupies the entire available niche space around the Kerguelen Islands, with its distribution constrained by the seafloor’s steep drop beyond the plateau, preventing southward dispersal to potential refuges [13]. In the SO, projections anticipate mean sea surface temperature increases of 1.8 to 3.7 °C by the end of the century, along with more frequent and intense marine heatwaves during austral summers for the Kerguelen Plateau [13,20,21]. The species is thus expected to exhibit high thermal sensitivity and limited resilience to near-future seawater warming, with its response to global change depending on its ability to adapt through physiological plasticity [22]. In marine invertebrates, a suite of physiological mechanisms, including the heat shock response, oxidative stress management, membrane remodelling, and metabolic depression, may contribute to thermal stress resilience [23–25]. Among these, microbiome-mediated host acclimation may be crucial for enhancingA. cordatus’ resilience to rising seawater temperatures [26].
The gut microbiome plays a fundamental role in host development, nutrition, immunity, and behaviour, with broad implications for both host ecology and ecosystem functioning [27–29]. Its plasticity enables rapid acclimation to environmental changes through various microbiome-mediated mechanisms, potentially mitigating the impact of stressors more efficiently than the host alone [30–32]. Over longer timescales, the microbiome may also contribute to host evolutionary potential by facilitating novel ecological interactions and expanding phenotypic variability [33]. Conversely, shifts in microbiome diversity can lead to dysbiosis, defined as a disruption in microbial community structure that negatively affects host physiological performance and fitness [34]. For instance, alterations in the gut microbiome, such as those caused by antibiotic exposure, have been associated with reduced immunity and increased mortality, likely due to a greater susceptibility to opportunistic infections [35]. While the importance of gut microbiomes in sea urchin ecology and evolution is increasingly recognized [18,36,37], the impact of temperature on their microbiomes remains understudied.
A recent meta-analysis of over 1300 microbiomes from 43 terrestrial and aquatic host species revealed that experimental warming typically reduce microbiome phylogenetic diversity and alter community composition [38]. However, this global analysis included no data from sub-Antarctic or Antarctic ecosystems, exposing a critical geographic and climatic gap in our understanding of microbiome responses to temperature increase in cold-adapted species. In echinoids specifically, most available studies focused on tropical species, reporting natural seasonal shifts in microbiome composition influenced by temperature among other abiotic factors [39,40]. However, these in situ approaches often struggle to isolate temperature effects due to multiple confounding factors. To our knowledge, only three experimental studies have examined the effects of near-future warming on sea urchin microbiomes under controlled conditions, focusing on warm-adapted (sub)tropical echinoid speciesEchinometrasp.AandLytechinus variegatus. These studies reported minor alterations in gut microbiome composition but significant predicted functional shifts at 30 °C and a 50% mortality rate after 14 days at 32 °C forLytechinus variegatus[41,42]. Additionally, temperature increase and elevatedpCO2induced bacterial taxa-specific responses in the gonad microbiome ofEchinometrasp.A[43].
Given their cold-water specialization, ecological relevance and expected vulnerability to global change [13,44], it is crucial to assess the microbiome responses of sea urchins likeA. cordatusunder warming scenarios. This study evaluates the gut mucosa microbiome responses ofA. cordatusexposed to two near-future seawater temperature treatments (7.5 °C and 10.5 °C), assessed at three time points (day 2, day 5 and day 13). A third treatment at 6 °C, corresponding to the local average summer temperature, was used as a control. In addition, samples were collected from in situ sea urchins prior to the experimental setup, serving as a baseline for natural microbiome structure. At each experimental time points, we contextualised the gut microbiome responses with those of the surrounding sediment microbiome, which represents the feeding source forA. cordatus. This approach allowed us to assess whether observed shifts in the gut microbiome are host-specific or reflect changes occurring in the environmental microbial inputs from ingested sediments.We hypothesise thatA. cordatuswould exhibit high thermal sensitivity and that its gut microbiome exhibits plasticity in response to seawater warming, undergoing temperature-driven shifts in diversity and composition that may contribute to host resilience under thermal stress.
During the experiment, six of 60A. cordatusindividuals died, not necessarily at the highest temperature or longest experiment duration: three at 6 °C (two on day 2 and one on day 12), one at 7.5 °C (on day 3), and two at 10.5 °C (on days 4 and 7). Thus, only 3 of 60 individuals died under elevated temperature conditions. No significant differences in sea urchin ellipsoidal volume were detected among temperatures at any experiment duration (Kruskal–Wallis, D2:χ2= 2.94,p= 0.23; D5:χ2= 4.94,p= 0.09; D13:χ2= 0.23,p= 0.89), indicating that sea urchin size was comparable across experimental groups.

After curation, the dataset comprised 6,730,721 sequences representing 4527 unique Amplicon Sequence Variants (ASVs). To detect bacterial shifts at a finer taxonomic scale, and better identify temperature-related responses that might be masked at the community level, we used DESeq2 to compare ASV relative abundances at 7.5 and 10.5 °C against the control (6 °C) at each time point. Across all temperatures and experiment durations, most gut microbiome ASVs (71.3–99.1%) showed no significant changes in their relative abundances (Additional File 1: Table S4). In the gut microbiome, ASV depletions were more frequent than enrichments, with 133 ASVs depleted and only 33 enriched in treatments compared to the control. The sharpest ASV depletion occurred at 7.5 °C on Day 5, where 102 ASVs were significantly depleted compared with the control. These AVSs accounted for 64.2% of the total relative abundance in the control, but their cumulative abundance dropped to 0.24% under the 7.5 °C treatment. More limited depletion occurred on Day 2 (8 ASVs; 13.9% in controlvs.0.02% in treatment) and on Day 13 (4 ASVs; 1.2%vs.0.01%). At 10.5 °C, depletion was comparatively weaker, with 7 ASVs on Day 2 (5.0%vs.0.00%), 10 ASVs on Day 5 (3.7%vs.0.00%), and only 2 ASVs on Day 13 (0.5%vs.0.00%) (Additional File 1: Table S4). In total, more ASVs were depleted at 7.5 °C (n= 114) than at 10.5 °C (n= 19). Most of the depleted ASVs on Day 5 at both 7.5 and 10.5 °C belonged to sulphate-reducing Desulfobacterota (e.g.Desulfobacula,Desulfocarbo,Desulfoconvexum,Desulfovibrio) (Fig.6). In contrast, enrichment events were observed in both 7.5 and 10.5 °C treatments (Additional File 1: Table S4). These enrichments were particularly important on Day 5, involving five ASVs at 7.5 °C and two ASVs at 10.5 °C, which initially represented 0.2 and 0.08% of the gut microbiome in the control condition, and reached relative abundances of 19.2 and 11.9% of the total community in the 7.5 and 10.5 °C conditions, respectively. The enriched ASVs primarily belonged to Gammaproteobacteria, especially Pseudomonadales (e.g.Enhydrobacter,Pseudomonas) andEnterobacteriaceae, in both treatment temperatures, with an additional enrichment of Firmicutes (e.g.Exiguobacterium,Hungateiclostridia,Enterococcus) at 7.5 °C (Fig.6).

WithinSpirochaeta2—previously identified as a potential keystone taxon in theAbatusmicrobiome—three ASVs were depleted at 7.5 °C across all time points, while three others were enriched at 10.5 °C (Fig.6). Few altered ASVs were shared between sediment and gut microbiomes, indicating distinct responses to temperature and experiment duration (Additional File 1: Fig. S4). For example, two ASVs depleted in sediment at 7.5 °C on Day 2 were enriched in the gut, while one ASV was consistently enriched in both microbiomes at 10.5 °C on Day 2.
Climate change, and particularly the pronounced seawater warming observed in recent decades, poses a major threat to the—predominantly endemic—wildlife of the SO through [45–47]. Microbiomes are increasingly recognized as key mediators of host adaptation, contributing to thermal tolerance and resilience [48,49]. Yet, the effects of rising temperatures on the microbiomes of marine invertebrate in this region remain poorly understood [50]. Our study indicates thatA. cordatuscan tolerate a + 5 °C temperature increase over 2 weeks, as suggested by low mortality rates (5% at 7.5 °C and 10% at 10.5 °C). While its upper thermal limit remains to be determined, our results challenge previous observations and predictions that linkedA. cordatussurvival strictly to a narrow seafloor temperature range of 3–4 °C [12,13,15]. Although sea urchins were handled carefully during sampling, transport and experimental setup to minimize stress, we cannot exclude the possibility that minor impacts caused undetected shell damage, possibly explaining the observed mortality independent of experimental conditions. Our findings suggest that, under the conditions tested in this study, short- to mid-term seawater warming in the Kerguelen Plateau may not constitute an immediate threat toA. cordatuspopulations. However, mortality alone is insufficient to fully assess species tolerance to warming [51], and additional host physiological, ecological and environmental responses (e.g. reproduction, growth, behaviour, and stress physiology) warrant further investigation [52–54]. For instance, rising temperatures can alter the timing and magnitude of phytoplankton blooms, disrupting organic matter deposition on the seafloor [55], which is critical for deposit feeders likeA. cordatus[56,57]. Indeed, recent evidence suggests that food resources may be a more decisive factor in determiningA. cordatuspopulation densities than temperature [44].
Despite having limited effects on the organism’s mortality rate, increasing temperature impacted the structure and composition of gut microbiomes, with potential consequences for host fitness. These effects may be either beneficial (e.g. promoting microbial taxa that enhance thermal tolerance or metabolic efficiency) or detrimental (e.g. dysbiosis, impairing digestion, immunity or reproductive performance) [58]. At the community level, our results showed that the gut microbiomes ofA. cordatuswere less stable than those of the surrounding sediment during the experiment, particularly in terms of composition, although no significant loss of alpha-diversity was observed. Other indicators of gut microbiome plasticity included a higher phylogenetic divergence in bacterial community assembly and a lower abundance of the core microbiome community, compared to sediment. Albeit low, temperature played a statistically significant role in altering the gut microbiome composition at the whole community level. While additional evidence would be required for further confirmation, these findings suggest that the low mortality ofA. cordatusmay be supported, at least in part, by microbiome plasticity, allowing some adaptive response to thermal stress rather than relying on a highly stable microbiome. The sensitivity of theA. cordatusgut microbiome to both temperature fluctuations and experiment duration contrasts with a previous experiment involving the tropical sea urchinLytechinus variegatus, where gut microbial community composition remained unchanged in response to increased seawater temperature [41]. This discrepancy suggests that sub-Antarctic species may exhibit greater sensitivity to rising seawater temperatures, due to their adaptation to cold and stable environments [59,60]. Although daily variations in gut microbiome composition have been observed in some benthic organisms [61], such dynamics remain unexplored inA. cordatus. In our study, all temperature treatments shared the same photoperiod conditions, minimizing diurnal influences. While seasonal effects have been demonstrated in other sea urchin species microbiome [39], our short-term experiment (2 weeks) and simultaneous setup of all treatments likely excludes any seasonal effects. Nevertheless, the temporal dynamics of the gut microbiome inA. cordatusremain to be investigated. Conversely, the enhanced stability of sediment microbiome in response to temperature increases is intriguing and contrasts with previous reports in coastal sediments [62]. External microbiomes, like those in sediment, are typically more influenced by environmental factors than host-associated microbiomes [63]. This stability raises the hypothesis that sediment microbiomes in sub-Antarctic environments might possess inherent resilience to thermal variations, which could be due to the dominance of generalist taxa capable of thriving across a wide range of environmental fluctuations. Accordingly, a recent study revealed a significantly higher proportion of generalists in Antarctic and sub-Antarctic sediment microbiomes compared toAbatusgut mucosa microbiomes [64].
The limited temperature-driven alterations inA. cordatusmicrobiome—especially the absence of a common shift at the community level—could be explained first by the high inter-individual variability, masking community-level responses. In this case, the majority of the gut community would diverge among individuals over time, while only some minor taxa would respond consistently to temperature increase in all individuals. Consistently, when focusing on core microbiomes per experimental condition, this variability was mitigated, enhancing the detection of temperature compositional effects on gut mucosa microbiome. Second, the weak temperature influence may reflect the overwhelming impact of transitioning from wild to captive conditions, which drastically alters diversity and destabilizes host-associated microbiomes, including those of marine invertebrates, through handling, transport and confinement-related environmental changes [65–67]. This strong initial perturbation may have obscured the direct effects of temperature onA. cordatusgut microbiome composition. It is also worth noting that the seawater supplied to the aquaria was collected at 7 m depth, whereasA. cordatusindividuals were sampled in shallow waters (0–3 m) at a different site. Seawater microbiomes can vary with both depth and location [68], which may partly explain the initial shift between in situ gut microbiome composition and that observed at D2, as well as the progressive divergence observed along the experiment. Third, the experimentation duration may have been too short to detect community-scale shifts. Although studies on warming effects in sea urchins are limited, microbiome responses in other marine invertebrates have been observed over periods ranging from 3 days in mussels [69] to 15 days in Antarctic sponges [50], similar to our study’s timeframe. Lastly, elevated temperature may have affected the host immune system, potentially altering its capacity to regulate the microbiome and leading to increased temporal variability and more phylogenetically divergent gut microbial communities throughout the experiment [70–72]. This variability could partly explain why temperature had a weaker effect on microbiome composition compared to the duration of the experiment. While a significant effect of experiment duration onA. cordatusgut microbiome composition was observed, this pattern is unlikely to result solely from altered microbial acquisition due to captivity.Abatusindividuals maintained natural feeding behaviour throughout the experiment, and the sediment microbiome became increasingly stable over time, in contrast to the more dynamic and divergent trajectories observed in the gut. Furthermore, temperature-altered ASVs were inconsistent between gut and sediment samples. Taken together, these results suggest that internal, host-related processes (such as physiological acclimatization to temperature or modulation of immune responses) rather than shifts in the surrounding sediment microbiome, are the primary drivers of the observed changes in gut microbiome composition. Ultimately, given the absence of visible host impairment, the observed compositional shifts likely reflect gut microbiome plasticity, with functionally redundant taxa potentially compensating for community taxonomic changes to help host-associated function under thermal stress [73].
Beyond community-level changes, subtle but consistent temperature-driven shifts in bacterial ASVs were detected within the gut microbiomes ofA. cordatus. Although the physiological consequences for the host remain to be fully elucidated, these compositional adjustments suggest potential functional reorganization of the microbiome that could mitigate thermal stress [74]. Sulphur metabolism plays a key role in various sea urchin species [18,40,75]. Here, sulphate-reducing bacteria (SRB), includingDesulfobacula,Desulfovibrio,DesulfocarboandDesulfoconvexum, were consistently depleted at higher temperatures.Desulfobaculawas identified as a potential keystone taxon in the gut ofAbatus agassizii, a congeneric Antarctic species ofAbatus cordatus[18], whileDesulfocarboandDesulfoconvexumwere previously reported as specific to the gut ofBrisaster townsendi, another deposit-feeding sea urchin [76]. SRB’s abundance decline with increasing temperatures aligns with previous findings in cold environments where SRB psychrophilic members such asDesulfoconvexumandDesulfotaleahave been isolated [77–79]. SRB are key players in sulphur cycling of anoxic-to-microoxic transitions zones [80], reducing sulphate and other sulphur compounds, such as sulphated polysaccharides from marine algae debris in theAbatusdiet, into hydrogen sulphide (H2S) [81]. Moreover, SRB likeDesulfobaculaalso contribute to sulphide detoxification by precipitating sulphides with iron, thereby preventing the harmful H2S accumulation in the gut [82,83]. Additionally, some SRB oxidize fermentation products from other microbial community members into acetate, a short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) that is particularly abundant in the gut of the deposit-feeding sea urchinEchinocardium cordatum, where it supports host metabolism and nutrition [82–84]. Consistent with findings inB. townsendi[76], both complete and incomplete oxidizers coexist inA. cordatusgut, withDesulfobaculaandDesulfoconvexumas potential partial oxidizers [78,85] andDesulfocarbolinked to complete oxidation [86]. Furthermore, while typically anaerobic, some SRB can tolerate low levels of oxygen and perform aerobic respiration at high rates [83,87]. By consuming residual oxygen, these SRB help maintain an anaerobic gut environment, preventing the oxidation of acetate and supporting host nutrition. While SRB depletion has been linked to disease in other sea urchins [88,89], and with reduced detoxification and nutrient acquisition under thermal stress in vertebrate ectotherms [90], the high survival rate ofA. cordatusin our study suggests resilience, potentially supported by compensatory enrichment of other ASVs.
Fermentative bacteria, including facultative and obligate anaerobes such asEnterococcus[91],Hungateiclostridia[92] andExiguobacterium[93], comprised a substantial fraction of enriched ASVs across both temperature treatments, reflecting a possible metabolic shift inA. cordatusgut in response to thermal stress-induced alterations. In sediment-burrowing sea urchins, fermenters degrade polysaccharides from detrital particles into SCFAs, which can supply energy to host epithelial cells [82,84,94]. Although the contribution of fermentation toA. cordatusmetabolism is unknown, estimates in the regular sea urchin speciesParacentrotus lividussuggest that gut bacterial fermentation may provide up to 10% of host metabolic energy [95]. The concurrent enrichment of fermenters and depletion of SRB may reflect competition for the substrate, or reduced oxygen solubility at higher temperatures [96], potentially favouring fermenters, which also tolerate higher temperature than SRB, as observed in Arctic marine sediments [97]. While this metabolic shift is less thermodynamically efficient [98], it could mitigate toxic H2S accumulation, potentially reducing the host’s detoxification requirements. Similar declines in metabolic activity has been observed in the sediment-burrowing sea urchinBrissopsis lyriferaunder oxygen depletion [99]. Thus, the taxonomic shift from SRB- to fermentation-based SCFA production may represent an energy trade-off that helps maintain digestive function under thermal stress.
Other altered taxa exhibited complex patterns, notablySpirochaeta, previously identified as a potential keystone genus inAbatusgut microbiomes [18,100]. While genus-level abundance increased along the experiment, temperature-specific responses were evident at the ASV level, with some ASVs being depleted at 7.5 °C and others enriched at 10.5 °C. These shifts suggest that differentSpirochaetastrains may fulfil distinct ecological roles under thermal stress. Variations inSpirochaetastrains with differing lifestyles (attached or free floating) and SCFA production pathways have been reported in other schizasterid sea urchins [76]. Maintaining a diverse set of symbionts, whose abundance and composition adapt under suboptimal conditions, can support functional redundancy and persistence of essential host functions [101].
The directionality of the interaction between gut microbiome andA. cordatusremains to be elucidated. Microbiome shifts may result from temperature-induced selection pressures or indirectly through host physiological responses to warming, as observed in other marine invertebrates [102,103]. Similarly, host responses themselves may be driven by temperature itself or mediated by microbiota-mediated effects, highlighting a complex, bidirectional interplay between the host and its microbiome. This dynamic relationship suggests that compositional plasticity of the gut microbiome could contribute to host phenotypic adaptability under environmental stress [104], potentially accelerating evolutionary responses by allowing selection to act on both direct and microbiota-mediated traits [105]. In our experiment, several observations support this hypothesis. The low host mortality, the absence of gut microbiome diversity loss, and the high gut microbiome variability collectively suggest that temperature-induced taxonomic shifts may result from the horizontal acquisition of new bacterial taxa and/or the expansion of rare, pre-existing minor taxa, facilitatingA. cordatusacclimatization to thermal stress. Furthermore, high microbiome variability among individuals may itself represent an adaptive trait, enhancing population-level phenotypic plasticity and potentially improving population fitness under environmental perturbations [101]. This trait could be particularly relevant forA. cordatus, whose limited dispersal capacity restricts its ability to escape shifting environmental conditions.
In summary, this is the first study to demonstrate the ability ofAbatus cordatusto cope with short-term seawater warming at near future temperatures, along with the dynamic restructuring of its gut microbiome. Our findings support the idea that microbiome plasticity may serve as a key mechanism supporting the resilience of benthic invertebrates facing rapid environmental change, particularly in ecologically vulnerable sub-Antarctic ecosystems. While the compositional shifts observed here may reflect adaptive responses, the long-term ecological and evolutionary implications remain to be fully elucidated. Given the correlative nature of our approach, future research should incorporate more comprehensive assessments of host physiological and molecular responses to thermal stress, through transcriptomic or metabolomic profiling, to fully understand the functional outcomes of temperature-induced microbiome changes. Experimental approaches targeting microbiome manipulation, including antibiotic treatments, microbiota transplants or substrate-tracing experiments (e.g. labelled sulphate or acetate to track microbial consumption and potential transfer to the host) will be needed to establish causal links between microbiome dynamics and host thermal tolerance. Understanding the specific roles of thermally responsive microbial taxa will be essential to predict the adaptive capacity of polar invertebrates under climate change.
Abatus cordatusindividuals were hand-collected from sediment by scuba diving at Île Haute, Kerguelen Islands (seawater temperature 7.0 °C, 2.0-m depth, 49° 23′ 14.856″ S, 69° 56′ 28.211″ E) in December 2022. Surficial coastal sediment from the immediate vicinity of theAbatuspopulation was also collected. Both the sea urchins and sediments were transferred to the “Port-aux-Français” French research station in a 50-L sealed drum filled with in situ seawater, with boat transport lasting < 1 h. As baseline controls, gut mucosa samples from five sea urchins and five sediment samples from the sampling site were processed immediately upon arrival, to represent in situ conditions (hereafter “T0”; Additional File 1: Table S5).
Six 30-L aquaria (45 cm height × 30 cm width × 30 cm length) were set up, each containing a 10-cm layer of sediment (3–3.5 L) and housing 20 sea urchins randomly distributed after careful inspection; only apparently healthy individuals were included in the experiment. Each aquarium was filled with 20 L of seawater, continuously supplied at a rate of 1.5 L min−1via a pump drawing coarsely filtered seawater (Ø 5 mm) from Port-au-Français Bay at a depth of 7 m. Seawater was aerated using airstones connected to a 4000 L h−1Aquamedic aquarium air pump. The experimental design included two control aquaria (6.0 °C) and four temperature-increased aquaria (two at 7.5 °C and two at 10.5 °C), resulting in two independent replicates per temperature condition. The control temperature was maintained with three Teco TK500 chillers and a Blue Marine BM Chiller 400 arranged in series, while the increased temperature treatments were controlled with Inkbird ITC-306 T and AquaMedic 300 W TH-300 heaters. The control temperature reflects the average summer seawater temperature (6.2 °C) recorded at Île Haute during the experiment year. The 7.5 °C temperature treatment represents the upper quartile temperature from the hottest month (March) over 2012–2023, while the 10.5 °C treatment corresponds to the highest recorded temperature during the same period (Additional File 1: Fig. S5). Sea urchins were acclimated at 6.0 °C under natural photoperiod conditions for 24 h, after which the temperature was increased by 1.5 °C per day to reach the target conditions, with the experiment duration set to 13 days due to climatic and logistical factors (i.e. < 20 days between ship rotations). Sea urchin mortality was monitored through observations of spine movement absence, spine loss, and tissue necrosis leading to an exposed test. Dead individuals were removed from the tanks along the experiment.
Sea urchins and sediment were collected from all aquaria after 2, 5 and 13 days of the experiment. For each temperature treatment, 5–7 sea urchins were randomly selected, aiming for an even distribution between the two replicate aquaria (typically 2–4 individuals per aquarium). Sea urchins were measured (Length: 2.58 ± 0.08 cm, Width: 2.36 ± 0.07 cm, Height: 1.32 ± 0.04 cm), and their gut mucosa was aseptically sampled for microbiome analysis, following previously described procedures [18] (Additional File 1: Table S5, Additional File 2: Table S6). Simultaneously, 3–5 sediment samples (350 mg each) were collected from each aquarium, avoidingAbatusindividuals to prevent disturbance. The number of sampling points varied depending on the spatial distribution of individuals, with fewer points available when individuals were more dispersed. All samples were frozen at − 20 °C immediately after collection and stored until DNA extraction.
Prior to DNA extraction, samples were homogenized using a FastPrep-24® instrument (MP Biomedicals, USA) for three cycles of 15 s at 4 m s−1with 2.5-min rests between cycles. Genomic DNA was subsequently extracted from thawed gut mucosa and sediment samples using the DNeasy PowerSoil Pro Kit (Qiagen, CA, USA), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Extractions were performed under a sterile laminar flow hood using newly opened, sealed reagents.
The metabarcoding approach targeted the V3–V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene using 341F/806R primers [106]. Amplification, library preparation and sequencing on the Illumina NovaSeq PE250 platform were performed by Novogene (Beijing, China), following Schwob et al. [107]. As part of Novogene’s standard procedure, negative PCR controls were included to monitor potential contamination during amplification. FASTQ files were processed in R using DADA2 (v 1.32.0) [108]. Briefly, raw forward and reverse reads were quality-filtered and trimmed using the standard parameters (maxN = 0, maxEE = 2, truncQ = 2, rm.phix = TRUE). Chimaeras were removed using theremoveBimeraDenovofunction with theconsensusmethod. Taxonomic assignments of the amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) were made with SILVA v138.1 database. The contingency table of 16S rRNA ASVs and samples metadata (Additional File 2: Table S6 and Table S7) were imported in R and processed with thePhyloseqpackage (v 1.48.0) [109]. ASVs assigned to Archaea, Eukarya, chloroplasts, or mitochondria were removed. To reduce the influence of spurious or contaminant sequences, ASVs occurring in fewer than two samples or represented by fewer than 10 total reads across all samples were filtered out. The aligned ASV sequences were used to reconstruct a phylogenetic tree following the methodology previously described [110]. The sediment and gut mucosa ASV tables were rarefied to 35,084 and 16,874 sequences per sample, respectively.
To assess potential aquarium-specific effects, a PERMANOVA was performed separately on sediment and gut mucosa samples, using the strata option to account for the nesting of samples within each experimental condition (i.e. temperature and sampling day). Ellipsoidal volume (V= 4/3 ×π× Length/2 × Width/2 × Height/2) was calculated as a proxy for overall sea urchin body size, and compared across temperatures at each experiment durations (i.e. 2, 5 and 13 days) to evaluate potential confounding effect (Additional File 2: Table S6). Alpha-diversity indices (total ASVs, Faith’s phylogenetic diversity, Pielou’s evenness, Chao1 richness and Simpson diversity) were estimated from rarefied datasets usingPhyloseqandPicantepackages [109,111]. Outliers identified using the interquartile range (IQR) method were excluded from alpha-diversity analysis. Relative standard deviation (RSD) was calculated in R using a custom script. Differences across experimental conditions were tested using Dunn’s post hoc tests with Holm correction.
Beta-diversity metrics (Jaccard, Bray-Curtis and weighted UniFrac indices) were calculated from unrarefied datasets. The effects of temperature, experiment duration and their interaction on bacterial composition in sediment and gut mucosa were assessed through PERMANOVA usingVeganpackage [112]. PairwisePermanovaGanalyses integrating the three beta-diversity metrics into a single omnibus test were performed to identify significant differences among groups [110]. These analyses were carried out either on the global community or on core subset communities common to all the replicates of a given condition (i.e. a given combination of temperature and time) to reduce individual variability. Community composition in sediment and gut mucosa across experimental conditions was visualized using non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) on Bray-Curtis distances computed from the rarefied ASV tables, applying the formulatemperature+duration. The homogeneity of beta-diversity dispersion (Bray-Curtis values) among experimental conditions was assessed using thebetadisperfunction, followed by ANOVA tests. Bray-Curtis pairwise dissimilarity means were compared among experimental conditions using Dunn’s tests. A fixed baseline analysis was conducted to assess variability accumulation over time by comparing the mean dissimilarity in in situ samples (D0) to later time points (D2, D5 or D13), and the differences were tested using Wilcoxon tests.
Core microbiomes were determined separately for each gut mucosa and sediment under each experimental condition using a 70% prevalence threshold with no minimum abundance criterion. The 10 most abundant ASVs within each core were identified, and their relative abundances were calculated for each condition.
Phylogenetic relatedness in external sediment and gut mucosa microbiomes was assessed using the standardized effect size of phylogenetic community structure (ses.MNTD) in the R packagePicante[111]. This metric compares the differences between observed communities and null communities generated with 999 randomizations, standardized by the standard deviation of phylogenetic distances. Negative values indicate that species are more phylogenetically related than expected by chance (i.e. convergence), while positive values suggest an overdispersion (i.e. divergence), with greater absolute values reflecting stronger deterministic processes.
Differential abundance analysis for gut mucosa and sediment microbiomes was performed independently using DESeq2 [113]. ASVs were classified as enriched (p< 0.055 and log2FC > 0.5), depleted (p< 0.055 and log2FC < − 0.5) or unchanged ASVs (p> 0.055 and − 0.5 < log2FC < 0.5) by comparing each treatment temperature (7.5 °C; 10.5 °C) to the control (6.0 °C) at a given time point (Day 2, 5 or 13).
