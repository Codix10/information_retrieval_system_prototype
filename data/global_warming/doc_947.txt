Climate change affects people’s mental health directly and indirectly. Climate anxiety, characterised by persistent worry and distress about environmental changes, is increasingly recognised as a factor affecting mental well-being. This study focused on potential implications of climate change worry for mental health.
To assess whether climate change worry is associated with an increased risk of depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance across European countries.
The study used longitudinal data from the European Social Survey-10 (2020–2022) and the follow-up CROss-National Online Survey 2 wave 4 (2022). A total of 5155 participants across 11 European countries were included in the analysis. Logistic regression models were used to examine the relationship between climate change worry and mental health outcomes (anxiety, depression and sleep), adjusting for potential confounding factors. Stratified analyses were conducted to assess variations between countries.
Climate change worry was associated with increased risk of anxiety (OR: 1.38, 95% CI: 1.13 to 1.68), but not depression (OR: 1.10, 95% CI: 0.94 to 1.29), or sleep disturbance (OR: 1.08, 95% CI: 0.92 to 1.27), in pooled analyses across countries. Country-specific analyses revealed notable differences, with the strongest associations between climate worry and anxiety observed in Slovenia and Italy.
These findings suggest that the psychological impact of climate change worry is not uniform across Europe and may be influenced by national policies, environmental risks and sociocultural factors. Given the varying effects across countries, policy-makers should consider contextual factors when designing strategies to address climate anxiety. Integrating mental health considerations into climate policies may enhance public engagement and resilience in the face of environmental challenges.
Traditional therapeutic approaches may not fully capture the unique distress associated with environmental worries, necessitating the development of specialised interventions that validate individuals’ concerns while equipping them with coping mechanisms.

Climate anxiety has emerged as a growing concern as the effects of climate change become increasingly evident. However, limited research has explored how climate worry influences mental health outcomes, and existing studies often suffer from methodological limitations, such as non-representative samples and cross-sectional designs.
This study provides a cross-national perspective on climate worry and its relationship with mental health across Europe. It identifies substantial variation in climate worry levels, with the highest concerns reported in Portugal, Austria and Slovenia, and the lowest in Sweden, Iceland and Italy. Climate worry was found to be associated with an increased risk of clinically significant anxiety, but not with depression or sleep disturbance. Country-specific analyses further highlighted that people living in Slovenia and Italy exhibited the strongest associations between climate worry and anxiety.
The study underscores the need to integrate mental health considerations into both climate and public health policies. Given the cross-national variations observed, future research should explore the specific cultural, environmental and policy-related factors that shape the relationship between climate worry and mental health. Additionally, longitudinal studies are needed to better understand potential causal mechanisms.
A growing global health crisis is being driven by the combustion of fossil fuels, a major factor in accelerating global heating. The climate emergency poses significant threats to health through both direct and indirect effects. Direct effects of climate change on health include consequences of extreme weather events, such as heatwaves, floods and droughts.1Conversely, indirect effects operate through social, economic and environmental determinants of health,2such as changes in geographical patterns of vectorborne diseases, food insecurity and threats to mental health.3In recent years, a growing body of literature has explored the psychological and emotional responses to climate change, with a particular focus on climate anxiety—defined as chronic worry, distress or fear about the consequences of environmental change and climate-related disasters.3This concept focuses on the general awareness of climate change, rather than a response to traumatic extreme weather events.4Climate worry is characterised by verbal-linguistic thoughts (rather than images) about changes taking place, or which are anticipated, regarding the climate system and potential consequences of these changes.5 6
Climate anxiety is increasingly prevalent, particularly among younger generations, who perceive climate change as an existential threat to their futures.7While some individuals can channel their concerns into proactive environmental behaviours, others experience distress that can lead to functional impairment and disengagement.7Given the escalating prominence of climate anxiety, it is crucial to understand its relationship with specific mental health conditions and how it manifests across different populations.
Despite growing concerns about the mental health implications of climate change, research on this topic remains relatively limited, particularly in the context of cross-national comparisons. Existing studies have been constrained by methodological limitations, including non-representative samples and cross-sectional designs, making it difficult to establish causal relationships between climate worry and mental health outcomes. Climate change policies and public attitudes towards environmental issues vary significantly across countries, potentially shaping the degree to which climate worry influences well-being.
There are many reasons for climate change worry. Awareness of climate change is extensive, with the media providing wide coverage on the issue and usually with a pessimistic narrative.4Climate change can lead to feelings of helplessness, fear and grief.2Many young people in particular feel frustrated and betrayed by governments who are failing to act against climate change, therefore, putting their futures in jeopardy.8This is further driven by individual-level knowledge and education, which can empower people into climate action, but can also have the opposite effect and induce a sense of hopelessness and overwhelm.4
Worry about climate change can have multiple consequences. It can lead to disempowerment—when feeling that efforts to reduce climate change are inadequate, as well as feelings of isolation among youth due to dismissal by peers and adults around them when expressing their worries. However, in some studies, climate change worry has been linked to a green identity and adoption of proenvironmental behaviours.3Interestingly, the opposite has also been found in other studies, with climate change worry found to be associated with disengagement from proclimate actions,3potentially due to feeling paralysed at the extent of the situation.9Lastly, there are potential links between worry about climate change and negative mental health outcomes, including insomnia.3Mental disorders are a leading cause of disability, the burden of which is continuing to rise globally.10Therefore, if climate worry is related to adverse mental health outcomes, strategies are needed to channel it into positive action to reduce any potential mental health burden.
This study aims to address the above research gaps by examining the association between climate change worry and future risk of depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance across 11 European countries. Specifically, it seeks to (1) identify the countries with the highest levels of climate change worry, (2) assess whether climate change worry is associated with an increased risk of mental health conditions over time and (3) explore the extent to which these associations vary between countries. This study contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of climate anxiety and its implications for public mental health across diverse sociopolitical contexts.
This study used secondary data from the 10th European Social Survey (ESS) (collected during 18 September 2020–3 September 2022),11 12and subsequent follow-up CROss-National Online Survey 2 (CRONOS-2) wave 4 (collected during 3 May 2022–22 December 2022),13allowing for a longitudinal study design. All European countries are welcome to participate in the ESS and the data are openly accessible (on registration) athttps://www.europeansocialsurvey.org/. ESS10 is a cross-national survey conducted across 31 countries.14During this survey round, data were collected by a face-to-face interview (via computer-assisted personal interview, web-based video interview or paper/web self-completion methods). The target population was people aged 15 years and over resident within private households, regardless of their nationality, citizenship, language or legal status.14Individuals were selected by strict random probability methods, including simple, stratified and multistage methods. Further detailed information regarding the methodology of ESS10 is found elsewhere.14
CRONOS-2 is a cross-national, probability-based input-harmonised web panel. It was conducted across 12 of the countries which took part in ESS10: Austria, Belgium, Czechia, Finland, France, Hungary, Iceland, Italy, Portugal, Slovenia, Sweden and Great Britain—as a follow-up panel survey across six waves.15Since the data used in this study were taken from wave 4 (when mental health was assessed), this limited the sample to 11 countries (excluding Hungary which did not complete wave 4). The CRONOS-2 web panel included additional inclusion criteria; participants were required to be 18 years or older and needed internet access.15CRONOS-2 participants were offered unconditional incentives for a value of £5/€5 per wave. Since participants were recruited after taking part in ESS10, this allowed for panel member recruitment from random probability samples and longitudinal follow-up. Response rates ranged from 34.6% in Italy to 86.5% in Iceland.15The two datasets (ESS10 and CRONOS-2 wave 4) were merged together using the participant identification number, country and survey mode. A total of 18 637 ESS10 respondents were eligible for CRONOS-2 recruitment.15 16
Climate change worry (exposure variable) was measured via the question which asked, “How worried are you about climate change?”, during the ESS10 survey (baseline). Respondents were asked to choose an option from a 5-point Likert scale which ranged from 1 (not at all worried) to 5 (extremely worried). The responses ‘not at all worried’, ‘not very worried’ and ‘somewhat worried’ were grouped as ‘not worried about climate change’, whereas the responses ‘very worried’ and ‘extremely worried’ were grouped as ‘worried about climate change’ to form a binary variable.
To measure depression (outcome variable), the Centre for Epidemiologic Studies-Depression Scale (CES-D 8)17block of questions was used from CRONOS-2 wave 4. These questions form an 8-item adapted version of the original CES-D questionnaire18—a 20-item measure asking respondents to rate how often over the past week they have experienced symptoms of depression. The four possible response options were ‘rarely or none of the time’, ‘some of the time’, ‘often’ and ‘most or all of the time’, which were scored from 0 to 3, respectively. To assess anxiety (outcome variable), the Generalised Anxiety Disorder Assessment-7 block of questions19was used from the CRONOS-2 wave 4 web panel. These questions explored how often respondents experienced symptoms of anxiety over the past 2 weeks. The four possible response options were ‘rarely or none of the time’, ‘some of the time’, ‘often’ and ‘most or all of the time’ and the responses were scored from 0 to 3, respectively. For depression and anxiety, the overall scores for each block of questions were calculated, and a cut-off of 10 was applied for potential clinical (moderate/severe) depression or anxiety, according to relevant guidelines.19 20
To assess sleep disturbance (outcome variable), one of the questions from the CES-D 8 block was used, which asked respondents how often in the past week they had experienced restless sleep. Sleep disturbance was converted into a binary variable by grouping the responses ‘rarely or none of the time’ and ‘some of the time’ as not experiencing sleep disturbance, and grouping ‘often’ and ‘most or all of the time’ as positive for experiencing sleep disturbance.
Baseline characteristic data of the respondents were obtained from the ESS10 survey. This included the age of respondents, gender, education level, income level, whether they lived in a rural or urban setting, their country of residence and whether they were a migrant. These were included as model covariates as they were thought to potentially confound the relationship between climate change worry and the outcomes. Education level was determined in ESS10 using an adapted version of the International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED) 2011.21The European Survey-ISCED (ES-ISCED) split highest educational attainment into eight tiers, ranging from less than primary education to a doctoral degree or equivalent.14This was categorised into low, intermediate and high levels of education by grouping ES-ISCED 1–2, ES-ISCED 3–5 and ES-ISCED 6–8, respectively.
A decile approach was used to measure income, based on national deciles of the actual household income range in each country.14Respondents estimated their household’s total income from all sources, after tax and compulsory deductions, and selected 1 of 10 income deciles which applied to them. Income levels were then grouped into low, middle and high income by grouping deciles 1–3, deciles 4–7 and deciles 8–10, respectively.
To assess living in an urban or rural setting, respondents chose the most appropriate description of their domicile, which ranged from ‘a big city’, ‘suburbs or outskirts of a big city’, ‘town or small city’, ‘country village’ to ‘farm or home in countryside’.14The former three categories were grouped as urban and the latter two were grouped as rural. Migration status was derived from a survey item which asked participants whether they were born in their current country of residence (yes/no).
Poststratification weights for CRONOS-2 developed by the ESS team were applied during the analysis to minimise the impact of non-response bias and attrition. This incorporated the poststratified ESS10 design weight and adjustments for non-response at both ESS10 and the respective CRONOS-2 wave.15
Three analyses were conducted to investigate the research questions. First, to determine which countries had the highest levels of climate change worry, climate change worry was summarised descriptively using percentages and graphs, stratifying respondents by country. Next, to investigate the relationship between climate change worry and risk of depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance, two logistic regression analyses were applied. The associations were reported as ORs with 95% CIs.
The first set of models investigated the relationship between climate change worry and depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance in a pooled cross-country analysis. Great Britain was chosen as the reference country for all analyses. The first model of this analysis adjusted for country fixed effects, the second model adjusted for country fixed effects, age and gender, and the third model adjusted for country, age, gender, migrant status, urban/rural status, education and income level. The second set of models investigated the relationship between climate change worry and depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance while stratifying by country to investigate potential differences between countries, adjusting for confounders as in the first set of models.
Respondents who had missing values for any of the variables included in the models were excluded from the analyses to conduct a complete case analysis. All tables and analyses were weighted as recommended.15All analyses were performed using Stata/MP V.18.0.
A total of 6032 of the eligible respondents took part in CRONOS-2 wave 4, and 877 (14.5%) respondents were excluded due to missing data, limiting the number of respondents included in this study to 5155 across the 11 countries. Most of the missing data stemmed from baseline income level (493 missing values) and mental health in the CRONOS-2 wave 4 questionnaire (386 missing values). Our study assessed levels of climate change worry across Europe and its relationship with future risk of depression, anxiety and sleep disturbance. The highest levels of climate change worry were found in Portugal, Austria and Slovenia, reflecting heightened public concern in these countries. However, associations between climate change worry and at least one mental health outcome were only observed in Austria, Italy, Slovenia and Belgium. The pooled analyses indicated that climate change worry was associated with increased risk of clinical anxiety, but not depression or sleep disturbance. Stratified analyses demonstrated heterogeneity in the strength and significance of these associations between countries, with the strongest relationship between climate worry and anxiety observed in Slovenia and Italy.
The contextual differences between countries are important to consider when interpreting these findings. Geopolitical priorities, media narratives and environmental policies may all influence the salience of climate change as a psychological concern.9Countries such as Italy, which reported stronger associations between climate worry and anxiety, may place less emphasis on climate change in their political agendas,22potentially leaving individuals feeling unsupported and helpless. Conversely, countries with more robust climate policies and more visible government action, such as Sweden and Great Britain, may foster a sense of collective efficacy, helping to mitigate the psychological burden of climate worry. This aligns with prior evidence suggesting that belief in governmental action may help to alleviate climate anxiety, particularly among younger populations.7
Differences in climatic conditions further underscore the need to interpret the results within broader national contexts. Southern European countries such as Portugal and Italy are more vulnerable to the direct effects of climate change, including heatwaves and droughts, which could amplify climate anxiety through lived experiences of extreme weather. When combined with weak climate policies, worries around climate change may be compounded and likely to shape public perceptions of climate change as a pressing threat, influencing both the prevalence and mental health impacts of climate anxiety.
Access to healthcare facilities and mental health services may also play a role in shaping the observed associations. Countries with greater investment in mental health infrastructure and more readily available access to mental healthcare could buffer against the adverse effects of climate worry, particularly among vulnerable populations, such as people living with existing mental health conditions. Further research is needed to explore how health system capacity interacts with climate anxiety across different regions.
The current study provides a snapshot of climate worry and mental health outcomes at two time points, but broader trends in mental health may help contextualise the findings. The increasing prominence of climate change in public discourse, coupled with the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic, may have intensified anxiety levels over time.23Longitudinal studies that incorporate repeated measures of climate anxiety and mental health outcomes are needed to better understand how these relationships evolve in response to global and national events.
Strengths of this study include its longitudinal follow-up and cross-national approach. This allowed for temporal assessment of the exposure and outcomes in a more representative sample across Europe than has been conducted before, increasing its external validity in comparison to existing research. In addition, we examined three important outcomes using validated measures.
However, there were also several limitations. Our measure of climate change worry is relatively simple, and future studies comparing findings with validated measures of climate anxiety would be helpful. Longitudinal population-representative surveys containing climate anxiety measurement tools across several countries are, to the best of our knowledge, non-existent. Survey developers should consider adding appropriate climate anxiety measures to existing surveys, given its increasing prominence. The number of respondents from each country was also inconsistent, with smaller samples in some countries limiting the precision of model estimates. Relatedly, our sample was limited to those countries participating in ESS10 and CRONOS-2, which did not cover all European countries and provides no information on countries outside of Europe, particularly those within the Global South. Respondents in this study had to be 18 years or older to take part in CRONOS-2. Previous studies have indicated that youth and children are more likely to be aware of climate change impacts and are more vulnerable to adverse mental health effects.3 5As a result, the overall levels of climate change worry and its impact on mental health may be underestimated in this study. A significant proportion of missing values stemmed from the CRONOS-2 mental health questionnaire. This potentially introduces bias, particularly for the Czech Republic and Portugal, whereby 17% and 9% of respondents had to be excluded (respectively) due to missing information regarding mental health. CRONOS-2 was conducted via an online survey, therefore, excluding people without internet access, which may introduce some bias. Furthermore, using differing methods of data collection may introduce variability into findings,24as well as variability in response rates.25Lastly, despite the findings of this study, the study design cannot attribute poor mental health to climate change worry. Further research with repeated measures of climate change worry and mental health outcomes is required to establish causality.
Our findings have several implications for policy and practice. The study highlights the need to incorporate mental health considerations into both climate policies and health strategies.26It should be highlighted that climate anxiety can be a healthy response to environmental change but may also develop into a potential public health issue. This underscores the importance of interdisciplinary and cross-sector collaborations between mental health professionals, climate scientists and policy-makers to design prevention strategies and interventions that address both the psychological and environmental dimensions of climate change.27
The observed heterogeneity between countries suggests that one-size-fits-all approaches to climate anxiety interventions may be ineffective. Future research should focus on identifying country-specific drivers of climate worry and developing tailored interventions that account for national contexts, including vulnerability to climate hazards, climate policies and healthcare infrastructure. Additionally, more work is needed to evaluate the efficacy of community-based interventions that empower individuals to engage in collective climate action, potentially transforming worry and anxiety into positive environmental behaviours.
Finally, the lack of practice frameworks for addressing climate anxiety highlights an urgent gap in mental health services. Traditional therapeutic models, which often target irrational fears, may not adequately address climate anxiety, which is rooted in legitimate concerns about the future. Developing new frameworks that validate climate anxiety while equipping individuals with coping strategies will be essential in supporting mental health in the context of climate change.28
In conclusion, this study provides novel evidence linking climate change worry to increased risk of anxiety in several European countries. The findings emphasise the importance of considering the broader sociopolitical and environmental context when assessing the mental health impacts of climate anxiety.
