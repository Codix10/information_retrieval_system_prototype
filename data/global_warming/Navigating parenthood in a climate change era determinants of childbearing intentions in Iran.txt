Climate change has significantly altered global ecosystems, including population dynamics, and consequently, decisions regarding childbearing. Climate change is considered one of the factors influencing childbearing intention. Therefore, the present study was conducted to explore the determinants of childbearing intention in women and men of reproductive age in the climate change era in Iran. The present qualitative study employs a conventional content analysis approach. The participants comprised 40 men and women of reproductive age residing in Qom, who were selected from February to July 2024 through purposive sampling while adhering to the principle of maximum diversity and were included in the study after providing informed consent. Data were collected through semi-structured individual interviews until theoretical saturation was achieved and were analyzed concurrently. During the inductive data analysis process, 360 codes and 5 main categories with 16 subcategories emerged. The main categories encompassed “Climate Change Awareness and Its Perceived Relevance to Childbearing Intention,” “Ethical Dilemmas and Parental Responsibility,” “Environmental and Economic Barriers to Childbearing,” “Health Implications of Climate Change,” and “Policy Demands for Climate-Resilient Parenthood.” Childbearing intentions in the climate change era are shaped by a dynamic interplay of environmental, economic, health, and cultural factors. This understanding emphasizes the necessity of implementing multidisciplinary solutions to protect human health and fertility rates in the context of climate change.

Climate change has emerged as one of the fundamental challenges of the 21st century, exerting widespread impacts on global ecosystems and human behaviors, including decisions related to childbearing1,2. While the direct effects of climate change, such as water scarcity, agricultural damage, and the occurrence of extreme climatic events, have influenced fertility rates3. These effects are often intertwined with broader social, political, and economic factors that shape the desire to have children4.
Research indicates that the global Total Fertility Rate (TFR) has declined significantly, from 4.84 in 1950 to 2.23 in 2021. Projections suggest that this downward trend will persist, with TFRs forecasted to decrease to 1.83 by 2050 and 1.59 by 21005,6. Iran, in particular, has undergone one of the most rapid fertility declines in history, with its TFR plummeting from 7.41 in 1980 to 1.52 in 2021. Forecasts suggest that Iran’s TFR may fall further to 1.31 by 20507. Similarly, in the city of Qom, the TFR has decreased from 2.23 in 2015 to 1.74 in 20218, reflecting a broader trend of declining fertility. This decline has been attributed to a confluence of factors, including environmental concerns, economic pressures, shifting gender roles, evolving cultural norms, and changes in reproductive patterns9–11. Studies show that young people, in particular, are increasingly factoring the long-term consequences of climate change into their decisions about family formation10,12. Concerns about the future well-being of children, rising living costs, and economic instability are frequently cited as deterrents to childbearing10,12–14. These patterns highlight the intricate interplay of environmental, economic, and social factors in shaping fertility rates14,15.
Beyond climate-related factors, political instability and economic inequality also play a significant role in shaping childbearing decisions. Economic downturns, high unemployment rates, and insufficient social welfare systems can diminish individuals’ willingness to have children16–18. Similarly, political factors such as government policies on family planning, childcare support, and environmental regulations can either amplify or alleviate the challenges associated with climate change19. Furthermore, social transformations, including increased educational attainment among women, delayed marriage, and a growing emphasis on career development, have contributed to declining fertility rates in many regions20.
The Islamic Republic of Iran, a prominent nation in the Middle East, offers a compelling case study for examining the interplay of climate change, economic pressures, and cultural shifts on fertility21. Iran has confronted significant environmental challenges, including rising temperatures, reduced heavy precipitation, prolonged droughts, temperature variability, declining rainfall, desertification resulting in the expansion of arid lands, frequent dust storms that degrade air quality, and severe water scarcity exacerbated by both reduced precipitation and overexploitation22,23. These environmental stressors are particularly acute in Iran’s warm and arid regions, such as Qom, where they intensify drought conditions, deplete water resources, and strain water infrastructure and agricultural systems, thereby directly and indirectly affecting livelihoods22,23. Compounding these environmental issues are economic sanctions, inflation, and restricted access to resources, which together create a multifaceted context for reproductive decision-making22,24. Studies show that young people are increasingly expressing hesitancy about having children18, driven by concerns about the future, economic difficulties, and environmental degradation15,18,25.
Despite growing scholarly attention to the role of climate change in shaping fertility patterns, the relationship between the two remains complex and multifaceted. Some researchers show that environmental stressors may contribute to reduced fertility rates, while others highlight the potential for adaptive strategies such as migration or alterations in family structures to mitigate these impacts2. Moreover, the interplay between climate change and fertility is frequently mediated by cultural, economic, and political contexts, underscoring the need for a more nuanced understanding of these dynamics2.
Given the paucity of research on how climate change interacts with social, economic, and political factors to shape reproductive intentions, this study aims to investigate the determinants of childbearing decisions among women and men of reproductive age within the context of climate change. By exploring the experiences and perceptions of young people, this research seeks to elucidate the factors influencing fertility trends in a rapidly evolving global landscape. Furthermore, identifying these determinants can inform the development of targeted policies and interventions that address the challenges posed by climate change while promoting sustainable population growth.
This qualitative study utilized a conventional content analysis approach and was conducted from February 2024 to January 2025 in Qom, Iran. Situated in a dry and semi-arid region of central Iran, Qom experiences a hot and dry climate. The eastern, central, and northern parts of the province are characterized by extensive desert and arid landscapes, rendering Qom one of Iran’s driest regions. Over 95% of Qom’s population resides in urban areas26–28, a demographic feature relevant to the study’s focus on climate change impacts.
Participants were recruited through visits to healthcare centers in Qom. Upon presenting a letter of introduction and securing informed consent, the researcher employed a purposive sampling method to identify individuals willing and able to express their perspectives. To ensure diverse representation, maximum variation was sought across age, education, and socioeconomic status.
Data were gathered through semi-structured, in-depth interviews conducted by the primary researcher (Z.A.). Each interview, lasting 30 to 45 min, was digitally recorded, with brief handwritten notes taken concurrently. Interviews took place at healthcare centers in Qom. Eligible participants were young adults aged 18–45 years, Iranian nationals, and residents of Qom for at least five years, who demonstrated willingness to participate, the ability to articulate their views, and a commitment to remain in the study.
Data saturation was informally evaluated through an iterative coding process, tracking the emergence of themes across interviews. Initial guiding questions included: “How do you perceive the impact of climate change on your childbearing intentions and plans?” and “Can you describe how climate change influences your views on childbearing intentions?” Follow-up interviews were conducted as necessary to verify data and address gaps (see supplementary file). Interviews were transcribed and coded on the same day, with transcripts shared with participants for verification and corrective feedback. Participants retained the right to withdraw from the study at any point.
Qualitative data were analyzed following the conventional content analysis framework outlined by Lundman and Graneheim (2004)29. The process entailed: immersion in the data through repeated reading of interview transcripts, extraction of codes and identification of relationships among them, classification of codes based on similarities and differences, and categorization of codes into themes. To ensure analytical rigor, the study adhered to Lincoln and Guba’s (1985) criteria30: credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability. Dependability was supported by detailed documentation of all research stages. Credibility was enhanced by sharing preliminary findings with participants for feedback. Confirmability was maintained through meticulous recording of decisions and interpretations. Transferability was facilitated by providing comprehensive details about the research context and participants.
Researcher reflexivity and positionality were addressed to minimize bias through:1documenting personal reflections and assumptions during data collection and analysis;2discussing findings with external colleagues to challenge interpretations;3cross-verifying themes with multiple coders and integrating diverse data sources; and4validating preliminary findings with participants. Comparisons with existing studies and identification of common patterns were conducted to support generalizability, as elaborated in the discussion section. Data analysis was performed using MAXQDA 20 software (VERBI Software Group, Berlin, Germany).
Written informed consent was obtained from all participants. Anonymization procedures, including the use of numerical identifiers for quotes, were implemented to protect participant identity. The study complied with the principles of the Helsinki Declaration and received approval from the Ethics Committee of Qom University of Medical Sciences (code: IR.MUQ.REC.1403.123).
This qualitative study was conducted using a descriptive-exploratory design and content analysis method. In this type of descriptive study, the aim is to obtain more information about a specific experience and to gain a picture of a natural situation, while the exploratory design focuses on revealing potential variables and the relationships between them. The participants included 40 individuals, with 30 (75%) being female and 10 (25%) males, ages ranging from 18 to 44 years (31.7 ± 6.6). Of these, 32.5% had no children, 15% had one child, 42.5% had two children, and 10% had three children. The demographic characteristics of the participants are shown in Table1. Data analysis resulted in the emergence of 360 codes, 5 main categories, and 16 subcategories (see Table2). The main categories encompassed “Climate Change Awareness and Its Perceived Relevance to Childbearing Intention,” “Ethical Dilemmas and Parental Responsibility,” “Environmental and Economic Barriers to Childbearing,” “Health Implications of Climate Change,” and “Policy Demands for Climate-Resilient Parenthood.” each of which is detailed in the text along with the relevant subcategories.

Themes and subthemes related to the experiences of women regarding the effects of climate change on decision-making about childbearing.
According to the participants, climate change has affected childbearing intention, and quotes highlighted direct links between environmental instability and reproductive choices. Participants expressed in this regard:
“Childbearing and climate are two interrelated topics. The weather impacts lifestyle and quality of life.”(Woman 1, 18 years old).
Another participant also commented on climate change and childbearing intention:
“In my opinion,global warming and water scarcity are the most important climate changes that can affect people’s lives and the issue of having children.”(Woman 24, 34 years old).
From the perspective of the participants, one way to adapt to the current climatic conditions is to live in a larger space and a location with a better climate. In this regard, one participant stated:
“I would like to have one or two more children,and if we have suitable climatic conditions andspace,people are inclined to have children!”(Woman 4, 28 years old).
Participant number 17 was looking for new solutions to optimize living conditions in the climate crisis:
“In Qom,there is a lot of dust. In this dust,the windows and doors should be double-glazed. Orin extreme heat and sunlight”(Woman 17, 43years old).
Participant number 5 saw the solution for having children in bad climatic conditions and extreme heat as relocation as a strategy to escape climate stressors:
“Changing the living environment has a significant impact; most migrations are due to climatechange. If my house were in the north,I might have had another child.”(Woman 5, 37 years old).
The opinions of participants regarding the impact of climate change on childbearing decisions were completely contradictory. Some participants dismissed climate’s relevance, prioritizing economic/cultural factors: In this regard, he stated:
“In my opinion,the weather doesn’t affect fertility; right now,it’s more about economic issuesthat are influencing young people.”(Man 26, 34 years old).
Other participants also mentioned that climate change does not affect their decision to have children.
“To be honest,I haven’t thought about the connection between climate and having children,andit doesn’t matter to me. If I want to have a child,I will have one regardless of the circumstances.”(Man 8, 34 years old).
Parents expressed anxiety about raising children in resource-scarce futures:
“I often think about the bad weather conditions,and it’s a concern for many parents. I keep thinking that in these conditions of drought and bad weather,I might not be able to handle parenting.”(Woman 12, 33 years old).
Another participant in this area stated:
“I believe climate change influences our choice to have fewer children. Concerns about resource shortages in the future make potential parents feel a greater responsibility when considering another child.”(Woman 2, 24 years old).
Parents may develop new attitudes towards having children in light of climate challenges.
According to the participants’ views, climate change has led to a reconsideration of decisions regarding parenthood, and among some participants, negative thoughts about having children were quite evident, and climate concerns prompted deliberate limitations on family size. In this regard, one participant stated:
“Given the current climate conditions,having a child may be a mistake,and if I choose to get pregnant,having just one child seems more acceptable.”(Woman 6, 34 years old).
Some participants listed climate change as a barrier to having children and expressed concerns about parents’ abilities in these conditions. They believed that climate change could make parenting difficult or even impossible: Another participant said in this regard:
“When I hear someone has had a baby,I feel sad instead of happy. We want to be parents and bring a child into a world where basic resources like water,electricity,and air may be rationed.”(Woman 33, 36 years old).
Moral concerns about overpopulation and resource depletion shaped decisions. One of the participants in this field stated:
“The focus on water scarcity is concerning. As the population grows,water use and shortages will increase,harming nature. Therefore,having fewer children is a better option.”(Man25, 35 years old).
Poor environmental conditions discouraged childbearing. From the perspective of many participants, poor living conditions and lack of access to resources discourage them from having children. One participant noted:
“In apartment living,air pollution and extreme heat often force us to stay indoors,making it difficult to keep kids entertained. These challenges can lead families to reconsider having more children due to concerns about the impact on their mental health.(Woman 21, 25 years old)
According to other participants, increasing air pollution and dust have led them to stay indoors more, and in this weather and small apartment living, taking care of children has become very difficult for them. This has been cited as a major reason for their reluctance to have more children. One of the participants stated:
“The air is so dirty that it feels like it’s raining dirt. My son has to stay home,and with his little sister,we’re cramped in our 50-square-meter house. Honestly,having another baby seems pointless; I’m scared for their future.”(Woman 39, 28 years old).
Several participants made connections between climate change and its effects on jobs and income, rising living costs, and the issue of childbearing. One participant remarked:
“When pollution and extreme heat disrupt our lives,it impacts working conditions and wages. This drop in income makes having children feel unattainable. Climate change,heat,drought,and lack of rainfall are interconnected issues that discourage people from considering parenthood.”(Woman 17, 40 years old).
Additionally, according to another study participant who is a father of two children, economic difficulties and anxiety over business stagnation had an impact on the motivation to have children:
“Hot weather and dust storms lead to more holidays,reducing profitability for many businesses and decreasing motivation to have children.”(Man 40, 36 years old).
The rising cost of living caused by climate change has influenced parents’ decisions about childbearing, to the point where one participant identified unsuitable living conditions as a reason for high living costs and stated:
“Currently,extreme heat and rising costs make me hesitant to have another child. The high cost of diapers is daunting,and I can’t use cloth ones like before due to my living situation,which also increases expenses.”(Woman 23, 32 years old).
Another participant expressed:
“Poor weather and polluted air have increased health risks and challenges for families,making it easier to choose a more comfortable life with fewer or no children.”(Woman 7, 43 years old).
Participants linked climate change to physical health threats:
“Abnormalities in children are rising… my friend had abortions due to polluted air.”(Woman 3, 43 years old).
Drought, inadequate resource supply, and economic issues have led some attendees to feel that physical illnesses are a result of climate change. One participant mentioned:
" Climate change and drought disrupt nutrition,affecting metabolism and causing irregular ovulation,which can lead to early menopause. I’m concerned I might experience early menopause as well.”(Woman 5, 37 years old).
The negative impacts of climate change on parents’ mental health can affect their ability to have children and increase feelings of inadequacy. For some participants, in these climatic conditions, the future seemed bleak, to the point that the current worrying conditions posed a barrier to their childbearing. In this regard, one participant stated:
“Climate change significantly affects the decision to have children. Concerns about dwindling resources like water,electricity,and gas make me question how a child would thrive in the future. This worry has led to my reluctance to have kids in recent years.”(Woman 2, 24 years old).
Several participants expressed their concerns about climate change, as worries for the future and.
the next generation. The anxiety about the next generation progressed to the point where one participant was willing to limit childbearing due to worries about the future:
“Well,I think bringing children into the world during this time might be a mistake,and even if adecision is made to get pregnant,a child considering their future would be more acceptable.”
(Woman 2, 24 years old)
One of the participants expressed doubt about accepting her reproductive role during this time of climate change and uncertainty about the future:
“Should I bring a child into the world when I’m not sure about the conditions for them tolive?”(Woman 18, 23 years old).
Changes in government policies to adapt to climate change can impact parents’ beliefs and choices about having children, especially regarding financial and social support. Creating sustainable infrastructure, access to healthcare, economic security, and education, and raising awareness can shape a new overall attitude toward parenthood and parental responsibilities.
Investing in sustainable and climate-resilient infrastructure can assure parents that their living environment will be safer for their children. One participant in this area stated:
“Hearing good news about climate change actions,like transferring water to deserts,cloud seeding,rainwater harvesting,and tree planting,would inspire hope and potentially motivate couples to have children.”(Man 24, 34 years old).
Providing healthcare and mental health services can help reduce stress and anxiety for parents,
bringing them more peace of mind. One participant believed that the government should increase its support during climate change and said:
“We need to see which aspect of human life is most affected by climate change. For example,if it impacts health,the government should provide health support and universal insurance to give.
families peace of mind. Access to sufficient and free healthcare can alleviate this concern for the younger generation.”(Man 40, 36 years old).
Providing financial and economic support can help parents feel more secure about their finances and not worry about meeting their children’s needs. Financial security was deemed critical.
Participant number 3 believed the government could take cost-free actions:
“If someone avoids debt,they can have children. Many people having kids are not financially stable. The country can implement cost-free measures that wouldn’t be impacted by climate conditions.”(Woman 3, 43 years old).
Participant number 5 said:
“Job stability and government support would reduce fears.”(Woman 5, 37 years old).
Educational programs about climate change and ways to adapt to it can help parents better face challenges and feel more in control.
A 22-year-old woman expressed her thoughts on this matter:
“If our cities are facing drought,how can we prevent that? How can we preserve our underground? resources? And what can we do to ensure that future generations will have a better quality of life? Well,young generations will feel more hopeful about life.”(Woman 9, 22 years old).
This qualitative study investigates the determinants of childbearing intentions among Iranian women and men in the context of climate change, revealing a complex interplay of socio-economic, cultural, and health-related factors. While climate change is an influence on reproductive decisions, its impact is embedded within broader concerns. This discussion highlights the study’s novel contributions, refines comparisons to existing literature, and proposes a conceptual framework linking climate stressors to reproductive decision-making.
The study uniquely positions climate change as a critical determinant of childbearing intentions within Iran’s socio-cultural and environmental context, where rising temperatures, water scarcity, and air pollution amplify its relevance. Key novel findings include: Climate Change Awareness and Relevance: Awareness of local climate impacts (e.g., drought, pollution) initiates concern, modulated by education, age, and occupation. Educated women and older freelance workers perceived greater relevance to childbearing. Participants identified rising temperatures and water scarcity as critical stressors, aligning with global narratives that frame climate change as a threat to future security31.
Environmental and Economic Barriers to Childbearing: Participants in freelance roles, vulnerable to climate-related income disruptions, expressed heightened hesitancy toward childbearing. This extends prior research on economic uncertainty and fertility32,33by integrating a climate-specific lens, emphasizing how environmental instability compounds economic precariat.
Ethical Dilemmas and Parental Responsibility: Some participants viewed limiting childbearing as a moral duty to preserve the environment, contextualizing global sustainability discourses within Iranian norms, where family size carries cultural weight. Another study has also noted that an increasing number of young people consider climate change in their childbearing intentions, arguing that having fewer children reduces carbon emissions and significantly helps mitigate climate disasters18. Individuals with more children reported greater anxiety about resource shortages and their offspring’s future well-being, influencing reluctance to have additional children. In line with the results of this study, these findings align with the idea that young adults experiencing climate change and the anxiety it brings likely perceive greater threats to the future well-being of their children, which in turn affects their motivations related to embracing parental roles13,18,34.This builds on climate anxiety literature35by highlighting the role of existing parental responsibilities.
Health Implications of Climate Change on Reproduction: The study identifies climate-related health issues such as increased miscarriages, early menopause, and reduced sperm quality as deterrents to childbearing. Consistent with the findings of this study, global trends over the past 50 years have repeatedly demonstrated that declining fertility rates, reduced sperm counts, and rising abortion rates remain significant even after accounting for socio-economic conditions caused by climate change, and this is observed on a global scale36–38. In this study, participants mentioned that with climate change and drought, their nutritional quality has decreased, and their metabolism and ovulation have been disrupted, leaving women worried about early menopause. Consistent with the current study’s findings, Gaskin and colleagues showed that a 1-degree Celsius increase in temperature during the 90 days before the ovarian reserve test was associated with lower AFC (Antral Follicle Count)39. Data indicates that climate-related disturbances may impact women’s long-term reproductive health40, and it’s concluded that climate change poses a significant risk to reproductive health41. Awareness of these issues has made women fearful of climate change and their effects, which has also impacted their decisions about having children.
Policy Demands for Climate-Resilient Parenthood: Participants emphasized the need for policies addressing climate change’s intersection with fertility (e.g., financial support, sustainable infrastructure). This finding advances an underexplored area, underscoring proactive policymaking’s role. Comparisons to prior work are focused on these contributions. For example, while Schneider-Mayerson and Leong (2020)18link climate anxiety to reproductive choices globally, this study specifies how localized stressors economic, health-related, and ethical shape intentions in Iran.
A conceptual framework emerges from the study’s five main categories, illustrating pathways through which climate stressors influence childbearing intentions: Awareness of local climate impacts (e.g., drought, pollution) initiates concern, modulated by education, age, and occupation. Educated women and older freelance workers perceived greater relevance to childbearing. Ethical considerations about environmental strain and future generations’ quality of life, intensified by parental roles, discourage larger families. Climate change worsens living conditions (e.g., air quality, small apartments) and economic stability, deterring reproduction, especially among those in precarious employment. Physical (e.g., reproductive health decline) and mental (e.g., future-oriented anxiety) health effects of climate change reduce childbearing willingness, with women particularly citing fertility concerns. Policy Demands: Calls for government intervention (e.g., healthcare access, economic security) reflect a need to mitigate climate impacts on reproductive choices, forming a feedback loop with awareness and barriers. These pathways interlink: economic insecurity heightens health concerns, while ethical dilemmas are exacerbated by inadequate policy support. Demographic factors, notably the predominance of female participants (75%), suggest a gendered perspective on these dynamics.
One of the strengths of this study was the deep and accurate understanding of the feelings, experiences, and concerns of married women and men of childbearing age, which led to a better understanding of the factors influencing childbearing intention. Additionally, by using methods like in-depth interviews, the researcher collected diverse data that represented various perspectives. The flexibility of this approach also allowed the researcher to address new questions during the research process and change the research direction if necessary. One of the limitations is the study’s small sample size (n= 40), and focus on Qom, a dry region, limits generalizability across Iran’s diverse climates. Nonetheless, it advances understanding of climate change’s role in reproductive decision-making, offering a framework for future research and policy development in climate-affected contexts.
This study highlights the complex factors influencing childbearing intentions in Iran amid climate change, revealing that while climate anxiety affects educated populations, its impact on reproductive choices varies. Limitations, such as reliance on self-reported data and a lack of demographic diversity, suggest the need for larger, more varied samples and longitudinal studies. Future research should also examine how specific climate events, like water scarcity, influence reproductive decisions and explore the role of government policies in shaping these intentions.
Given Iran’s unique sociopolitical context, the following recommendations are proposed for policymakers: Integrate Climate Change Education into Family Planning Services: Create culturally sensitive programs that inform families about climate change impacts and support smaller family sizes aligned with cultural values. Invest in Sustainable Technologies: Promote green technologies that address climate change while boosting economic growth, alleviating financial pressures on childbearing decisions. Support Community-Based Adaptation Initiatives: Empower communities to develop resilience strategies, reducing environmental stressors that influence family planning. Establish a National Task Force: Form a multi-sectoral task force to create tailored strategies addressing climate resilience and reproductive health equity, ensuring cultural relevance.
