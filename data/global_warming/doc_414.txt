Worldwide, exploited fish populations are increasingly affected by the combined effects of warming and fishing. Disentangling the relative effects of these factors is challenging yet crucial for designing management strategies. We used a temperature-dependent population dynamics model to assess the impacts of lake warming and fishing on 521 freshwater fish populations in the Midwestern United States—a transitional zone between cold- and warmwater species. Overall, most warmwater species (65% of populations) exhibited increases in productivity from warming, while slightly more than half of the cool-/cold-water species (53% of populations) experienced reduced productivity. Populations closer to their carrying capacity showed greater resilience to warming. For the majority of populations (92%), fishing had a more pronounced effect on population dynamics than warming during the time period examined. Therefore, while warming is likely to increasingly threaten many fish populations, effective local fishery management remains a key lever to mitigate these impacts.
Fishing has had a greater impact than warming on most exploited freshwater fish populations in the US Midwest since 1980.
Rapid warming experienced by inland freshwater systems has resulted in fundamental shifts in the physiological, biochemical, and ecological processes that define them (1–4). These changes affect the dynamics of fish populations that inhabit these waters, many of which support important recreational and subsistence fisheries (5). For example, lake warming exposes some populations to temperatures above their optimal ranges while making thermal conditions more suitable for other populations (2). Warming can also affect the dynamics of freshwater fish populations through decreases in dissolved oxygen (6), reduction and shortening of ice cover (7), changes in plankton phenology (8), and increases in toxic algae blooms (9).
Fish populations display varied responses to warming, both within and between species (10). Individual fish growth rates follow a dome-shaped thermal performance curve (11): Growth increases with temperature up to an optimal point, after which further increases in temperature negatively affect growth and ultimately survival. Yet, population-level impacts of warming are more complex and less predictable. Demographic properties shape the response of populations to warming, as fish at different life stages have different thermal preferences and ability to adapt to a warming environment (12). Ecosystem-specific factors, such as food availability, habitat quality, and the effects of warming on predators further modulate the response of fish populations to warming (13). Given the complexity of theoretical predictions regarding the impact of warming on fish populations, standardized quantitative analysis of empirical data is needed to consistently compare warming impacts across species.
Using empirical data to assess the impact of warming on freshwater fish populations presents a challenge as warming often occurs concurrently with other anthropogenic stressors, resulting in complex and interconnected effects on fish population dynamics (14–17). Fishing is a substantial stressor for populations targeted by recreational, subsistence, or commercial fisheries and can obscure the true impact of warming if not correctly factored into analyses (18). As most biological surveys are designed for monitoring exploited fisheries, the influence of fishing often implicitly affects the resulting biomass index data. To discern the role of warming, it is critical to use methods that can differentiate the intertwined impacts of fishing and warming on fish population dynamics.
Understanding the relative impacts of fishing and warming on fish populations is also important for developing adaptive fishery management strategies in light of climate change (19). Harvest regulations may be suitable for populations that are largely affected by fishing, but for populations negatively affected by warming, reducing harvest alone might offer limited recovery. In addition, directing fishing effort from populations negatively affected by warming to those that thrive in warmer conditions could potentially safeguard vulnerable populations while preserving angling opportunities (20,21). For marine fisheries, it has been argued that improvements in fisheries management can, in many fisheries, counteract the effects of climate change (22). It is not yet clear whether this holds true for most freshwater fisheries.
In this study, we investigate the relative impacts of fishing and warming on 521 populations of eight fish species in 197 lakes in the Midwestern United States over the past four decades (1980 to 2021) (Fig. 1and fig. S2). This region presents an instructive case study because its lakes support multispecies fisheries with fish exhibiting diverse thermal preferences and are subject to universal warming during the study period (Fig. 1and fig. S3). Quantifying the effects of multiple stressors on these populations is challenging because the presence of a large number of geographically dispersed lakes means that surveys of fish biomass are intermittent and rarely occur in consecutive years in the same lakes (23). Furthermore, unlike large-scale commercial fisheries with mandatory catch reporting, recreational harvest estimates—based on voluntary angler interviews (“creel” surveys)—are even less frequent than biomass surveys and subject to greater uncertainty (24). Here, we developed a temperature-dependent population dynamics model to disentangle the impacts of fishing and lake warming on population dynamics while overcoming the common challenges posed by sparse and uncertain monitoring in inland fisheries. We fit the lake-specific dataset to each population (i.e., species-lake pair) independently. This approach allows us to estimate population-specific dynamics without assuming uniformity across species or lakes, thereby capturing the unique characteristics of each population. We conducted rigorous simulation testing and sensitivity analysis to confirm the model’s ability to estimate key parameters despite limited data. The application of this scalable approach in other regions could improve our understanding of the relative importance of fishing and climate change on the fluctuations of freshwater fish populations globally.

Species-specific relationships to warming were closely tied to their thermal preferences. Productivity of all warmwater species (represented by 230 populations of smallmouth bass, bluegill, black crappie, and largemouth bass) on average increased with temperature, while productivity of cold-water species (represented by three populations of cisco) on average responded negatively to temperature (Fig. 2B). Cool-water species (represented by 288 populations) exhibited more variable relationships with warming. Productivity of northern pike increased with warming, whereas walleye and yellow perch productivity decreased with temperature on average (Fig. 2B). Overall, most warmwater species (65% of populations examined) benefited from warming, while slightly more than half of the cool-/cold-water species (53% of populations examined) experienced reduced productivity. A weak (−0.14) but significant (P= 0.002) negative correlation was observed between the absolute values of warming impacts and stock status (ratio of biomass over carrying capacity) in the initial year (Fig. 2C). This indicates that populations farther below their carrying capacity tended to experience greater impacts from warming. The negative correlation between the absolute values of warming impacts and initial stock status were unaffected or became slightly stronger when populations near carrying capacity levels were excluded (fig. S12). This suggests that our conclusion is not solely driven by reduced responsiveness of populations near carrying capacity.
The range of average growing degree days (GDDs) among lakes is substantial, spanning from 1941 to 3116—a 61% difference. However, the geographic distribution of estimated effects of warming showed no clear latitudinal pattern (Fig. 3). Instead, populations of the same species in close proximity sometimes responded differently to warming, suggesting that lake-specific environmental or ecological factors may play an important role (Fig. 3).

Stock status in the terminal years showed notable variations in both inter- and intraspecies (fig. S13). Only 1% of populations were more negatively influenced by warming impact than by fishing. For 48% of the populations where biomass declined with temperature, its marginal impact was less than that of fishing. For 7 and 44% of populations, respectively, warming fully or partially compensated for biomass reduction caused by fishing (Fig. 4).

To test the robustness of our results to data availability, we increased the minimum requirement from four biomass index points and two harvest data points to six biomass index points and two harvest data points, reducing the dataset from 521 to 437 populations. Further increasing the requirement to six biomass index points and three harvest data points reduced the dataset to 182 populations. Despite these stricter thresholds, the key findings—including the proportion of populations with significant positive or negative warming impacts (figs. S14 and S15) and the relative influence of warming and fishing (figs. S16 and S17)—did not change substantially. However, under the strictest data requirement, only one cold-water population remained in the dataset, which showed a positive response to warming (fig. S15B). In addition, the relationship between the absolute values of warming impacts and stock status in the initial year became statistically insignificant (fig. S15C). We also performed a sensitivity analysis by splitting the dataset by state to assess whether differences in survey protocols across agencies might affect the results. The primary findings remained consistent: Warming has not been a stronger driver of population dynamics than fishing for most evaluated populations in either Wisconsin or Minnesota (fig. S18).
Our results indicate that, in the four decades from 1980 to 2021, warming has not been a stronger driver of population dynamics than fishing for most evaluated populations in this region. As a result, local fishery management remains a key lever for maintaining freshwater fish populations and supporting inland fisheries. Projection studies on global marine fisheries suggest that adopting climate-adaptive management, which accounts for shifts in fish productivity and spatial distribution, could mitigate the adverse effects of warming (22). The quantified impacts of warming and fishing in this study emphasize that, for inland lake fisheries, effective management of harvests may also have the potential to support many fisheries despite the challenges posed by current levels of warming.
We also found that populations with lower estimated biomass relative to their carrying capacities are associated with greater sensitivity to warming (Fig. 2C). This relationship is consistent with findings from marine fisheries (10) and may be attributable to the dual role of fishing in reducing population size and altering population age/size structure (25). Selectively removing larger-bodied fish results in a population increasingly composed of younger, smaller, and less fecund individuals, who exhibit reduced resilience to environmental changes (26). Consequently, fishing may amplify fluctuations in population size caused by temperature variability. These findings underline the value of conserving sufficient biomass levels for maintaining the resilience of fish populations in the face of warming.
Our results highlight the value of population-specific fisheries management in certain cases, as population biomass and vulnerability to climate change can vary within the same species, with some populations deviating substantially from the mean (Fig. 2Band fig. S13). While some US states agencies manage populations of the same species using statewide or regional regulations, certain lakes are subject to more customized, site-specific rules. Many agencies are expanding the list of lakes exempt from statewide regulations as more lake-specific data become available (27). On the other hand, there are also proposals to simplify regulations by implementing uniform, often more conservative, statewide measures (28). While uniform regulations can enhance clarity and compliance, population-specific management better accounts for the unique characteristics of different populations. This approach could be more effective in targeting and protecting climate-vulnerable populations while maximizing fishing opportunities in lightly depleted or warming-benefited populations. The availability of detailed, lake-specific data is essential to support this type of management, which may remain limited for many lakes.
While warming impacts can differ greatly among populations within the same species, the average response to warming is closely related to a species’ thermal affinity (Fig. 2B). Our findings show that historical warming has had pronounced effects on both cold- and warmwater species, consistent with theoretical predictions. Specifically, cold-water species (in our analysis, represented solely by cisco with three populations) have predominantly experienced negative impacts from warming, whereas warmwater species have generally benefited. In the study region, populations of cool-water species appear to be at a critical juncture, exhibiting a balanced mix of both positive and negative effects from historical warming. However, the benefits some populations have gained from past warming may not persist. Continued warming might push temperature past the species’ thermal optima. Therefore, mitigating global warming is crucial to prevent widespread adverse impacts on cold- and cool-water species, many of which underpin valuable and culturally important fisheries for both indigenous and recreational users (e.g., walleye). Conserving cold- and cool-water species, given their wide diversity in temperate regions, is important for maintaining biodiversity (29).
Climate change is widely regarded as a substantial threat to ecosystem services. The impact of future climate change often lacks historical precedent, given the magnitude, complexity, and unpredictability of its effects (30–34). While our study presents evidence that fishing has had a more pronounced impact on the population dynamics of evaluated freshwater fish populations than warming in recent decades—indicating that effective fisheries management remains an important tool for mitigating many of the negative effects of warming—it is crucial to acknowledge the study’s limitations. Although the data analyzed represent one of the largest compilations of US freshwater fish population biomass indices and harvest records to date, they still cover only a small proportion of the region. The evaluated populations are numerically biased toward cool-water species (288 populations) and warmwater species (230 populations), with only 3 cold-water populations (all cisco). The community-level impacts of climate warming may have appeared more negative had more cold-water species been available for inclusion. In addition, the evaluated populations only include exploited populations for which we had harvest data. As a result, unfished populations for which harvest would have no influence on population dynamics and where warming may have a notable influence are not included in this study. Furthermore, this study is based on historical data (1980 to 2022) and assumes a linear and monotonic warming impact, which may not provide a precise projection for the future, as continued warming could push populations historically benefitting from warming past their thermal optima. A future marked by rapid and severe warming could also alter the relative importance of warming and fishing, and increasingly frequent temperature extremes (35,36) may result in nonlinear impacts on fish, such as mass mortality events (37,38). Last, our analysis focused solely on temperature without directly accounting for other climate-related stressors. These limitations highlight the need for caution when extrapolating our findings. They also underscore the importance of adopting both climate mitigation and adaptive fisheries management within a precautionary framework, including ongoing monitoring to track whether population responses to temperature shift over time.
Our temperature-dependent population dynamics model requires a time series of three lake-specific datasets for each population (i.e., species-lake pair): (i) an index of relative biomass, (ii) fishery removals and subsidies from stocking programs, and (iii) water temperature. Each population is modeled independently using these lake-specific datasets.
Indices of relative biomass for each fish population were derived from fishery-independent surveys carried out by natural resource agencies in Minnesota and Wisconsin. These surveys used various fishing gears, including boom electrofishers, gill nets, fyke nets, trap nets, and frame nets, which were strategically selected by each state agency for specific focal species. To determine the most accurate index reflecting changes in biomass for each species and state, agency biologists responsible for the surveys were consulted. Our analysis encompassed eight species: one cold-water species, cisco (Coregonus artedi); three cool-water species: yellow perch (Perca flavescens), walleye (Sander vitreus), and northern pike (Esox lucius); and four warmwater species: smallmouth bass (Micropterus dolomieu), bluegill (Lepomis macrochirus), black crappie (Pomoxis nigromaculatus), and largemouth bass (Micropterus salmoides).
All evaluated populations were subject to recreational fishing, and some were also targeted by indigenous fisheries. Recreational harvests were estimated from creel surveys conducted by state management agencies and were obtained from the US Inland Creel and Angler Survey Catalog (39). Since creel surveys are not a census, the estimated harvests are later treated with uncertainty (observation error) in the temperature-dependent population dynamics model. Indigenous harvests were provided by the Great Lakes Indian Fish and Wildlife Commission. Since all indigenous fishing operations are monitored with mandatory creel surveys with 100% coverage, these figures are treated as known quantities in the model. Indigenous fisheries primarily target walleye but occasionally harvest largemouth bass, smallmouth bass, northern pike, bluegill, and yellow perch.
Some of the evaluated populations receive support through hatchery supplementation, whereby fish reared in hatcheries are released into the wild. The biomass of fish added to each population was obtained from stocking reports released by each state’s natural resource agency. Stocking biomass was also treated as known without error in the model. Harvests and stocking are either reported in weight (kilograms) or were converted to weight using published length-weight relationships.
Temperature data for each lake were obtained from a database of national model-based estimates of historical daily lake water temperature from 1980 to 2021 (40). We used GDDs—a cumulative measure of thermal energy above 5°C at the lake surface—as our primary climate warming index. This index reflects the total yearly thermal energy and has been commonly used to predict population fluctuations in past research (41–44). We validated the GDDs index by comparing its correlation with other warming metrics, such as GDDs with base temperatures of 0° or 10°C, peak annual temperature, and open water duration. Considering that early life stages of fish often have narrower range of temperature tolerances (12) and that the current fish population size is influenced by previous recruitment, there might be a lag in observing the environmental impacts on population dynamics. Consequently, we also analyzed average GDDs over 3- and 5-year intervals as warming indices and compared these to the impact estimates derived from the current year’s GDDs.

The logarithm of the shape parametermfollowed a skew normal distribution to penalize small values ofmthat imply implausibly high population growth rates at very low population sizes. Instantaneous fishing mortalityFwas assigned an exponential distribution that corresponds to a uniform prior bounded by [0,1] on the proportion of biomass removed by fishing harvest. The variances of observation errors associated with both the relative biomass index and recreational harvest followed an inverse gamma distribution that was conjugated to the normal distribution. The prior of the warming effect θ followed a normal distribution with mean equal to 0 and SD equal to 2. The distribution implies that the direction of warming effect (i.e., positive or negative) depends entirely on data. A prior for the warming effect following a normal distribution with a larger SD was tested as a sensitivity analysis to ensure that the magnitude of the warming effect was not underestimated by the confinement of the prior distribution (tables S2 and S4).
Model fitting was performed using the Bayesian statistical software Stan through the R (version 4.2) and package “rstan” (50). Four chains of 20,000 iterations each were run, with the first 10,000 designated as warmup. This process produced a total of 40,000 posterior samples for each fitted model. The convergence of each model was verified by split-R^(<1.05) and by inspecting the trace plot for each parameter. Models converged for 521 evaluated populations.
We quantitatively evaluated the relative impacts of warming and fishing on population biomass by conducting simulations for each population model under two hypothetical scenarios: one in which fishing was absent (which reveals the exclusive contribution of fishing to historical biomass change) and another in which warming was absent (which reveals the exclusive impact of warming on historical population biomass). In the scenario where fishing was absent, we set recreational fishing mortality (F) and indigenous harvest to zero while maintaining all other parameters at their estimated values. In the scenario where warming was absent, we set the warming effect (θ) to zero, effectively eliminating impacts of temperature variation, while keeping all other parameters at their estimated values. The population dynamics model was then rerun and stock status (B/k) in the terminal year was calculated for both scenarios. Comparing our estimate of status in the terminal year with those from the two hypothetical simulations, we were able to gain a quantitative understanding of the relative impact of fishing and temperature variation on historical population dynamics (1980 to 2021). Specifically, the difference of stock status in terminal year between the benchmark model and the no-fishing scenario represents the exclusive contribution of fishing to population change. Similarly, the difference of stock status in terminal year between the benchmark model and the no-warming scenario represents the exclusive contribution of warming to population change.
To validate the ability of the model to estimate the effect of warming, we performed (i) a simulation study and (ii) a sensitivity analysis.
The primary focus of this paper was to identify the impact of fishing and warming on fish populations using nonconsecutive relative biomass index data and annual recreational harvest data. To assess the model’s ability to achieve this objective, we conducted simulation tests to examine the model’s accuracy in estimating a population’s warming impact parameter and stock status in terminal year. This analysis helped to determine the minimum data required to generate useful estimates of both quantities, which was ultimately used as a threshold for determining which populations to include in the study. The skill of the model increased with data availability and estimated values were well correlated with the true values when at least 4 years of relative biomass index data and 2 years of recreational harvest data were available for a given population. Populations failing to meet these criteria were excluded from the analysis. See the Supplementary Methods and Results for details on the full simulation testing.
Previous studies have shown that the shape parameter in the Pella-Tomlinson model can be difficult to estimate accurately (51). To investigate the robustness of estimates of warming impact to different specifications of the shape parameter, we conducted sensitivity analyses in which the shape parameter was fixed at values corresponding to the widely used Schaefer and Fox surplus production models. In addition, to ensure that the magnitudes of the warming effects were not underestimated because of confinement by the normally distributed prior, we conducted a second sensitivity analysis in which the SD of the normal distribution was increased from two (the benchmark model specification) to five (tables S2 and S4). We also used the average GDDs over both 3-year (i.e., the previous 2 years and the current year) and 5-year (i.e., the previous 4 years and the current year) periods as warming indices and compare these results with those obtained using the current year’s GDDs. In addition, to test whether the estimates of warming effect came out by chance as an artefact of model structure, we compared the results from benchmark models with those from null models in which the time series of GDDs were randomly reordered. For each population, the corresponding time series of GDDs were randomly reordered for 20 times and each time series was used to fit the model and estimate the warming effect. See the Supplementary Methods and Results for details on sensitivity analyses.
