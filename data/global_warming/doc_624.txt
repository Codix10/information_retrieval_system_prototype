Marine habitat-forming species provide crucial ecosystem functions and services worldwide. Still, the individual and combined long-term effects of ocean acidification and warming on bryozoan populations, structures, and microbiomes remain unexplored. Here, we investigate the skeletal properties, microbiome shifts, and population trends of two bryozoan species living inside and outside a volcanic CO2vent, a natural analog to future ocean acidification conditions. We show that bryozoans can acclimatize to acidification by adjusting skeletal properties and maintaining stable microbiomes. However, we document a decrease in microbial genera playing essential functions under acidified conditions. Moreover, we show that ocean acidification exacerbates bryozoan cover loss and mortality caused by ocean warming. The observed shifts in the microbiome and cover suggest that, despite their morphological plasticity, bryozoan species will be heavily impacted by future ocean conditions, posing a threat to many benthic ecosystems in which they play a pivotal role.
Subject terms:Ecology, Microbial ecology
Ocean acidification and warming threaten bryozoan populations by altering skeletal properties, disrupting key microbial communities, and increasing mortality, with consequences for the benthic ecosystems they support.
Global environmental change brings multifaceted impacts to species worldwide1. In the marine realm, two major threats are ocean warming and acidification. The combined effects of these stressors, in particular, threaten marine calcifiers, which build carbonate structures and often are habitat-forming species, such as corals2. Some calcifying species may experience increased skeletal fragility and elevated energetic demands to maintain calcification under reduced pH and increased temperatures. These effects are particularly strong in those species made from more soluble mineral phases, such as aragonite or high Mg-calcite, which are prone to dissolution in acidified conditions3–5. Consequently, even in the most optimistic scenarios, temperatures and acidification are projected to increase and may compromise many calcifying species at multiple levels, resulting in shifts in marine biodiversity and functioning6, with adverse effects on other species and ecosystem services (e.g., pharmacological products). A better understanding and ability to predict the response of marine calcifiers is thus more urgent than ever.
Assessing responses of marine calcifiers to the multifaceted threats of climate change is challenging, as co-occurring environmental drivers can cause complex responses and sometimes unpredictable responses. Specifically, both drivers (seawater temperature and pH) can interact in synergistic or antagonistic ways—their combined effects being greater or lesser than the sum of their individual effects—or even show neutral responses under certain conditions, as demonstrated in species-specific studies7,8. Phenotypic plasticity, which includes physiological, developmental, mineralogical, and structural morphological adjustments, thus plays a key role in determining the ability of calcifiers to cope with such rapid environmental changes. For instance, species inhabiting dynamic coastal environments characterized by fluctuating pH and temperature regimes often display enhanced tolerance to these stressors. This suggests the potential for acclimatization—reversible, phenotypic adjustment of an individual to environmental change—and even local adaptation, which involves heritable genetic changes that enhance population fitness in specific environments9–12. However, whether such mechanisms can keep pace with the rate and magnitude of projected environmental change remains uncertain.
The host-associated microbiome—comprising bacteria, archaea, microbial eukaryotes, and viruses—is increasingly recognized as a key determinant of host physiology, health, and resilience. These microbial communities contribute to essential functions including nutrient recycling, structural maintenance, and pathogen defense. Their composition is dynamic and can shift in response to environmental changes, potentially impairing these functions and compromising host fitness or its ability to adjust to new environmental conditions—as evidenced in corals undergoing bleaching and disease under thermal stress13. However, microbial restructuring may also facilitate host acclimatization and adaptation to changing conditions14. For example, transplantation of corals to elevated temperature environments induced significant microbiome shifts that correlated with enhanced thermotolerance of the holobiont—the host and its associated microbial community—suggesting that microbiome shifts may contribute to the host’s acclimatization capacity15. Despite these insights, the interactive effects of increasing temperature and ocean acidification on microbiome structure and function remain largely unexplored. The limited studies available reveal species-specific and often contrasting responses among calcifying species, e.g., ref.16, highlighting our limited understanding of holobiont sensitivity under projected climate scenarios17.
Current studies on marine calcifiers are often limited to short-term, single-stressor laboratory assays, which provide valuable mechanistic insights (e.g., on calcification rates or metabolic responses), but they do not fully capture the complexity and variability inherent to natural systems. In contrast, long-term, in situ studies involving multiple co-occurring stressors are less common and often limited to a few taxa3,18, leaving large gaps in our understanding of other key calcifying groups. Expanding these studies to include a broader taxonomic scope and natural exposure systems is essential to assess the acclimatization and adaptive potential of the holobiont under near-future ocean scenarios.
Bryozoans, a phylum of colonial, filter-feeding aquatic invertebrates, are increasingly recognized as a promising model for studying the combined effects of global warming and acidification in marine calcifiers. Bryozoans are more diverse and widespread–occurring from poles to tropics and from shallow coasts to the deep sea19,20–than more well-studied calcifiers like corals21. Many species are habitat-forming organisms that contribute to sediment production, shoreline protection (e.g., Western Port bryozoan reefs), and even biotechnological applications through the production of bioactive products22,23. Their rich fossil record24provides an exceptional context for reconstructing evolutionary responses to past ocean changes25. Unlike many invertebrates, bryozoans can secrete skeletons with either or both mineral forms (calcite and aragonite) of calcium carbonate, with varying Mg-calcite content. This mineralogical variability, influenced by environmental factors, such as pH, temperature, and salinity, as well as biological traits like growth rate24,26,27, though causality has not been definitively established, makes them particularly suitable for studying mineral-based responses to acidification28. Warmer temperatures, for instance, have been shown to correlate with higher Mg incorporation in some bryozoans, increasing skeletal solubility under acidified conditions29. Despite their potential as model holobionts to study the effects of global change, studies on their short-term responses to both combined stressors remain scarce30–32, and research on their associated microbiomes is still in its early stages33–36. To date, no studies have examined the long-term, field-based impacts of combined global change drivers on bryozoan holobionts.
Shallow volcanic CO2vents, such as those in Ischia Island (Naples, Italy), provide a unique natural laboratory to study such long-term effects. These vents naturally reduce seawater pH to levels projected by 2100, while maintaining other physicochemical parameters within natural ranges2,37. Moreover, their location in the Mediterranean Sea, which is warming 20% faster than the global average38, enables the investigation of the combined effects of warming and acidification on natural populations. These sites serve as analogs to assess holobiont plasticity, resilience, and potential adaptive responses in situ.
In this study, we investigate two co-occurring bryozoan species with contrasting life-history traits and skeletal properties, the encrustingPentapora ottomuelleriana(Moll, 1803) and the erectMyriapora truncata(Pallas, 1766). We assess changes in skeletal structure, mineralogy, and microbiome composition in populations inhabiting inside and outside a volcanic CO2vent on Ischia Island. Additionally, we examine the five-year trajectory of bryozoan cover post-summer of the encrusting species to understand the effects of long-term acidification and its combination with warming. We expected that both bryozoan species living in the volcanic CO2vent would exhibit signs of phenotypic plasticity through distinct compensatory mechanisms (e.g., mineralogical adjustments) to counter the low pH due to their different life-history traits and skeletal mineralogy32,39,40. In addition, we expected that both species would show host-specific microbiome stability at the low pH site, given the microbiome’s ability to shift its composition to environmental changes quickly14. We also hypothesized that the encrusting species would display signs of stress-related mortality due to low pH and the exceptional thermal conditions experienced in this region during summers, consistent with recent reports from other calcifying species, including bryozoans41. This is the first interdisciplinary study documenting long-term shifts in skeletal structure and microbiome in response to future ocean acidification in bryozoan populations that naturally co-occur in volcanic CO2vents.

Our mineralogical and structural results reveal the compensatory responses to acidification may be more complex and distinct than initially expected. At the CO2vent, the encrusting speciesP. ottomuellerianaappears to reduce the solubility of its skeleton by incorporating lower Mg-calcite content and reducing its investment in calcification and volume growth to counter acidification and/or lower Ωc and Ωa saturation states at the CO2vent (Fig.1; Supplementary Data1,2and4), consistent with previous results in other bryozoan species30,42. The production of weaker skeletons under low pH conditions, leading to reduced carbonate thickness, may also increase susceptibility to damage and compromise this species’ long-term survival.
In contrast,M. truncatademonstrates an opposing strategy, forming thicker skeletons at the CO2vent, likely due to energy reallocation to calcification to overcome the decrease in pH (Fig.1; Supplementary Data4). Consequently, this process could drive a reduction in zooid volume as energy trade-offs limit overall growth (Supplementary Data3). The higher investment of energy into calcification supports previous findings, where colonies of the coral speciesAstroides calycularis, which naturally co-occur at the studied sites, also experienced a shift to less porous and denser skeletons at the CO2vent site37. In addition, the calcitic and IMC skeleton ofM. truncatais potentially less soluble than the bimineralic, HMC skeleton ofP. ottomuelleriana(Supplementary Data1), which could confer to pH changes and contributes to explaining whyM. truncatadid not exhibit significant mineralogical changes between sites. Organisms with slow calcification/growth rates, likeM. truncata, may possess traits, such as lower metabolism rates that confer increased resilience to ocean acidification–an observation consistent with patterns seen in other marine calcifiers, including the scleractinian coralsPorites astreoidesandSiderastrea siderea43, as well as the red abaloneHaliotis rufescens44. Supporting this explanation,M. truncatahas shown higher thermal resistance compared to the faster-growingP. fascialis31, yet, as each growth habit is represented by only one species in our study, such interpretations remain tentative. Future research should disentangle the influence of life-history traits, phylogenetic constraints, and environmental conditions on bryozoan vulnerability.
To the best of our knowledge, this study provides the first microbiome characterization of these two bryozoan species and among the first in the phylum33–36. These results offer insights into identifying bryozoan-stable microbial associates and their potential functions within both calcifying holobionts. Notably, acidification did not significantly affect microbial alpha diversity in either bryozoan species. This contrasts with findings in some coral species, where acidification has been shown to reduce microbial alpha diversity, likely due to a diminished ability to regulate and/or exclude incoming microbial taxa from the surrounding environment13,45. While beta diversity differed significantly between species and sites, PCA showed that microbial community is more strongly influenced by host species than by site—that is, interspecific differences exceed intraspecific variation across sites. This pattern suggests a stable and host-specific core microbiome, underscoring the tight association between microbial communities and their bryozoan hosts. In addition, despite differences in beta diversity, microbiomes from both bryozoan species exhibit similar directional shifts under future pH conditions (Fig.2; Supplementary Fig.6), indicating a shared response to acidification. Altogether, these findings thus suggest that the microbial communities of both bryozoan species are stable and may be acclimatized or adapted to projected future pH conditions.
Across both sites, Proteobacteria dominated microbiomes (Supplementary Figs.8and9), as previously found in the bryozoan speciesBugula neritina(Linnaeus, 1758)35,36. Within Proteobacteria, the dominant orders were Cellvibrionales, Rhizobiales, and Rhodobacterales, which are generally abundant in corals13. Members of Bacteroidota, particularly the order Flavobacteriales, which are suggested to be opportunistic pathogens involved in coral diseases46, were also abundant. Similarly, Planctomycetota was prevalent at both sites, with the microbiome dominated by the order Pirellulales (Supplementary Figs.8and9), which consists of anaerobic ammonia-oxidizing bacteria and is also prominent in some coral species47. Members of this order are also likely involved in the removal of metabolic waste within the host48.
Nine abundant bacterial genera were shared across both species and sites, which indicates they are intimately related to the host and are probably part of their core microbiome (Fig.3). Consistent with this, two of these dominant bacterial genera (BD1-7 clade andAquimarina) and the genusMycoplasma, which is only found inM. truncata, are suggested to be a key part of the microbiome of various temperate Gorgoniidae and are likely essential to the holobionts, e.g. ref.49. Interestingly, two dominant bacterial genera, “CandidatusEndobugula” andEndozoicomonas, of which the former was only observed inP. ottomuelleriana, have been previously retrieved from other bryozoan species35,36,50(Fig.3). Symbionts of “CandidatusEndobugula” have been isolated from several species ofBugulaand are known to be involved in the production of bryostatins, polyketides metabolites that provide chemical defense to the host51. In the case ofEndozoicomonas, some members are commonly found in various marine organisms. These are suggested to have important roles in host health by providing nutrients through sulfur, nitrogen, and/or methane cycling and synthesis of amino acids, and by bacterial community regulation by producing secondary metabolites45,49,52.
This study is also the first to document patterns in microbiome shifts in response to future ocean acidification in bryozoans. While the bryozoan microbiomes were generally stable, a trend towards bacteria involved in organic matter degradation was observed in both species under low pH conditions (Fig.4). In particular, members of the genusZobelliaandUlvibacterhave specialized in the degradation of polysaccharides, a major component of marine organic matter53,54, andLutibacter profundiin degrading organic matter55. Consistent with these results, declines in pH increase the polysaccharide and other organic matter degradation56.
InP. ottomuelleriana, sulfate-reducing bacteria, strict anaerobes, were enriched at the CO2vent (Fig.4), which have also been associated with higher rates of organic matter degradation, suggesting their role in carbon degradation57.P. ottomuellerianaencrusts the substrate, forming an anaerobic environment that could thus favor the proliferation of anaerobic bacteria, as suggested for corals58. Our findings thus suggest that acidification modifies the microbiome environment of this bryozoan species, inducing the community’s shift towards anaerobes under these low pH conditions. This shift from aerobic to anaerobic and facultative anaerobic bacteria under low pH conditions was previously found in other marine invertebrates, such as the bivalveCrassostrea gigas59.
The increase in the relative abundance of sulfate-reducing bacteria may also be a mechanism to counteract the low pH, as these bacteria may increase local alkalinity and induce CaCO3precipitation60. This could explain howM. truncataincreases calcification under low pH conditions. In agreement, the extrapallial fluid of the eastern oysterCrassostrea virginica, which is responsible for shell formation, is enriched in sulfate-reducing and denitrifier bacteria61.
Other genera involved in sulfur cycling showed opposite patterns, such asEndozoicomonas, which was enriched inP. ottomuellerianaand depleted inM. truncataat the CO2vent. Similarly, species-specific changes inEndozoicomonasin response to low pH conditions were previously found in coral hosts, with a significant increase or loss, e.g. ref.16. Shore et al.16suggested that the enrichment inEndozoicomonasmay benefit the host under ocean acidification conditions. By contrast, significant losses ofEndozoicomonasmay be lowering the host’s ability to control the growth of this bacteria16.
Bacterial genera, which are assumed to have essential functions to the host, were significantly depleted in both bryozoan species at the CO2vent site, which suggests early warnings of potential deterioration of bryozoan health under near-future ocean conditions. Some of the functions are biosynthesis of biologically active compounds with anti-bacterial, bacteriolytic, agarolytic, and algicidal activities (PseudoalteromonasandCandidatus Entotheonella), probiotic thermal protection (CobetiaandHalomonas)62, degradation of lipids and/or hydrocarbons (Marinobacter)63, and nitrification (SZB85), which might play key roles through facilitating the nitrogen cycle within cave ecosystems64(Fig.4). These genera have been previously isolated from different coral, sponge, and tunicate species, including bryozoans65–67. The genussubgroup 10belonging to the phylum Acidobacteriota was also depleted inM. truncataat the CO2vent, and some members may also be beneficial to their host (e.g., production of diverse B-vitamins) as suggested in sponges68.
We also observed an increase in bacteria previously associated with dysbiosis. In particular,Acinetobacter, which has commonly been isolated from marine invertebrates such as corals, was enriched inM. truncata, and some members are pathogens (Acinetobacter baumannii)69(Fig.4). InP. ottomuelleriana,HalioglobusandOceanibulbusalso increased at the CO2vent.Halioglobushas been found in bleached corals and may be associated with bacterial dysbiosis70andOceanibulbusbelongs to the disease coral-associated Rhodobacterales, and some members of this order also increased with lower pH71.
These microbial shifts parallel broader ecological patterns we observed. Our model indicates a decline in total bryozoan cover over time, primarily driven by rising seawater temperatures, with the effects exacerbated at the CO₂ vent site (Fig.5a, b; Supplementary Data6; Supplementary Fig.10). Necrosis also increased at a faster pace in the CO2vent site than in the Ambient pH site, likely due to combined acidification and thermal stress (Fig.5c, d; Supplementary Data7). These observations are consistent with previous experimental and field studies showing that elevated temperatures and reduced pH can negatively impact bryozoan growth rates32,72,73, ultimately contributing to population declines.
Such local patterns are particularly concerning given the broader environmental context. The Mediterranean Sea is recognized as a climate change hotspot, with observed warming expected to continue at a greater rate than the global average38. The 2015–2019 period was the warmest on record since the beginning of satellite observations in 1982, with average temperatures 1.2 ± 0.23 °C higher than during the 1982–1986 baseline period41. This warming trend notably overlaps with the timeframe of our study (2016–2020), underscoring the relevance of our findings in the context of ongoing climate change. During this period, several mass mortality events were reported across the region, with bryozoans ranking as the second most severely impacted taxonomic group41. In summer months, sea temperatures frequently approached or exceeded the upper thermal tolerance limits reported for common Mediterranean bryozoan species. In particular, a congeneric species ofP. ottomuelleriana, the erect bryozoanP. fascialis, has exhibited increased mortality at temperatures above 25 °C–26 °C31,41. Similarly,M. truncataexhibits reduced survivability under elevated temperature conditions, with significant mortality occurring when temperatures exceeded 28 °C31,41.
There is a growing body of evidence highlighting the detrimental effects of warming —particularly extreme temperature events—on natural bryozoan populations across various species in the Mediterranean Sea31,41,74. On a global scale, similar patterns have been observed. For instance, in southern California, bryozoan mortality increased when peak temperature anomalies reached an average of +3.1 °C during the 2014–2015 period, highlighting the vulnerability of bryozoan species to such thermal stress75. Although such studies remain limited, they reveal consistent global trends in bryozoan mortality under warming scenarios, further reinforcing our finding that rising seawater temperatures alone can drive substantial declines in bryozoan cover.
Moreover, our findings suggest the projected decline in ocean pH by the end of the century is expected to exacerbate these warming-driven impacts. These results underscore the need to evaluate stressor interactions, as the biological responses observed cannot be reliably predicted by assessing the effects of ocean warming or acidification in isolation. Understanding the combined influence of these major stressors is therefore critical for forecasting future changes in bryozoan populations and the habitats they support.
Our study is the first to assess responses to both stressors in bryozoans over a relatively extended period (five years), but there are aspects that need to be considered when interpreting our results. Although this duration exceeds that of typical experimental studies, it still represents a relatively short time frame compared to the multi-decadal scale relevant to anthropogenic climate change. Additionally, while CO2vents are not perfect proxies for the future ocean, these natural systems can provide insights into how species may survive under future acidification scenarios (see ref.37). Ideally, multiple comparisons between ambient pH sites and CO2vents across geographic locations should also be performed to confirm the observed patterns. However, our findings provide the first insights into calcifying holobiont responses to environmental change in a poorly studied phylum so far.
In summary, our results highlight novel, differing, and unpredictable responses to how bryozoans can cope with low pH. While both bryozoan species show indicators of acclimatization, such as phenotypic plasticity (e.g., adjusting skeletal properties) and stable microbial communities under acidification conditions, the bryozoan status is still dependent on the effects of warming and other local environmental impacts. Consistent with this, we observe the most adverse impacts arise from the combined effects of warming and acidification in the encrusting speciesP. ottomuelleriana. These findings thus suggest thatP. ottomuellerianamay be particularly at risk of future impacts of warming and acidification in the Mediterranean Sea. TheM. truncataappears to be more resilient to ocean acidification, although the observed microbiome shifts may indicate a decrease in its resilience to near-future ocean conditions (Fig.6). Both bryozoan species could thus potentially act as sentinels, providing early warnings of health deterioration in bryozoan populations. Our results thus emphasize that more interdisciplinary research combining both environmental stressors on a wider range of calcifying species is needed to better understand the acclimatization and adaptive capacity of the holobiont to a changing environment.

The study sites included an underwater volcanic CO2vent and one reference site with ambient pH and no vent activity. The CO2vent system is located at 5 m depth inside a semi-submerged cave of volcanic origin named Grotta del Mago (40°42′41.87″N, 13°57′51.06″E, hereafter CO2vent). The cave has a main outer chamber (10 m wide x 30 m long), where the CO2vent activity has increased over the last 50 years, with a more intense activity from 201437,76. The reference site named Punta Vico is also a semi-submerged cave at the same depth, which has a main chamber with the same dimensions (40°45′32.28″N,13°52′55.38″E, hereafter Ambient pH site). The present study was performed in the main chamber of both caves. The vent gas at the vent site was predominantly CO2, with undetectable levels of hydrogen sulfide (<0.0002%)37. Water carbonate chemistry and in situ monitoring of seawater pHT(pH on the total scale) at the CO2vent site showed mean pHT= 7.88 and 7.65 with 14 and 56% of the pHTmeasurements below 7.8 in June and September at 2 m depth, respectively. These values below 7.8 correspond to the projected average global sea surface pH value for the year 2100 with the high-CO2emission scenario SSP5 RCP8.577. Calcite (Ωc) and aragonite (Ωa) saturation states were 2.2 and 1.44 in June and 1.68 and 1.58 in September. The Ambient pH site showed pHT= 8.05, Ωc = 5.06, and Ωa = 3.86 (for more information see37).
We deployed temperature data loggers (HOBO TidbiT v2, Onset) in both sites over a 6-year period (2016–2021) at 2 m depth that registered temperature every hour, following ambient seasonal fluctuations (Supplementary Fig.1). Additionally, coastal seawater temperature data from 5 m depth in Ischia between 2012 to 2023 were obtained from the T-MEDNet temperature database (www.t-mednet.org). Daily averages were obtained from hourly records.
We collected three fragments from three individual colonies per species and site (n= 12) at 2 m depth in May 2021 and preserved them in solutions of 95% ethanol. We performed mineralogical analyses cutting the growing edge (2 × 2 mm2) following previously described methodology78. The excised pieces are estimated to have been formed within a period of ∼1–6 months prior to the date of collection, as the estimated vertical growth ofM. truncataandP. ottomuellerianais 0.41 cm yr−1and 3.5 cm yr−1(estimated from another species belonging to the same genus,P. fascialis), respectively79,80.
We carried out X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) measurements with a Bruker D8-A25 diffractometer, equipped with a Cu Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation source generated at a voltage of 30 kV and a current of 40 mA and a LynxEye position-sensitive detector at the Geosciencies Barcelona (GEO3BCN-CSIC). The scans were acquired between 25.40° and 32.70° in 2θ at 0.015° steps and time per step of 384 s. Phase identification was performed with the DIFFRAC.EVA software together with the Powder Diffraction File PDF-2 and the Crystallography Open Database. The mol% MgCO3in calcite was calculated by measuring the position of the d104 peak, assuming a linear interpolation between CaCO3and MgCO3and recalibrated for the specific machine used (Supplementary Data1).
The skeletons were categorized as IMC (4–8 mol% MgCO3), and HMC (>8 mol% molCO3) (Rucker and Carver 1969). The differences in the Mg content between sites were analyzed using a t-test.
We collected three fragments from three individual colonies per species and site (n= 12) at 2 m depth in May 2021, rinsed with distilled water and then air dried. We then scanned the growing edges using micro X-ray computed tomography (Micro-CT) at the Service of Non-Destructive Techniques of the Museo Nacional de Ciencias Naturales (MNCN-CSIC). The Micro-CT has been demonstrated to be a valuable tool for studying internal features of recent and fossil bryozoans81–83. All scans were executed using an XTH 160 Nikon Metrology, with a molybdenum target. During the analysis, the X-ray source was operated at a voltage of 160 kV with current ranging from 70–85 μA. A total of 3015 projections were taken at an exposure of 354 ms. The resulting dataset had a voxel size of 11 μm3. The CT Pro 3D software was used for the reconstruction of the raw projection.
The image analysis was achieved using Avizo® software of the CORELAB Laboratory from Universitat de Barcelona. The post-processing images workflow was developed to perform a morphometric analysis of samples (i.e., carbonate structure thickness and zooid volumes, length, and surface) (Supplementary Movies1and2).
The initial step involved applying a non-local means algorithm to denoise the scalar volume data, followed by implementing a delineation filter to enhance the edges of objects, thereby improving object differentiation. The second step was to segment the image data to divide the bryozoan images into various subregions (i.e., carbonate skeleton and zooids volumes), which was obtained through the application of multiple morphological transformations. Finally, the subregion analysis allowed the extraction of various measurements from the images, such as volume (Supplementary Data2and3). ForP. ottomuelleriana, the thickness analysis was conducted on a total section of the colony (Supplementary Data4). In contrast, forM. truncata, the analysis was focused on the distal part of the branches to avoid potential biases (Supplementary Data4).
We collected four fragments from four individual colonies per species and site (n= 16) at 2 m depth in May 2021, placed in plastic bags filled with seawater from the collection point and transported in ice to the laboratory. Samples were then rinsed in milliQ water, preserved in 75% ethanol and frozen at –20 °C until DNA extraction. A total of four pieces (~1 mm2) were cut from the growing edge of each colony (n= 64) and transferred into a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube (Supplementary Data5). DNA was extracted from each sample using the DNeasy Blood & Tissue Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). All samples were stored at −20 °C until further processing.
At each site, we collected 1 L of seawater (n= 2) and transported in ice to the laboratory. We then filtered seawater samples using 0.2 μm pore-size sterile membrane filters. We placed membrane filters in plastic bags and stored at −20 °C until DNA extraction. We finally extracted DNA from each sample using a Powersoil DNA extraction kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). All samples were stored at −20 °C until further processing.
DNA concentrations from samples were measured with a Qubit fluorometer. Each sample yielded a concentration above 1 ng/μ and was subsequently sent to the Integrated Microbiome Resource facility at the Center for Comparative Genomics and Evolutionary Bioinformatics at Dalhousie University, Canada for 16S rRNA gene amplification and sequencing. The V4 region of the prokaryotic 16S rRNA gene was PCR-amplified using the newly modified 515 F/806RB primers (515 f modified: GTGYCAGCMGCCGCGGTAA; 806r modified: GGACTACNVGGGTWTCTAAT)84,85with Illumina overhang adapter sequences. Sequencing was performed on Illuminia MiSeq platform.
Raw sequencing data have been deposited on NCBI (BioProject: PRJNA1047010). Primers and adapters used for sequencing were removed from paired-end reads using Cutadapt86. Reads were then processed in R using the DADA2 package87. First, forward reads were truncated at 250 bp and reverse reads at 200 bp according to their quality profiles and filtered using the parameters maxN = 0 and maxEE = c(2,2). Paired-end reads were then merged and chimaeras removed, and reads grouped into ASVs. Taxonomy was assigned to ASVs using the SILVA v138 database as reference88. The resulting ASV table and taxonomic assignment were combined with metadata using the phyloseq package89. ASVs classified as chloroplasts, mitochondria, or eukaryotes were removed from the dataset. Samples with 1000 or fewer reads were discarded. After this filtering, 3737 ASVs and 59 samples were used for statistical analysis using the microbiomeMarker and vegan packages90,91. The percent relative abundance of each microbial phylum for each sample was calculated, and ASVs with a total relative abundance <0.001% were removed. All additional analyses were conducted at the genus level. Plots were constructed using ggplot2 and tidyverse packages92,93. Alpha diversity was calculated using Shannon and Chao1 diversity indices. Beta diversity was estimated using the Aitchison distance, and patterns in microbial community composition were visualized by Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Heatmaps were generated using the Ampvis294. To test for differences in beta-diversity between groups, an analysis of similarities (ANOSIM) within the vegan package was used, using 999 permutations. To account for potential differences in group dispersion, a homogeneity of multivariate dispersions test (betadisper) was performed to ensure that differences in beta-diversity were not confounded by unequal dispersion. Analysis of Compositions of Microbiomes with Bias Correction (ANCOMBC) were used to find significantly enriched and differentially abundant taxa between sites.
To study the effects of acidification on cover and necrosis ofP. ottomuelleriana, we performed transects of permanent photographic quadrats (25  × 25 cm) at 2 m depth in both study sites over time. A total of 111 photographs (CO2vent = 55; Ambient pH site = 56) were taken after summer from 2016 to 2020 (except spring of 2020 due to the COVID-19 lockdown). We then analyzed cover (total area of living tissue) and tissue necrosis (dead tissue or denuded skeleton) with the ImageJ image software (National Institutes of Health,https://imagej.net/ImageJ).
To estimate the differences in the temporal trends of the total area and the necrosis area ofP. ottomuellerianabetween CO2vent and Ambient pH site, we used multilevel generalized Bayesian models. We fitted two separate models, one with total area and another one with the necrosis as response variables. The fixed effects of both models were acidification (two levels, with and without acidification), mean temperature (°C; derived from coastal seawater temperature data recorded at 5 m depth around Ischia Island between 2012 and 2023), year and their interaction. Measuring the magnitude of temperature change over time was beyond the scope of the study. Instead, we measured the association (potential effect) of temperature on biomass and necrosis through the inclusion of temperature as a covariate in the models. We considered the ID of each quadrat as the random intercept. To account for the temporal non-independence of the total area and necrosis area data, we included an autoregressive-1 temporal process, which assumes neighboring observations within the time series to be more similar than further ones.

Statistical analyses were adapted to each study component and performed in R (v4.0.0). Mineralogical differences (mol% MgCO₃) between sites were assessed using t-tests. Micro-CT data were processed in Avizo® to extract morphometric features of carbonate skeletons and zooids. Microbial community analyses followed DADA2-based sequence processing87; alpha diversity was evaluated using Shannon and Chao1 indices, and beta diversity using Aitchison distances visualized by PCA. Group differences in beta diversity were tested with ANOSIM (vegan package)91, and dispersion effects were assessed with the betadisper function. Differentially abundant taxa were identified via ANCOM-BC90. Temporal trends in bryozoan cover and necrosis were analyzed using multilevel generalized Bayesian models with autoregressive terms, implemented in the brms package (v2.1.0)95. Model convergence and fit were confirmed through trace plots, Rhat values, and posterior predictive checks. Plots were generated using ggplot2 and tidyverse92,93.
