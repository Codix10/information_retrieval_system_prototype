Global change factors are known to strongly affect soil microbial community function and composition. However, as of yet, the effects of warming and increased anthropogenic nitrogen deposition on soil microbial network complexity and stability are still unclear. Here, we examined the effects of experimental warming (3°C above ambient soil temperature) and nitrogen addition (5 g N m−2year−1) on the complexity and stability of the soil microbial network in a subtropical primary forest. Compared to the control, warming increased |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion by 7% and decreased network vulnerability by 5%; nitrogen addition decreased |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion by 10% and increased network vulnerability by 11%. Warming and decreased soil moisture acted as strong filtering factors that led to higher bacterial network stability. Nitrogen addition reduced bacterial network stability by inhibiting soil respiration and increasing resource availability. Neither warming nor nitrogen addition changed fungal network complexity and stability. These findings suggest that the fungal community is more tolerant than the bacterial community to climate warming and nitrogen addition. The link between bacterial network stability and microbial community functional potential was significantly impacted by nitrogen addition and warming, while the response of soil microbial network stability to climate warming and nitrogen deposition may be independent of its complexity. Our findings demonstrate that changes in microbial network structure are crucial to ecosystem management and to predict the ecological consequences of global change in the future.
Soil microbes play a very important role in maintaining the function and health of forest ecosystems. Unfortunately, global change factors are profoundly affecting soil microbial structure and function. In this study, we found that climate warming promoted bacterial network stability and nitrogen deposition decreased bacterial network stability. Changes in bacterial network stability had strong effects on bacterial community functional potentials linked to metabolism, nitrogen cycling, and carbon cycling, which would change the biogeochemical cycle in primary forests.
KEYWORDS:climate warming, nitrogen deposition, microbial network stability, microbial network complexity, old-growth subtropical forest
Industrialization has seriously degraded the planet and continues to threaten its future (1). By the end of the 21st century, global surface temperature is expected to increase by 1.5–4.5°C and has risen by 0.78°C over the past 100 years (2). Since the 1860s, atmospheric nitrogen deposition has more than doubled due to fossil fuel combustion and fertilizer applications (3). The soil environment would undoubtedly be affected by these modifications.
Global soils contain more organic carbon than both vegetation and atmospheric CO2combined (4,5). Unquestionably, soil microorganisms serve a significant role in global change feedback, as they are primarily responsible for the soil organic carbon cycle (6). Any changes to the soil microbiome may correspondingly influence the emission of greenhouse gases like nitrous oxide, methane, and carbon dioxide (7,8). Given the significance of soil microbes for the functioning of ecosystems (9), understanding how global change factors affect soil microorganisms and their underlying mechanisms is a key question in microbiology and global change research (10).
Old-growth forests cover more than 30% of the global forest area (11). Old-growth forests have commensurately resilience to disturbance, greater carbon sequestration, and more stable hydrological services than other forests (12). It is estimated that the carbon storage of subtropical old-growth forests accounts for an important part of the carbon storage of the total terrestrial ecosystem (13,14). In many boreal and temperate old-growth forests, anthropogenic nitrogen deposition may increase carbon storage potential, as these temperate ecosystems are often limited by nitrogen (15,16). However, (sub)tropical forests are often limited by phosphorus rather than nitrogen, and soil microbial community responses to global change factors are expected to differ from those in other temperate and boreal forest ecosystems (17). Soils in subtropical old-growth forests accumulate carbon at a high rate, where soil microbes are important regulators in forest carbon storage potential (18,19). Clearly, it is essential to explore how these microbes may be perturbed by global change. To fully explore this, we need to consider the different life strategies of bacterial and fungal communities, which are critical to the soil microbiome. For example, the fungal community exhibits slow turnover and often “prefers” low in nutrients, obstinates organic matter, which has a high carbon-nitrogen ratio and a long substrate cycling period (20). Studies have found that bacteria are more vulnerable to future climate change than fungi (21). In one nitrogen addition experiment, a fungal co-occurrence network remained more stable than a correspondingly bacterial network under nitrogen addition (22). However, few experiments have yet illustrated the related mechanisms of warming, nitrogen addition, and their interactions on soil microbial community in a subtropical old-growth forest.
Co-occurrence networks have recently emerged as a popular tool for examining microbial community interactions and predicting the complexity and stability of these interactions (2,23). It is well understood that most soil microbes live in cohabitation, rather than in separate populations, and they have complex interactions with each other (23). Some research has explored how these networks may be perturbed by global change. For example, climate warming gradually increased the complexity and stability of bacterial network in a grassland ecosystem (2). Warming also drove changes in the structure of fungal network in tundra soil (24). With increasing nitrogen addition rates, which peaked at 20 g N m−2year−1, gradually increased bacterial network complexity, and decreased network stability (22). Although these studies have revealed that the soil microbial network may respond to warming or nitrogen addition, their interactions on microbial networks have not been fully explored.
Ecological networks are essential to describe species interactions and ecosystem dynamics (2). Whether and how the ecological network complexity affects ecosystem functions are fundamental and highly debated question (25). Several recent studies have described how ecological network and ecosystem function change with disturbances or gradients in environmental conditions (2,26,27). Although it has been documented that changes in microbial network structure affect ecosystem function in grassland ecosystems under climate warming (2), little is known about whether altered microbial network structure will change microbial community functional potentials under different global change factors and their interactions.
In this study, we examined the complexity and stability of bacterial and fungal networks under three conditions:in situwarming (+3°C), nitrogen addition (5 g N m−2year−1), and warming plus nitrogen addition experiment. We hypothesized that warming would reduce soil moisture and result in higher complexity and stability for microbial networks due to increasing soil temperature (Hypothesis 1). We also predicted that nitrogen addition will decrease microbial network complexity and stability by alleviating nutrient stress, and the negative influence of nitrogen addition may offset the positive influence of warming on the complexity and stability of microbial networks (Hypothesis 2). Finally, we speculated that changes in the complexity and stability of microbial networks by experimental treatments will drive the alteration in microbial community functional potentials (Hypothesis 3).
Warming primarily impacts soil microclimate by increasing soil temperature but reducing soil moisture (Fig. S1a and b). Nitrogen addition significantly reduced soil respiration by 0.581 µmol m−2s−1(β= −0.58,P< 0.01) (Fig. S1c), but increased soil dissolved organic carbon (β= 16.07,P< 0.01) (Fig. S1e). Furthermore, warming decreased soil dissolved inorganic carbon (β= −0.35,P= 0.01) (Fig. S1f). Both warming and nitrogen addition significantly increased soil NO3−–N (β= 0.25,P= 0.04;β= 0.28,P= 0.01, respectively) (Fig. S1h). Warming had strong negative effects on total C-degrading enzyme activities (β= 652.97,P< 0.01), but positive effects on soilcbbLgene abundance (β= 5.50 × 106,P= 0.01) (Fig. S2a and d). Additionally, the negative warming effect on total C-degrading enzyme activities was also supported by single C-degrading enzyme activity decreases as determined by α-glucosidase enzyme activity (β= −75.43,P= 0.03), β-galactosidase enzyme activity (β= −83.35,P= 0.02), chitinase enzyme activity (β= −387.81,P< 0.01) (Fig. S3a, c, and e). However, nitrogen addition only had significant negative effects on enzyme C:P ratio (β= −0.03,P= 0.04) (Fig. S3i).
Experimental treatments did not have a significant effect on bacterial and fungal networks complexity (Fig. 1; Fig. S4 and S5). Permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) tests revealed that network complexity varied predominantly with the season (P< 0.01) (Table 1). Warming increased bacterial |negative cohesion| (β= 0.01,P= 0.04) and |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion (β= 0.01,P= 0.03), but reduced network vulnerability (β= −2.42 × 10−5,P= 0.02) (Fig. 2a, c, and d). On the contrary, nitrogen addition reduced bacterial |negative cohesion| (β= −0.03,P< 0.01) and |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion (β= −0.06,P< 0.01), but increased network vulnerability (β= 7.58 × 10−5,P< 0.01) (Fig. 2a, c, and d). Warming, nitrogen addition, and season also affected bacterial |negative cohesion|, network vulnerability, and |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion (P< 0.05) (Table 2). Warming significantly reduced fungal |negative cohesion| (β= −0.01,P= 0.02), and it was not influenced by nitrogen addition (Fig. 2e). While only warming influenced fungal |negative cohesion|, season significantly influenced fungal |negative cohesion|, |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion, and network vulnerability (P< 0.05) (Table 2). Compared to the control, nitrogen addition and warming plus nitrogen addition significantly decreased bacterial |negative cohesion| and |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion, and increased bacterial network vulnerability (P< 0.05) (Fig. S6a, c, and d). No significant difference was found among treatments for fungal network stability indexes (Fig. S7). In addition, soil microbial network complexity and stability exhibited strong seasonal dynamics (Fig. S8 to S11). Bacterial network stability showed a pattern with summer > autumn > spring > winter (Fig. S10).

Nitrogen addition and warming significantly affected most soil microbial community functional potentials linked to metabolism, nitrogen cycling, and carbon cycling (P< 0.05) (Fig. S12 to S14). However, experimental treatments had significant effects on only a small number of fungal functional groups (P< 0.05) (Fig. S15). Overall, bacterial network stability exhibited significant correlations with most soil microbial community functional potentials linked to metabolism, nitrogen cycling, and carbon cycling (P< 0.05) (Fig. 5a through c), while bacterial network complexity, fungal network complexity, and fungal network stability showed significant correlations with very few or no soil microbial community functional potentials (P< 0.05) (Fig. 5).

Our results showed that simulated warming did not alter bacterial network complexity (Fig. 1a through f;Table 1). This result is inconsistent with our Hypothesis 1 and previous studies in grassland and coastal nontidal wetlands (2,28). Soil bacterial network structure has been well demonstrated to be impacted by soil physicochemical properties (29) and global change (30). Exploring the resistance of microbial communities to climate warming and nitrogen addition aids in predicting the ecosystem’s functional stability under future global change scenarios (2,22). Studies have found that changes in soil and plant phenotypic characteristics caused by warming and nitrogen would affect microbial community structure and diversity (31–33). Plant species are considered to be key controllers of the responses of soil microbial community to warming, as root exudates and litter traits connected to various species mediate warming in a number of ways (34,35). This may explain why our soils appear resistant to warming, as despite it slightly impacting soil physicochemical properties, there were ultimately no changes in bacterial community complexity in our experiment. Warming did, however, enhance bacterial network stability (Fig. 2a through d;Table 2). This result supports our Hypothesis 1. Increased soil temperature and decreased soil moisture due to warming may have increased competition among bacterial species, leading to increased network stability (Fig. 4a). In addition, our study demonstrated that warming had no effect on either fungal network complexity or stability (Fig. 1g through l,2e through h, and4b). This result indicates that the fungal community was better able to withstand the effects of climate warming than the bacterial community, consistent with results from a simulated drought experiment (26).
We discovered that bacterial network complexity was not affected by nitrogen addition (Fig. 1a through f). These results are inconsistent with our expectation that nitrogen addition would decrease bacterial network complexity (Hypothesis 2). Nitrogen addition has a very important impact on species interactions (36), and significantly alters network complexity within soil microbial communities (37,38). Our results, however, underscore that the impact of nitrogen addition on bacterial community interactions is likely determined by a multitude of local environmental factors (22,39). We did observe that nitrogen addition reduced bacterial network stability in our study (Fig. 2a through d). It is likely that appropriate nitrogen addition can alleviate ecosystem nutrient constraints (22,40). The stress gradient hypothesis suggests that lower stress may enhance the amount of cooperative interactions and decrease competitive interactions (41). Increased resource availability and reduced nutrient stress may allow more bacterial species to grow independently (42), decreasing interspecies competition (43). It is reported that bacterial communities can enhance resource use efficiency by promoting cooperation (39), supported by the increased soil dissolved organic carbon and NO3−–N observed in our results (Fig. S1e and h). Our results indicated that nitrogen addition decreased bacterial network stability mainly because nitrogen addition reduced nutrient limitation stress, increasing resource availability and altering soil respiration (Fig. 4a). Contrary to our expectation that nitrogen addition would decrease the complexity and stability in bacterial and fungal network (Hypothesis 2), nitrogen addition did not alter fungal network complexity and stability (Fig. 1g through l,2e through h, and4b). These results imply that there is a greater resistance of the fungal community to future global change as opposed to the bacterial community (21,26). This may be attributed to the special physiological features of fungi (e.g., aerial hyphae, spore reproduction, and active dispersal through airborne spores) (44). Moreover, fungi have longer turnover times and may respond more slowly to environmental changes than bacteria (45).
The impact of multiple global change drivers on microbial community is complicated because these global change drivers are complex in time and space and frequently arise at the same time (1). Additive or antagonistic effects may be produced through two or more global change drivers and their interactions (46,47). In this study, interactions of warming and nitrogen addition had no significant effect on microbial network complexity and stability (Fig. 1 and 2). These findings implied that the positive impact of warming was not enough to offset the negative impact of nitrogen addition. As in our results, the magnitudes (absolute value ofβ) of the impact of nitrogen addition on bacterial network stability were greater than that of warming (Fig. 2a through d).
Changes in bacterial network stability alter microbial community functional potentials (Fig. 5). These findings documented that soil microbial interactions are crucial to sustain ecosystem functions (48). In a microbial community, microorganisms collaborate and interact with each other to create a complex ecological network that maximizes their ecological functions (49). It is reported that functional cooperation behavior widely exists in microbial communities (50). Analysis of co-occurrence networks identified microbes with cooperative or co-dependent associations, which usually indicates that they have common functions (51). Changes in network stability may alter cooperation and competition among species within microbial communities, leading to changes in microbial community functional potentials.
The present study described how microbial network complexity and stability in the old-growth subtropical forest responded to warming and nitrogen addition. Warming and nitrogen deposition showed opposite effects on bacterial network stability. Neither warming nor nitrogen addition changed fungal network complexity and stability. Elevated soil temperature and reduced soil moisture following soil warming led to higher bacterial network stability suggesting that the associated ecosystem functions may have shifted in a warmer world. Furthermore, we observed that nitrogen addition inhibited soil respiration and increased resource availability leading to decreased bacterial network stability. Our results suggest that bacterial communities respond more sensitively to nitrogen addition than to warming, indicating that conservation should prioritize reducing atmospheric nitrogen deposition. Interestingly, warming and nitrogen addition significantly affected most microbial community functional potentials, and bacterial network stability displayed typically greater correlations with various microbial community functional potentials. Collectively, our findings suggest that the warming-induced and nitrogen-induced bacterial network stability could have major impacts on microbial community functional potentials, which would change the biogeochemical cycle in primary forests.
This study was conducted at the Ailaoshan Station for Subtropical Forest Ecosystem Studies of the Chinese Ecological Research Network, Yunnan Province, Southwest China (24°32′N, 101°01′E; 2,480 m above sea level). This area experiences a typical subtropical monsoon climate. From 2015 to 2020, the mean annual precipitation was 1,931.9 mm, and the mean annual temperature was 11.5°C. In the region, untouched natural monsoon evergreen broadleaf forests are over 300 years old and have been protected from destruction (52). At the study site, the dominant plants includeLithocarpus xylocarpus,Castanopsis wattii,Schima noronhae,Hartia sinensis,Vaccinium duclouxii,Lithocarpus chintungensis,Sinarundinaria nitida,Manglietia insignis, andRhododendron leptothrium(53). The soils are loamy Alfisols.
We considered three replications per treatment in our experiment, which consisted of twelve 2 m by 2 m plots randomly assigned: control, warming, nitrogen addition, and warming plus nitrogen addition. Carbonizing infrared radiator was used to heat the warming and warming plus nitrogen addition plots, with three 200-W carbon infrared radiant heating lamps positioned in a triangular shape of about 1.0 m above each plot. We used continuous heating mode from January 2018 to December 2020, providing continuous power. NH4NO3(5 g N m−2year−1) for nitrogen addition and warming plus nitrogen addition plots was applied monthly starting in January 2018. A hand-held sprayer was used to spray NH4NO3dissolved in 300 mL of deionized water onto the forest floor during each application. Each month, 300 mL of deionized water without NH4NO3was provided to the control and warming plots.
We randomly collected soil samples from the surface layer (at a depth of 0–10 cm) in April 2019, July 2019, October 2019, and January 2020, which corresponded to the spring, summer, autumn, and winter. A composite sample was constructed for each experimental plot by mixing three subsamples. Following collection in the field, fresh samples were chilled on ice, and then transported in a cooler to the laboratory, where roots and rocks were removed by sieving (2 mm pore size). Fresh soils were divided into two subsamples. One subsample was frozen at −80°C for DNA extraction within 1 month of collection. The other subsample was used to determine soil variables. Details of soil variables analysis, DNA extraction, amplification, and sequencing can be found in our supplementary information.
The high-quality clean sequence reads were obtained from the process of cutting off low-quality sequences using Cutadapt (54). UCHIME was applied to detect and eliminate chimeric sequences (55). The cleaned sequences were clustered according to their 97% similarity through the UPARSE to determine operational taxonomic units (OTUs) (56). Using the Ribosomal Database Project Classifier tool (57), taxonomy annotations of OTUs were determined using a confidence threshold of 0.8 in comparison to Silva 132 (58) for bacteria and UNITE 7.2 (59) for fungi. Subsequently, the bacterial functions and metabolic pathways were inferred by PICRUSt (60), and the bacterial chemical cycling process functions were identified by FAPROTAX (61). FUNGuild was implemented to predict the functional group of fungi (62).
Co-occurrence networks from each season were constructed according to Spearman’s correlation matrices. To reduce the number of rare OTUs and offer more accurate and reliable results for subsequent network analysis, OTUs with a relative abundance of <0.01% were removed (63,64). Networks were constructed using strong correlations with Spearman’s correlation coefficients (r) having an absolute value >0.6 and Benjamini and Hochberg false discovery rate adjustedPvalues <0.01 (64,65). Following the description of Ma et al. (30), the topological properties of the network for each sample were performed in the igraph package’s subgraph function. To evaluate the soil microbial network’s complexity, topological attributes of the networks were computed, which included edge number, node number, average path length, average degree, clustering coefficient, and graph density (66).
To assess the microbial network’s stability, the vulnerability and cohesion of each sample were determined. Network vulnerability was calculated following Yuan et al. (2). In general, a lower network vulnerability indicates a more stable community. Cohesion (a measure of microbial connectivity) was also calculated, following the protocol laid out in Herren and McMahon (67). The relative proportion of |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion can also be used to express microbial network’s stability (68), where a high value of |negative cohesion|:positive cohesion indicates a more stable community.
Linear mixed effects models were applied to examine the influence of experimental treatments on soil variables, microbial network stability and complexity indexes, and microbial community functional potentials (69).Pvalues from the linear mixed effects models were calculated using Wald type IIχ² tests. Besides, we conducted PERMANOVA in order to investigate the main effects of warming, nitrogen addition, and measurement date (season) on soil microbial networks complexity and stability indexes.
To better understand the direct and indirect effects of environmental drivers on microbial network complexity and stability, piecewise structural equation model (70) analyses were carried out to determine the links among experimental treatments, soil variables, and microbial network complexity and stability. The piecewise structural equation model’s goodness-of-fit was assessed using Fisher’sCstatistic, where non-significant values (P> 0.05) denote a satisfactory model fit (70).
To identify whether soil microbial community functional potentials varied by microbial network complexity and stability, we performed linear mixed effects model analysis with sampling season and plots as random intercept effects (69). All statistical analyses were completed in the R software (v4.2.2;https://www.r-project.org/). See our supplementary information for details on the R packages used and statistical analysis.
