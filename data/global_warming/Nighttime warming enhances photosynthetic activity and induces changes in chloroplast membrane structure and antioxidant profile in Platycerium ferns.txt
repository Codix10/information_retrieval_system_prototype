Global warming leads to asymmetric shifts in daily temperature, with nighttime temperatures increasing more rapidly, which may significantly impact plant physiological processes. Ferns are among the species sensitive to climate change, however their responses to rising nighttime temperatures remain poorly understood. The aim of this study was to evaluate changes in the photosynthetic apparatus and antioxidant profile of two popular ornamental fern species:Platycerium bifurcatumandPlatycerium alcicornein response to an increase in nighttime temperature to daytime levels (resulting in a 2.3 °C increase in the daily mean). The analysis included measurements of chlorophyllafluorescence, gas exchange parameters, pigment profile, antioxidant enzyme activity, non-enzymatic antioxidant content, lipid peroxidation level and physicochemical properties of chloroplast membranes. For the first time in ferns, it was demonstrated that an elevation in nighttime temperature stimulated gross photosynthesis and increased the efficiency of photosystem II. Furthermore, an increase in chlorophyll and flavonoid content, a reduction in malondialdehyde levels (MDA), and greater chloroplast membrane elasticity was observed, particularly within galactolipids fraction. Moderate nocturnal warming may stimulate acclimation processes, improving the photosynthetic efficiency of ferns and enhancing their adaptive potential, which is relevant in the context of the predicted expansion of climate-resilient species and their role in urban ecosystems. The experiment provides a foundation for further research on the effects of long-term warming on the reproduction, growth and population dynamics ofPlatyceriumand other fern species.

One of the main effects of ongoing climate change in recent years is the rise in mean daily air temperature. However, the rate of temperature change between day and night is asymmetric. Especially in urbanized areas, minimum nighttime temperatures are increasing more rapidly than maximum daytime temperatures1. This is mainly due to increased concentrations of greenhouse gases, which trap heat during the night in the absence of direct solar radiation. Research by Duan et al.2in Australia showed that the minimum daily temperature rose by an average of about 0.13 °C per decade. At the same time, the State of the Climate 2024 report shows that the average minimum temperature in 2024 was 1.43 °C higher than the average from 1961 to 1990, while in Australia alone, minimum temperatures increased by 1.51 ± 0.23 °C between 1910 and 2023, accompanied by a fivefold increase in the frequency of extremely warm nights (currently 10% per year)3.
A moderate increase in the mean daily temperature can lead to beneficial physiological changes, such as enhanced photosystem II (PSII) efficiency, increased antioxidant enzyme activity, or greater biomass production in some seed plant species, e.g.,Arabidopsis thaliana4. However, temperatures above the species-specific optimum often intensify photorespiration and impair photosynthesis, negatively affecting plant development, and the response is species-specific5. The literature reports a wide range of responses to warming: positive effects on photosynthesis, e.g., in poplar6, no effect of temperature increase, e.g., in heather7, and a decrease in photosynthetic efficiency, as observed in maize5. Previous studies indicate that increasing temperature may affect the photosynthetic function of ferns differently than in seed plants. John et al.8demonstrated that inPleopeltis polypodioides, high-temperature during hydration leads to a strong reduction in photosynthetic activity, suggesting that ferns may be particularly sensitive to temperature stress under conditions of high-water availability. Comparative anatomical and physiological analyses have shown that many fern species exhibit lower maximum photosynthetic rates and limited mesophyll conductance compared to seed plants, potentially limiting their capacity to adapt to a warming climate9. Species differences in response to increased temperature are also confirmed by the results of Anderson et al.10, who documented divergent photophysiological responses in two common fern species under the influence of summer temperature fluctuations. Collectively, these studies highlight that the effects of warming fern photosynthesis are complex and strongly species-dependent, which justifies the need for further research, especially in the context of asymmetric diurnal warming.
Phytogeographic analyses of ferns indicate that global warming is shifting the climatic range, particularly of epiphytic species, to higher mountain elevations. This is associated with the reduction and loss of some habitats11. This is confirmed by a potential range model developed for four neotropical fern species, which predicts a 46% decline in their distribution area over 50 years12. Despite these threats, rising temperatures (often accompanied by increased solar radiation) result in adaptive changes in ferns at the morphological level, such as increased leaf density13. The impact of nocturnal warming on plant metabolism and productivity is currently the subject of intensive research. The phenomenon of asymmetric diurnal warming has significant ecological and agricultural implications14. Elevated temperatures may promote biomass accumulation in some crop species and increase yields, such as maize and sorghum15. However, there is still a lack of information on the physiological response, particularly regarding the functioning of the photosynthetic apparatus, to increasing ambient temperatures in fern sporophytes (including ornamental species).
Increased temperatures affect not only the natural habitats of fern epiphytes of the genusPlatycerium, such as the forests of northeastern Australia, but also urban areas in subtropical climates. Due to the widespread occurrence ofP. bifurcatumin gardens and private crops, there is concern that it may become an invasive species due to its high resistance to drought stress and air pollution16,17. Current reports indicate naturalization and distribution of individuals on trees with cracked bark and cinder walls in urban spaces if fertileP. bifurcatumspecimens occurred within a 100 m radius18. Increased nocturnal warming may, on the one hand, result in a reduction in the ranges of some plant species, including ferns, while on the other hand, it may promote the naturalization and spread of species more resistant to abiotic environmental factors.
The aim of this study was to compare the photosynthetic response to a nighttime temperature increase relative to daytime temperature (an increase in the daily mean by 2.3 °C) in two popular cultivated epiphytic fern species:P. alcicorneandP. bifurcatum(commonly considered species with varying resistance to abiotic stresses). This comparison provides insight into the acclimation potential of these species in the context of changing environmental conditions and the possibility of occupying new ecological niches, especially in cities, as well as increase their suitability for broader as ornamental plants. To analyze changes in photosynthetic activity, PSII activity was assessed by measuring the kinetics of Chlafluorescence and gas exchange parameters of sporotrophophyll leaves. Furthermore, changes in leaf pigmentation, antioxidant profile, and chloroplast zeta potential were determined, as well as physicochemical modifications of model membranes derived from chloroplast lipids. We formulated the following hypotheses: (i) a moderate increase in night temperature increases PSII activity and is associated with an increase in gas exchange efficiency in epiphytic ferns; (ii) an increase in nighttime temperature induces chloroplast lipid part of membrane reorganization and enhances membrane elasticity; (iii) alterations in the chloroplast antioxidant profile enhance the activity of both enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidants, leading to reduced lipid peroxidation and improved stabilization of chloroplast membranes; (iv) there are differences between species ofPlatyceriumferns in their ability to acclimate to an increase in night temperature.
The genusPlatyceriumbelongs to theFilicidaeand consists of epiphytic species native to various tropical and subtropical regions, although the genus is currently considered pantropical19,20.Platycerium bifurcatum(Cav.) C. Chr. is the main representative of the Javanese-Australian group and occurs naturally in northeastern Australia and New Guinea.Platycerium alcicorneDesv. belongs to the African-American group. Its natural habitats include eastern Africa and the Indian Ocean islands21. Both species have two types of leaves: sporotrophophylls, which are responsible for assimilation and periodically develop sporangia at the ends, and nest leaves, responsible for the accumulation of organic matter, storing water, or performing mechanical functions related to maintaining the plant on the substrate19.P. alcicorneandP. bifurcatumexhibit a number of morphological differences resulting from adaptation to environmental conditions19,20. The sporotrophophylls ofP. alcicorneare slightly shorter (up to ca. 60 cm) and more regularly forked than those ofP. bifurcatum, whose leaves can reach up to 1 m in length, with a gentle, broad fork at the end of the leaf blade. However, both species are widespread and often cultivated as valued ornamental plants20.P. bifurcatumis more popular in cultivation due to its more xeromorphic leaf structure and therefore greater resistance and ease of care compared toP. alcicorne.
The study used 28-month-old sporophytes ofP. alcicorneandP. bifurcatumfrom the private collection of the University of the National Education Commission, Krakow. The sporophytes were cultivated from spores of plants belonging to the collection. A total of 14 plants of similar size from each species were selected for the experiment and placed in the greenhouse of the University of the Commission of National Education in Krakow, in climatic chambers covered with shading netting. Half of the plants (the warmed group) were placed for 30 days in a climate chamber ensuring a constant temperature of 24 °C (± 0.5) and relative humidity (RH) of 60% (± 4) 24 h a day. The other half of the plants (the control group) were placed in a climate chamber at a temperature of 24 °C day/17°C night, maintaining a 16/8 h cycle and RH of 60%. The temperature and humidity conditions were preset in the acclimation chamber interface and continuously maintained, with automatic monitoring at a sampling frequency of every 10 min. The temperature settings were based on the standard cultivation protocols used in our facility, which reflect average conditions representative of the species’ natural environment. All plants had access to natural sunlight for an average of 15 h 25 min per day and were watered with tap water once a week to constant weight. On sunny days, the PPFD did not exceed 200 µmol photons m⁻2s⁻1, as verified using a Danoplus PAR-414 quantum sensor (Danoplus, Hong Kong, China). Measurements were conducted on sporotrophophyll leaves, which constitute the main assimilatory organs inPlatycerium. The leaves were fully developed prior to warming and had comparable assimilatory surface areas. Because sporotrophophyll leaves ofPlatyceriumgrow slowly, their assimilatory area did not change substantially during the course of the experiment. All meaurements were performed after 30 days, with the number of repetitions depending on the technique used.
Fluorescence induction curves and Chlafluorescence kinetics parameters were measured using Handy-PEA (Hansatech Instruments, UK) according to the method of Strasser et al.22. Fluorescence was induced by 3500 µmol quantum m–2s–1radiation after dark acclimation for 35 min. The results were read using the PEA + program (Hansatech Instruments, Narborough, UK). Fluorescence induction curves (OJIP) were determined by assuming the following step values: O – 20 µs, J – 2 ms, I – 30 ms, P – 300 ms. Then, the curves were double normalized to steps O and P. In turn, the differential fluorescence kinetics curves were calculated by subtracting the values of the normalized OJIP curves in warmed plants from the control curves, according to the methodology of Oukarroum et al.23, which revealed bands indicating differences in the PSII response to temperature increase.
Gross photosynthesis (PG) and dark respiration (R) of sporotrophophyll leaves ofP. bifurcatumandP. alcicornewere quantified using a Clark-type oxygen electrode (LD2 chamber, 5 ml volume; Hansatech Instruments Ltd., Norfolk, UK) connected to a CB1D data reading device. To read and analyze the data, the Acquire software was used (Hansatech Group, Norfolk, UK). Leaf fragments (0.8–1.2 cm2) were dark-adapted for 30 min before measurement. The assay medium consisted of 20 mM HEPES-KOH buffer (pH 7.5) supplemented with 5 mM NaHCO3and was continuously stirred with a magnetic stir bar to prevent diffusion limitations. The electrode was calibrated at 24 ± 0.1 °C using sodium dithionite (zero O2) and air-saturated medium (100% O2saturation). Calibration drift was checked every four measurements. After inserting the leaf sample into the chamber, the signal was allowed to stabilize for 90 s before recording. Measurements were conducted in a closed chamber with 21% O2(gas phase). To buffer CO2, the assay medium contained 5 mM NaHCO3, yielding an estimated CO2concentration in the headspace around 350 µmol mol⁻1(within the range 300–400 µmol mol⁻1). Photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) at the leaf surface was set to 100 µmol photons m⁻2s⁻1and verified with a calibrated quantum sensor (Danoplus PAR-414 quantum sensor, Danoplus, Hong Kong, China). This PPFD level corresponds to the low-light photosynthetic optimum reported for shade-adaptedPlatyceriumspecies e.g16,24. , . PG was measured as the linear rate of O2evolution in the light, and R as the rate of O2consumption in darkness. Rates are expressed per area (µmol O2m⁻2s⁻1), with a leaf area of 1.13 cm2(disc diameter 12 mm). Each parameter was measured with 4 biological replicates. The measurement conditions and the use of a Clark-type electrode are commonly applied in studies of photosynthesis in ferns16,24.
The content of chlorophyll, flavonoids, anthocyanins in the leaves and Nitrogen Balance Index (NBI) was measured using a Dualex (METOS, France) at 24 °C, according to the instructions for use issued by the manufacturer. Measurements were made on the upper and bottom sides of the leaf blade. Measurements were made on leaves with a similar degree of maturity in the apical part of the leaf. The results presented in the study are the mean of 6 independent biological replicates (4–5 measurement points on each plant).
Fresh leaves collected from the plants were immediately homogenized in a buffer solution containing 50 mmol dm⁻³ Tris-HCl, 5 mmol dm⁻³ EDTA, and 0.33 mol dm⁻³ sorbitol (Chloroplast Isolation Buffer, CIB). The homogenate was first filtered through a suitable mesh and then centrifuged. The resulting chloroplast-enriched pellet was resuspended in CIB and further purified using a Percoll gradient (40%/80%) to isolate pure organelles. All procedures were carried out at 4 °C.
Superoxide Dismutase (SOD) Activity: SOD (EC 1.15.1.1) activity was measured using a cytochrome c reduction assay, following the method described by McCord and Fridovich25. Samples were mixed with a reaction mixture containing 1 mM cytochrome c, 0.1 mM EDTA, phosphate buffer (pH 7.8), and 1 mM xanthine. The absorbance was recorded at 550 nm. One unit of SOD activity was defined as the amount of enzyme required to inhibit 50% of the reduction of cytochrome c in a system catalyzed by xanthine and xanthine oxidase.
The peroxidase (POD; EC 1.11.1.9) activity was detected using a modified method based on Lück’s26procedure. The reaction involved measuring the amount ofp-phenylenediamine (1%) reaction products formed in the presence of H2O2(0.03 mM) within a phosphate buffer (PB) at a wavelength of 485 nm.
The ascorbate peroxidase (APX; EC 1.11.1.15) activity was determined using the Nakano and Asada method27. The assay was performed in a solution containing Hepes–KOH buffer (pH 7.6) with 0.1 mM EDTA, 0.5 mM ascorbic acid, and 0.2 mM hydrogen peroxide, measuring absorbance at 290 nm. The change in absorbance was divided by the molar extinction coefficient of ascorbate (2.8 mM⁻¹ cm⁻¹), and enzyme activity was expressed as µmol H2O2consumed per minute per milligram of protein.
The catalase (CAT; EC 1.11.1.6) activity was detected following Aebi28. The assay was conducted using a mixture of phosphate buffer (PB) and 0.03 mM hydrogen peroxide, with absorbance measured at λ = 240 nm.
The activity of glutathione reductase (GR; EC 1.6.4.2) was determined by measuring the decrease in NADPH absorbance at 340 nm, using an extinction coefficient of 6.2 mM cm–129.
The determination of reduced glutathione and ascorbic acid content was performed using a spectrophotometric method. The glutathione (GSH) assay followed the procedure described by Griffith30, in which GSH is oxidized by the sulfhydryl reagent 5,5’-dithio-bis(2-nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB). This reaction produces a yellow derivative, 5’-thio-2-nitrobenzoic acid (TNB), which can be quantified by measuring absorbance at 412 nm. Ascorbic acid was measured according to protocol described by Kapur et al.31.
The degree of lipid peroxidation in chloroplasts was assessed by measuring 2-thiobarbituric acid (TBA)-reactive substances, primarily malondialdehyde (MDA), as described by Heath32, Barbasz et al.33and Sieprawska et al.34. Freshly extracted chloroplasts were homogenized in 5 mL of 0.5% TBA prepared in 10% trichloroacetic acid (TCA). The homogenate was incubated at 95 °C for 30 min in a dry thermoblock. After cooling, the samples were centrifuged, and the absorbance of the supernatant was measured at 532 nm using a 96-well plate optimized for small-volume measurements. Non-specific absorbance at 600 nm was subtracted. The MDA concentration was calculated using an extinction coefficient of 155 mM⁻¹ cm⁻¹.
For lipid extraction, the isolated chloroplasts were treated with a mixture of chloroform and isopropanol (1:1, v/v), followed by re-extraction with chloroform, based on a modified version of the Bligh and Dyer method35. Phospholipids (PL) and glycolipids – specifically monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) and digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG) – were separated using adsorptive and distributive column chromatography on silica gel under nitrogen at low pressure. Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was used to assess the purity of the fraction. The identification and quantification of individual phospholipids were performed by visualizing TLC bands in comparison with appropriate phospho- and galactolipid standards.
Fractions of extracted lipids from chloroplasts – namely PL, DGDG, and MGDG – were utilized in experiments employing the Langmuir monolayer technique (Minitrough, KSV; Finland). Monolayers were formed by spreading chloroform-dissolved lipid samples onto the surface of water, which served as the subphase (HLP5, Hydrolab, Poland). Compression of the monolayers was carried out at a rate of 5 mm/min. Surface pressure was measured using a platinum Wilhelmy plate. All measurements were conducted at a constant temperature of 25 °C. Each experiment was repeated three to four times to ensure repeatability, with observed variations in the isotherms within ± 0.1– 0.3 Å².
Using dedicated software, surface pressure (π) vs. molecular area (A) isotherms were obtained, according to protocol, performed previously for plant extracts36.
On the basis on the isotherm, the static compression modulus was calculated using the formula Cs⁻¹ = – (dπ/dlnAm), where π is surface pressure, and Am– is a value characterizing the surface area occupied by a molecule in the membrane), which reflects the mechanical resistance of the monolayers and provides insights into their stability and fluidity. To clear represent the physicochemical properties of the membrane under natural cellular conditions, data for the static compression modulus were described at a surface pressure of 30 mN/m – represent the estimated membrane pressure in natural biological membranes37.
Freshly extracted chloroplasts were used to determine the zeta potential parameter using the Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) method with a Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZS analyser (Malvern Instruments, Great Britain), following the protocol of Filek et al.38and Sieprawska et al.34. Measurements were carried out at 20 °C in media of defined ionic composition and strength, with samples protected from light. The stock supporting electrolyte contained chloroplast isolation buffer (CIB). Zeta potential was automatically calculated from electrophoretic mobility using the Smoluchowski equation.
The obtained numerical data were analyzed using two-way ANOVA in Statistica 13 (TIBCO Software, USA). Statistical significance of differences was tested using Duncan’s or Tukey’s test at a significance level ofp≤ 0.05 and indicated in tables or graphs using letter symbols.

Rising temperatures – including increases in nighttime values – play a significant role in shaping photosynthetic performance, biomass accumulation, and species distribution39. In our study, nighttime warming did not impair the functioning of the photosynthetic apparatus in eitherPlatyceriumspecies. Instead, we observed enhancements in PSII performance and gross photosynthesis (PG), indicating that the applied warming fell within the thermal tolerance ranges of both ferns and acted as an acclimatory rather than a stressful factor. This aligns with reports showing that temperatures below the stress threshold can stimulate biochemical activity, enhancing carboxylation and photochemical efficiency40, while excessive heat impairs electron flow and PSII stability41. Because photosystem thermostability is highly species-dependent42,43, PSII represents a critical point of evaluation – it is the most temperature-sensitive component of the photosynthetic electron transport chain, particularly due to the vulnerability of the D1 protein and oxygen-evolving complex44. For this reason, chlorophyll a fluorescence and OJIP kinetics provide an effective approach for detecting warming-induced changes in photochemical efficiency, energy transfer and PSII connectivity45,46. Below, we integrate photochemical, pigment-related, biochemical, and membrane-level changes to propose a mechanistic model of thermal acclimation in these epiphytic ferns.
Nighttime warming induced distinct OJIP-related signatures in both species, reflecting species-specific adjustments of PSII structure and function. Changes in between O–J and J–I steps were especially pronounced inP. bifurcatum, whileP. alcicorneshowed more subtle modifications. The positive L-band inP. alcicornesuggests reduced energetic connectivity among PSII units and decreased structural stability of LHCII, a pattern reported under temperature-induced antenna–RC reorganization47,48. Such changes are typically associated with rearrangements of PSII complexes and alterations in thylakoid ultrastructure49,50. Conversely, the negative K-band observed inP. bifurcatumreflects increased efficiency of the PSII donor side and higher OEC activity51, further supported by elevated Fv/Fo values52. Negative K-bands have been associated with undamaged or reinforced OEC stability at moderate temperature increases46, consistent with our findings.
Additionally,P. bifurcatumdisplayed a positive band between J and I, indicating localized disruptions in plastoquinone reduction similar to those documented under light stress in the genus53. Importantly, both J and I steps remained distinct, whereas these phases often collapse during heat stress46. Positive G-band values suggest increased efficiency of terminal electron acceptors (ferredoxin, NADP⁺, RuBP)50, paralleling responses observed in plants exposed to abiotic stress17,54.
JIP-test parameters further support improved PSII vitality. Both species showed substantial increases in PIabs, Fv/Fo, RC/ABS, and Fv/Fm – particularly the > 80% rise in PIabs – indicating reinforced photochemical performance under warming46. Species-specific responses were also evident:P. alcicornedisplayed increases in Fm, (1–Vj)/Vj, ABS/RC, and DIo/RC, reflecting antenna expansion and photoprotective dissipation. In contrast,P. bifurcatumexhibited reductions in Fo and increased Vj, indicating adjustments primarily on the donor side. Similar genotype-dependent patterns have been documented in crops55. Together, the OJIP data show that nighttime warming enhances PSII function through distinct, species-specific structural adjustments rather than through stress-induced damage.
Gross photosynthesis increased in both species under warming (by approximately 11% inP. alcicorneand 9% inP. bifurcatum), while respiration remained unchanged. This indicates that enhanced carbon assimilation outweighed any potential increase in respiratory expenditure. Such patterns are consistent with warming-induced stimulation of enzymatic kinetics in the Calvin–Benson cycle, particularly through increased RUBISCO activity and maintenance of functional RUBISCO activase (RCA) under moderate warming56. RCA regulates the fraction of active RUBISCO and is one of the main “bottlenecks” at higher temperatures. If RCA remains functional under moderate warming conditions, Rubisco activation increases and thus PGincreases. Numerous studies indicate that differences in RCA thermostability determine the species’ response to increased temperature57. Simultaneously, changes in thylakoid membrane fluidity induced by increased temperature may transiently facilitate electron transfer and balancing the PSI/PSII ratio58. Although further measurements of Vcmax, Jmax, and RCA expression would clarify these mechanisms, our results support a model in which nighttime warming improves photosynthetic capacity inPlatycerium. Similar warming-induced increases in PGhave been reported in other species59, while the fernRumohra adiantiformisdisplays enhanced growth under elevated daytime temperatures60, indicating that ferns can benefit from moderate temperature elevation.
Both species responded to nighttime warming with increased chlorophyll and flavonols content, with stronger responses inP. alcicorne. These changes are consistent with improved light-harvesting capacity and targeted enhancement of non-enzymatic antioxidant protection. Increased chlorophyll suggests stabilization or expansion of PSII antennae, aligning with elevated ABS/RC values. The rise in flavonols – compounds known to support ROS quenching and photoprotection under heat-induced oxidative pressure61– likely contributed to maintaining PSII function. Similar trends have been observed under nighttime warming in other plant species, where temperatures stimulate pigment accumulation and metabolic activity62. The stable levels of anthocyanins, coupled with selective increases in flavonols, indicate that only the latter group played a central role in maintaining redox balance and supporting photosynthesis under moderate warming, consistent with adaptive responses of xerophytic ferns63.
The warming treatment modulated enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidant systems in both species, but without inducing oxidative stress. CAT activity decreased, consistent with reports of temperature-induced catalase inactivation at elevated H2O2levels64. In parallel, POD activity increased, suggesting compensation through alternative peroxide-scavenging pathways. Decreases in SOD and APX observed inP. bifurcatumdid not result in greater lipid peroxidation, as MDA levels remained stable. This tolerance may result from the fact that fewer polyunsaturated fatty acids undergo oxidation, as the saturated fatty acids present in cell membranes are less susceptible to oxidative damage65,66. Moreover, other pathways of the antioxidant system (e.g., increased POD and GR activity, higher GSH and AsA levels) may take over part of the detoxification functions, allowing redox balance to be maintained despite the decrease in SOD and APX activity. The strengthened activity of GR and the elevated GSH and AsA pools inP. alcicornealign with established roles of the AsA–GSH cycle in maintaining redox homeostasis67. Although thermotolerance-related patterns vary across species - with GR activity decreasing in tomato68but increasing in maize, wheat, and cucumber64,69]– [70– our results indicate thatPlatyceriummaintains effective ROS detoxification under warming. The increased GSH content inP. alcicorneparallels observations in species exhibiting enhanced thermotolerance71. Notably, previous research on ferns has shown increased antioxidant activity in polyploid gametophytes exposed to heat and drought72, supporting the idea that ferns possess flexible redox regulation pathways.
Cell membranes modify their lipid composition, allowing plants to acclimate to lower or higher temperatures. This membrane remodeling leads to changes in their fluidity, which in turn influences the distribution and interactions of various compounds. To understand the mechanisms underlying changes in the mechanical properties of cell membranes, the Langmuir modeling technique was applied to lipid monolayers extracted from membranes. This technique allows for the assessment and observation of changes in physicochemical parameters associated with individual lipid components in the dynamics of plant membranes36,73,74. Chloroplast membrane lipids, primarily galactolipids and phospholipids, are rich in polyunsaturated fatty acids. According to Hölzl and Dörmann75, chloroplasts are the primary site of fatty acid synthesis in plant cells.
Based on the analysis of changes in the static compression modulus, it was observed that theP. alcicornemembrane is stiffer than theP. bifurcatummembrane and demonstrated a tendency toward increased elasticity with rising temperature. However, a comparison of physicochemical and biochemical results reveals that the observed changes form a consistent picture of the responses of the studied ferns to nighttime warming. The decrease in malondialdehyde content at higher temperatures (compared to the control group in both species), combined with physicochemical data, may indicate two possible mechanisms: (i) the membranes were remodeled, but the unsaturated bonds in the fatty acids were not structurally damaged thanks to effective antioxidant systems; (ii) the decrease in the elastic modulus of the model membrane, typical for systems containing a higher proportion of unsaturated lipids, reflects increased membrane elasticity (see36.
Among the three analyzed lipid fractions, the galactolipid fractions (particularly MGDG) play a key role in shaping the mechanical and physicochemical properties of chloroplast membranes. This is supported by the most pronounced changes in the compressibility modulus observed in both ferns. A species-specific characteristic is the differentiated initial galactolipid-to-phospholipid mass ratio (GL: PL mass ratio) in the overall membrane lipid composition:P. alcicorne1.45 : 1.12,P. bifurcatum1.22 : 1.23.
Because the isolated thylakoid membranes ofP. alcicorne(compared toP. bifurcatum) contain a higher proportion of galactolipids, it is assumed that this contributes to enhanced membrane adaptability under stress conditions, thereby supporting more optimal photosynthetic performance. It is also worth emphasizing that the nocturnal temperature increase did not significantly affect the total surface charge of chloroplasts. The zeta potential, typical for chloroplast membranes, remained stable under warming conditions, with differences observed between species. The measured zeta potentials of chloroplast surfaces isolated from the studied ferns were consistent with literature trends (− 8 mV to − 12 mV)76, and even up to − 20 mV, in agreement with the data of Filek et al.73.
In summary, the observed changes at the biochemical and physicochemical levels suggest that antioxidant systems may play a key role in protecting unsaturated membrane lipids from degradation, thus contributing to the maintenance of membrane integrity and functionality under warming conditions.
Integrating photochemical, biochemical, and biophysical responses, our findings reveal that moderate nighttime warming stimulates a convergent acclimatory response inPlatycerium: (i) PSII electron transport becomes more efficient, likely linked to species-specific reorganization mainly within donor and antennal structures; (ii) gross photosynthesis increases while respiration remains unchanged, thus impacting the overall carbon balance; (iii) chlorophyll and flavonol accumulation increases, allowing for the preservation of photochemical activity and increasing the potential antioxidant capacity; (iv) redox homeostasis is preserved, with no signs of oxidative membrane damage despite changes in enzymatic antioxidant activity; (v) chloroplast membranes undergo adaptive remodeling, particularly within the MGDG fraction, supporting stable PSII function.
Together, these adjustments indicate that simulated nighttime warming enhances the functional resilience ofPlatyceriumspecies, potentially facilitating their ecological expansion particularly in urban habitats. The observed interspecific differences indicate thatP. alcicornedemonstrates a greater adaptive capacity of its photosynthetic apparatus to temperature increases thanP. bifurcatum.
