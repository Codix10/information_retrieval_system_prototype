In polar and alpine regions, global warming and landform changes are draining lakes, transforming them into permafrost with altered microbial communities and element cycling. In this study, we investigated bacterial and archaeal (prokaryotic) community changes in the newly exposed sediment of Zonag Lake (Tibetan Plateau), focusing on prokaryotic diversity, community structure, and genes involved in carbon fixation and nitrogen cycling across lateral (up to 800 m) and vertical (up to 80 cm) horizons. The results showed that prokaryotic richness decreased across the lateral horizons, coinciding with reductions in carbon concentrations. Dramatic changes in community structure were also observed, primarily influenced by the distance from the lake and then by sediment depth, with environmental filtering and dispersal limitations shaping the lateral and vertical distributions, respectively. Based on PICRUSt2 results, the relative abundance of genes related to carbon fixation increased along the lateral horizon, suggesting that microbial carbon fixers are counteracting the carbon loss during permafrost formation. In contrast, the genes related to denitrification also increased, which may lead to nitrogen loss and contribute to global warming by releasing nitric oxide gas. This study highlights the resilience of prokaryotic communities in drained lake basins and their ecological implications under global warming.
Keywords:exposed lake sediment, microbial community succession, richness and community structure, elemental cycling, lateral and vertical scales
This study examines how global warming-driven lake drainage alters microbial communities and element cycling in the Tibetan Plateau’s Zonag Lake, with implications for carbon and nitrogen dynamics.
The Tibetan Plateau harbors the largest number of high-altitude lakes and is the world’s third-largest permafrost area, following the Antarctic and Arctic. Most lakes on the Tibetan Plateau have expanded in recent years due to increased precipitation (Zhou et al.2021). Zonag Lake, situated in the northern Tibetan Plateau, serves as an example of this phenomenon, experiencing increased rainfall due to climate warming. However, the lake was unable to contain the rapidly rising water levels, leading to an outburst in 2011. This outburst resulted in a reduction of ~100 km² in the lake’s surface area (Liu et al.2016). The outburst exposed sediment that subsequently transformed into permafrost due to the lack of thermal protection from the lake water (Zhang et al.2022) (Fig.1A). Typically, sediments exposed by drained lakes lose organic carbon through enhanced microbial degradation, a process facilitated by increased oxygen availability (Mueller et al.2015, Liu et al.2020a). Despite this, there is limited understanding of how carbon and microbial communities change in such exposed sediments on the Tibetan Plateau. Gaining insights into these changes will enhance our understanding of the carbon stored in the exposed sediment and the potential impacts on the metabolisms of carbon and nitrogen in alpine regions.

After the lake has been drained, microbial activity alters the physicochemical properties of the exposed sediments, creating a gradient of environmental heterogeneity that varies with drainage durations. This heterogeneity presents a unique opportunity to investigate how changes in sediment properties affect the diversity, composition, and functions of microbial communities. Key physicochemical properties, such as soil moisture, pH, and fertility, are significant drivers of bacterial communities in both riparian surface soils and dryland soils (Malik et al.2018, Annala et al.2022, Yang et al.2023). These findings reveal critical drivers of microbial community structure and function following drainage. Further complexity arises from changes associated with varied exposure times or distances from the lake shore (lateral horizons) but also across sediment depth (vertical horizons) (Chu et al.2016, Upton et al.2020). Vertical changes are typically influenced by factors such as aeration, pH, sediment texture, nutrient availability, and microbial dispersal capacity, which distinguish them from lateral patterns (Hsiao et al.2018, Trivedi et al.2019). On a broader geospatial scale, microbial diversity and community functions are typically driven by environmental heterogeneity (Wang et al.2017, Li et al.2022a). However, at local scales, stochastic processes may exert a greater influence, as evidenced by the patchiness of microbial community structures observed at centimeter distances (O’Brien et al.2016).
The rapidly changing physicochemical properties of exposed sediments in drained lakes present a particularly unique context. We hypothesize that the primary drivers influencing the changes in microbial community and function in these sediments are local environmental factors, such as moisture, pH, nutrient availability, and electrical conductivity (EC). To test this hypothesis, we employed amplicon sequencing to examine microbial community dynamics, including both bacteria and archaea, in the newly exposed Zonag Lake sediments along an 800-m transect with an 80-cm depth profile.
Zonag Lake is located at the northeastern part of the Hoh Xil National Nature Reserve in the central Tibetan Plateau (35°30′–35°65′N, 91°50′–92°10′E, altitude 4758 m; Fig.1A). The climate of Zonag Lake area is cold and dry, and the annual average temperature is −5.1°C, with below zero temperatures from October to April of the following year. The annual average precipitation is 299.3 mm, with the rainy season (May–September) accounting for 90% of the annual precipitation.
In September 2011, the Zonag Lake experienced an outburst that resulted in a significant loss of water, reducing the lake’s area from 274.1 to 160.2 km² within a month. As the breach deepened, the lake’s ability to store water diminished, leading to a gradual decline in its water area. Between 2012 and 2014, the lake’s area further decreased by 11.04 km². Consequently, a vast portion of the drained lake basin was revealed, which quickly developed into permafrost (Zhang et al.2022).
Sediment samples were collected in May 2021 using a soil drill (Rhino Multi-Pro) with a diameter of 3.5 cm. Sediment cores were collected along three transects perpendicular to the lake shore spaced ∼20 m apart at 2, 77, 173, 256, 348, 449, 526, 612, 697, and 781 m away from the lake shore. The core length was 80 cm for samples 2, 173, and 348 m away from the lake shore, but due to the hindrance of the presence of rocks, only 40 cm long cores could be obtained from the remaining locations. In total, 30 sediment cores were collected, including 21 cores with a length of 40 cm and 9 cores with a length of 80 cm. For consistency, only the top 40 cm samples were used for analysis.
The collected sediment cores were stored at −20°C until transported to and processed at the laboratory in Lanzhou University. In the laboratory, the sediment cores were first cut into 10-cm intervals. To ensure that the DNA samples remained uncontaminated, a sterile metal piece measuring 5 mm in width, 1 mm in thickness, and 10 cm in length was used to collect DNA samples from the center of the 10-cm sediment cores. After samples for DNA extraction were collected, the remaining sediment was used for physicochemical analysis with the outer 1–2 mm of the sediment being discarded.
Sediment water content was quantified by the amount of weight lost from a known amount of wet sediment that had been dried at 105°C to a constant value (Liu et al.2010). For other sediment physicochemical properties, sediments were first air dried at dark and homogenized by passing through a 2-mm sieve. Sediment pH and conductivity (EC) were measured using a pH meter (PHS-3C) in a 1:2.5 (g/g) sediment-to-water suspension at room temperature of 25°C. Total carbon (TC) was quantified using a total organic carbon (TOC) analyzer (TOC-VCPH). Ammonium (NH4+), nitrate (NO3−), and available potassium (AP) were quantified using an element analyzer (Smartchem200, AMS), after extraction with 2 M KCl solution (NH4+and NO3−) or 0.5 M NaHCO3solution (AP) (Liebner et al.2009).
Total DNA was extracted using a DNeasy PowerSoil Pro Kit (Qiagen, Germany) from 0.5 g sediment. The concentration of the extracted DNA was determined using a Qubit fluorometer (Qubit 4.0, Thermo Fisher Scientific) with dsDNA HS Assay KitTM, with the DNA concentration extracted from the sediment ranged from 0.0512 to 6.24 ng/µl, equivalent to 11.9–1837.6 ng DNA/g dry sediment (Table S2). The microbial community was investigated using amplification sequencing of the V4–V5 hypervariable region of the 16S rRNA gene using primers 515F (5′-GTGCCAGCMGCCGGTAA-3′) and 907R (5′-CCGTCAATTCMTTTRAGTTT-3′) (Biddle et al.2008). Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) assays were performed with a total volume of 25 µl, included 1 × PCR buffer, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 0.4 µM DNTP, and 1 µM final concentration of the forward and reverse primers. 0.5 U of Ex Taq (Takara) and 20 ng DNA templates. PCR cycling conditions were set to: initial denaturation at 95°C for 3 min, followed by 27 cycles: 95°C for 30 s, 55°C for 30 s, and 72°C for 45 s, and final extension for 10 min at 72°C. The PCR products gel purified using the OMEGA Gel Extraction Kit (Omega Bio Tek). PCR products were pooled from different samples at equimolar amounts. Prior to amplification, asymmetric barcode sequences were ligated to the PCR primers. The OnePure Magnetic beads Kit (Findrop Biosafety Technology) was then used to ligate adapters to the amplicons at both ends during library preparation. Sequencing was performed on the Illumina HiSeq PE250 sequencer instrument at Magigene Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Guangdong, China.
Sequencing data were processed using the USEARCH pipeline (Pauvert et al.2019). Briefly, the paired-end reads were assembled and quality filtered using the following settings: sequences with 2 bp mismatch with primers, homopolymers longer than 8 bp, or sequences with a maximum expected error probability greater than 0.5 were removed. Then, sequences were classified into operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at a 97% sequence identity threshold. There is debate on the usage of OTU and amplicon sequence variant (ASV) in amplicon sequencing studies (Liu et al.2020b). The OTU and ASV offer different advantages in amplicon sequencing-based analysis. ASV offers better separation on prokaryotic species, yet may fail to distinguish very closely related true biological sequences and clump them together into a single ASV (Regueira-Iglesias et al.2023). Due to the large dataset, we used the OTU approach instead. The representative OTUs were taxonomically annotated against the Silva database (2019.12, release 138) with a minimum confidence score of 80% (Wang et al.2007). We then removed all eukaryotic, chloroplast, mitochondria, and unknown sequences from the OTU table, which were then rarefied to an equal depth of 55 744. We employed PICRUSt2 to investigate the distribution characteristics of carbon and nitrogen metabolism functional genes across lateral and vertical horizons in exposed sediments (Douglas et al.2020), a methodology capable of predicting the abundances of genes or pathways based on 16S rRNA gene sequencing data.
We assessed environmental heterogeneity by quantifying the overall differences in physicochemical parameters using Euclidean distance (Huber et al.2020). We then analyzed the correlation between this heterogeneity and geospatial distance across both horizontal and vertical horizons. This relationship is referred to as the distance decay of environmental heterogeneity, with the slopes representing the decay rates. Similarly, we also examined the community similarity decay of prokaryotic communities across the lateral (the transect with increasing distance from the lake shore) and the vertical (the transect with increasing sediment depth). The community similarity is calculated by the 1-Bray–Curtis dissimilarity between the Hellinger transformed OTU table (Jiao et al.2020), while the decay rate is the slope of the correlation between community similarity and differences in the distance across lateral or vertical horizons.
Main statistical analyses were performed in the R environment (v-4.3.1;http://www.r-project.org/) using the R-package subset. Sediment microbial diversity was characterized using the richness index, which is the number of OTUs identified in a sample. Spearman correlation was used to investigate the association among sediment physicochemical properties (moisture, TC, EC, NH4+, pH, NO3−, and AP), geographical distance, sediment microbial richness, and the relative abundance of dominant microbial phyla. The significance of the full models and cross-validatedR2values for the impact of physicochemical properties on microbial richness was assessed using the “A3” package with 999 permutations of the response variable (Jiao et al.2018). The relative importance of sediment physicochemical properties was assessed using the regression methods of random forest modeling with the “rfPermute” package. We set the parameters for the random forest modeling as follows: ntree = 5000 and nrep = 1000. Differences in the community structure of sediment microbial communities were visualized using the nonmetric multidimensional scaling analysis based on Hellinger-transformed Bray–Curtis dissimilarity. Analysis of similarities (ANOSIM) was used to test the significant effects of lateral and vertical horizons on the overall community structure using the adonis2 command in R with 999 permutations. Determinism refers to the influence of deterministic processes on microbial community changes, such as environmental filtering, interspecies competition, and predation. Comparatively, stochasticity refers to the random events that can affect population and community dynamics. Here, we used the modified stochastic ratio parameter (MST) to estimate the contribution of stochasticity in shaping microbial community assembly (Zhou et al.2014, Ning et al.2019). Distance-based linear model (DistLM) was used to further analyze the influence of sediment physicochemical properties on microbial community structure (Lamelas-López et al.2021), with the sediment physicochemical properties being normalized [(x-mean)/standard deviation] before analysis. Additionally, variance partitioning analysis (VPA) was used to disentangle the influence of geographical distance and environmental parameters on microbial community structure. For this, the geospatial distance was converted into spatial predictors using the principal coordinates of neighbor matrices method (Borcard and Legendre2002), and then normalized to improved normality.

In the vertical horizons, sediment moisture significantly increased in all three sediment cores (P< .01), while no consistent changes were observed for the other physicochemical parameters (Table1). The distance decay rates of environmental heterogeneity in the vertical horizons were the lowest for sediment core S1, which is closest to the lake shore. The rates then increased with distance from the lake shore, with slopes of 0.0149, 0.0239, and 0.0450 for S1, S3, and S5, respectively (Fig. S2).
The richness of the prokaryotic (bacterial and archaeal) community significantly declined with increasing distance from the lake shore across all lateral horizons (Fig.1BandFig. S3A, Spearman correlation, allP< .01). The strength of the correlation, as indicated by theR2value, decreased with depth: 0.7 for the 0–10 cm horizon, 0.5 for 10–20 cm, 0.4 for 20–30 cm, and 0.2 for 30–40 cm. Additionally, the slope of correlation also decreased with depth, with the deepest horizon exhibiting the lowest value (Fig. S3A).
Random Forest regression analysis revealed that 53% (P< .001) of the variations in prokaryotic richness were explained by the combined effects of the measured physicochemical parameters (Fig.1C). TC demonstrated the highest explanatory power, while sediment moisture, EC, NH4+, and pH played significant but comparatively less important roles. The importance of these physicochemical parameters varied among the different lateral horizons (Fig. S4). Specifically, TC was more influential in the 0–10 cm and 30–40 cm horizons, whereas NH4+and EC were more influential in the 10–20 cm and 20–30 cm horizons, respectively.
The changes in prokaryotic richness across the vertical horizons were influenced by the distance of sediment cores from the lake shore. At coring site S1, prokaryotic richness did not change significantly with depth (Spearman correlation,R2< 0.001,P= .98). Comparatively, a significant decline (R2= 0.25,P= .01) and increase (R2= 0.21,P= .02) in richness were observed for S3 and S5, respectively (Fig. S3B). Random Forest regression analysis showed that the measured physicochemical parameters explained 40% (P< .001) of the variations in prokaryotic richness (Fig.1C). TC exhibited the highest explanatory power and was identified as the most important physicochemical parameter across all vertical horizons (Fig. S4B).
We identified 68 bacterial and 9 archaeal phyla, of which 13 phyla were considered dominant due to their average relative abundance exceeding 1% (Fig.2andFig. S5). In the 13 dominant phyla, the relative abundances of Bacteroidota, Firmicutes, Chloroflexi, Caldatribacteriota, Desulfobacterota, and Campylobacterota significantly decreased with increasing distance from the lake shore (allP< .05). Conversely, the relative abundances of Actinobacteriota, Gammaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria, Acidobacteriota, and Gemmatimonadota significantly increased (allP< .05), while Cyanobacteria and Planctomycetota showed no changes across the four depths (Fig.2).

Across the vertical horizons, only the relative abundance of Bacteroidota consistently changed (reduced) with increasing sediment depth (allP< .05). For Gammaproteobacteria, its relative abundance exhibited opposite trends in S1 and S5. Additionally, more prokaryotic phyla exhibited significant changes in S5, which is the sediment core furthest away from the lake shore. Specifically, the relative abundances of Bacteroidota, Gammaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria, Desulfobacterota, and Gemmatimonadota significantly decreased with increasing depth (allP< .05). In contrast, the relative abundances of Firmicutes, Actinobacteriota, Chloroflexi, and Cyanobacteria significantly increased with depth.
The results of the ANOSIM revealed that variations in community structure were primarily influenced by the distance from the lake shore (R2= 0.35), followed by sediment depth (R2= 0.19; Fig.3). Across the lateral horizons, significant distance decay in community similarity was observed, with deeper horizons demonstrating a lower decay rate (the slope) andR2value (Fig.4A). VPA showed that spatial distance and environmental parameters together explained 21%–43% of the variations in community structure across lateral horizons (Fig. S6A). The explanatory power decreased with increasing sediment depth, with 43%, 33%, and 21% of the variations being explained in the 0–10 cm, 10–20 cm, and 20–30 cm horizons, respectively. However, the proportion of the community variations being explained increased to 30% in the 30–40 cm horizon. Spatial distance consistently explained greater variations in community structure than physicochemical parameters. DistLM analysis showed that EC had the greatest explanatory power in the 0–10, 10–20, and 30–40 cm horizons, while TC was the dominant factor in the 20–30 cm horizon (Table S1). The modified stochastic ratio (MST) index showed that stochasticity explained less than 50% of the microbial community variations across lateral horizons (Fig.5A), suggesting that the community assembly was primarily influenced by deterministic processes. Additionally, a higher contribution of stochasticity was observed in the deeper horizon (20–40 cm) compared with the upper horizons.

VPA analysis indicated that spatial distance consistently had greater explanatory power than physicochemical parameters (Fig. S6B). Among the physicochemical parameters, pH significantly explained the vertical community structure variations in S1 and S3 (Table S1, bothP< .05), while TC significantly explained the variations in S5 (P< .001). In contrast, it positively correlated with the relative abundances ofamoAandnifH. TC exhibited a similar influence on the relative abundances of these genes as the NH4+. The concentrations of carbon and NH4+declined with increasing distance from the lake shore in the exposed sediment (Table1). This decline can be attributed to several factors. As the exposed sediment further from the lake shore was less influenced by lake water and are more accessible by oxygen, carbon and nitrogen became more readily degraded by aerobic respiration by microorganisms (Hopkins et al.2014, Wu et al.2024). Additionally, the leaching of organic matter from the surface may also contribute to this depletion (Bristol et al.2021, Nakhavali et al.2021), which together explains the changing patterns of carbon and nitrogen across the lateral and vertical horizons.
Limited studies exist on the influence of lake drainage on prokaryotic communities. The positive correlation between microbial richness and TC (Fig.1CandFig. S7) suggests that microbial richness is primarily constrained by carbon limitation. This finding is consistent with the results from carbon-poor environments, such as recently deglaciated soils (Kwon et al.2015), forest soils at various altitudes (Ren et al.2018), and alkaline soils across the Tibetan Plateau (Chu et al.2016). This suggests that lake drainage will cause a substantial loss of prokaryotic diversity due to changes in environmental parameters. While consistent changes in richness patterns were observed across lateral horizons, the patterns across the vertical horizons varied, exhibiting no change, decline, or increase (Fig.1BandFig. S3B). This is consistent with the mixed results regarding microbial richness changes across vertical horizons in previous studies (Yuan et al.2020, Wang et al.2021). The observed variations are reflective of the influence of lake water, as the sediment core closer to the lake exhibited higher sediment moisture (Table1). Water acts as a medium that can distribute microorganisms and nutrients throughout different sediment horizons. Consequently, the less pronounced variations in physicochemical parameters in the vertical horizons of S1, compared to S3 and S5, align with the absence of significant differences in prokaryotic richness (refer toFig. S2). The notable influence of sediment moisture is consistent with a previous study that high precipitation causes the homogenous microbial vertical distribution in soils (Tian et al.2023). In Tian et al. (2023), high precipitation drives the hydraulic events in soil and can potentially affect the vertical flow of soil water. Thus, the enhanced vertical mobilization of microbes with water flow may act as an important influx of biodiversity to the deeper sediment preventing microbial community stratification.
Across the lateral horizons, prokaryotic community similarity significantly declined along the lateral horizons (Fig.4A), confirming the presence of community similarity distance decays. Additionally, the rate of decay declined with increasing sediment depth (Fig.4A), traditionally attributed to the reduced environmental heterogeneity with increased sediment depth (Anderson et al.2011, Graco-Roza et al.2022). However, this does not fully explain our results, as the slope of the environmental heterogeneity gradient was the highest in middle horizons (10–30 cm;Fig. S1). Thus, we speculate that the low rate of decay in the deep horizon is due to higher water content since increased sediment moisture levels can facilitate microbial dispersal, leading to greater similarity in prokaryotic communities. This observation aligns with weak influence of environmental parameters, as consistent with the VPA, which indicated that the contribution of spatial distance was greater (Fig. S6). However, the pronounced influence of stochasticity contradicts the previously observed dominance of deterministic processes (Fig.5A). Therefore, other unmeasured environmental parameters, such as total organic carbon content, the C/N ratio, and the total extractable lipids ratio, or microbial interactions (Philippot et al.2024) may have significantly contributed to the observed patterns.
The community similarity exhibited a significant declined with increasing sediment depth (Fig.4B), highlighting the presence of distance decay across vertical horizons. This finding is consistent with a previous study on farmland and forest soil profiles (Chen et al.2020). The decay rate was higher in sediment cores S3 and S5 compared to S1, indicating that sediment exposed for longer durations exhibited a higher decay rate. Furthermore, the microbial community dynamics of sediment cores S3 and S5 were more strongly influenced by the stochasticity than those of S1 (Fig.5B). Thus, we propose that dispersal limitation serves as the primary assembly mechanism responsible for the differences in decay rates, which is supported by the greater explanatory power of spatial distance indicated in the VPA analysis (Fig. S6). Therefore, variations in dispersal limitation could be the key driver of the different decay rates observed. A similar conclusion has been reached in previous publications (Hu et al.2022). Water is the main dispersal medium across soil depths by assisting microbial dispersal (Chen et al.2020, Li et al.2022b). Given that sediment cores S3 and S5 exhibited lower water content (Table1), this reinforces the notion that dispersal limitation has a greater impact on sediment cores located further from the lake compared to those found near the lake. Environmental parameters explained the community structure variations only to a modest extent, accounting for between 7.7% and 19.2% (Table S1), which is consistent with the strong influence of stochasticity (Fig.5B). Additionally, pH and TC were identified as the primary explanatory factors, consistent with findings from a previous study (Xue et al.2022). This suggests that the microbial community structure across vertical horizons is similarly influenced by the same environmental parameters observed in lateral horizons, albeit to a lesser degree.
The changes in the diversity, community structure, and taxonomy in prokaryotic community across the lateral and vertical horizons will alter their carbon and nitrogen cycling capacities (Lin and Lin2022). Except the Wood-Ljungdahl (WL) pathway (K15023) and 3-hydroxypropionyl-coenzyme A dehydratase, the other examined carbon-fixation pathways negatively correlated with TC and ammonium ions across the lateral horizons (Fig. S7). This suggests that carbon-fixation microorganisms are enriched after carbon depletion in the exposed sediment. Therefore, the bacterial carbon fixers that are adapted to the exposed sediment condition provide additional organic carbon into the ecosystems, that counteract the loss of TC during permafrost formation. In comparison, the WL pathway is anaerobic and mainly found in the Firmicutes and Chloroflexi (Adam et al.2018), whose relative abundance declined across the lateral horizon (Fig.2), likely influenced by the reduction in soil moisture (Jiao et al.2021). For these genes across the vertical horizon, different patterns were observed in various sediment cores. Nevertheless, anaerobic carbon-fixation pathways, including reductive citrate cycle (Steffens et al.2021), hydroxypropionate–hydroxybutylate cycle (Garritano et al.2022), and reductive acetyl-CoA pathway (Schöne et al.2022) were relatively more abundant in deeper sediment (Fig.6), reflecting environmental stratification determines the ecosystem functions.
For nitrogen cycling, the relative abundances of genes were more constrained by physicochemical properties in deeper horizons (Fig. S7). Specifically, the relative abundance of genes associated with nitrate reduction significantly increased across lateral horizons, which negatively correlated with TC and NH4+. High concentrations of NH4+can favor NH4+-utilizing bacteria and archaea (Verhamme et al.2011), which may outcompete denitrifies for the available nitrogen. This competition can reduce the relative abundance of denitrifiers, as the microbial community shifts toward those that preferentially utilize NH4+. Alternatively, microbial activity consumes sediment carbon and depletes oxygen, creating anaerobic conditions that favor denitrification (Enrich-Prast et al.2016), which is consistent with the increasing trend of denitrification-related genes (Fig.6). This increase in denitrification-related genes can contribute to nitrogen loss by releasing nitrate into nitric oxide, nitrous oxide, and dinitrogen gas (Xia et al.2017). Additionally, nitric oxide is a greenhouse gas, with a global warming potential 300 times greater than that of CO2over a 100-year time horizon. While it is widely recognized that the draining lake could be a potential source of greenhouse gas CO2and CH4(Chen et al.2023), our study hints that it may also be a spot of nitric oxide emission.
In this study, we investigated the microbial community dynamics across lateral and vertical horizons in the recently exposed sediment of a drained lake basin on the Tibetan Plateau. Our results revealed consistent patterns of microbial richness with increasing distance from the lake shore across different sediment depths. In contrast, the vertical patterns of microbial richness were inconsistent, modulated by the distance to the lake (impact of lake water). Both changes in richness were driven by carbon content. This study enhances our understanding of microbial succession patterns following lake drainage in alpine regions. Moreover, our results highlight the significant differences in the distribution of functional genes related to elemental cycling in exposed sediments at varying distances from the lakeshore, underscoring the crucial roles of environmental changes in the functional potentials in drained lakes of the alpine regions. These findings are important for understanding the carbon and nitrogen cycles in different sediment environments.
Our work reveals significant variations in carbon and nitrogen loss, alongside changes in microbial community structure and functions. However, we recognize that the sampling design is limited by the fact that only one time point was analyzed, 10 years after the drainage of Zonag Lake. This temporal limitation restricts our ability to fully assess potential successional patterns and the influence of pre-existing conditions on the observed changes. Moreover, local environmental heterogeneity may further complicate interpretations of our data. In light of these constraints, we recommend further long-term monitoring of the exposed sediment, which should include assessments of organic matter composition, bioavailability of organic matter, and dissolved oxygen content. Such studies would provide deeper insights into microbial community dynamics and their roles in sediment element cycling following lake drainage and during the transformation into permafrost.
