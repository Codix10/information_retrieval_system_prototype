Limited understanding of microbial metabolism under long-term warming, especially in (sub)tropical forests, impedes accurate predictions of carbon-climate feedbacks. Microbial carbon use efficiency (CUE, the partitioning of metabolized carbon for microbial growth) is crucial to the fate of soil carbon, but how it changes with warming remains unknown. Here, in contrast to the often hypothesized negative temperature dependence of microbial CUE, we observed a positive microbial CUE-temperature relationship after a decade of modest ecosystem-level warming (+1.0° and +2.1°C) in subtropical China. The warmed microbial community shifted toward moreK-strategists, and the increased network stability drove metabolic thermal adjustments. These thermal adjustments resulted in homeostasis, such that warmed soils did not differ from unwarmed (+0°C) soils in microbial respiration and growth, weakening the initially stimulated carbon decomposition. These results suggest that warming-induced soil-plant system imbalance can recover to a prewarming steady state within a few seasons, mitigating predicted increases in soil carbon losses.
Large soil carbon loss under future warming in subtropical forests may not occur due to microbial thermal adjustments.
Soil represents the largest carbon reservoir in the terrestrial ecosystems (1), and even the tiny fraction of that respired annually by soil microbes amounts to an impressive 40 to 60 Pg of carbon transferred into the atmosphere (2). Climate warming is expected to further enhance soil microbial respiration, resulting in positive carbon-climate feedbacks (3) and perhaps declining soil carbon stocks. Unfortunately, despite decades of extensive experimental research informing increasingly complex Earth System Models (3–8), little progress has been made in accurately predicting carbon-climate feedbacks, and substantial uncertainty still remains. This uncertainty arises in several ways. First, in some warming studies, the initial increase in soil carbon loss has faded over time due to any one or a combination of thermal adjustments (Table 1) of microbial metabolic rate (e.g., microbial respiration returns to pretreatment levels after having been heightened during the first several years of warming) (9–12) and/or reduced carbon inputs available for respiration. Whether such thermal adjustments (that dampen system change) will persist over time remains unclear (13–16). Second, the attenuated soil carbon loss over time may also be attributed to some combination of decreased microbial activity caused by warming-induced resource limitations (e.g., soil drying and depletion of the bioavailable carbon pool,Table 1) (10,17). However, substantial controversy remains regarding which pathways—microbial thermal adjustments or resource limitation—predominate (9,12,13,18–20), an important issue because the two mechanisms can lead to contrasting outcomes for soil carbon sequestration and climate feedback (Table 1). Therefore, gaining insights into the thermal response mechanisms of microbial carbon metabolism under long-term warming is essential for improving carbon-climate predictions (3,21–25).
Microbial carbon use efficiency (CUE), defined as the proportion of substrate carbon used for microbial growth (26), is determined by a combination of microbial catabolic and anabolic pathways (22). Therefore, changes in microbial CUE can simultaneously affect the fate of soil organic carbon (SOC) decomposition and accumulation (25,27). Microbial CUE, as a model parameter, has been shown to have the capacity to enhance the predictive accuracy of soil carbon dynamics when incorporated into Earth System Models (7,11,22,25,28,29). However, because of limited research on the mechanisms driving microbial CUE, it is challenging to integrate the spatial or temporal dynamics of microbial CUE into soil carbon models (21). For instance, recent soil carbon models typically do not impose precise constraints on the mechanisms controlling microbial CUE and, in some cases, even treat it as a constant (28,30).
One of the primary knowledge gaps concerns the temperature dependence of microbial CUE (26,29,31), as warming experiments have observed decreases (31), increases (16,29), or no significant changes (26) in microbial CUE under warming. Several factors may contribute to the different outcomes. First, the temperature response of soil microbial CUE may exhibit temporal variability due to resource availability and/or microbial thermal adjustments after several years (10,13,17). Second, experimental designs that include or exclude changes in plant carbon inputs due to warming can yield divergent outcomes, because of their different rates of fresh carbon inputs into soils. Studies focusing solely on soil warming or laboratory incubations without additional carbon often result in substrate depletion (11,12,20), neglecting the potential replenishment from increased plant carbon input in realistic warming scenarios, which ultimately reduces microbial CUE. Conversely, ecosystem-level warming field experiments detecting enhanced plant carbon input or lab experiments with added labile carbon substrates (e.g., glucose) suggest that soil microbial CUE may not be constrained by carbon availability (26,32). Instead, microbial CUE appears to be driven by the thermal adjustment of microbial metabolism, potentially showing a positive or nonsignificant CUE-temperature relationship (26,29). Third, biogeographic variation in precipitation, substrate availability, microbial composition, and “intrinsic” metabolic temperature sensitivity across different ecosystems and climatic zones may lead to disparate outcomes (33,34). For example, a recent large-scale sampling study in forest ecosystems demonstrated that in regions with higher annual mean temperatures, the thermal adjustment of microbial metabolism exhibited a more dominant role in the thermal response of microbial metabolism (34). Therefore, the temperature-dependent response of microbial CUE likely depends on the relative importance of microbial thermal adjustment and resource availability, which can vary across ecosystems, climate conditions, and warming duration (16,21). More long-term, multilocation experiments are needed to validate these patterns.
Another knowledge gap is that the intrinsic driving mechanisms of microbial thermal metabolic adjustments (e.g., CUE) at the community level remain elusive (21), despite several attempts conducted thus far (16,18,19,23,27,35,36). Soil microbial diversity is generally considered positively correlated with soil functions (21,36,37). Correspondingly, a recent study demonstrated a positive relationship between microbial diversity and microbial CUE (36). However, other studies have suggested that, owing to the functional redundancy of microbial communities (38), microbially driven carbon processes may not be significantly affected by changes in microbial diversity (39). Instead, it is the composition of the soil microbial community, rather than diversity, that may drive microbial metabolic thermal adjustments (13,18). For instance, a recent experiment shows that an increase inK-strategist microbes (slow growth but high resource use efficiency) contributes to the reduced thermal sensitivity of microbial respiration under long-term warming (13). In the real world, both soil microbial diversity and community composition may change over time in response to warming (13,33), potentially driving microbial metabolic thermal adjustments in microbial metabolism, though their relative importance remains uncertain. In addition, a recent microbial manipulation experiment demonstrated that the temperature response of community-level microbial respiration is significantly influenced by species interactions (24,35). Several previous studies suggest that microbial co-occurrence networks—representing species interactions and associations within microbial communities—become more stable and complex under long-term warming (40,41), which may in turn affect microbial metabolism (35,42), yet this potential mechanism remains largely unexplored.
Tropical and subtropical forests exert a major impact on global climate because their soils contain over a third of global soil carbon and contribute a major portion of soil-atmosphere carbon exchange (24,43). However, unlike the many warming experiments conducted previously in temperate zones (9,10,26), warming experiments in tropical and subtropical forests have only recently been initiated (32,33,43–45). We still know little about whether there is lower than expected stimulation of microbially driven carbon decomposition as found in other ecosystems (9,17,46). Furthermore, these field experiments in tropical and subtropical forests have predominantly focused on soil-only warming, failing to capture warming-induced changes in plant growth, which could be important given that these systems have high carbon productivity (47,48). These limitations make tropical forests a primary source of uncertainty in global soil carbon predictions (5).
Herein, based on an ecosystem-level field warming experiment (+0°, +1.0°, and +2.1°C, respectively; figs. S1 and S2) established in 2012 in a subtropical forest (32,40,49,50), we investigated the temperature response of microbial CUE and its underlying mechanisms after 10 to 11 years of warming. Our previous observations showed increased soil respiration and decreased microbial biomass during initial warming (32,51), indicating a negative thermal response of microbial CUE. However, after 6 years of warming, the enhanced soil respiration was suppressed (32), yet whether the temperature response of microbial CUE shifted remains unclear. On the one hand, the soil moisture synchronously decreased after 6 years (32), which may directly inhibit microbial activity or indirectly affect it by reducing substrate mobility and availability, still reflecting a negative temperature-microbial CUE relationship (Table 1). On the other hand, microbial community composition also changed after 6 years (40), resulting in microbial metabolic thermal adjustment and a positive temperature-microbial CUE relationship (Table 1). We therefore hypothesized that after a decade of warming, the temperature-CUE relationship may shift to positive (hypothesis 1), driven mainly by microbial thermal adjustments rather than resource limitation (hypothesis 2), given abundant precipitation of this area (fig. S3) and the compensation of warming-enhanced plant C input.
Considering that microbial metabolism is influenced by seasonal variation, we conducted cross-seasonal sampling during the 10th to 11th years of warming treatment. Microbial biomass carbon (MBC), CUE, growth rate, and specific growth rate were slightly higher under warming than in the unwarmed treatment across both wet and dry seasons, but the differences were not significant (P> 0.05;Fig. 1, A, D and F). The microbial respiration and specific respiration rate under warming treatments were slightly lower than those in the unwarmed treatment, but the differences were not significant (P> 0.05;Fig. 1, C and D). Similarly, the results based on alignment against the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes database showed that soil microbe–related carbon metabolism functions, such as metabolic function (level 1, fig. The results based on Fungal Functional Guild functional annotation also showed that there was increased abundance in soil saprotrophs and endophyte litter/soil saprotrophs after 10 to 11 years of warming (fig. Litterfall production is widely recognized as a proxy for plant productivity (52); therefore, we used litterfall during the sampling seasons to indicate plant carbon input. Litterfall increased significantly under warming (P< 0.05; fig. S6A). SOC did not differ among different treatments (P> 0.05; fig. S6B). Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) increased under +1.0°C of warming (P< 0.05; fig. S6C) but did not change under 2.1°C of warming. These results suggested that increased warming-enhanced plant carbon inputs can offset potential SOC losses in terms of total soil carbon stocks. Warming effects on soil moisture depended on both season and magnitude: +1.0°C had no significant impact during the wet season, whereas +2.1°C consistently reduced soil moisture in the dry season (P< 0.05; fig. S7A).
After 10 to 11 years of warming, the Shannon-Wiener index [amplicon sequence variant (ASV) level] for fungi and bacteria showed no significant differences between the unwarmed and warming treatments, but their relative abundance (phylum level) and composition (ASV level) changed significantly (figs. S7 and S8). The relative abundance of Proteobacteria, Planctomycetota, Verrucomicrobiota, RCP2-54, Myxococcota, and Firmicutes decreased under long-term warming, while Chloroflexi, Actinobacteriota, GAL15, and Gemmatimonadota increased (fig. S7A). Among fungi, there was an enrichment in Chytridiomycota and Calcarisporiellomycota under warming treatment, whereas Mortierellomycota, Rozellomycota, and Mucoromycota decreased (fig. S7B). The results of nonmetric multidimensional scaling further showed that the composition of bacteria and fungi differed under long-term warming across both wet and dry seasons (fig. S9); the unwarmed and +2.1°C treatments had very little overlap at any time; however, the +1.0°C treatment was not consistently near or far from the other treatments across time.
The topological attributes of microbial networks, including the number of nodes (n), number of links (L), average degree (averageK), average clustering coefficient (average CC), average path distance, maximal betweenness, centralization of betweenness, transitivity, density, and connectivity (Con) increased with warming (table S1 andFig. 3A), suggesting more complex microbial networks. The networks subjected to +1.0° and +2.1°C warming treatment showed significantly higher robustness (P< 0.05;Fig. 4A) and lower vulnerability (Fig. 4B) compared to the unwarmed treatment in both wet and dry seasons, indicating an increase in microbial network stability. Regression analysis showed that the robustness and vulnerability of networks were significantly correlated with the network topological attributes, includingn,L, averageK, average CC, transitivity, density, Con, and maximal betweenness (Fig. 4E), suggesting that the increased complexity of microbial networks after 10 to 11 years of warming enhanced the stability of microbial community.

We constructed structural equation models (SEMs) to examine how resource availability (soil moisture and soil carbon availability) and microbial traits (alpha diversity and network stability) drive microbial metabolic responses under long-term warming (Fig. 5and figs. S13 and S14). The SEMs revealed that microbial carbon metabolism was primarily regulated by microbial network stability (Fig. 5and fig. S13). Microbial CUE, specific growth rate, and specific respiration rate were positively associated with the stability of microbial networks (standardized coefficient:r= 0.47, 0.55, and 0.28, respectively;Fig. 5and fig. S13). In contrast, resource availability (soil moisture and carbon availability) and microbial alpha diversity did not have significant direct effects on microbial CUE, specific growth rate, or specific respiration (Fig. 5and fig. S12). Instead, soil moisture significantly regulated microbial CUE, specific growth rate, and specific respiration rate by indirectly influencing the stability of the microbial network (standardized coefficient:r= 0.48, 0.34, and 0.22, respectively;Fig. 5and fig. S12). Plant carbon input (indicated by litterfall) positively affected soil carbon availability (standardized coefficient:r= 0.71 and 0.68, respectively; fig. S14), which indirectly regulated microbial CUE and growth rate through influencing the microbial network stability.

Our limited understanding of how microbe-driven soil carbon dynamics in tropical and subtropical forests respond and adjust to long-term warming greatly hinders an accurate prediction of future carbon-climate feedback and terrestrial soil carbon sequestration potential (5,43,47,48). Here, we provide insights into the consequences of long-term ecosystem-level warming on microbial metabolism in subtropical forests. After a decade of warming, we found no significant differences in MBC, respiration, growth, and CUE between warming and unwarmed treatments. However, regression analysis revealed a positive relationship between microbial CUE and soil temperature (in support of hypothesis 1;Fig. 2). These findings are not mutually exclusive, as the effect size detected through regression may be too small to be captured by variance analysis. This interpretation is further supported by the results of the linear mixed model (table S2), which showed positive but nonsignificant effects of warming on microbial CUE. Nevertheless, these results suggest that, in contrast to the negative temperature response of microbial CUE observed during initial warming of this experiment (32,51), the microbial community in this subtropical forest soil has developed one or more metabolic or community thermal adjustments under long-term warming. This microbial thermal adjustment also explains our earlier observation of reduced temperature sensitivity of soil respiration and decreased carbon loss after 5 to 6 years of warming, along with the positive effect of stimulated plant carbon input that allows SOC to recover to levels similar to the control (32). However, unlike decreases in microbial biomass, respiration, and CUE due to the carbon depletion observed in soil-only warming experiment (10,20), we found that the microbial metabolic thermal adjustment in this subtropical forest was primarily driven by the more stable microbial network (in support of hypothesis 2;Fig. 5and fig. S14). Together, our results suggest that large carbon losses recently observed in the early years of several experimental warming experiments in subtropical forests due to promoted microbial respiration (44,51) may diminish after several additional years. In addition, if the warming does not exceed the optimal temperature for plant growth, it can potentially promote the soil carbon sequestration of the subtropical forest through increased plant growth (21,22,25).
Changes in microbial carbon metabolism response to warming over time have been attributed in some previous researches, in part, to continually depleting substrates (10,12,16,20). However, in this study, no significant correlation was observed between the soil carbon availability (SOC and DOC) and microbial growth, respiration, and CUE (fig. S6), which contrasts with results from the soil-warming experiments (10,20). A plausible explanation is the difference in the experimental effects of simulated warming. On the one hand, compared to the higher warming amplitude (+5°C) of other studies, such as in Harvard Forest (10,20), the lower warming amplitude (maximum amplitude of +2.1°C) may have a lower stimulation to microbial activity and substrate consumption rates. On the other hand, unlike soil-only warming experiments that do not capture the impacts of changes in aboveground plant carbon input driven by aboveground warming, our ecosystem-level warming experiment observed a significant increase in plant productivity (32), partially offsetting substrate consumption and preventing microbial activity from being limited by substrate availability (Fig. 5and figs. S6 and S14). However, it should be noted that the positive response of aboveground plant carbon to warming (<2.1°C) observed in our experiment may not hold in lowland or core tropical and subtropical regions, where temperatures are already higher than in the montane subtropical forests studied here. In addition, although the positive response of sapling growth to warming observed in this study was also seen in other mature tree species with comparable temperatures (53), some drought-sensitive mature tree species may be adversely affected by increased water limitations (54). Increased temperature in these regions might reduce plant growth and carbon input, potentially failing to offset the depletion of the labile carbon pool. Therefore, the extent to which warming-induced substrate depletion affects microbial carbon metabolism needs further verification across additional tropical and subtropical forests.
According to the theory of complementarity effect (37), higher microbial diversity may positively influence microbial growth and/or CUE through niche differentiation and/or cross-feeding on resources among coexisting species (36). However, in this study, microbial diversity did not exert a significant regulatory influence on microbial metabolism (Fig. 5and figs. S10 and S14). This different result may be attributed to the following two reasons. First, under natural conditions, only a small portion (1 to 20%) of soil microbes are metabolically active, and soil microbial metabolism mainly originates from these active components rather than the entire microbial community (21,23). Correspondingly, a recent study found that the increased community growth of bacteria under warming was primarily associated with a greater number of active bacterial taxa (23). Second, microbial community composition may play a more dominant role when differences in diversity covary with differences in species identities (13). Therefore, there is a possibility that predicted biodiversity-ecosystem functioning relations may not be observed if identity drives the systems in the opposite direction as diversity (39). For instance, a recent study found that microbial communities dominated byK-strategists exhibit stronger thermal adaptation of microbial respiration than those dominated byr-strategists (fast growth but low resource use efficiency) (18). Similarly, we observed that after 10 to 11 years of warming, the microbial phyla that increased were mostlyK-strategists (Chloroflexi, Actinobacteriota, GAL15, and Gemmatimonadota for bacteria; Calcarisporiellomycota for fungi), whereas those that decreased were mostlyr-strategists (Proteobacteria and Firmicutes for bacteria; Mortierellomycota, Rozellomycota, and Mucoromycota for fungi) (fig. S8). However, the insignificant relationship of microbial diversity and CUE in this study does not imply that conserving soil microbes is unimportant for maintaining current carbon pools and process rates. Although soil harbors thousands to millions of species, only a limited number contribute to specific processes (55,56). The extinction of a few species might not affect general processes significantly, but it could greatly affect specialized processes (24,39,56), such as the decomposition of specific carbon sources like lignin and cellulose (56), thereby influencing overall carbon dynamics.
Consistent with a recent observation in a grassland (41), we observed that after a decade of warming treatment, the microbial network under warmed plots exhibited greater complexity and increased stability compared to the unwarmed treatment (Fig. 3). Although there is continuing debate about whether microbial network correlations accurately represent species interactions (57–59), they may indicate potential cooperation (e.g., cross-feeding on metabolic by-products) or competition (e.g., competition for limiting abiotic resources) through positive and negative associations. Therefore, more complex microbial networks may potentially optimize resource utilization through direct or indirect interactions among microorganisms (35). Correspondingly, a recent microbial interaction manipulation experiment demonstrated that interactions (synergistic or antagonistic) among microbes can significantly influence the temperature sensitivity of community-level microbial metabolism (35). In this study, we found that the topological attributes and stability of microbial networks were significantly positively correlated with microbial growth, respiration, and CUE (Figs. 3and4). In addition, SEM results also showed that more stable microbial networks under long-term warming primarily drove the recovery of microbial growth, respiration, and CUE (Fig. 5and fig. S14). These results indicate that the more stable microbial networks could drive the microbial metabolic thermal adjustment of the subtropical forest under long-term warming, supporting the crucial role of microbial community and structure in regulating microbial carbon metabolism (9,11,13,16,18).
However, it should be noted that the stability and structure of microbial networks are also controlled by soil moisture (42,58), especially under low moisture conditions (17,42), thus affecting the microbial carbon metabolisms. This was also confirmed in our cross-seasonal sampling. We found that, compared to the wet season, the dry season exhibited lower microbial network stability (Fig. 3), with suppressed microbial growth and respiration (Fig. 1). In addition, we found that soil moisture during the dry season was significantly positively correlated with microbial growth and respiration, and significantly negatively correlated with microbial network vulnerability (fig. S7), which may be related to the inhibition of substrate transport and microbial activity under low moisture conditions (42). The results of SEMs further showed that soil moisture could indirectly regulate microbial carbon metabolisms by influencing microbial network stability (Fig. 5and fig. S14). Subtropical forests generally receive abundant rainfall and are typically considered water unlimited. However, increasing evidence suggests that future climate warming may lead to more frequent and severe seasonal droughts in tropical and subtropical forests (60). Thus, warming-induced drought, especially during the dry season, may potentially weaken or even disrupt the microbial metabolic thermal adjustment capacity in these tropical and subtropical forests. Therefore, warming experiments across a broader range of tropical and subtropical forests with different moisture conditions are needed to comprehensively assess the thermal adjustment capacity of soil microbes in these ecosystems under future warming.
In addition, the plasticity of microbial traits (e.g., structure and function of microbial biofilms) at the individual level could enable the microbe to acclimate to warming, as observed in a recent single-species culture experiment (19,61). Therefore, thermal acclimation at the individual microbial level may have also contributed to the thermal adjustment of microbial metabolism at the community-level observed in this study. However, because of differences in metabolic or trait plasticity among species, as well as challenges in quantification methods, its role in community-level thermal adjustment under long-term warming was not measured in this study. Future research aimed at quantifying the contribution of microbial thermal acclimation to community-level thermal adjustment may provide a better understanding of microbially driven carbon dynamics in response to warming.
Because this experiment is a mesocosm ecosystem translocation experiment rather than an in situ warming experiment conducted in a natural forest, it inherently shares limitations similar to those of other translocation experiments (33). First, the translocation design cannot test the response of lowland subtropical forest to further warming (33,48), and the extent of warming is limited. Second, translocation design cannot fully simulate all environmental conditions of natural forests, such as vegetation composition, soil structure, and microclimate. However, the translocation approach tested the temperature responses of soil carbon dynamics under relatively consistent soil, plant, and microbial conditions, thereby overcoming potential interference caused by heterogeneity in in situ forest soils. Notwithstanding these limitations, our results clearly demonstrate the thermal adjustment potential of subtropical forest soil microbes under long-term warming and reveal the potentially important regulatory role of microbial interactions in these thermal adjustments, particularly under conditions of relatively low warming magnitude that promote plant carbon input.
In conclusion, our study provides empirical evidence of the response and adjustment of soil microbial carbon metabolism to long-term (>10 years) ecosystem warming in a subtropical forest. We found that interactions among microbes increased after 10 years of warming, leading to a more stable microbial network, which drove the microbial metabolic thermal adjustment to suppress the stimulated loss of soil carbon. This finding can help enhance the accuracy of subtropical forest soil carbon predictions and improve soil carbon management in these subtropical forests, as follows. First, although microbial CUE has been increasingly included in many models in recent years (6), it is typically limited to a fixed value or specific range, rather than being constrained by the physiological and ecological processes of microorganisms. Our results suggest that the negative thermal response of microbial CUE in the early stages of warming (51) might change after several years due to microbial thermal metabolic adjustments (Fig. 2). Therefore, Earth System Models should include information of microbial interactions to constrain the dynamic temporal changes in the temperature-dependent response of microbial CUE. Second, studies have shown that microbial inoculants, widely used in agriculture and ecological restoration, can significantly enhance microbial network stability (62). Therefore, if the observed result that microbial community stability drives microbial metabolic thermal adjustment applies to other tropical and subtropical forests, developing microbial inoculants tailored for forest soils and applying them in practices such as plantation fertilization, afforestation, and forest restoration could help mitigate the adverse effects of soil carbon-climate feedback caused by warming. However, it should be noted that the warming magnitude in this study was relatively modest, yet the observed microbial and soil responses highlight the sensitivity of these ecosystems to oncoming climate change. Under higher warming magnitudes, the thermal adjustment capacity of microorganisms in these ecosystems may be inhibited or even disrupted, leading to the potential persistence of the previously observed substantial carbon losses (24,33,43). Therefore, although our study suggests that any initial plant-soil system imbalance and substantial carbon release induced by moderate warming may return to a quasi-steady state after several seasons, this recovery capacity could decline or even be lost under greater warming magnitudes, which warrants further investigation.
The warming experiment in this study was constructed at the Dinghushan National Field Forest Ecosystem Research Station (23°09′25″N, 112°30′40″E) in Guangdong Province, China, located at the southern edge of the subtropical climate zone. Consequently, the climate in this area is a typical subtropical monsoon climate. The average annual temperature is approximately 21.4°C at the elevation of 600 m, with the hottest month (June) averaging 27.3°C and the coldest month (January) averaging 13.2°C. The annual precipitation is about 1800 mm, with around 80% occurring from April to September. The zonal vegetation type in this area is monsoon evergreen broad-leaf forest. At an elevation of 600 m, the dominant forest type is mountain evergreen broad-leaf forest, with dominant tree species such asMachilus breviflora,Syzygium rehderianum,Schima superba,Itea chinensis,Myrsine seguinii, andArdisia lindleyana. The soil depth of mountain evergreen broadleaf forest averages around 70 to 80 cm, and the soil type is classified as Ultisols.
The warming experiment in this study was established in May 2012, using the principle that temperature rises as elevation decreases to achieve passive warming of simulated montane evergreen broad-leaved forest mesocosms (fig. S1). The mesocosms measured 3 m in length, 3 m in width, and 0.8 m in depth, where the plants and soils were all sourced from the same natural montane evergreen broad-leaved forest at an altitude of 600 m. The plants of each mesocosm included 36 individuals: six 1-year-old seedlings (~4 mm base diameter and ~30 cm height when planted) for each tree species (six dominant tree species:M. breviflora,S. rehderianum,S. superba,I. chinensis,M. seguinii, andA. lindleyana). During years 10 to 11, ~70% of these initial plantings survived and their average height was 3.05 ± 1.31 m. The soil was divided into three layers (0 to 20, 20 to 40, and 40 to 70 cm) to be collected from the same natural forest, homogenized separately, and transferred to the mesocosms. Most soil properties in the mid-sized mesocosms did not differ significantly from those in the natural forest at the time of establishment (table S3). We selected three altitudes corresponding to three temperature treatments: one unwarmed treatment (600 m) and two different levels of warming (300 and 30 m). Each treatment consists of three replicates of mesocosms, spaced 3 m apart.
To ensure that the mesocosms under the three treatments have similar habitat conditions except for temperature, the locations selected for translocating the mesocosms at each altitude have similar slopes and aspects. All sites should be open areas to ensure that each region receives ample sunlight and rainfall without being shaded by surrounding vegetation. Our multiyear monitoring data indicated that precipitation at the three sites is similar (fig. S3). The relative humidity at the 300- and 30-m sites was slightly lower than at the 600-m site, but the difference was not significant, possibly due to the close proximity of the three sites (63), with horizontal distances of 1.67 and 3.11 km from the +0°C site to the +1.0° and +2.1°C sites, respectively. These observations suggested that the water inputs were largely consistent across the tree treatments, so the observed reduction in soil moisture at altitudes of 300 and 30 m (figs. S3 and S7) was primarily driven by increased evapotranspiration under warmer conditions. In addition, to prevent interference from plant roots, soil microorganisms, and soil leachates from outside the mesocosms, the surrounding and bottom of the underground part of each mesocosm are sealed with cement and impermeable bricks. In addition, to prevent waterlogging and to collect surface and underground water from each mesocosm, drainage holes are provided at the top and bottom of the underground section. Although mesocosms differ in plant composition and soil structure from natural forests, all treatments used the same construction methods. Therefore, the differences between the unwarmed treatment and the warmed treatments are primarily attributed to temperature differences from translocation of the mesocosms (40).
Air temperature and relative humidity (HMP155A, Vaisala, Helsinki, Finland), precipitation (TB4MM, Campbell Scientific, Logan, USA), soil temperature (TB4MM, Campbell Scientific, Logan, USA), soil moisture (TB4MM, Campbell Scientific, Logan, USA), and other environmental factors were monitored and recorded (TB4MM, Campbell Scientific, Logan, USA) hourly in each mesoscale ecosystem. Our continuous observations over 11 years indicate that the experiment achieved temperature manipulation of both air (+0°, +0.99°, and +1.88°C) and the entire soil profile (+0°, +1.0°, and + 2.1°C) in the three treatments (figs. S1 and S2). More details about this warming experiment design have been described by previous studies (32,40,49,50).
Given that seasonal variation in moisture may affect microbial responses to warming, we conducted four soil sampling surveys during the 10th and 11th years of warming (2022 to 2023). These surveys were timed to capture varying moisture conditions: two during dry seasons (March 2022 and December 2022) and two during wet seasons (June 2022 and May 2023). Soils at 0-10 cm depth were collected using a soil auger (3.5 cm inner diameter) from each mesocosm. For each survey, we collected six replicate samples per treatment, totaling 72 soil samples across all surveys. All soil samples were sieved (2 mm) and separated into two parts. One part was stored at 4°C for DOC, MBC, and CUE analyses, and another part was stored at −20°C for microbial community analyses.
In each mesocosm, four litterfall traps (0.2 m by 0.2 m) were installed 0.3 m above the soil surface. Litterfall was collected monthly from these traps and oven dried at 65°C to constant weight. The total litterfall during the dry season (October to March of the following year) and wet season (April to September) was used as a proxy for plant growth in the respective seasons. The SOC content was measured by the K2Cr2O7oxidation method (64). DOC was determined by extracting fresh soil following the method of Jones and Willett (65) and measuring the organic carbon concentration in the extract using a total organic carbon (TOC) analyzer (Shimadzu Corp., Kyoto, Japan).
MBC was determined using the chloroform fumigation-extraction method. Briefly, two portions of 10 g of field-fresh soil sieved through a 2-mm mesh were weighed. One soil sample was placed in a crucible and set inside a desiccator, which contained a shallow dish with 30 ml of alcohol-free chloroform. The other (not fumigated) portion was taken in a crucible and placed in a separate desiccator without chloroform. The desiccators were covered and kept in dark at room temperature for 24 hours. After the fumigant was removed, fumigated soils were extracted with 0.5 M K2SO4. The nonfumigated soils were also extracted with 0.5 M K2SO4. Microbial networks and the topological attributes were constructed with the Molecular Ecological Network Analysis Pipeline (http://ieg4.rccc.ou.edu/mena/), using default settings (57). Briefly, the fungal and bacterial ASV abundance data from six replicate samples of the same treatment in a single sampling are combined. To ensure the reliability of the correlation calculations, ASVs that appear in less than 50% of the six replicate samples were removed. Then, Pearson correlation coefficient was calculated using the log-transformed abundances of the filtered ASVs. Last, the cutoff threshold of the correlation matrix (R> 0.97 in this study) was calculated with the random matrix theory–based approach for the microbial network construction (78). Moreover, in the network, nodes represent ASVs, and edges represent the Pearson correlation between ASVs. We also identified keystone nodes by calculating within-module connectivity (Zi) and among-module connectivity (Pi) from the microbial co-occurrence network, considering nodes with Zi > 2.5 and Pi > 0.62 as keystones (table S4). All the microbial networks were visualized using Gephi (version 0.9.2).
In this study, the robustness and vulnerability of microbial networks were calculated to characterize the microbial network stability. The robustness of microbial networks refers to the proportion of remaining ASVs in the network after random or targeted removal of nodes (ASV level) (46). The vulnerability of microbial networks refers to the speed at which ecological events and their biological consequences spread through part or the entirety of the network, characterized by the maximum vulnerability of the nodes within the network (41,55).

Before analysis, the normality and homogeneity of variances for all variables were tested using the Shapiro-Wilk test and the Levene test, respectively. The significance of all statistical tests was determined at the α = 0.05 level. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to detect differences in biotic and abiotic variables among different warming treatments in a single sampling survey, including litterfall, microbial growth rate, microbial respiration rate, CUE, MBC, SOC, DOC, soil moisture, and the Shannon-Wiener index. Nonmetric multidimensional scaling and permutational multivariate ANOVA, based on Bray-Curtis distance, were used to test the differences in microbial communities among different treatments using the R package “vegan” (79). A linear regression model was performed to investigate the relationship of MBC, specific growth rate, microbial specific respiration rate, CUE, and Shannon-Wiener index with litterfall, microbial network topological attributes, soil microbial network stability (robustness and vulnerability), SOC, DOC, soil moisture, and soil temperature using the R package “lme4.”
On the basis of insights from current researches and available data of this study, a conceptual SEM (fig. S13) was constructed to test the hypothesized mechanisms of how warming regulates microbial carbon metabolism (microbial CUE, specific growth rate, and microbial specific respiration rate) through resource availability and microbial traits. The SEMs included warming, plant carbon input (represented by litterfall), soil moisture, soil carbon availability (represented by litterfall SOC and DOC), microbial diversity, and microbial network stability. The final SEMs were selected based on the following criteria: chi-square tests (P≥ 0.05) with maximum likelihood estimation, high goodness-of-fit index (GFI > 0.85), and low Akaike information criterion value and root mean square error of approximation (<0.05). The SEMs were performed by SPSS Amos software (Version 24), and all data were standardized usingz-scores before analysis.
