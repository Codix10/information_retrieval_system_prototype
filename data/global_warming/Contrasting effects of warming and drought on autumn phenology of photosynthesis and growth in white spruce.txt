Evergreen conifers rely on photoperiod and temperature cues to regulate autumn phenology. Climate change delays autumn cooling, whereas photoperiod remains unaffected, and this mismatch might have consequences for the timing of autumn phenology. Climatic stresses, such as drought and heat during summer, might also impact the timing of phenological events, including photosynthetic downregulation and growth cessation. We investigated the single and combined impacts of summer drought and autumn warming on photosynthetic downregulation and growth cessation in a northern and southern family of white spruce (Picea glauca). In a temperature free-air controlled experiment combined with rainout structures, we exposed white spruce seedlings to ambient temperature and rainfall, summer drought, autumn warming, or a combination of summer drought followed by autumn warming. Warming delayed the downregulation of photosynthesis in both families compared to the control seedlings, with the southern family showing increased photosynthetic activity compared to the northern family. Despite extended photosynthetic activity, secondary growth cessation was not delayed by warming and did not vary between families. Warming affected latewood xylem development, an important component of secondary growth, by increasing and decreasing lumen area in the northern family and southern family, respectively, indicating the northern family may have been more cold-limited under past selective pressures. Summer drought had minimal impacts on photosynthetic downregulation and growth cessation in either family. We conclude that warming-induced extension of photosynthetic activity in autumn may not translate into increased growth and carbon sequestration due to strong photoperiodic and genetic constraints on growth cessation in white spruce.
Warming delays the autumn downregulation of photosynthesis in white spruce; however, local adaptation to photoperiod constrains the timing of growth cessation, prevailing over warming and drought.
Global surface temperature is projected to increase up to 3.3 to 5.7 °C by 2100 under the SSP5-8.5 emission scenario, with the most severe warming in northern latitudes (IPCC 2021). This substantial rise in temperature will impact boreal forests (Gauthier et al. 2015). Elevated temperature has already extended the growing season by ∼2.5 days per decade in boreal regions by delaying autumn downregulation of photosynthesis (Euskirchen et al. 2006,2007;Beck et al. 2011;Danielewska et al. 2015;Richardson et al. 2018). However, these benefits may be negated by drought, as growth limitations have been observed in areas of low water availability (Peng et al. 2011;D’Orangeville et al. 2016;Sulla-Menashe et al. 2018;Luo et al. 2019). Increased atmospheric vapor pressure deficit has led to higher incidences of drought, plant water stress, and forest mortality on a global scale over the last several decades (Treydte et al. 2024). Consequently, drought-induced tree mortality has caused global forest dieback (Allen et al. 2010), including in Canada's boreal forest with a ∼4.7% annual increase in mortality rates of four common tree species: quaking aspen (Populus tremuloides), jack pine (Pinus banksiana), black spruce (Picea mariana), and white spruce (Picea glauca;Peng et al. 2011).
In conifers, cold acclimation involves physiological and molecular processes that allow plants to cope with chilling and freezing stresses, including acquiring cold hardiness, accumulating soluble carbohydrates, downregulating photosynthesis, and inducing growth cessation (Öquist and Huner 2003;El Kayal et al. 2011;Chang et al. 2020). Growth cessation, controlled by photoperiod, temperature, and genetic makeup, refers to the termination of shoot elongation and secondary growth (Chang et al. 2020). During secondary growth, the vascular cambium undergoes periclinal division to produce new secondary phloem and xylem cells, leading to wood formation and xylem differentiation (Mellerowicz et al. 2001). The secondary xylem produced during secondary growth transitions from wide, thin-walled earlywood cells to narrow, thick-walled latewood cells over the growing season before growth cessation in the fall (Cuny et al. 2014). The transition from earlywood to latewood is critical due to the differing structural and functional properties that these two wood types provide. Earlywood is designed primarily for water transport whereas latewood enhances mechanical support and water storage (Domec and Gartner 2002). Conifer species that prioritize hydraulic safety (i.e. resistance to cavitation) have thicker tracheid walls relative to lumen area (LA) (Bouche et al. 2014). On the other hand, physiological measures of hydraulic efficiency (i.e. hydraulic conductivity,Ks) have been positively correlated with increased vessel size (i.e. hydraulic diameter,Dh), LA, and growth rates in cottonwood (Poplar;Hajek et al. 2014). Structural differences in xylem anatomy also offer enhanced resilience to climatic stress. For example, xylem anatomical traits have been observed to be more sensitive to environmental conditions during the growing season in latewood compared to earlywood cells, especially those related to structural integrity and hydraulic safety (Fonti et al. 2013;Gricar et al. 2015;Pampuch et al. 2021). Before cell division and expansion slow under low temperatures in late autumn, photosynthates are redirected from sink tissues to carbohydrate stores (Oleksyn et al. 2000;El Kayal et al. 2011;Palacio et al. 2014). Growth cessation reduces energetic and metabolic sink capacity in overwintering plants and, as a result, can inhibit photosynthetic activity.
As photosynthesis is downregulated in autumn, there is a need for effective photoprotective mechanisms to manage the absorbed light energy that continues to be harvested by chlorophyll (Chl), albeit at diminished rates of photochemistry (Ensminger et al. 2012). Needles in evergreen conifers retain Chl with a relative increase in photoprotective carotenoids, such as lutein and xanthophyll cycle pigments (Porcar-Castell 2011;Demmig-Adams et al. 2012;Chang et al. 2016;Fréchette et al. 2016). Xanthophyll cycle pigments drive nonphotochemical quenching (NPQ), a photoprotective mechanism to dissipate excess light as heat. NPQ depends on the de-epoxidation of light-harvesting pigment violaxanthin into antheraxanthin and zeaxanthin (Zea). During the growing season, the rapid and reversible interconversion of the three xanthophyll pigments in response to short-term stress is referred to as dynamic NPQ. This is contrasted by the so-called sustained NPQ that can be observed during winter, together with an accumulation of Zea. Changes in Chl and carotenoid concentrations lead to changes in leaf optical properties that can be observed through measurements of leaf spectral reflectance and can be used to estimate vegetation indices such as the photochemical reflectance index (PRI) and the chlorophyll carotenoid index (CCI) (Junker and Ensminger 2016). PRI and CCI are effective proxies for tracking photosynthetic phenology and CO2assimilation in evergreen conifers (Wong et al. 2019;Fréchette et al. 2020).
Low temperature cues necessary to signal the end of the growing season may be delayed under climate change while photoperiod signals remain unchanged (Busch et al. 2007;Busch et al. 2008). There are uncertainties about the consequences of this mismatch between photoperiod and warmer autumn temperatures as many conifer species require the integration of both photoperiod and temperature to initiate cold acclimation, including white pine (Pinus strobus;Chang et al. 2016;Fréchette et al. 2016), white spruce (Silim and Lavender 1994;Galindo González et al. 2012), and jack pine (Busch et al. 2007;Busch et al. 2008). The effects of autumn warming on cold acclimation have been studied in conifer species in both growth chamber and field experiments, with contrasting results. For example, jack pine seedlings exposed to +15/+13 °C (day/night) autumn warming and short days did not increase photosynthetic carbon uptake in a growth chamber experiment (Busch et al. 2007). In a temperature free-air-controlled enhancement (T-FACE) field experiment, white pine seedlings subjected to mild +1.5/+3 °C (day/night) autumn warming did not delay photosynthetic downregulation. Conversely, white spruce seedlings extended their photosynthetic activity under warming of +5 to +14 °C in growth chamber experiments (Hamilton et al. 2016;Stinziano and Way 2017). The two studies on white spruce seedlings also demonstrated that when photoperiod and low temperature signals are conflicting under climate warming, growth cessation can become decoupled from photosynthetic downregulation. Literature to date suggests the sensitivity of photosynthetic downregulation to warming varies by species but also by the magnitude of warming applied.
Similar to the timing of photosynthetic downregulation, growth cessation in boreal conifers will likely be impacted by warming in addition to drought. Drought can have long-lasting effects on secondary growth in trees, which can manifest through shorter growth duration caused by earlier growth cessation or later growth onset in subsequent years (Miller et al. 2017;Gazol et al. 2020). Models predict that climate, species, and local adaptation will influence the timing of secondary growth cessation in future forests (Ford et al. 2017;Mu et al. 2023). However, experimental data is limited. For example,Dox et al. (2021)observed that summer drought advanced secondary growth cessation in oak (Quercus) by 3 wk, while beech (Fagus) remained unaffected. Nevertheless, it remains uncertain how summer drought will affect the timing of secondary growth cessation in conifer species.
Variation in autumn phenology within temperate and boreal tree species arises due to local adaptation to photoperiod. Local adaptation emerges from environmental selective pressures, as many boreal conifers span large latitudinal and elevational gradients, resulting in genotypes that fit with their local environment (Aitken et al. 2008). Adaptive variation affects the timing of cold acclimation and the magnitude of cold hardiness acquired, impacting a conifer's ability to endure subfreezing temperatures. Thus, adaptive variation may impact the timing and phases of photosynthetic activity throughout the year, (i.e. photosynthetic phenology), as well as the overall survival of boreal conifers in future climates. Studies on Sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis;Holliday et al. 2008), Douglas-fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii;Noordermeer et al. 2021), and white pine (Fréchette et al. 2020) show intraspecific differences at the population level in cold acclimation patterns. Generally, northern provenances acquire cold hardiness more quickly and strongly than southern provenances under similar conditions (Holliday et al. 2008;Cooke et al. 2012;Fréchette et al. 2020). While adaptive variation in response to summer heat and drought stress in conifers has been documented at a population level (Bigras 2000;Bigras 2005;Depardieu et al. 2020), research is limited on how intraspecific variation contributes to the effect of drought on the timing of growth cessation.
Trees are facing more frequent summer droughts and warmer autumns due to climate change. Climate models predict an increased frequency and intensity of summer droughts as global temperatures rise (Spinoni et al. 2018;Senf et al. 2020). Autumn temperatures have already risen by ∼0.8 °C in northern latitudes over the past two decades (Piao et al. 2008). Large-scale observations show that summer droughts generally advance the end of the growing season (Wang et al. 2024), with drought-induced growth cessation observed between June to August in deciduous and evergreen conifers (Swidrak et al. 2013). In contrast, autumn warming can delay the timing of photosynthetic downregulation (Fréchette et al. 2020) and can either advance or delay the timing of growth cessation (Zohner et al. 2021).
The interaction between drought and warming is critical, yet their combined impact on autumn phenology, including photosynthetic downregulation and growth cessation in conifers is unknown. Furthermore, it remains uncertain whether intraspecific variation in conifers from different latitudinal origins provides selective advantages in exploiting warmer autumn temperatures and enduring summer drought stress under climate change. In order to address these uncertainties, we conducted a field experiment to test the following hypotheses: (i) summer drought advances autumn photosynthetic downregulation while autumn warming delays autumn photosynthetic downregulation; (ii) there are differences in the responses of photosynthesis and growth to drought and warming between families of white spruce; (iii) the phenology of photosynthesis is decoupled from the phenology of growth. For this purpose, seedlings derived from two selected white spruce families from southern and northern regions in Canada (seeFig. 1for details) were transplanted in a common garden experiment and subjected to ambient temperature and rainfall, summer drought, autumn warming, or a combination of summer drought followed by autumn warming.

Daily maximum air temperature ranged from 21.9 to 35.1 °C from July and August, dropping below 20 °C in the second week of September (Fig. 2A). The first night frost with below 0 °C recorded was on September 19 in control plots. Following a warm spell in early November, air temperature ranged between −15 °C and +10 °C until late December. The control plots maintained an average soil moisture content of 0.28 ± 0.02 m3/m3throughout the experiment (Fig. 2C). The rainout (RO) treatment, initiated on July 22, resulted in a 10% decrease in soil moisture compared to the control (Fig. 2C). The warming treatment, which began on August 31, increased the mean daily leaf temperature by an average of 3.6 °C, with maximum increases exceeding 4.5 °C in the warming plots relative to the control (Fig. 2B). Warming alone led to a 2.4% decrease in soil moisture compared to the control, while the combination of warming and RO produced the highest decrease of 13.2% compared to the control treatment.

The invisible phenology of photosynthesis in conifers was reflected by the transient decline of both PRI and CCI from August to December (Fig. 3AandD). Warming delayed photosynthetic downregulation in both families, as shown by higher PRI and CCI values in autumn (Fig. 3AandD;Table 1). Seedlings in the warming treatments had a less steep decline in PRI and CCI compared to control seedlings, albeit not significantly, suggesting slower photosynthetic downregulation under warming (Fig. 3BandE;Table 2). Summer drought did not affect the timing of photosynthetic downregulation, as CCI values were similar between control and RO seedlings in autumn (Fig. 3D;Table 1). PRI was higher in the RO seedlings in late summer and early autumn, but equalized by late autumn, indicating no delay or advance in photosynthetic downregulation due to summer drought (Fig. 3A;Table 1). Interactions between summer drought and warming did not impact the timing of photosynthetic downregulation, as observed through PRI and CCI measurements (Fig. 3;Table 1). However, the southern family showed delayed photosynthetic downregulation compared to the northern family, with higher CCI throughout the experiment (Fig. 3D;Table 1) and higher CCI and PRI in late December (Fig. 3CandF;Table 2).

Earlywood and latewood xylem development did not show any direct responses to the warming or RO treatments, but was controlled by family and environment interactions (Fig. 7). Hydraulic safety, characterized by cell wall thickness (CWT) and tracheid wall reinforcement ([t/b]2), was unaffected by warming alone (Fig. 7A, B, F, andG;Table 3). However, CWT was higher in the northern family than the southern family in latewood (Fig. 7F;Table 3). Interestingly, [t/b]2varied by family in response to summer drought in the earlywood and warming in the latewood (Fig. 7BandG;Table 3). Summer drought increased [t/b]2in the southern family and decreased in the northern family in the earlywood (Fig. 7B;Table 3). In the latewood, [t/b]2was higher in the southern family and lower in the northern family under warming (Fig. 7G;Table 3). Hydraulic conductivity, characterized byDhand LA, was higher under warming in the northern family compared to the southern family in latewood xylem (Fig. 7HandI;Table 3). Of those two parameters,Dhwas also higher in the northern family while LA was similar between the two families in earlywood xylem (Fig. 7CandD;Table 3). Summer drought did not impact traits related to hydraulic conductivity in earlywood or latewood (Fig. 7C, D, H, andI;Table 3). Mechanical support, characterized by relative wood density (RWD), showed opposite trends to traits related to hydraulic conductivity: summer drought decreased RWD in the northern family and increased it in the southern family, with similar trends under warming in latewood (Fig. 7EandJ;Table 3).

Moderate summer drought had little effect on the timing of autumn photosynthetic downregulation in field-grown 4-yr-old white spruce seedlings. Even in late autumn, seedlings that had experienced water stress in the RO treatment showedFv/Fm,ϕPSII, and CCI values comparable to control seedlings, indicating that summer drought alone did not advance the downregulation of photosynthetic activity (Figs. 3,4, and5). This is contrary to our hypothesis that summer drought would advance photosynthetic downregulation. The reason for our finding is likely the intensity of the drought severity as a key factor in the downregulation of photosynthesis. We maintained soil volumetric water content (soil VWC) above 20% to prevent seedling death, unlike other experiments where severe water stress (as defined by soil VWC < 15% or water potential < −2mPa) led to photosynthetic downregulation during summer (Sullivan et al. 2021;Bigras 2005). Mature white spruce have also shown reduced radial growth and sap flux density when soil VWC is below 15% (Sullivan et al. 2021) and higher mortality rates have been attributed to growing season drought in natural forests (Lu et al. 2019). These negative effects of drought on overall productivity and tree health suggest that white spruce would likely undergo earlier autumn photosynthetic downregulation under more severe drought conditions than those observed in our study. There are few studies that have investigated the effect of drought on autumn photosynthesis in conifers, including a recent study using satellite and ground data, which showed that summer drought, leads to an earlier end of the growing season globally (Wang et al. 2024). However, Wang et al. included conifers in their dataset as part of deciduous forests and did not analyze conifers separately. Our findings indicate that the impact of summer drought on the timing of photosynthetic downregulation in autumn depends on critical threshold values for the severity of the drought, which remain to be determined. However, the moderate drought conditions applied in our experiment align with predictions of the future climate in our study region within Eastern Canada, which is expected to become warmer with more rain under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 scenarios (Wang et al. 2022).
Unlike summer drought, autumn warming of +5 °C significantly delayed the timing of autumn photosynthetic downregulation in field-grown white spruce seedlings, which is in agreement with our first hypothesis. The higherFv/FmandϕPSIIobserved in the warming treatment compared to the control treatment in December indicated that seedlings maintained higher maximal photochemical efficiency (Fig. 4AandB). Warming also led to decreased accumulation of Zea and xanthophyll pigment levels, while Chls remained unaffected. Since increased Zea and xanthophyll pigments are required for the development of sustained NPQ during low temperature-induced cold hardening (Demmig-Adams et al. 2012), it is not surprising that the development of sustained NPQ was also delayed by the warming treatment (Fig. 5E). The temperature threshold of sustained NPQ (TT50) was shifted to a higher temperature in warming seedlings (∼−2 °C) compared to the control seedlings (−3.1 °C;Fig. 5E). Control seedlings experienced leaf temperatures of −3.1 °C for the first time on October 31 whereas the warming seedlings did not experience leaf temperatures of −1.7 °C until after November 16 (Fig. 2B), suggesting that upregulation of sustained NPQ occurred later in the season as a result of shortening photoperiods since low temperature cues were not sufficient under warming. In our experiment, +5 °C day/night warming led to a delay in both the downregulation of photosynthesis and the upregulation of photoprotection whereas a previous study that used a +1.5/+3 °C day/night warming treatment observed no delay in either process in white pine seedlings (Chang et al. 2015). Growth chamber experiments have also shown delayed photosynthetic downregulation under warming for white spruce with sufficiently high temperature treatments of +5 °C day/night (Stinziano and Way 2017) and +14 °C/+12 °C day/night (Hamilton et al. 2016). Similarly, NPQ upregulation has also been observed to be delayed in growth chamber experiments under more extreme warming treatments of +5 to 15 °C in white pine (Fréchette et al. 2016) and Douglas-fir (Noordermeer et al. 2021). Overall, low temperature appears to be the dominant signal for photosynthetic downregulation in white spruce, and a critical warming threshold of +5 °C likely needs to be surpassed to observe the effects of warming on photosynthetic downregulation in conifers.
Shifts in energy partitioning during autumn translate into observable changes in carotenoid-based vegetation indices, and these changes in leaf optical properties can be used to track photosynthetic activity at the leaf level, canopy level and remotely on a larger scale. In our study, effects of warming from autumn to winter were evident in the seasonal changes in the two carotenoid-based vegetation indices PRI and CCI in our white spruce seedlings (Fig. 3), while summer drought had minimal impact. Changes in PRI correlate with changes in the epoxidation state of the xanthophyll cycle pigments and is hence often used as a proxy for NPQ and to track short-term changes in photosynthetic activity (Porcar-Castell et al. 2012;Fréchette et al. 2016). CCI, on the other hand, is more sensitive to changes in the carotenoid-to-Chl ratio and captures seasonal adjustments in photosynthetic capacity (Wong et al. 2019;Fréchette et al. 2020;Yin et al. 2020). Both PRI and CCI were higher under warming in December, indicating a delayed downregulation of photosynthesis. These elevated values corresponded with the retention of Zea and increases in total carotenoids (Fig. 6B;Supplementary Table S2). Overall, carotenoid-based vegetation indices effectively detected delayed photosynthetic downregulation under autumn warming in white spruce, whereas summer drought effects were negligible.
Warming delayed photosynthetic downregulation in both families, whereas summer drought had limited effects. As our hypothesis predicted, the timing of these physiological changes differed between the two families based on their latitudinal origin located along a transect of ∼400 km with an annual minimum air temperature difference of ∼6.2 °C (Fig. 1;Supplementary Fig. S3). The southern family showed higher maximal PSII efficiency at the end of December compared to the northern family, indicating higher photosynthetic activity into autumn (Fig. 4A). This reflects that the southern family would experience freezing temperatures in its original environment at a shorter photoperiod later into autumn compared to the northern family (Yamori et al. 2014;Fréchette et al. 2020). Consequently, the TT50of the downregulation ofFv/Fmwas shifted in the southern family toward a lower minimum air temperature and resulting in a delayed downregulation in photosynthetic activity (Fig. 5B). This delay in photosynthetic downregulation corresponded with a delay in the development of sustained NPQ (Fig. 4D) and thus lower Chl degradation and total xanthophyll pigments by end of December (Fig. 6AandC). Given that the southern family's original environment was closer to the common garden site than that of the northern family, there remains uncertainty regarding whether the family responses would be similar at a common garden site situated further north. Notably, CCI was able to effectively detect genetic variation in photosynthetic phenology between the white spruce families (Fig. 3D). Both CCI and PRI indicated higher photosynthetic activity in the southern family, particularly when strong phenotypic differences between families were observed in late December (Fig. 3CandF). The differences in the timing of autumn photosynthetic downregulation between the families were considerable; however, as we tested only two controlled crosses, this experimental design will not have captured the full phenotypic and genetic variation previously observed in white spruce across a broader geography (Godt et al. 2001;Prunier et al. 2017;Depardieu et al. 2020).
To the best of our knowledge, no studies except this one have investigated the impact of summer drought and autumn warming on the timing of phenological events in autumn and winter in different families or populations of conifers in a field experiment. Previous studies in common garden experiments showed similar trends for other phenological traits such as autumn cold acclimation, which was delayed in southern provenances of several conifers compared to their northern counterparts (Holliday et al. 2008;Fréchette et al. 2020). Interestingly, summer drought did not reveal differences in the timing of phenology between the northern and the southern family, whereas autumn warming revealed delayed timing in phenology in the southern family. Monitoring white pine and the downregulation of photosynthesis and modulation of NPQ across different provenances in a warming experiment,Fréchette et al. (2020)observed that southern provenances also experienced the greatest delays compared to northern provenances. Sensitivity to warming may vary by species and the distance and latitudinal range between the origins of the parental seeds used, but it is evident that the timing of autumn phenology of conifers from southern origins is more strongly delayed by warming than in northern origins.
Overall, the delay in photosynthetic downregulation observed in the southern family and under warming did not result in increased primary or secondary growth, in agreement with our third hypothesis that the phenology of photosynthesis is decoupled from the phenology of growth. Based on previous studies (Cooke et al. 2012;Hamilton et al. 2016;Stinziano and Way 2017) and our own observations of primary and secondary growth, photoperiod appears to be the dominant cue on the timing of growth cessation. Interestingly, xylem development did differ by family in response to warming and summer drought (Fig. 7). Warming increased the latewood xylem LA in the northern family and decreased it in the southern family. Previous research on conifers has found latewood development to be more sensitive to climate than earlywood development (Lebourgeois 2000;Pampuch et al. 2021). In fact, latewood density and proportion have been shown to be under moderate genetic control in white spruce (Soro et al. 2022). Our study revealed that white spruce families exhibited distinct responses in the development of xylem traits related to hydraulic efficiency and safety to summer drought conditions during earlywood development and to warming during latewood development in autumn. Xylogenesis in conifers typically spans from May–June to September–October in cold climates (Deslauriers et al. 2003;Rossi et al. 2006,2010). The peak period for cambial activity and earlywood cell production occurs around the summer solstice, coinciding with the longest photoperiod of the year, followed by a decline in cell division and a transition toward latewood production. As such, the earlywood development was likely near completion when the RO treatment began, which may explain the differential impacts of drought and warming on earlywood and latewood observed in our study. Interestingly, the northern family invested significantly more in traits related to hydraulic transport (Fig. 7D, H, andI), whereas the southern family invested more in traits related to mechanical support in both the earlywood and latewood xylem (Fig. 7B, E, G, andJ). Trees adapted to cold-limited climates have been shown to increase their hydraulic conductivity in response to warming (Petit et al. 2011). In our study, the northern family was likely more cold-limited in its parental origin, which may explain why it showed a greater response of hydraulic conductivity to warming compared to the southern family. Overall, white spruce families appear to employ different strategies to achieve high water conductivity, with the southern family producing more tracheids (Supplementary Fig. S4DandTable S3) and the northern family increasing the LA of fewer tracheids when conditions are favorable.
At a cellular level, our study found opposite effects on secondary growth development in response to warming and drought by family. However, on a seedling level, summer drought had minimal impacts on the timing of photosynthetic downregulation and growth cessation in white spruce seedlings from either family. Warming delayed photosynthetic downregulation in the southern family, but seedlings did not exhibit longer growth into autumn (Fig. 8AandB). Interestingly, the combination of summer drought and warming also did not affect the timing of growth cessation. Growth cessation in most Northern hemisphere conifers is primarily regulated by decreasing photoperiods, with growth rates peaking at the summer solstice and cessation beginning in late summer to early autumn (Repo et al. 2000;Rossi et al. 2006;Ježík et al. 2021). Growth cessation is primarily regulated by the sensing of light and photoperiod through the conserved CONSTANS (CO)/FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) module, as well as phytochrome A and cryptochromes (Chang et al. 2020). In contrast, photosynthetic downregulation is often triggered by low temperatures, which affect membrane fluidity and the redox state of the chloroplast plastoquinone pool, leading to changes in the expression of photosynthetic and cold-regulated (COR) genes (Bräutigam et al. 2009). As these two key processes in cold acclimation rely on different signaling pathways, it is not surprising that their sensitivity to warming differed in our experiment. Our findings show that field-grown white spruce seedlings do not benefit from extended photosynthetic activity, as characterized by light reactions and carotenoid-based vegetation indices, under warming due to strong photoperiodic control on growth cessation.
Warming even resulted in decreased stem diameter growth (Fig. 8A). Seedlings under warming experienced maximum leaf temperature up to 38 °C that September (Fig. 2B). Although we cannot fully explain how warming leads to reductions in radial growth, this was also observed during hot summers in the absence of drought in mature white spruce (Lapenis et al. 2022) and may be related to increased respiratory costs associated with warming (Atkin and Tjoelker 2003). Both families had similar cumulative primary and secondary growth over the experiment despite delayed photosynthetic downregulation in the southern family and under warming (Fig. 8AandB;Supplementary Fig. S2). Although determining the allocation of these late-season photosynthates is beyond the scope of this study, previous research has shown that spruce rebuilds and increases its total reserves of nonstructural carbohydrates (NSCs) and nonstructural carbon compounds through late fall and winter as a resource to help conifers endure harsh winter temperatures and promote growth upon the onset of favorable spring conditions (Körner 2003;Schoonmaker et al. 2021) Another possibility is that electrons were being rerouted from linear electron flow to alternative and cyclic pathways. Alternative electron flow delivers electrons to acceptors not involved in CO2assimilation, such as through the Mehler reaction, to help prevent over-reduction of the photosynthetic machinery (Grebe et al. 2024). Cyclic electron transport around PSI is another important sink for electrons, which yields ATP without the concurrent production of NADPH (Fréchette et al. 2015). Both pathways have been observed in evergreen conifers during the recovery of photosynthesis in spring to prevent photoinhibition and optimize light energy use (Fréchette et al. 2015;Grebe et al. 2024). Therefore, depending on where the excitation energy was being directed, white spruce seedlings in our experiment may have benefited from delayed photosynthetic downregulation through an increased allocation to NSC pools and a quicker growth onset in the next growing season, or through rerouted electron flow to better protect against photoinhibition under low temperatures.
Overall, our findings suggest that during autumn, the downregulation of photosynthesis is delayed under warming in white spruce whereas growth cessation appears to be primarily regulated by photoperiod. However, the benefits of extended photosynthetic activity under warming do not translate into increased aboveground apical and radial growth due to local adaptation to photoperiod. At our common garden site, the southern family experienced delayed downregulation of photosynthesis compared to the northern family. This has important implications on assisted migration strategies as the movement of southern families northward may result in delayed photosynthetic downregulation; however, benefits to tree growth and carbon uptake may be more limited. Nevertheless, juvenile conifer seedlings are often more sensitive to environmental changes than mature trees (Ruiz-Benito et al. 2015), and hence our findings should not be generalized without some caution to mature white spruce. Moderate summer drought did not impact the timing of photosynthetic downregulation nor secondary growth cessation in either white spruce family, suggesting temperature and local adaptation to photoperiod are more important than projected water limitations in the regulation of autumn photosynthetic phenology.
The experiment was conducted at the University of Toronto's Koffler Scientific Reserve, near King City, ON (44°01′44.3″N 79°32′19,2″W). Plots were excavated and filled with a mixture of one-third peat, one-third sand, and one-third local soil (New Roots Garden Center) prior to planting. One-year-old bare rooted white spruce (P. glauca[Moench] Voss) siblings from two different families were planted. The first family was obtained from a controlled cross between a northern genotype from Lac Simard, Québec (47°46′44″N, 73°38′58″W) and a more southern genotype from Petawawa, Ontario (45°53′40.27″N, 77°16′48.25″W). The second family resulted from a cross between two southern genotypes from Hungerford, Ontario (44°28′02″N, 77°15′01″W). All seedlings, generated by Natural Resources Canada (Laurentian Forestry Centre) in 2017, were selected for their differential responses to temperature and photoperiod. The four parent genotypes span a latitudinal transect of ∼400 km (Fig. 1). Six seedlings per family were planted per plot. For further details on planting and plot maintenance seeSupplementary Method S1.
The experiment included four treatments introduced over time: (i) control, (ii) reduced soil water content (SWC) (i.e. RO), (iii) warming, and (iv) warming combined with RO (Fig. 3). The RO structures were installed on July 22 and the warming began on August 31, 6 wk after RO installation (Fig. 9). Each treatment was replicated in three plots. SWC was manipulated using rainout structures from late July to late November, and leaf temperature was increased by 5 °C during night and day in warming plots using infrared heaters from late August to late December to mimic predicted air temperature for 2080 in southern Ontario under a high-emission climate scenario (RCP 8.5;Wang et al. 2022). We used a rainout structure in our experiment that was designed to simulate drought conditions where some amount of ambient rainfall is intercepted and removed. Similar experimental designs have been described byYahdjian and Sala (2002);Hoover et al. (2018)and others, and are also referred to as “rainfall-exclusion” or “rainfall manipulation” experiments. For technical details on the temperature free-air-controlled enhancement (T-FACE) system seeFrechette et al. (2020).Figure 9provides a schematic of the experimental setup and illustrates the shape of the RO structures. For the RO treatment, six plots had rainout structures with open profiles (longitudinally halved PVC pipes) installed between the seedlings at an incline near the ground to drain 50% of the precipitation. Control non-RO treatment plots had dummy profiles installed, where the open side was facing downwards to allow rainfall to run down the profile and drip into the soil. Control non-RO treatment plots were watered for target soil moisture of 25% during summer to prevent water stress from ambient environmental conditions. Rainout structures were removed on November 27 in preparation for snowfall.

Each plot was equipped with an air temperature and humidity sensor (Models VP3 and VP4, METER Group), soil temperature and moisture sensor (Models 5TM and TE11, METER Group), and an infrared leaf temperature sensor (Model IRT-P5, Apogee Instruments) to record temperature and humidity data for each plot at 30-minute intervals using a CR1000 datalogger (Fig. 2;Supplementary Method S2; Campbell Scientific Inc.). A weather station with an air temperature and humidity sensor (Model Rotronic HC2A-S3, ITM Instruments), a PAR sensor (LI-COR LI-190R Quantum Sensor, Li-Cor Biosciences), a solar flux sensor (Model SP Lite2 Pyranometer, OTT HydroMet), and a tipping bucket rain gauge (Model 52202, R.M. Young Company) recorded data every 15 min on-site using a CR1000 datalogger (Campbell Scientific Inc.). Occasional gaps in precipitation data were filled with data from the King City North weather station, 8.5 km from the field site (Environment Canada, 2014).
Thirteen measurement campaigns were conducted between July and December 2020, including Chl fluorescence, leaf spectral reflectance, and growth. To minimize the removal of seedlings and conserve tissue, destructive needle sampling for pigment analysis was limited to four campaigns: July 21–22, August 25–26, October 27–28, and December 18. Physiological measurements were conducted between 10:00 and 16:00 h, followed by sample collection at 16:00 h. Physiological measurements and tissue sampling for xylem analyses were performed using two seedlings per family per treatment. Values obtained from the two seedlings per family were used to calculate the plot average for each family. Each plot average was used as a biological replicate, yielding three replicate plots per treatment. Tissue sampling for pigment analyses was performed using one seedling per family per plot and used as a biological replicate.
A Dual-PAM-100 fluorometer (Walz) was used to measure Chl fluorescence between ∼10:00 to 16:00 h. A bundle of attached, current year sunlit needles were arranged into a flat layer and placed into a dark leaf clip (DLC-8, Walz). Needles were dark acclimated for 30 min prior to measurement. Minimal fluorescence (Fo) was recorded and a short saturation pulse was applied for the determination of maximal fluorescence (Fm) of the dark acclimated leaves. Fully expanded current year needles were collected following fluorescence and spectral reflectance measurements. Needles were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80 °C until they were homogenized to a fine powder in liquid nitrogen. Photosynthetic pigments were analyzed according toJunker and Ensminger (2016). Briefly, pigments were extracted from 50 mg of frozen ground needle sample in a buffer comprised of 98% methanol and 2% 0.5mammonium acetate. The extracted pigment samples were analyzed using a high-performance liquid chromatography system (Agilent 1260 system, Agilent Technologies) with a quaternary pump, autosampler set to 4 °C, column oven set to 25 °C and photodiode array detector. Pigments were separated using a reverse-phase C30 column (YMC Carotenoid; YMC America, Inc.) and detected at 450 and 656 nm wavelengths. The mobile phase was comprised of a gradient of methanol, methyl-tert-butyl-ether, and water buffered with 0.2% ammonium acetate at a flow rate of 1 ml min−1. For calibration and peak detection, commercially available standards were obtained from Sigma Aldrich and DHI Lab products. Peak detection and pigment quantification were performed using ChemStation software (Agilent Technologies).

Xylem anatomical traits were assessed followingVon Arx et al. (2016). Basal stem segments were harvested using shears during the final campaign in late December and stored in 80% ethanol at 4 °C. Sample preparation followed a modified protocol byChen et al. (2016)(seeSupplementary Method S3). Cross-sections 14 μm thick were imaged using a Leica DVM6 digital transmitted microscope with a mid-magnification lens and LAS X v3.0.6 (Leica Biosystems) at 252× magnification for a resolution of 2.2 pixels/μm. Individual images were captured with a 30%xandyaxis overlap and a z-stack of five images, then stitched into a complete cross-section using PTGui Pro v11 (New House Internet Services). Two 20° xylem sectors from opposite radii were analyzed from pith to cambium using ImagePro v7 (Media Cybernetics) with the image analysis tool ROXAS v 3.0.654 (von Arx and Carrer 2014). Annual growth rings were manually delineated and assessed for primary and derived xylem anatomical traits (seeSupplementary Method S4).
To assess differences between families, we compared xylem anatomical traits in control seedlings from 2017 to 2020 for every year across all samples. We selected ring area, total cell number (CNo), and cell LA as proxies for radial growth and cell density, thickness of all cell walls (CWTall), and RWD (cell wall area per total cell area) as proxies of structural integrity to investigate intraspecific differences in xylem anatomy. To assess treatment effects on xylem anatomical development within the 2020 growth ring, we distinguished earlywood and latewood cells using Mork's Index (Denne 1989). Mork's index (rTSR) is defined as the radial thickness-to-span ratio of a cell and distinguishes earlywood (rTSR < 1) and latewood (rTSR > 1). We calculated the mean of two traits related to hydraulic efficiency (LA;Dh) and three traits related to hydraulic safety (CWTall; RWD; cell wall reinforcement, [t/b]2) separately for earlywood and latewood using R v.4.2.2 (R Core Team 2022). Hydraulic efficiency refers to the ability of xylem to transport water efficiently, while hydraulic safety refers to the resistance of xylem to cavitation and embolism (Meinzer et al. 2010).
During each measurement campaign, height and stem diameter were measured for all seedlings in all plots. Stem height was measured from the base at the soil surface to the highest point on the main stem using tape measures. Stem diameter was measured at the base of the stem near the soil surface with standard digital calipers.
Linear mixed-effects ANCOVA models were used to assess the effect of temperature, the effect of rainout treatment (denoted as SWC), and family on Chl fluorescence, spectral reflectance, and photosynthetic pigments, accounting for the effect of seasonal variation introduced by photoperiod and minimum ambient daily air temperature. For repeated measures of Chl fluorescence and spectral reflectance, the ANCOVA models used temperature, SWC, and family as categorial fixed factors, photoperiod and minimum ambient daily air temperature as continuous numeric covariates, and plot and ID as nested random factors. For photosynthetic pigments where no repeated measures occurred, the ANCOVA model used temperature, SWC, and family as categorial fixed factors, photoperiod and minimum ambient daily air temperature as continuous numeric covariates, and plot as a random factor.
Xylem anatomical traits were analyzed using two-way ANOVA models to test family and time as categorical fixed factors with plot as a random factor. To test for earlywood and latewood differences in the 2020 growth ring, three-way ANOVA models were used, with temperature, SWC and family as categorial fixed factors and plot as a random factor.
All ANOVA and ANCOVA models were run using thelme4package (Bates et al. 2015) in R v.4.2.2 (R Core Team 2022). For each parameter, the best-fit model including temperature, SWC, family and their interactions was chosen according to the lowest Akaike information criterion (AIC) using the package AICcmodavg (Mazerolle 2023) also in R v.4.2.2 (R Core Team 2022). The maximum likelihood model was used to select and run the best-fit models to prioritize accurate estimates of the fixed regression parameters.
