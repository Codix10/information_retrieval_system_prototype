Australian rainforests occur from temperate to tropical latitudes, but how climate warming will affect tree growth along this climate gradient remains poorly understood. We examined how changes in biomass allocation, leaf area, and photosynthetic capacity were linked to the capacity to maintain growth rates with +3.5, +7 and +10.5 °C warming in seedlings of 12 tropical, subtropical, and temperate rainforest tree species. Temperate species maintained or increased final biomass (+14%), leaf area, and leaf area ratio (the ratio of leaf area to plant dry mass) with warming, along with similar or increased leaf mass fraction (+14%). Subtropical species increased biomass with +3.5 °C (+28%) and +7 °C (+17%) warming but were negatively impacted with >10 °C warming (−31%). Tropical species reduced leaf area, leaf area ratio, photosynthetic capacity, and leaf nitrogen, along with 14% increased root allocation in response to warming, resulting in large biomass reductions with +3.5 °C (−20%) and +7 °C (−53%). Tropical species were more susceptible to climate warming with reduced photosynthetic capacity and reduced biomass, which can lead to a reduced carbon sink in the future, especially in late-successional tree species, which are the most abundant with a large role in carbon assimilation of tropical rainforests.
Keywords:Biomass, gradient, nitrogen, latitudes, photosynthesis, temperature
Along a climate warming gradient, Australian tropical rainforest tree species showed reduced growth with more allocation to roots whereas temperate rainforest tree species allocated more biomass to leaves.
Temperature is one of the main drivers of geographical distributions of plant species (Turesson, 1925;Linhart and Grant, 1996;Joshiet al., 2001) and these geographical distributions may shift with climate warming depending on the temperature tolerance of the species. Species can migrate to track suitable conditions or will need to adapt via natural selection. Given that warming is occurring at an unprecedented rate, with predictions of 2–5 °C warming likely to occur this century (Huntingfordet al., 2012;Masson-Delmotteet al., 2022,Slatyer, 1977) long-lived tree species will need to make adjustments in phenotype, including physiological changes in these new, warmer conditions (Jump and Penuelas, 2005). Species with limited capacity to physiologically adjust to warmer temperatures may experience reduced growth (Ghannoum and Way, 2011). Although plant warming responses can vary over large geographical scales (Joshiet al., 2001;Brezaet al., 2012) our understanding of plasticity of species to warming along a climate gradient spanning their current distribution is still limited.
Some plasticity of leaf chemistry and metabolic rates is commonly observed in response to increased temperatures (Nicotra and Davidson, 2010). Temperature can directly influence cell division and expansion of leaves (Tardieuet al., 2000;Rymenet al., 2007;Kanno and Makino, 2010), which may influence specific leaf area (SLA) and leaf area ratio (LAR) and ultimately affect relative growth rate. Biomass and leaf area generally increase with warmer temperatures in temperate species (Way and Oren, 2010), while tropical species tend to decline with warming (Cheesman and Winter, 2013a;Drakeet al., 2015;Slotet al., 2016;Nottinghamet al., 2023). Here we investigate the growth responses of Australian rainforest trees to warming from temperate to tropical climates, using seedlings from late-successional species.
The plastic response of leaf traits to growth temperature can contribute to the thermal acclimation of metabolic factors, suggesting a tight coupling between thermal acclimation and phenotypic plasticity (Atkinet al., 2015).Eucalyptus tereticornisprovenances grown across a set of common growth temperatures from 18 °C to 35.5 °C (Drakeet al., 2017) experienced limited physiological thermal acclimation of respiration and constrained carbon availability for leaf development at high temperatures, resulting in reduced LAR (Drake etal., 2017). While thermal acclimation of plant metabolism depends on a change in growth temperatures and can be dynamically reversible (Atkinet al., 2005;Way and Yamori, 2014;Crouset al., 2022) it is currently unclear if and by how much thermal acclimation differs in plants depending on the climate-of-origin (Crouset al., 2022).
Changes in growth temperatures can alter carbon allocation patterns (Littonet al., 2007;Poorteret al., 2012,Qi et al., 2019) including a shift between above- and belowground allocation (Way and Oren, 2010), although the direction of this shift is unclear in tropical versus temperate trees.Cunningham and Read (2003a)reported increased allocation to roots at the expense of leaves in tropical rainforest tree species, while temperate species showed no significant changes in biomass allocation in response to growth temperature. However,Cheesman and Winter (2013a)found a decrease in root-to-shoot ratio with higher temperatures. A global synthesis found positive correlations of root-to-shoot ratio with elevation and latitude, suggesting different responses in biomass allocation between temperate and tropical species, but the general direction remained unclear. Changes in biomass allocation in response to warming will ultimately affect plant growth and carbon storage. Hence, we need to understand whether shifts in allocation patterns with warming differ between tropical and temperate trees and how changes in biomass allocation link to the capacity of tropical, subtropical, and temperate species to adjust their growth to warmer temperatures.
The capacity of plant species to alter photosynthesis, growth, and leaf traits may depend on the growth temperatures of their current native environments (Cunningham and Read, 2003a;Drakeet al., 2015). The growth increase with warming in species from higher latitudes is often related to increased photosynthetic capacity and total leaf area (Cunningham and Read, 2002;Gundersonet al., 2010;Way and Oren, 2010;Sendallet al., 2015), although boreal species at the warmest edge of their distribution showed reduced growth (Girardinet al., 2016;Reichet al., 2022). The response of photosynthesis to warming was reduced by 5.3% for every 10 °C increase in growth temperature based on a meta-analysis across 101 evergreen species (Crouset al., 2022). Decreased growth in species from lower latitudes could generally be associated with reductions in photosynthetic capacity, leaf N, and total leaf area with warming (Feeleyet al., 2007;Way and Oren, 2010;Drakeet al., 2015,2017;Scafaroet al., 2017;Crouset al., 2018), thoughDusengeet al. (2021)found increased leaf area (LA) despite reduced photosynthesis with warming in montane tropical species growing at higher, lowland temperatures as part of the Rwanda-TREE project.
Tree species from warmer climates are thought to be operating closer to their upper thermal limit than cool‐climate species (Doughtyet al., 2023) and therefore may show reduced capacity to cope with additional warming (Sage and Kubien, 2007;Clarket al., 2010). Tropical rainforest species are particularly at risk in this regard because they already experience high daytime temperatures with little seasonality throughout the year (Wrightet al., 2009;Slotet al., 2014;Sullivanet al., 2020). Despite this risk, species can have different temperature acclimation capacities and growth responses to warming, even within the same biome (Cheesman and Winter, 2013a;Slot and Winter, 2017b). Several recent studies on tropical species reported that fast-growing pioneer species acclimated better to warmer temperatures compared with slower growing late-successional species (Cheesman and Winter, 2013b;Slotet al., 2021;Manishimweet al., 2022;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023;Ntirugulirwaet al., 2023), calling for a need to differentiate plant functional types in evergreen forests. Moreover, it remains important to understand and compare the resilience of evergreen species from different latitudes to climate warming. We addressed this question using a wide range of growth temperatures to investigate the mechanisms underlying the growth responses of rainforest trees to warming and extreme temperatures.
Australian rainforests occur along the eastern margin of Australia in tropical, subtropical, and temperate climates and provide a natural climate gradient in which warming responses can be evaluated for different rainforest groups. We tested the direct effects of warming on growth rate, total leaf area, LAR, SLA, biomass fractions, and photosynthetic capacity in seedlings of 12 rainforest tree species from different latitudes, grown across a broad range of temperatures in controlled glasshouse conditions to address the following hypotheses: (i) temperate species will maintain or increase their growth in response to warming, whereas subtropical and tropical species will reduce their growth; (ii) an increase in growth temperature will increase leaf area and shoot dry mass allocation in temperate species, whereas warming will decrease leaf area and shoot allocation in subtropical and tropical species; and (iii) in response to warming, photosynthesis and photosynthetic capacity (measured at a common temperature) will be increased or maintained in temperate species and will be reduced in subtropical and tropical species. Thus, based on these hypotheses, we expected to see an interaction between rainforest group and growth temperature.
Twelve late-successional Australian rainforest species were selected, comprising four species from each of three different climates of origin (aka ‘rainforest group’): tropical, subtropical, and temperate (Table 1). Each species was exposed to four growth temperatures reflecting its climate zone, according to its rainforest group. WorldClim climatology data (WorldClim 1.4;Hijmanset al., 2005) were used to calculate the average summer temperature (December–February) of the southernmost and northernmost latitude of the native occurrence of each species, using the ‘get_worldclim_rasters’ function from the ‘speciesmap’ R package (Duursma, 2016) (Table 1). Seedlings were obtained as tubestock from three nurseries (Kalanga nursery in Victoria, Burringbar Rainforest nursery in New South Wales and Yuruga nursery in Queensland) with seeds from one local seed source for each species. Plant height at purchase ranged from 10 to 32 cm across species but was similar within species. Seedlings were transplanted individually into 7 litre pots of locally sourced loamy sand soil. Ten seedlings of each species were randomly assigned to each of the four temperature treatments of their respective climate zones. Plants were kept well-watered using automated drip irrigation throughout the experiment and fertilized weekly with a commercial fertilizer (50 ml, N:P:K=25:5:9, Thrive soluble, Yates, Australia).

The experiment was conducted in a naturally sunlit, temperature and humidity-controlled glasshouse at Western Sydney University (Richmond, NSW, Australia) in the austral summer of 2017–2018 (November–February). As inChouryet al. (2022) species in each rainforest group were exposed to four growth temperatures, representing the range of the average summer temperature along the eastern margin of Australia, which corresponded to temperatures between 17 °C and 34.5 °C using 3.5 °C increments (followingDrake et al., 2015). Six growth temperature treatments were randomly assigned, one to each of six identical glasshouse bays. The mean diel temperatures for temperate species were set to 17, 20.5, 24, and 27.5 °C, while subtropical and tropical species experienced 24, 27.5, 31, and 34.5 °C, all with a diurnal range of 10 °C. The ‘home’ temperatures were taken to be 17 °C for temperate species, 24 °C for subtropical species, and 27.5 °C for tropical species, reflecting the average summer temperatures experienced in their respective native ranges. Thermal limits were examined in temperate and subtropical groups, reflecting temperaturesoutsidetheir current native environment with warming up to 10.5 °C, while tropical species were only warmed up to 7 °C, as this already reflected much higher growth temperatures than they currently experience. The capacity to respond to +3.5, +7, and +10.5 °C warming was compared against the respective ‘average summer temperatures’ (i.e. home) of each rainforest group to determine their upper physiological thermal limits and assess their ability to adjust to temperatures beyond their native range. Extra plants of tropical and temperate species grown in the above-described conditions were used in an independent experiment contrasting root respiration traits with 6 °C warming (Nohet al., 2020).
Before the start of the experiment, 12 additional seedlings of each species from the same batch of tubestock plants were selected randomly for harvest to determine the initial height and diameter as well as root, stem, and leaf biomass. First, leaves without petioles were removed to determine the total plant leaf area (Li-3100C, LI-COR, Lincoln, NE, USA) and oven-dried (70 °C) and weighed. Stems and branches were oven dried separately to determine stem biomass. The root system of each plant was isolated using a washing station and sieves (2 mm), oven-dried, and weighed. While initial heights and diameters were consistent within species, there was variation among species. During the experiment, height and diameter were measured weekly for the first month then fortnightly until the final harvest (Supplementary Figs S1, S2).Diameters were measured at the base of the main stem, indicated with a pen mark.
A final harvest was conducted at the end of the experiment (107 d) on eight of the 10 plants (randomly chosen) per species per growth temperature (384 plants=12 species×8 replicates×4 growth temperatures). During the final harvest, total leaf area, stem, and root mass were determined using the same approach as for the initial harvest after determining the height and diameter of the seedlings (Supplementary Fig. S3).

In addition, the biomass and leaf area data from the final harvest were used to calculate whole-plant LAR (m2leaf area g−1plant dry mass), leaf, stem, and root mass fractions (LMF, SMF, RMF; g leaf, stem, or root g−1plant) and SLA (m2leaf area g−1leaf dry mass). These ratios were used to robustly reflect the allocation patterns that emerge (and accrued through time) at final harvest.

Gas exchange measurements were conducted in the middle of the experimental period during December 2017 and January 2018 using several cross-calibrated portable open gas exchange systems (LI-6400XT, LI-COR) on newly developed and fully expanded leaves of each of three replicates per species and growth temperature (3 rainforest groups ×4 species ×4 growth temperatures × 3 replicates = 144 plants). At each growth temperature, light-saturated net photosynthesis (Anet) was measured at ambient CO2concentration of 400 ± 5 µmol mol−1and a flow rate of 300 μmol s−1, followed by CO2response curves (Anet–Ci) at a standard leaf temperature of 25 °C and saturating light (1800 μmol m−2s−1) measured between 08.30 and 15.00 h. EachAnet–Cicurve consisted of at least eight CO2concentration levels (40, 150, 235, 330, 415, 700, 1200, 1500, 1800 μmol mol–1). Leaf temperature was controlled to be within ±1 °C of the standard temperature of 25 °C by adjusting the temperature of the chamber block. In some cases, plants were temporarily moved to a cooler bay to achieve good temperature control. The relative humidity in the cuvette was controlled between 50% and 70% by adjusting the LI-6400XT desiccant column. Additional gas exchange data focused on temperature response curves of photosynthesis in six of the 12 species are published inChouryet al.(2022). Afterwards, leaves were collected, scanned for leaf area using a leaf area meter (LI-3100C, LI-COR), oven-dried at 70 °C, and then weighed to determine SLA (m2g−1). Subsequently, dried leaves were ground into a fine powder to determine total leaf nitrogen (N) concentration (mg g−1) using a Vario EL cube combustion elemental analyser (Elementar Analysensysteme GmbH, Hanau, Germany).

Statistical analyses were performed in R (version 3.4.3; R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). The sample size for gas exchange measurements wasn= 3 replicates for each species, while the sample size for growth variables wasn= 8 except when there were fewer surviving individuals. While mortality was recorded (see Results), all analyses were conducted using the surviving replicates. Additional statistical analyses accounting for mortality rendered similar results with stronger significances, and thus we present the more conservative analyses here. A mixed model with growth temperature and rainforest group as fixed factors and species as a random factor nested within group was used to assess the effects of +3.5, +7, and +10.5 °C warming in reference to the home growth temperature, using functionlmerfrom thelme4R package (Bateset al., 2015). Comparison across rainforest groups came from the summary output of thelmerfit in R (Bateset al., 2015). The mean percentage change in the figures was calculated by subtracting the value of the variable at home temperature from its value in response to warming divided by the variable at home temperature and multiply the result by 100. Variables were normalized to the maximum mean value for each species across growth temperatures (except for the stem volume timeline) to visualize the growth patterns to different temperatures, but the data analysis was performed on original, non-transformed data.
Most species showed an exponential increase of stem volume throughout the experiment (Fig. 1). Plant height and diameter followed similar patterns over time as volume (Supplementary Figs S1, S2).The rate of increase depended on growth temperature and species (Fig. 1;Table 2). The temperate speciesTasmannia lanceolatagrew best in cooler temperatures of <24 °C (Fig. 1D) with optimal growth rates at about 19 °C (Fig. 2A), whereas other temperate species responded better to warmer temperatures (≥24 °C). In both subtropical and tropical rainforest groups, two species out of four did not grow much over time in the hottest diel temperature of 34.5 °C (yellow lines inFig. 1G–J). Moreover, at the time of final harvest, fewer than eight replicates survived for four out of eight subtropical and tropical species in the hottest room with a diel average of 34.5 °C: two replicates ofSyzygium wilsonii, four replicates ofDysoxylum fraserianum, and five replicates ofCryptocarya mackinnonianaandSyzygium luehmanniisurvived. Furthermore, all replicates of cool-temperateTasmannia lanceolatadied in 27.5 °C after 2 months and only four individuals of this species survived in 24 °C at the time of the final harvest. No pests or diseases were observed to cause these deaths, and instead they indicate that the temperature conditions were too high for these species to grow and survive. In the following analyses, only data from replicates that survived are included.

Higher growth temperatures had a significant effect on the RVGR (P<0.001;Table 2). The temperature for optimal growth rate in warm-temperate species (excludingT. lanceolata) was ~24 °C (Fig. 2A) while subtropical and tropical species grew best at ~26 °C (Fig. 2B,C). Most subtropical and tropical species showed a clear decline when grown at the highest growth temperature (34.5 °C). Rainforest groups differed in their growth rate response to warming relative to their home environment (P<0.001;Table 2). Comparing the RVGR among the three rainforest groups, warm-temperate species and subtropical species maintained their RVGR at +3.5 °C and +7 °C warming (P>0.05), but had diverging responses at +10.5 °C, where RVGR significantly increased in warm-temperate species (+11%;P<0.001) and decreased in subtropical species (−47%,P<0.001). By contrast, tropical species had significantly lower growth rates with +3.5 °C (−21%;P=0.003) and +7 °C warming (−52%,P<0.001) relative to their home temperature of 27.5 °C.
Final biomass responded differently to warming in different rainforest groups (P<0.001;Table 2). Both temperate and subtropical rainforest groups increased final biomass on average in response to +3.5 °C and +7 °C warming (P<0.001;Fig. 3;Table 2). However, with >10 °C warming, subtropical species reduced their final biomass by 31% (Fig. 3). Tropical species reduced their final biomass by 20% (P<0.001) at +3.5 °C and 53% (P<0.001;Fig. 3;Table 2) at +7 °C warming (Fig. 3).

Similarly, biomass fractions showed a significant response to warming (P<0.001;Fig. 4) which differed among rainforest groups (P<0.001;Table 2). Warming generally involved a shift in biomass allocation, which varied with rainforest group. Tropical species increased their RMF and reduced their LMF with warming, whereas temperate species reduced their RMF and increased their LMF with warming (Supplementary Fig. S4). In detail, temperate species maintained their LMF at +3.5 °C and significantly increased LMF at +7 °C and +10.5 °C by 14% (P≤0.017;Supplementary Fig. S5). Subtropical species displayed an increase of LMF by 16% at +3.5 °C (P<0.001;Table 2), which remained unchanged at +7 °C, then significantly reduced by 21% with +10.5 °C. Tropical species showed a consistent decline of LMF by 7% and 28% at both +3.5 °C and +7 °C warming, respectively (P≤0.006;Supplementary Fig. S5). Their maximum LMF was 25.1 °C in temperate species while subtropical and tropical species had their maximum LMF at 27.3 °C and 27.0 °C, respectively (Fig. 4A–C). SMF followed similar patterns to LMF (Fig. 4;Supplementary Fig. S5).

RMF also showed a significant interaction between warming and rainforest group (P<0.001;Table 2). Temperate species maintained their RMF at +3.5 °C (P=0.98) but reduced their RMF at +7 °C (−23%;P=0.002;Supplementary Fig. S5) and +10.5 °C warming (−19%;P<0.001;Supplementary Fig. S5). While subtropical species significantly reduced their RMF at +3.5 °C (−10%;P=0.012;Supplementary Fig. S5), they maintained their RMF at +7 °C and +10.5 °C. By contrast, tropical species significantly increased their RMF at both +3.5 °C and +7 °C by +5% and +23%, respectively (P<0.05;Supplementary Figs S4,S5). The root: shoot ratio (R/S) followed a similar pattern to RMF (Supplementary Fig. S5). These different allocation responses between rainforest groups contributed to the overall reduced final biomass in tropical species while temperate species were able to maintain their final biomass at higher growth temperatures.
Rainforest groups differed significantly in their responses to warming for LA and LAR (P≤0.05) and differences were marginally significant for SLA (P=0.053;Table 2). Although all groups show a peaked response, the response to warmer growth temperatures was principally an increase in total LA, LAR, and SLA in temperate species and a decrease in subtropical and tropical species (Fig. 5). Optimal growth temperatures for LA were 23.2 °C in temperate species, while subtropical and tropical species had maximum LA at 27.5 °C and 26.4 °C, respectively (Fig. 5). Warm-temperate species increased their LA on average by 87% with +3.5 °C and 133% with +7 °C warming (Fig. 5;Supplementary Fig. S6). Subtropical species increased LA with warming, with the increase becoming smaller with more warming from +78% in +3.5 °C to +25% in +7 °C warming (P≤0.001). By contrast, tropical species reduced LA with warming from −31% in +3.5 °C to −74% in +7 °C (P<0.001;Supplementary Fig. S6). LAR and SLA followed similar patterns to LA (Fig. 5;Supplementary Fig. S6). Temperate species increased their LAR at both +3.5 °C and +7 °C (Fig. 5). Subtropical species maintained LAR at +3.5 °C and +7 °C warming, but LAR was reduced by 40% with +10.5 °C warming (P<0.001;Supplementary Fig. S6). Tropical species reduced LAR by 42% with +7 °C warming (P<0.001;Fig. 5;Supplementary Fig. S4). SLA increased by 43% and 26% in temperate species at +7 °C and +10.5 °C warming (P=0.009), respectively (Supplementary Fig. S6). SLA reduced by 35% in subtropical species with +10.5 °C (P<0.05) warming and by 30% in tropical species with +7 °C warming (Fig. 5;Supplementary Figs S4,S6).

Photosynthetic capacity was reduced in response to increased growth temperatures and varied with rainforest group (bothVcmax25andJmax25;Table 3). While the response ofVcmax25to warming was not significant in temperate species, subtropical species switched from a positive response to +3.5 °C (+22%) and +7 °C (+21%) warming to a negative response with +10.5 °C warming (−41%). Tropical species had reducedVcmax25in both +3.5 °C (−28%) and +7 °C warming (−59%;Fig. 6D), perhaps linked to the decline in leaf N (Fig. 6C). Similar toVcmax25,Jmax25remained unchanged with warming in temperate species but decreased with more warming in both subtropical and tropical species (Fig. 6E). In subtropical species,Jmax25only decreased at +10.5 °C (−35%;P=0.0019;Table 3) while in tropical species,Jmax25reduced by 34% and 60% with +3.5 °C and +7 °C warming, respectively (P≤0.003).
Growth responses of rainforest trees to warmer temperatures varied along a climate gradient with increased growth in cooler climates (below the optimum) and reduced growth in warmer climates (above the optimum). On average, warm-temperate and subtropical species maintained their final biomass with +3.5 and +7 °C warming through an increase in LA and LAR with higher growth temperatures (Fig. 4;Supplementary Fig. S4). Subtropical trees reduced their biomass with +10.5 °C warming. Tropical species considerably reduced (>20%) final biomass with warming (Fig. 3), related to reduced LA and LAR (Supplementary Figs S4,S5). From the reduced biomass, reduced photosynthetic capacity at a common temperature and reduced LMF and LAR in tropical species (Supplementary Fig. S4), it was clear that tropical species did not cope well with warming, even with a moderate +3.5 °C warming and several replicates died in +7 °C warming. These trends are in line with other warming studies contrasting temperate and tropical populations (Cunningham and Read, 2003a;Way and Oren, 2010;Drakeet al., 2015). Thus, some warming could be beneficial to the growth of cool-origin and temperate plants, where plant growth tends to be temperature-limited and physiological and structural traits cope better with a broad range of temperatures. By contrast, warming is generally detrimental to plants already experiencing warm growth temperatures, with reduced tree growth under warmer air temperatures reported in many studies (Feeleyet al., 2007;Way and Oren, 2010;Vlamet al., 2014;Drakeet al., 2015,2017;Hubauet al., 2020).
While these average trends were similar for most variables across a climate gradient, climate-of-origin was a factor in the diversity of species responses. Large impacts were seen with warming of +10.5 °C in the subtropical and +7 °C in the tropical rainforest group, where species grew less well. On average, warm-temperate species continued to respond positively, but the cool-temperate speciesTasmannia lanceolatawas not able to survive at this warming level. Similarly, in both the subtropical and tropical rainforest groups, some species showed higher mortality rates at the highest growth temperatures (+10.5 °C and +7 °C warming, respectively). These species also had slow growth rates and did not thrive (Fig. 1). Their mortality indicates a higher sensitivity to warming and inability to survive at these growth temperatures. Despite these species-specific differences, overall rainforest groups had different responses depending on their climate-of-origin.
Cunningham and Read (2003a)found the highest growth rates for seedlings of tropical tree species at 30 °C with day/night temperatures of 30/22 °C (mean of 26 °C). These growth conditions were similar to our 33/23 °C range (diel mean of 27.5 °C), in which we found the highest growth rates. However, their growth temperatures were considerably lower compared with 40/30 °C, which represented the highest growth temperatures in our experiment with a diel average of 34.5 °C, a temperature range warmer than currently experienced in the native range of the tropical species in our study. The negative responses of tropical species to these higher growth temperatures likely induced photo-oxidative stress and damage to the thylakoid membranes (Teskeyet al., 2015) as physiological tolerance limits were approached (or exceeded). At high temperatures, reduced photosynthesis, increased photorespiration, and increased respiration can tip the leaf into a negative C balance leading to reduced growth (Teskeyet al., 2015). These observed impacts with a moderate degree of warming imply that tropical species are already operating at their thermal growth limit and point to the susceptibility of tropical rainforest species with warming. These results likely reflect the sensitivity of tropical trees to warmer temperature especially in longer lived late-successional trees (Manishimweet al., 2022;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023). Early-successional species with a more acquisitive growth strategy seem less vulnerable to warming compared with slower growing late-successional rainforest species (Mau etal., 2018;Manishimweet al., 2022;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023;Ntirugulirwaet al., 2023).Sullivanet al. (2020)showed from a global network of permanent plots in tropical rainforests that biomass and productivity declined with high day-time temperatures.Baumanet al.(2022)reported increased mortality rates in Australian rainforest in recent years, whileHubauet al. (2020)found saturation of the carbon sink in the Amazon tropical rainforest. The decline in longer lived late-successional species can potentially lead to shifts in community composition with a loss of heat-sensitive species and immigration of other tropical species with higher performance at warmer temperatures (Smithet al., 2009).
Species can exhibit plasticity to warming via changes in shoot and root dry mass allocation (Kasurinenet al., 2016;Liet al., 2017), which can potentially explain the response of plant growth to warming. In temperate trees, elevated temperatures generally increased aboveground allocation and growth (Way and Oren, 2010), associated with increased carbon uptake via more leaf area for radiation interception and photosynthesis, also observed in our study (Supplementary Fig. S5;Table 2). By contrast, tropical species increased their root mass fraction with warming at the expense of leaves. Allocating more biomass to roots in response to warming was consistent with other tropical rainforest tree species (Cunningham and Read, 2003a;Raichet al., 2006;Cheesman and Winter, 2013b;Drakeet al., 2019;Dusengeet al., 2021). Thus, the decrease in final biomass and relative volume growth rate at higher temperatures (exceeding 27.5 °C) in tropical species resulted from a significant reduction in LMF and LA with warming, reduced LAR, while RMF increased with warming (Fig. 4;Supplementary Fig. S4).
These different biomass allocation responses to warming can be related to the growth temperatures they experienced. Temperate trees growing at lower temperatures could allocate more growth to leaves with warming whereas tropical species growing at temperatures above 27 °C reduced their leaf allocation with warming (Fig. 4). Growing more leaves in tropical rainforest trees may not be beneficial due to increased transpiration and higher vapour pressure deficit (VPD) with warming (Lebrija-Trejoset al., 2011), while acquiring more roots for nutrient uptake in generally poor-nutrient soils may be a better investment (Moseret al., 2010;Raichet al., 2014;Huascoet al., 2021). By contrast, increased biomass allocation to leaves with warming in temperate forests can be beneficial for growth in combination with a longer growing season in these cooler environments.
In addition to increasing leaf area to maintain or enhance carbon gain under warming, trees can alter metabolic functions, for example via acclimation of photosynthetic capacity to warming (Gundersonet al., 2010;Way and Oren, 2010;Drakeet al., 2015). In our study, photosynthetic capacity, measured asAnet25,Vcmax25, andJmax25, responded negatively to warming (Table 3) with generally stronger reductions in tropical species compared with temperate species (Fig. 6). However, despite the reduced rates in response to warming, thermal acclimation of photosynthesis can also manifest as a favourable shift in the temperature optimum of photosynthesis (Crouset al., 2022). In a subset of the species represented here,Chouryet al.(2022)found a stronger increase in the temperature optimum of photosynthesis in subtropical and tropical species than in temperate species. Despite a shift in temperature optimum of photosynthesis, tropical species showed reduced photosynthetic capacity suggesting that these physiological adjustments alone were not strong enough to curb the negative effects of warming on growth. Previous studies found that warm-origin plants had constrained capacity to acclimate to warming, while cool-origin plants adjusted with warming over a larger temperature span (Cunningham and Read, 2003b;Way and Oren, 2010;Drakeet al., 2015). Limited physiological acclimation to warming has been reported in several tropical tree species (Crouset al., 2018;Huanget al., 2019;Carteret al., 2020;Dusengeet al., 2021). It is thought that the limited ability of tropical tree species to adjust to warming may be related to the limited temperature range under which they have evolved (Janzen, 1967;Cavaleriet al., 2015) resulting in a narrow thermal tolerance.
An important caveat to our results is that our plants were well-watered throughout the experiment. Rising temperatures can also lead to increased water stress via increased evaporation and higher VPD, even when rainfall is constant (Kumarathungeet al., 2020). Reduced water availability can lead to lower stomatal conductance and less carbon gain while stomatal conductance strongly responds to increased VPD (Lloyd and Farquhar, 2008;Middlebyet al., 2024). With lower stomatal conductance due to water stress or higher VPD, lower temperature optima of photosynthesis have been reported (Linet al., 2012;Kumarathungeet al., 2020;Slotet al., 2024). Increased stomatal limitation with either lower water availability or increased VPD eliminated most beneficial effects of increased temperatures on photosynthesis (Reichet al., 2018;Kumarathungeet al., 2020;Middlebyet al., 2024;Slotet al., 2024). Thus, plant responses to warming depend on soil moisture (Reichet al., 2018) with drought stress reducing positive effects or exacerbating already negative responses to warming.
Thermal acclimation represents the short-term (days–weeks) response to a change in growth temperature based on phenotypic plasticity, whereas adaptation occurs over generations, with genetic differences occurring among populations of a given species along their geographic distribution (Davis and Shaw, 2001). Few studies have separated the roles of adaptation and acclimation to understand the temperature response of plants at larger geographical scales (Linet al., 2013;Kumarathungeet al., 2019). While thermal acclimation can help plants to adjust to changing growth temperatures in the near term, the temperature tolerance of a given tree species may ultimately be determined by its long-term adaptation to its temperature niche. In a global vegetation model,Mercadoet al.(2018)showed the importance of considering both adaptation and thermal acclimation when modelling the thermal sensitivity of photosynthesis to predict future terrestrial carbon storage under warming. In a meta-analysis of global variation in the temperature optimum of photosynthesis,Kumarathungeet al. (2019)found that differences among mature trees in their native environments were mainly driven by acclimation to growth temperatures, rather than adaptation to temperatures at the climate of origin (Kumarathungeet al., 2019).
Other variables, such as growth rate, germination, and survival, may be more strongly determined by their respective range of thermal tolerances. In our experiment, cold-temperateT. lanceolatawas not able to survive in daily average growth temperatures >24 °C (Fig. 1). This growth temperature falls above the average summer temperature of ~17–20 °C experienced in its native distribution. Similarly, we found increased final biomass with warming in subtropical species up to +7 °C, but a lack of growth, and some mortality at +10.5 °C (Fig. 1G,H). Subtropical species also decreased LMF, LAR, and photosynthetic capacity with +10.5 °C warming, suggesting a thermal limit was exceeded compared with +7 °C warming. Another cool-temperate-origin species,Eucalyptus globulus, had limited capacity to adjust to warming when planted 700 km outside its native range (or +3.5 °C) (Crouset al., 2013). In line with our findings,Reichet al.(2022)reported the adverse impacts on growth and survival in boreal species when conditions were warmer or drier than at the warmest edge of their native distribution. Hence, it is clear that the plasticity of growth has an upper-temperature limit, beyond which growth rates are reduced and the species may not survive. Moreover, other processes such as interspecific competition from warm-adapted species can shift species composition in response to warmer temperatures (Penaet al., 2018). Evidence of a shift in species composition with climate warming has already been observed in several elevation gradients, including in the tropics (Boisvert-Marshet al., 2014;Liuet al., 2018;Fadriqueet al., 2021;Coxet al., 2023;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023).
Lastly, some species may exhibit more physiological plasticity in response to warming than others even when they have similar geographic distributions. This plasticity allows those species to thrive in highly variable environments where conditions can be suboptimal (Atkinet al., 2006;Ishikawaet al., 2007;Valladareset al., 2014;Zhanget al., 2015;Aspinwallet al., 2017). In our experiment, two tropical species,S. wilsoniiandC. mackinnoniana, were more vulnerable to warming, showing 75% and 63% mortality, respectively, in 34.5 °C, whereasX. chrysanthusfrom the same group had 100% survival and maintainedAnet25at the highestTgrowth.Atkinet al.(2006)found that fast-growing species exhibited more plasticity and had greater metabolic acclimation capacity in response to warmer growth temperatures, compared with their slow-growing counterparts from a similar biome. In our experiment, subtropicalB. citriodoraand tropicalX. chrysanthusdid not show significant reductions in the photosynthetic rate at +3.5 °C and maintained higher relative volume growth rates compared with slower-growing species within the (sub)tropical rainforest group (Figs 1,2). Several other studies highlighted contrasting growth rate responses with warming in fast-growing tropical pioneer species compared with slower-growing late-successional species (Cheesman and Winter, 2013a;Slot and Winter, 2017a;Dusengeet al., 2021;Wittemannet al., 2022;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023). These studies collectively point to a potential shift in species composition where species with an opportunistic and fast growth strategy may be more capable of adapting to warming, in contrast to late-successional species, which tend to decrease their growth rate in response to higher growth temperatures.
Our results demonstrate that the magnitude and direction of the response to warming differs between tropical and temperate rainforest tree seedlings. Temperate and subtropical species mostly maintained their growth in response to warming, even outside their native range, via an increase in total leaf area and leaf mass fraction, indicating that temperate and subtropical species are likely able to cope with a larger temperature increase than tropical species. Tropical species reduced their final biomass and growth rate at temperatures exceeding 27.5 °C via reduced leaf area, leaf mass fraction and photosynthetic capacity. These results suggest that even a warming of +3.5 °C will have a negative effect on the growth of tropical species, a temperature increase that is within the scope of predicted warming according to the RCP6.0 scenario (Masson-Delmotteet al., 2022). Our results are consistent with other studies indicating an overall reduced carbon sink in the tropics in the future (Huanget al., 2019;Hubauet al., 2020;Sullivanet al., 2020). However, early-successional species exhibit more plasticity to warming compared with late-successional species, which are the most abundant and long-lived species and may be particularly vulnerable to future warming (Cheesman and Winter, 2013b;Slot and Winter, 2017a;Mujawamariyaet al., 2023) but currently have a large role in the carbon uptake of tropical rainforests from the atmosphere.
