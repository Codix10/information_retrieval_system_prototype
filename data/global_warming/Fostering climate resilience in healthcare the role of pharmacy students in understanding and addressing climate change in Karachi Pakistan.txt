Climate change has emerged as a major global threat, exerting significant impacts on public health and healthcare systems.
The purpose of this study was to assess pharmacy students’ knowledge, attitudes, and practices (KAP) regarding climate change and its impacts on health.
A cross-sectional online survey was conducted among fourth- and fifth-year undergraduate pharmacy students across various public and private sector universities in Karachi, Pakistan. A content-reviewed questionnaire was used to collect demographic information, awareness, perceptions, and responses of students related to climate change.
The study revealed that 98.2% (n= 1211/1233) of the respondents were aware of the consequences of climate change, and most of themperceived it as a serious threat to public health. Approximately 43.1% of respondents reported taking steps to mitigate the impacts of climate change, even though 95% agreed that action must be taken to prevent it. Statistical analysis indicated that gender, university affiliation, and sources of information significantly influenced students’ opinions and understanding of climate-related issues.
The findings suggest that although pharmacy students possess theoretical awareness of climate change, they are not actively engaged in mitigation efforts. Integrating climate change education—emphasizing sustainable healthcare practices and the pharmacist’s role in addressing environmental challenges—into pharmacy curricula could help bridge this gap. Strengthening climate resilience through well-informed pharmacy professionals can make a substantial contribution to public health and sustainability initiatives.

The phenomenon of climate change has become an international threat that is harming the overall standard of human life [1]. There has been no investigation regarding the linkage between health risks associated with climate change dangers among university students in underdeveloped countries, such as Pakistan [2,3]. Global climate change is posing new challenges to maintaining a healthy environment, particularly in educational institutions. Weather conditions are changing as a result of increased atmospheric carbon pollution; some of these altered weather conditions include rising temperatures, heavy rainfall, and droughts, among others. Students, particularly youth, are the most sensitive to climate change; according to reports, climate change causes 88% of the disease burden, negatively impacting the quality of life [4,5]. The recent Intergovernmental Panel on Changes to the Climate Change (IPCC) Report proposes that governments must take strict measures to counteract climate change; it also provides a brief overview of the current state of climate change. It is a priority for governments to comprehend and raise awareness about how the climate is changing and the role of humans, the state of knowledge about potential climate futures, climate information relevant to regions, and minimizing human-induced climate change [6,7].
A research study conducted in Pakistan’s Punjab province identified the effects and relevance of climate change on the local communities. Awareness initiatives should be implemented to improve understanding of climate change scenarios. Excessive greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions into the atmosphere have been observed throughout Pakistan as a result of human activities such as inefficient energy use, rapid urban expansion, unsuitable disposal of waste, economic growth, expanded logistics, methods of agriculture, and improper livestock management [8]. Climate change affects vulnerable populations in developing countries, resulting in mental health issues; therefore, addressing the serious issue of mental health due to the rapid change in the climate, improving mental health support systems, and incorporating climate change education for community benefits are critical [9]. Depression, PTSD, and suicide have all been linked to the effects of climate change, particularly in vulnerable populations such as children, older adults, pregnant women, and chronic disease patients [10]. Sadness, fear, anger, and helplessness are some of the emotional responses generated by climate change, which has resulted in many obstacles such as forced migration and cultural issues [11]. Climate change has been related to psychological suffering, notably in children during the phases of their physical, psychological, social, and neurological development, and in adults physical, psychological, and emotional after-effects of climate change are also observed [12].
Natural systems that have significant effects on the planet include forest fires, earthquakes, oceans, permafrost, wetlands, mud volcanoes, and volcanoes. These systems, along with human activities, contribute to climate change. The majority of human activity is associated with the energy and industrial sectors, as well as with forestry, land use, and land-use change [13]. Developed nations are also concerned that vulnerable populations’ health issues will worsen due to climate change. The main root causes of climate change-related factors affecting health outcomes in both developing and developed countries are more intense summers, scorching temperatures, winters, pollen particles, pollutants in the air, floods, unexpected infections such as chikungunya, tick-borne encephalitis, and food-borne disorders [14,15]. Gender variations may be differently affected across the world by the escalated effects of climate change [16]. According to a survey that was published in the Lancet Planetary Health journal, more investigation and efforts are needed to address the risks associated with climate change. Worldwide health experts, policymakers, and researchers should push initiatives to support local communities and populations in battling the devastating repercussions of climate change, which adversely affect humanity [17]. Organizations worldwide have acknowledged the necessity of educating medical practitioners about the devastating repercussions of climate change on health [18]. A research on climate change indicates that for students to understand climate change and its effects on human health, educators and policymakers participating in curriculum development should be encouraged to include teaching and training programs linked to climate change. A significant number of medical students and educators have expressed the need to remove current obstacles that hinder the improvement of the attitudes and perceptions of future health experts and students regarding the dangers of climate change [19–21]. Hence, the purpose of this study was to assess pharmacy students’ knowledge, attitudes, and practices (KAP) regarding climate change and its impacts on health across different universities in Karachi, Pakistan.
A cross-sectional online survey was conducted among undergraduate 4th and 5th year pharmacy students of different public and private sector universities in Karachi between October to December 2024. All the data parameters were set as per the STROBE guidelines. For eligibility, the inclusion criteria were that participants must be regularly enrolled pharmacy undergraduate students in the 4th or 5th year of the Doctor of Pharmacy program. Students from the medical, engineering and finance fields were considered in the exclusion group.
The study was approved by the ethical review board of Hamdard University Karachi, Pakistan, with the reference number ERC-FOP-2024-020. An informed consent statement was added to the online Google form as well as to the hard copy of the questionnaire. Only those who agreed to participate proceeded with data collection.
A 60% response rate was defined a priori as inclusion criterion for participating university’s adequate representation and for data reliability. Assuming 2000 students in 4th and final years, 80% power and 95% confidence interval and 50% estimated prevalence (as there was no local prior data on KAP of climate change was available among pharmacy students in Pakistan), the sample size was calculated to be 323 using the sample size calculation formula asn= [DEFF*Np(1-p)]/ [(d2/Z21−α/2*(N-1) + p*(1-p)] using an online sample size calculator. As the analysis was conducted at the individual level without cluster-adjusted sampling, a design effect of 1.0 was assumed.
Climate change is considered to be the key determinant for various health outcomes, including morbidity and mortality. The climate shift of Karachi from its norm is a drastic change in pattern [22]. Changes in extreme temperatures contribute to climate warming and its impacts on public health and society [23]. The knowledge and perception of Karachiites areconsidered to be the crucial factors for sustainable urban planning . The questionnaire was adopted from a previously published article after obtaining the necessary approval [24]. There were a total 19 items in the questionnaire. The first part was demographics, then there were questions regarding climate change knowledge, attitude and practice . A 5-point Likert scale was used in most of the questions i.e. strongly agree, agree, disagree, strongly disagree, and neutral; however, multiple dichotomous questions were used to check the appropriateness in the nominal scale.
After obtaining permission from the original authors of the questionnaire, the questionnaire was disseminated among the consenting pharmacy schools of Karachi. The pre-defined response rate as an inclusion criterion for university participation was set to be 60% from each university. All the registered pharmacy schools consented to take part in the current research, and therefore, 4th and 5th-year students were included as they have completed the majority of the applied subjects that include climate change discussion. For data collection, the questionnaire was shared in the official class WhatsApp groups, along with a detailed elaboration of the purpose of the study, with special emphasis on their participation based on willingness and confirmation of anonymity. All the students were asked to respond. The data were checked for incomplete/missing information, errors, or questions with no information, and those cases were removed from the analysis.
Data were analysed using SPSS version 20. The initial frequency distribution of the demographic parameters and other variables of the questionnaire was calculated. Data were further analysed by Pearson’s chi-squared test of correlation between different variables and a dichotomous variable of public and private universities for exploratory purposes. Questions were categorized into three categories namely Knowledge, attitude and Practice. Likert scale questions were scored from 1 to 5. A score of 75% or above was considered indicative of good knowledge, positive attitude, and good practice. The KAP score was further analysed for effect size estimation using binary logistic analysis to identify predictors affecting the KAP score. Mediation analysis was conducted using regression-based methods to assess the indirect effect of knowledge on practice through attitude, consistent with KAP theory. Data was considered significant whenp< 0.05.
Responses to the open-ended question on diseases perceived to be associated with climate change were imported into NVivo (version 15.3.2, Lumivero Pvt Ltd., Denver, USA) for qualitative analysis. All responses were reviewed and cleaned to correct spelling errors and harmonize synonymous terms. Similar disease expressions (e.g., “lung disease,” “breathing problems,” and “respiratory illness”) were grouped under a unified disease label, while clinically distinct conditions (e.g., malaria, dengue, chikungunya) were retained as separate codes.
NVivo’s word frequency query was then applied to the coded dataset to quantify the occurrence of each disease term. Based on these frequencies, a word cloud was generated, in which word size represents the relative frequency of disease mentions. The word cloud was used as a descriptive visualization tool to summarize students’ perceptions and did not involve weighting or inferential analysis.
Out of 2046 students in different universities, 1080 students of the fourth year and 966 students of the final year were contacted. Among them, 720 students in the fourth year and 513 students in the final year responded to the questionnaire, giving a 60.3% response rate. Demographic details are mentioned in Table1, where 60.6% of private university students responded to the questionnaire, and 79% were female respondents. The major sources of information were found to be the internet (68.4%), television (75.2%) and teaching institutions (53.8%). Current data showed that 98.2% (n= 1211) of respondents had awareness about climate change. Around 95% (n= 1183) of participants agreed that actions must be taken against rapid climate changes. The majority 89% (n= 1100) of participants thought that climate change is affecting or going to affect individuals and 82.6% (n= 1018) thought measures should be taken to overcome the problem. Around 20% considered it is an individual’s responsibility to take action against climate change and others believed that the issue should be handled by the national or international institutions or management boards, as shown in Fig.1.

Binary logistic regression analyses were conducted to identify factors associated with climate-related practice, attitude, and knowledge among pharmacy students. Separate models were developed for each outcome variable.
The knowledge model was statistically significant (Omnibus χ² = 35.44,p< 0.001) with good fit (Hosmer–Lemeshowp= 0.210), but low explanatory power (Nagelkerke R² = 0.039). Overall classification accuracy was 63.2%, with higher accuracy for predicting poor knowledge. Attitude was a significant negative predictor of good knowledge (AOR = 0.92, 95% CI: 0.88–0.96,p< 0.001). Receiving information from family and friends was positively associated with good knowledge (AOR = 1.39,p= 0.020). Other predictors, including gender, university type, and practice, were not statistically significant (Table3).

The Hosmer–Lemeshow test suggested borderline fit (p= 0.041). The findings indicate that attitude plays a pivotal role in mediating the relationship between knowledge and practice (AOR = 6.744,p< 0.001), indicating that students with higher knowledge tend to develop more positive attitudes, which substantially enhance good practice as depicted in Table4. There is, however, also a significant individual effect of knowledge on practice (AOR = 0.143,p< 0.001) that suggests that there are some other unexplored pathways affecting the outcome and suggesting additional pathways through which knowledge influences practice beyond attitude. Students demonstrated a strong perception about association of climate change with a wide range of communicable and non-communicable diseases. The most frequently cited conditions were vector-borne diseases, particularlymalaria,dengue, andchikungunya, indicating high awareness of the impact of rising temperatures and altered rainfall patterns on mosquito proliferation. Respiratory diseases, includingasthma,chronic lung disorders, andcough, were also prominently reported, reflecting perceived links between climate change, air pollution, and respiratory health. Additionally, students associated climate change with water-borne infections such ascholera,diarrhoea, andtyphoid, suggesting recognition of climate-induced disruptions in water safety and sanitation. Notably, cardiovascular conditions (e.g.,stroke,hypertension), heat-related illnesses (e.g.,heatstroke,dehydration), and allergic disorders were also identified, highlighting students’ understanding of both direct and indirect health consequences of climate variability.
The findings of the present study revealed the pharmacy students’ perspectives on the phenomenon and related health risks of climate change. The literature review highlighted that no such research has been carried out in Pakistan to explore prospective pharmacists’ insights into this global issue, who plays central role in sustainable healthcare delivery, pharmaceutical waste management and patient education on environmental health risks. However, many studies have been carried out to determine the knowledge and opinions of university students in other fields [25,26]. The current study moves beyond prevalence estimates and explicitly explains how knowledge translates into practice. Furthermore, the majority of studies on students’ environmental awareness have been conducted in developed countries [27,28]. This study adds to the pool of knowledge on environmental education and awareness by identifying how students at higher education institutions in developing nations perceive climate change and sustainable development.
Climate change is one of the biggest challenges to public health in the twenty-first century and all nations will be impacted if the threat posed by this issue is not addressed promptly. The World Health Organization (WHO) predicts that climate change will cause about 250,000 fatalities globally each year between 2030 and 2050 [29]. Public awareness and concern about climate change and its effects are influenced by a variety of factors, including economic status, demographics, culture, educational level, and vulnerability to climate challenges. Students can contribute significantly to university-wide programs aimed at mitigating and adapting to climate change [30]. Students can gain the knowledge and skills needed to inspire the wider community to adopt the mitigation strategies by engaging with the realities of climate change and its future challenges. Therefore, to effectively integrate climate issues into formal curricula and extracurricular activities, it is essential to assess students’ awareness, attitudes, and perceptions of climate change and its adverse effects [31]. One study reported that the pharmacy educators believed that climate change is detrimental to human health (74.7%) and it has an impact on pharmacists and pharmacy practice (51.6%, p 0.001) or pharmaceutical scientists and pharmaceutical sciences (57.2%, p 0.001) [32]. According to the current study, the majority of students who responded to the survey thought that addressing climate change is vital.
Some studies reported that young individuals’ primary source of information was typically the internet [33]. As an environmentally friendly resource, prioritizing the Internet as a source of knowledge offers several advantages [34]. According to the current study, mass media, followed by teaching institutions, were the primary sources of information on environmental issues. Firstly, using the internet to obtain knowledge lowers the need for tangible materials such as books and paper, which can have a significant environmental impact in terms of waste, transportation, and resource extraction. Furthermore, the Internet facilitates rational decision-making about environmentally friendly behaviors by providing rapid access to a wide range of information, including peer-reviewed research articles. However, there is a need to raise awareness about identifying and selecting trustworthy electronic resources.
The current data showed that 98.2% (n= 1211/1233) of the students were aware of climate change. A study conducted in Bangladesh reported that 60% of respondents believed that human activities such as logging, dredging, sand extraction, industrial development, the use of carbon-emitting vehicles, and tree-cutting are ruining the environment and are responsible for climate change [30]. Another study reported that 78% of the responding students agreed that human activities are among the major causes of climate change [25]. Around 95% of students in the current study agreed that actions must be taken against rapid climate change. A study conducted by Abou et al., reported that of those who participated, 47.2% thought it was critical to take action against climate change, with females being more eager to take action [24]. The same was the case found in the current study where female participants were more eager and enthusiastic compared to their male counterparts. Ladhar et al., reported that 91% of respondents were inclined to support sustainable pharmacy practices in their future professions, and 97% of respondents expressed their interest in learning more about this topic [35].
When individuals understand how climate change may affect their health, they are more likely to take preventative actions to protect it, such as reducing their exposure to pollutants or increasing their physical activity. The majority of students in the current study stated that they had never taken any action despite their fears about climate change. The observed negative association between attitude and knowledge in logistic regression reflect this finding, where students with higher knowledge often adopt a more critical or cautious stance regarding climate-related health practices. In other words, knowledgeable students may recognize gaps in current practice or systemic barriers, resulting in more cautious or critical responses in attitude assessments. Adjustment for other covariates in the multivariable model may have further contributed to this inverse relationship. This highlights the complexity of interpreting attitude in the context of knowledge acquisition. Nevertheless, they showed a dedication to altering their conduct in order to lower the risks connected with climate change and considered it a moral obligation.
In the current study, students’ attitudes on climate change and sustainable development were usually positive regarding their responsibility for a sustainable future. These results show that the causes of climate change are well understood. They agreed that climate change is having an impact on them or will have an impact on them, and they emphasized that steps should be taken to address the issue. It was observed that a significant percentage of the students believed that addressing climate change was an individual responsibility. Another study reported that the majority of Ohio pharmacy interns who responded to the survey acknowledged that pharmacists have specific responsibilities in climate action and recognized the influence of climate change on patient health in their community [36]. In the present study, around 40% of the students consider that the international governing bodies have the main responsibility for tackling climate change issues. It is anticipated that coordinated action among all key stakeholders is essential to effectively address the global challenge of climate change [34]. Governments and international organizations such as the United Nations, bear the major responsibility as they can coordinate global efforts, facilitate negotiations, and set international standards for climate action. However, every individual can make a significant contribution by endorsing climate-friendly legislation and increasing public understanding of climate issues [37]. The respondents of the current study appeared to understand this well. However, the majority of the students denied that the government effectively wants to reduce the climate change impact. They believed that developing and enforcing comprehensive policies focused on reducing greenhouse gas emissions, promoting renewable energy, and enhancing climate resilience. The current study showed the students’ understanding that climate change affects everyone’s health and respiratory disorders, vector-borne diseases and skin conditions were the primary health hazards perceived by them which are mostly associated with climate change. The parallel findings were reported by another study [24]. Weather extremes brought on by climate change have increased deaths due to disrupted healthcare delivery, poor air quality that exacerbates cardiovascular and respiratory conditions, and the spread of vector-borne diseases [38]. Acute and chronic diseases brought on by rising temperatures, insufficient water and food supply, air pollution, and vector-borne illnesses are only a few of the apparent and subtle direct repercussions of climate change as depicted in Fig.4from our research. An efficient public health response to climate change is essential in preventing illnesses and injuries, improving public health preparedness, and lowering associated risks. If drastic measures are not taken immediately, future adaptation to such changes will be more challenging and costly [39].

The majority of the students in the current study believed that climate change is already having a significant impact on human health in Pakistan. It is documented that Pakistan has emerged as the focal point of climate change, being one of the top 10 nations most susceptible to natural disasters, such as droughts, floods, and glacial lake outburst floods (GLOF) [40]. Concerns about climate change should be incorporated into professional education and training to improve pharmacists’ preparedness for successful engagement. By encouraging sustainable practices within the healthcare system and increasing understanding of the environmental impact of pharmaceutical products, pharmacists can significantly contribute to climate change mitigation as future healthcare professionals. They can support waste reduction initiatives, such as encouraging the use of eco-friendly packaging and minimizing the disposal of unnecessary medications. Additionally, pharmacists can help ensure that the healthcare industry adopts more environmentally friendly practices, support the development of sustainable medical regulations, as well as taking part in research initiatives examining the effects of climate change on health. Pharmacy students can be further inspired to take action by incorporating sustainability ideas into their professional training, increasing community outreach, and encouraging collaboration with other healthcare professionals. As a result, they must have the appropriate resources to promote public health and lessen environmental effects.
The disease patterns identified by students closely align with established scientific evidence on climate-sensitive health outcomes. The predominance of vector-borne diseases reflects appropriate awareness of the role of climate change in modifying mosquito ecology and disease transmission dynamics. Similarly, the strong association of climate change with respiratory and water-borne diseases underscores students’ recognition of air pollution and water insecurity as major climate-mediated health risks. Importantly, the inclusion of cardiovascular and heat-related illnesses suggests an understanding that climate change exacerbates non-communicable diseases, an area often under-recognized in public discourse. Overall, these findings indicate a reasonably comprehensive conceptualization of climate–health linkages among students, although the breadth of responses also suggests potential gaps in differentiating direct versus indirect health effects of climate change. Students’ perceptions largely mirror global evidence, indicating effective conceptual awareness, though structured curricular reinforcement is required to translate knowledge into preparedness and adaptive practices.
The study included only 4th and 5th year pharmacy students due to the fact that this cohort completed almost the majority of the professional and clinical subjects, enabling them to perceive public health concepts properly along with experiential learning in the actual healthcare settings. Thus allows for a more informed assessment of knowledge, attitudes, and practices related to climate change and healthcare. Exclusion of junior-year students, however, may limit the generalizability of the findings to all pharmacy undergraduates.
Similarly, taking only one study setting (Karachi) does not allow us to generalize the data completely, but still, being the largest metropolitan city of Pakistan, it houses people from all over the country living and studying here. Additionally, the city has faced extreme climate challenges recently, including heat waves, urban flooding and air pollution, making it a good individual case study area. Data can, however, not be generalized to all Pakistani cities.
Formal assessment of non-response bias was not performed due to the lack of individual data of non-respondents. The possibility of residual non-response bias cannot be completely excluded.
A key limitation of this study is the absence of a pilot study and questionnaire validation in the local population. The instrument was adapted from previously published tools, though, and they must have reviewed for content relevance; measurement error cannot be fully excluded. Future studies should incorporate pilot testing and reliability and validity assessments before large-scale implementation. Given the cross-sectional design, causal inferences regarding the directionality of relationships among knowledge, attitude, and practice cannot be established.
We also relied on the respondents’ accuracy and honesty in their responses because the study employed a self-administered online survey, which may result in response bias. Notwithstanding these limitations, we believe the survey offers insightful information on students’ viewpoints that may enhance their motivation for environmental protection and support the development of climate change programs in higher education.
Future research should explore additional mediators and institutional factors that may influence the translation of climate-related knowledge into practice. Longitudinal and intervention-based studies are warranted to confirm causal pathways and evaluate the effectiveness of curriculum-based climate-health training.
The study provides important insights into pharmacy students’ understanding of climate change and its perceived health impacts in Pakistan, while going beyond conventional KAP analyses by elucidating the pathways through which knowledge translates into practice. The findings demonstrate that although knowledge regarding climate change is high, its influence on practice is largely mediated by attitude, underscoring the critical role of attitudinal factors in shaping health-related behaviors. The observed complexity in the knowledge–attitude relationship suggests that higher awareness may foster critical reflection rather than automatic behavioral change. Collectively, these results highlight the need for educational and policy interventions that not only strengthen knowledge but also actively cultivate positive attitudes and provide structured practice opportunities. By addressing these dimensions simultaneously, pharmacy education can better prepare future professionals to contribute meaningfully to climate change mitigation and public health resilience.
The findings suggest that climate-health education for pharmacy students should move beyond knowledge dissemination toward attitude-shaping and practice-oriented interventions. Pharmacy curricula should formally integrate climate change and health modules, emphasizing sustainable pharmacy practices, pharmaceutical waste management, and climate-related disease prevention. Experiential learning opportunities, such as community engagement and sustainability projects, may further strengthen the translation of knowledge into practice. At the policy level, accreditation bodies and higher education institutions should incorporate climate-health competencies into professional training standards to better prepare future pharmacists for emerging public health challenges. Furthermore, large-scale, multicenter studies on climate change are recommended, including pharmacy undergraduate students across all academic years as well as postgraduate trainees. Such a design would allow longitudinal assessment of changes in knowledge, attitudes, and practices over time. Comparative analyses should be conducted within and between universities across different geographic regions of Pakistan to examine regional variations in climate-related awareness and engagement. Repeated assessments across multiple academic years would further enable evaluation of temporal trends and curriculum-related effects.
