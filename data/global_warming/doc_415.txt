Rising global temperatures threaten species with environmental sex determination by skewing population sex ratios. However, predictions of demographic collapse often overlook the potential for multigenerational adaptation mechanisms. Here, we investigated the transgenerational impacts of elevated temperature on sex ratios and reproductive physiology in the European sea bass (Dicentrarchus labrax), a modern teleost with a polygenic sex determination system highly sensitive to temperature. Over more than a decade and using > 3000 fish, we reared three successive generations (F0–F2) under control or elevated temperatures. In this study, we report detailed data from ~1500 juveniles of the F2 generation distributed across eight groups with unique thermal histories. Direct developmental exposure consistently induced masculinization, but we uncovered a novel compensatory parental effect. The influence of ancestral thermal exposure did not follow a simple cumulative pattern. Instead, its direction depends on the line's inherent sex tendency. Paternal lines with baseline sex biases produced offspring with a sex ratio skewed in the opposite direction when exposed to elevated temperatures. This significant plasticity suggests a potential adaptive mechanism that may counteract extreme demographic imbalances. Gonad histological analysis further revealed a significant delay in spermatogenesis in heat‐exposed males at one year, whereas female gonadal development and chemical composition were unaffected by temperature. Our findings demonstrate that predicting the impacts of climate change requires a multigenerational perspective. Transgenerational plasticity can generate complex compensatory responses that may enhance demographic resilience, whereas genetic variability among lines can modulate these effects on sex ratios. Whether this phenomenon is unique to sea bass or widespread among other temperature‐sensitive species remains to be determined. By integrating ancestral thermal exposure into eco‐evolutionary models, our results expand the discussion on population responses to global warming, and highlight the need for comparable multigenerational studies across taxa.
Keywords:adaptation, climate change, European sea bass, global warming, phenotypic plasticity, temperature sex determination
Rising temperatures can unbalance the number of males and females in species whose sex is influenced by the environment. In a decade‐long experiment with European sea bass, we found that heat during development produces more males, but the thermal history of parents—especially fathers—can unexpectedly push offspring sex ratios back in the opposite direction. This surprising compensatory effect suggests that fish can respond across generations rather than only within a single lifetime. Such hidden flexibility may help populations stay stable as oceans warm, highlighting the importance of long‐term, multigenerational studies when predicting the impacts of climate change.

The escalating pace of global warming exerts immense pressure on biological systems. Rising temperatures impact fundamental processes, such as sex determination and reproduction, thereby influencing demographic stability and long‐term population persistence (Edmands2021). Among the most sensitive parameters is the sex ratio, which governs the effective population size, mating opportunities, and ultimately, evolutionary fitness (Waples et al.2018). In most sexually reproducing species, natural selection favors balanced sex ratios because individuals of the rarer sex gain a reproductive advantage, a concept known as Fisher's principle (Fisher1930). However, climate change is increasingly undermining this balance (Geffroy and Wedekind2020).
In many ectotherms, sex is not genetically fixed but is sensitive to the thermal environment during early development, a phenomenon known as temperature‐dependent sex determination (TSD). Fish are especially vulnerable since, in numerous species, even those with predominantly genetic sex determination (GSD), elevated developmental temperatures can override genotypic sex, producing highly skewed sex ratios (Ospina‐Álvarez and Piferrer2008). Therefore, the number of potentially affected species is larger than that of TSD species alone. Skewed sex ratios may occasionally confer short‐term fitness advantages in specific ecological contexts, favor the transition from GSD to TSD (Holleley et al.2015), but under ongoing warming they can result in demographic imbalances, reduce effective population size and, in extreme cases, threaten species survival (Blechschmidt et al.2020; McNeill et al.2016; Refsnider and Janzen2016). Indeed, modeling and empirical data suggest that shifts of only 1°C–2°C could have major impacts on sex ratios in many fishes (Ospina‐Álvarez and Piferrer2008). Given that the IPCC projects global surface warming of 1.5°C–2.5°C within this century (IPCC2022), and that shallow aquatic habitats already experience heatwaves exceeding these thresholds (Wiberg2023), sex determination emerges as an underappreciated axis of climate vulnerability. Thus, an unresolved question is whether sensitive species have the adaptive capacity to cope with rising temperatures or fall into demographic traps (Geffroy and Wedekind2020; Janzen1994; Mitchell and Janzen2010; Piferrer2016; Valenzuela et al.2019).
Most research to date has focused on the immediate effects of temperature within a single generation. These studies only capture short‐term effects. A species' thermal response in sex allocation is considered to play an essential role in evolution, providing that during development, certain environmental conditions favor one sex over the other, posing a strategic advantage (Charnov and Bull1977). In fishes, elevated rearing temperatures lead to masculinization of the offspring (Ospina‐Álvarez and Piferrer2008), which, in some cases, can result in dangerously skewed populations (Honeycutt et al.2019). Our capacity to predict how direct thermal exposure influences sex ratios is based on models (Conover and Voorhees1990; Schwanz and Georges2021). However, a major limitation is the lack of long‐term outcomes of elevated temperatures in a multigenerational context in the literature. This scenario would more realistically mimic global warming events, in which successive generations would be exposed to such rising temperatures.
Beyond skewing sex ratios, numerous studies report that elevated temperatures can also induce other critical phenotypic changes in fish. In the reproductive system, these include oocyte depletion in sheepshead minnow,Cyprinodon variegatus(Bock et al.2021), germ cell degeneration in Nile tilapia,Oreochromis niloticus(Pandit et al.2015), and delayed spermatogenesis in zebrafish,Danio rerio(Valdivieso et al.2022). Furthermore, thermal stress can impact overall fitness parameters, reducing male survival, growth, fecundity, gonad development, and sperm motility in rainbow trout,Oncorhynchus mykiss(Butzge et al.2021), and altering stress response and energy allocation in European sea bass,Dicentrarchus labrax(Samaras et al.2018; Tovar‐Bohórquez et al.2024).
While numerous studies have examined how elevated temperatures affect sex determination, growth, and reproduction, less attention has been given to their influence on the chemical composition of reproductive tissues. However, elemental composition is closely associated with growth, metabolism, and gamete quality. Temperature affects the chemical composition of poikilotherms, with cold‐tolerant species exhibiting higher nitrogen and phosphorus levels, reflecting greater protein and RNA content (Woods et al.2003). Phosphorus and other elemental components show a strong correlation with ribosomal RNA levels, and growth rates increase after exposure to elevated temperatures (Main et al.1997; Vrede et al.1999). This suggests that analyzing chemical composition may provide a new dimension to gather important insights into how organisms respond to warming. Thus, further research is needed to clarify the effects of temperature on elemental composition across taxonomic groups.
An increasing body of evidence suggests that the legacy of parental or even grandparental environments shapes offspring phenotypes. Such transgenerational effects may mitigate or exacerbate the direct impacts of heat. For instance, offspring of temperature‐exposed rainbow trout parents exhibited improved survival and growth rates when faced with similar conditions (Butzge et al.2021), and high parental temperature exposure affected offspring growth rate in sheepshead minnow (Chang et al.2021). A transgenerational study in a coral reef fish,Acanthochromis polyacanthus, showed that repeated thermal exposure has a mitigating effect against otherwise deleterious effects (Donelson and Munday2015). Multigenerational exposure to increasing temperatures could result in either such compensatory mechanisms or in cumulative sex reversal effects, the latter potentially leading to extreme demographic imbalances and threatening species. However, whether such compensatory responses or cumulative effects extend to sex determination, a trait tightly linked to population viability, remains largely unknown. Addressing this knowledge gap is essential, not only for fish but also for reptiles and amphibians with TSD, where climate‐induced sex ratio skews have already been documented in the wild (see Piferrer and Anastasiadi2021, for review).
The European sea bass is an ideal model for addressing this question. This ecologically and commercially important species exhibits polygenic sex determination (PSD) with strong thermal sensitivity, which can also be described as GSD + TE, meaning GSD + temperature effects (Piferrer et al.2005; Vandeputte et al.2007), whereby elevated temperatures during early development masculinize offspring (Geffroy et al.2021; Navarro‐Martín et al.2009). Under rearing conditions, good gametes are obtained after 2–3 years for males and 3–4 years for females. Previous studies have shown that the epigenetic landscape of sex‐related genes in this species is susceptible to changes when exposed to elevated temperature (Anastasiadi et al.2018), and that parental exposure can have a compensatory effect on methylation levels (Sánchez‐Baizán et al.2025). It remains to be determined whether transgenerational exposure leads to phenotypic changes and if it does so in a stochastic manner or follows a predictable pattern. Given both its ecological and economic importance (Vandeputte et al.2019) and its plastic response to warming, the European sea bass offers a critical system for evaluating how successive generations respond to rising temperatures.
Here, we investigate the transgenerational consequences of heat exposure for sex ratios in the European sea bass trying to answer two questions: (i) does repeated exposure across generations reinforce the classic sex ratio reaction norm, or can compensatory mechanisms emerge that mitigate sex‐reversal effects? and (ii) do ancestral temperature exposures generate directional, potentially adaptive responses, or do they merely introduce stochastic noise into offspring sex ratios? To address these questions, we conducted a three‐generation experiment (F0–F2), manipulating both developmental and ancestral thermal environments under controlled culture conditions. By disentangling the contributions of the successive thermal exposure of the offspring (F2, developmental effect), the sire (F1, parental effect), and the grandsire (F0, grandparental effect) on the offspring (F2) sex ratio, and integrating analyses of body size, gonadal histology, and elemental composition, we provide a comprehensive test of how successive generations of fish respond to warming.
Our study revealed an unexpected compensatory effect, since parental temperature exposures modulate offspring sex ratios in directions opposite to initial biases, and in some cases even leading to feminization. The magnitude of the parental effect sometimes surpassed the developmental effect, highlighting the importance of the ancestral thermal history. This suggests that transgenerational plasticity, potentially mediated by epigenetic inheritance, could buffer populations against runaway sex ratio skewness under climate warming. Furthermore, the histological analysis revealed a spermatogenesis delay in males exposed to elevated temperatures, while females showed no significant differences and element composition was minimally affected. Beyond sea bass, these findings highlight the importance of considering multigenerational processes when forecasting the demographic resilience or vulnerability of ectotherms to global change.
Previously, the F0 (Anastasiadi et al.2018) and F1 (Sánchez‐Baizán et al.2025) generations have been studied. The present study investigates the F2 generation of European sea bass derived from F1 males. The breeding scheme began with a wild West Mediterranean pair (F1), whose progeny (F2) was reared at either control temperature (C, 16°C), with balanced sex ratios, or elevated temperature (T, 21°C), with male‐skewed sex ratios. In the F0 generation, four males (sires S1–S4) were selected to account for genetic variability, two originating from the C condition (S1, S2), and two originating from the T condition (S3, S4), hence creating four lines. After mating these four males with a wild Western Mediterranean female, their offspring (F1) were again exposed to C or T conditions, and eight F1 males (one exposed to C and one to T, for each of the four F0 males) were then crossed, again to a wild Western Mediterranean female, to produce the F2 generation. This design resulted in eight experimental F2 groups, each carried out in duplicate and defined by its ancestral (F0 and F1) and developmental (F2) temperature history. Since at each generation only one female was crossed with all males (a different female for each generation), the expectation is that the differences between groups are due only to males, thus this study specifically addresses paternal inheritance temperature‐associated traits. The females used to produce all generations (F0, F1, F2) were wild females from the Western Mediterranean. Although their precise individual thermal history at the larval stage is not known, the average sex ratio in wild‐born Mediterranean sea bass does not differ from 1:1 on average (Vandeputte et al.2012) meaning that their thermal history is most likely of type C. Groups will henceforth be referred to by a letter code triplet (e.g., CCC, CCT, CTC, … TTT; Figure1a), whereby each letter represents the thermal condition for F0, F1 and F2, respectively. For example, “CCC” indicates that the F0, F1, and F2 generations were all reared under control temperature conditions (16°C), while “TTT” indicates elevated temperature exposure (21°C) across all three generations. To facilitate comparisons across specific generational exposures, groups with either C or T in one generation, are collectively marked using an “X” placeholder (which is either C or T). For instance, “XCC” includes both CCC and TCC groups, which share control temperature exposure in F1 and F2, regardless of the F0 condition.

All fish were maintained at the Ifremer Plateforme Expérimentale d'Aquaculture (Palavas‐les‐Flots, France), accredited to use and breed laboratory animals (no. E34121926). The project was approved by the Animal Care Committee no. 36 under project authorization no. APAFIS #42431‐2023032914106656 v6.
To generate the F2, the subject of this study, a batch of 1300 mL of eggs from a single wild Western Mediterranean female was divided into eight aliquots of 120 mL, each fertilized with 500 μL of cryopreserved sperm from a different sire (eight F1 sires in total, representing four combinations of thermal conditions with two F0 sires for each). Fertilized eggs were incubated in 30 L incubators at 13.5°C. Four days post fertilization, two batches of 2500 floating fertilized eggs per sire were transferred into a 110 L larval tank, resulting in 16 tanks in total (Figure1a). Thus, each original F0 sire was represented by two F1 sires (one from C and one from T), and for each F2 progeny we allocated one tank exposed to control (C, 16°C) and one tank exposed to elevated (T, 21°C) temperature during the thermosensitive period, known to induce masculinization (Bláquez et al.1998; Navarro‐Martín et al.2009).
Fish in the elevated‐temperature treatment groups were shifted to 21°C from 10 days post hatching (dph) at a rate of 1°C/day, while control groups remained at 16°C until 75 dph before being raised to 21°C also at a rate of 1°C/day. At 81 dph (T) and 96 dph (C), fish with uninflated swim bladders were removed, and fish numbers equalized to 120 per tank. One of the 16 larval tanks was lost due to technical malfunction. When fish had reached an average fork length of > 80 mm at which sex ratio is considered fixed (153 dph in T, 187 dph in C), 100 fish per tank were individually tagged with a Passive Integrated Transponder (TAM‐S ISO tag, Intellibio, Seichamps, France) injected in the dorsal muscle under anesthesia and subsequently split into two 1 m3pre‐growing tanks to account for possible tank effects and reared as previously described (Anastasiadi et al.2018).
The final F2 cohort consisted of 1465 fish with fully documented ancestry and rearing histories. At 368–369 dph, all fish were euthanized using benzocaine (150 mg/L), sexed, weighed, and measured. Gonadal tissue was collected from 50 fish for histological analysis and 20 fish for chemical composition analysis.
Gonads from 50 individuals (testes:n= 10 control,n= 15 elevated temperature; ovaries:n= 11 control,n= 14 elevated temperature) were analyzed to evaluate the effects of ancestral thermal exposure on germ cell abundance in the gonads. Gonads were dissected, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS for 48 h at room temperature, washed in PBS, and stored in 70% ethanol. Samples were dehydrated, embedded in glycol methacrylate historesin (Technovit 7100—Heraeus Kulzer, Wehrheim, Germany), sectioned at 3 μm thickness (non‐serial sections), and stained with 0.1% Toluidine Blue in 1% sodium borate.
Microscopic images were acquired at 100× magnification using a Leica DMI6000 BD light microscope (Leica, Heidelberg, Germany). For each gonad, 10 randomly selected non‐overlapping fields were analyzed per slide across four slides per animal. Quantitative histological assessment was based on a stereological point‐counting method in which a grid comprising 540 intersections (54 × 10) was superimposed onto each image. The proportion of the area occupied by each germ cell type was estimated by calculating the number of grid points overlaid on each specific cell type. For males, the germ cell categories included type A undifferentiated spermatogonia (Aund), type A differentiated spermatogonia (Adiff), type B spermatogonia (B), spermatocytes (Spc), and spermatids (Spt), following established protocols (Fallah et al.2021; Rodrigues et al.2022). For females, oogonia (Og) and primary growth oocytes (Pg) were recorded. No sperm in males and no cortical alveolar (CA), vitellogenic (Vtg), postovulatory follicles (POFs), or atretic oocytes (A) in females were identified in the analyzed fields of either group. For each individual, the final result (mean ± SEM) was derived by averaging the data obtained from 10 histological fields.
To study co‐occurring elemental variations related to temperature in male and female gonads, we used scanning electron microscopy (SEM) coupled with energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), which enables the simultaneous detection and mapping of multiple elements in a single scan. The elements detected include light elements (C, N and O), P, S, and main cations and anions present in cells: Na, Mg, Cl, K and Ca. This allowed us to study possible co‐occurring elemental variations in fish gonads, such as elevated Ca deposition or Na leakage, as well as variations in C and O relative to N and P, indicating a shift of biochemical composition towards lipid accumulation or towards growth. This multi‐elemental capability surpasses methods like ICP‐OES or AAS, which require separate analyses per element or destructive digestion of bulk samples, as EDS provides spatially resolved, correlative data on elemental shifts. The gonads were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS, dehydrated in a graded ethanol series and critical‐point dried with liquid carbon dioxide in a Leica EM CPD300 unit (Leica Microsystems, Austria). The dried samples were mounted on stubs and sputter‐coated with Cr in a Q150T ES Plus (Quorum Technologies Ltd.). The observation and EDS analyses (or X‐ray analyses) were done in a Field‐Emission Scanning Electron Microscope Hitachi SU8600 (Hitachi High Technologies Co. Ltd., Japan) equipped with two Bruker X‐Flash 7|30 EDS detectors (BRUKER NANO GmbH). X‐ray analyses were performed in three different areas per gonad, each covering a large portion of tissue of similar conditions. The analysis was performed at 15 kV of incident energy during 180 s life time on 5 gonads per treatment and sex. Quantification to obtain normalized mass percent of each element present in the x‐ray spectra was done using Esprit v2.5 using ZAF correction.
Due to the accidental loss of one of the two duplicate larval tanks in the CTT group, the missing value of the lost tank, corresponding to the grandsire S2, was imputed. The estimation was informed by two key observations: first, male proportions of the groups followed a normal distribution (Shapiro–Wilk test:W= 0.97,p= 0.851), second, across other grandsire lines, the mean increase in male proportion between XTC groups and XTT (X represents either C or T) was 0.258 ± 0.042 (mean ± SD) in close accordance with what has been reported in the European sea bass (Navarro‐Martín et al.2009). Adding this increase to the male proportion of the corresponding CTC group, the most probable estimate for the CTT group's male proportion was of 0.46. Further, to assess the robustness of this estimation, a range of alternative estimates was explored, by considering values ±1, ±2 and ±3 standard deviations of the observed mean increase, encompassing approximately 99.7% of the probability. Changing estimates within this range had no significant influence on our findings (further details on the imputation method in theSupporting Information).
Data analyses and visualizations were performed usingRversion 4.3.3 (R Core Team2023) withtidyverseversion 2.0.0 (Wickham et al.2019). Tests were conducted using packagescarversion 3.1‐3 (Fox and Weisberg2019),lmtestversion 0.9‐40 (Zeileis and Hothorn2002) andrstatixversion 0.7.2 (Kassambara2023). Data was made publicly available throughzenodo.org(Ecker‐Eckhofen et al.2025).
Two‐sided Fisher's exact tests were performed to compare sex proportions between groups and tanks. To test multigenerational effects, a full factorial probit regression model was employed using the glm function with probit link function. Sex (as binary: male = 0, female = 1) was tested against F1 and F2 temperature (C, T) and F0 lineage (S1, S2, S3, S4), as it was more informative than F0 temperature. The final model structure was determined by comparing the Akaike information criterion (AIC) between models and selecting the one with the lowest score. The best‐fitting model included all main effects and their interactions (Sex ~ F0_lineage * F1_temperature * F2_temperature). The AIC was lowest in this setting with 1823, compared to the next lowest with 1905, which used F0 temperature instead of F0 lineage as a predictor.
To visualize the parental effect (F1 temperature) on the F2 sex proportion, male proportions were calculated for every treatment group and F0 line and probit transformed. No one‐sex groups (0 or 1 values) were present among groups, hence no adjustments were made. Subsequently, the difference between transformed male proportion between F1 C and T groups (XCX − XTX) was linearly regressed against the F1 C groups' male proportion (XCX), representing the sex tendency. Residuals were normally distributed (Shapiro–Wilk test,W= 0.968,p= 0.879), homoscedastic (Breusch–Pagan test, BP = 0.35,p= 0.554), and met the assumption of independence of errors (Durbin–Watson test,p= 0.98).
Welch'st‐tests were utilized to compare body size variables (length and weight), since sample sizes were large enough to account for outliers. Using non‐parametric tests did not change the outcome. A type III Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was used to analyze the effects on body size, where the model included sex, developmental temperature, parental temperature, and grandsire line as categorical predictor variables. Grandparental temperature exposure was not included in the model, since it is nested with grandsire line (Lines S1 and S2 are control groups, and S3 and S4 are elevated temperature groups for F0). To test main effects in a more robust manner, follow‐up type II ANOVAs were performed on data which were subset by grandsire line and sex, including offspring and parental temperature in the model.
Statistical comparisons for the histological cell count data between groups were performed using a negative binomial regression due to zero inflation (occurrences of no observations for some cell types for some samples) using theMASSpackage version 7.3‐60.0.1 (Venables and Ripley2002). To compare whether the subsample had different weight and length, Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) tests were applied using the R base packages.
For the chemical composition data, outliers within the technical replicates were identified by using Dixon'sQtest from theoutlierspackage version 0.15 (Komsta2022). The mean values of the technical replicates were then computed, with zero‐read values replaced with the lowest read (0.01%). The data was then transformed with centered logarithmic ratios before performing a principal component analysis (PCA). Normality of mass percent was tested using the Shapiro–Wilk test for each sex and element separately. In most cases, the data did not follow a normal distribution, so a Wilcoxon Rank‐Sum test was applied to compare between sexes and temperatures.
Comparison of the male proportions between the replicate tanks of each of the eight F2 groups revealed no significant differences (FigureS1), indicating absence of tank effects. In the F2 generation, developmental exposure to elevated temperature consistently resulted in significant masculinization (Fisher's exact test:p< 0.001; Figure1b). The average proportion of males increased by 0.194, rising from 0.436 (XXC groups) to 0.629 (XXT groups), indicating a consistent masculinization trend. This direct effect was evident across all F0 lines (Fisher's exact test, all:p< 0.001). This outcome, as well as the fact that individuals derived from different sires tend to have various sex ratios in their offspring, is well known and has been reported in several studies (Anastasiadi et al.2018; Navarro‐Martín et al.2009). In the current study, groups of fish reared under the same conditions, but stemming from different grandsires (S1 and S2 vs. S3 and S4), exhibited different sex ratios (Figure1c), which had also been observed in the previous F1 generation (Sánchez‐Baizán et al.2025).
The primary aim of this study was to investigate the influence of repeated exposures to elevated temperatures on sex ratios across generations. To disentangle grandsire‐ and sire‐specific effects, sex ratios were analyzed separately for each F0‐derived sire (S1–S4; Figure2a), based on the present F0‐specific variation described above.

For the S1‐derived groups, a clear cumulative masculinization was observed when both the F1 and F2 generations were exposed to elevated temperature (CTT). Male proportions increased progressively across the CCC, CCT, CTC, and CTT groups, from 0.40 to 0.56, to 0.60 and up to 0.84, respectively. A similar trend was evident in the S4 line, where male proportions rose from 0.25 (TCC) to 0.33 (TCT), 0.43 (TTC), and 0.74 (TTT). In both cases, groups with parental temperature exposure (XTC) displayed higher male proportions than their control counterparts (XCC), demonstrating a cumulative response across generations. However, this pattern was not consistent across all lines. In the S3‐derived groups, sex ratios did not differ between TCC/TCT and TTC/TTT (Fisher's exact test:p= 1), suggesting no parental temperature effect. The S2 line, although missing the CTT group, nevertheless revealed a strong parental temperature effect: male proportions were significantly lower in CTC than in CCC (Fisher's exact test:p< 2.2e−16). However, in contrast to S1 and S4, the direction of the response in S2 was opposite, with parental temperature exposure reducing rather than increasing masculinization.
This observed pattern becomes more evident when examining the results of the probit regression where sex was regressed against F0 lineage, F1 and F2 temperature as interaction terms (full results in TableS1). While there was no significant interaction between developmental temperature exposure (F2) and F0 line, the parental exposure interacts with the F0 line S2 (Estimate = 1.850,p< 0.001) and S3 (Estimate = 0.602,p= 0.020), both leading to feminization, confirming previous direct comparisons. No significant interaction between parental and developmental exposure was found (p= 0.184); nor were any three‐way interactions (p> 0.594) significant. Including the imputed data for S2 CTT did not change these results, nor did it yield significant interaction terms for S2 (TableS2).
In addition to the developmental temperature effects (Figure2b, left panel), the ancestral (parental) temperature effect is further illustrated in Figure2b(right panel), which compares the mean male proportion in F2 between groups where F1 fish were either in control or exposed to temperature (i.e., comparing the mean of XCC and XCT with the mean of XTC and XTT). Among the F0 lines, S1 and S4 showed a positive trend (cumulative masculinization), S3 showed a flat trend (no parental temperature effect) and S2 exhibited a negative trend (feminization). This pattern is more evident in Figure2c, where male proportions (probit‐transformed) of all control groups of F1 (XCX) are compared with the F1 temperature effect by comparing C and T groups (XCX—XTX). No significant correlation was found between sex tendency (x‐axis on Figure2c) and parental effect (y‐axis;r(5) = −0.67,p= 0.1). However, when including the imputed value for S2 CTT (see methods), this model closely resembled the original linear model (y= −1.3x+ 0.15 vs.y= −1.3x+ 0.14, respectively), yet with a stronger, and significant negative correlation (r(6) = −0.77,p= 0.0246).
This significant negative correlation suggests that the effect of parental temperature exposure on offspring sex ratio depends on the original sex tendency of the parent line. F0 lines that naturally produce more males under control conditions tended to show less masculinization (or even feminization) when exposed to elevated temperatures, while lines with lower male proportions under control conditions tended to show stronger masculinization in response to temperature independent from developmental exposure. This suggests that parental sex tendency modulates the impact of temperature on offspring sex ratios.
All 1465 fish were weighed and measured at 368–369 dph, with summary statistics found in TableS3. Females were significantly larger than males in terms of weight (69.0 ± 18.6 g vs. 55.4 ± 15.5 g; Welch'st‐test:t(1426) = −15,p< 0.001) and in length (156.4 ± 13.3 mm vs. 145.9 ± 13.1 mm; Welch'st‐test:t(1323) = −15.1,p< 0.001). When comparing fish exposed to elevated temperature (T) to those in control conditions (C), females, but not males, were significantly heavier (71.5 ± 23.5 g vs. 67.6 ± 15.2 g; Welch'st‐test:t(347) = −2.31,p= 0.021) and larger (158.4 ± 16.2 mm vs. 155.3 ± 11.3 mm; Welch'st‐test:t(365) = −2.69,p= 0.008). A type III ANOVA revealed that, similar to grandsire‐specific sex tendencies, significant interaction effects were observed between the F0 line, sex and developmental temperature for length (p< 0.026; TableS4) and approached significance for weight (p= 0.065; TableS5).
Due to strong interaction terms in the full ANOVA model, a type II ANOVA was performed for each sex and F0 line separately (exact results in TableS6). This approach highlights the factors that affect growth parameters and the degree to which they are dependent on the lineage. Notably, one condition (CTT) is missing for the S2 line; hence, interaction terms were not estimated (NAs in TableS6).
Briefly, (i) in the S1 and S2 lines, whose grandsires were reared at control temperatures, body weight was not significantly affected by any temperature treatment; (ii) however, in the S1 line, standard length was significantly affected by the F1 temperature in both males (p= 0.019) and females (p= 0.04); (iii) in the S3 and S4 lines, which stem from grandsire lines reared at elevated temperatures, developmental temperature significantly affected both weight and length; (iv) the S4 line showed a sex‐dependent response to developmental temperature, where females (but not males) were significantly affected by the F2 temperature in both body weight (p< 0.001) and standard length (p< 0.001); (v) conversely, in the S3 line, males were significantly affected by the F2 temperature (Temp F2) for both body weight (p< 0.001) and standard length (p< 0.001), while females were instead affected by the F1 temperature for both traits (p= 0.027 for weight,p= 0.017 for length). All other groups were unaffected by temperature, indicating that both the magnitude and the direction of response were F0 line‐ and sex‐specific. To summarize, these findings confirm that growth in European sea bass is lineage‐dependent, but that responses to identical rearing conditions vary by genetic background and sex, stressing the importance of considering F0 lineage in developmental studies.
The distribution of standard length (mm) among the fish selected for histological analysis showed no relevant differences between the eight experimental groups, each defined by a unique combination of developmental and/or ancestral exposures. This consistency in body size was observed in both sexes. Sampled female fish (n= 25) had an average length of 156.6 ± 13.4 mm, closely resembling the overall female population (156.4 ± 13.3 mm; mean ± SD; KS test:D= 0.107,p= 0.944). Sampled male fish (n= 25) were slightly longer on average (152.8 ± 12.15 mm) than the overall male population (145.9 ± 13.1; mean ± SD), but this difference was not statistically significant (KS test:D= 0.246,p= 0.107). These results confirm that the histology samples are representative of body size across both sexes and experimental conditions.
Histological analysis revealed a delay in spermatogenesis in males (counts in TableS7) exposed to elevated temperatures. Comparative analysis between control (C) and temperature‐exposed (T) males for each germ cell type revealed a statistically significant reduction in the number of B‐stage spermatogonia (p< 0.001) and spermatocytes (Spc) (p< 0.01) in the testes of T males. No significant differences were found for the early stages, undifferentiated (Aund) and differentiated (Adiff) spermatogonia A, and no spermatids (Spt) were found in T males (Figure3a). In control males, germ cells were observed progressing up to the Spt stage (Figure3b). In contrast, most temperature‐exposed males showed only early‐stage germ cells. In a few T males, spermatogenesis progressed further, reaching spermatogonia B (B, Figure3c), but these cases were rare. Most T males showed only Aundand Adiffspermatogonia (Figure3d). Overall, these results show a marked delay in spermatogenesis in males directly exposed to elevated temperature relative to their control counterparts.

Histological analysis of ovaries from both control (C) and temperature‐exposed (T) females (counts in TableS8) revealed that reproductive development was unaffected by the exposure to elevated temperatures. Consistent with these observations, no statistically significant differences were observed in the number of oogonia (Og) and primary‐growth oocytes (Pg) between the C and T groups (Figure3e). In contrast to the delayed spermatogenesis observed in males, female reproductive development was unaffected by the exposure to elevated temperatures.

To determine the effect of heat exposure, the gonadal chemical composition was compared between control and temperature‐exposed fish, analyzing each sex separately (values in TableS9). In males, both PCA (Figure4c) and box plots (Figure4d) indicated no significant differences between the control and heat‐exposed fish. A similar scenario was observed in females, although Na levels showed larger, yet non‐significant differences in the ovaries of heat‐exposed females (Figure4e,f).
Our study, involving 1465 F2 fish across eight experimental conditions and more than 10 years of work between the grandparental (F0) temperature exposure and the F2 offspring sampling, provides the first robust multigenerational evidence that ancestral and developmental thermal exposures interact in a sex tendency‐dependent manner to shape sex ratios in European sea bass. Crucially, we show that while direct exposure to elevated temperatures during development consistently leads to masculinization, parental thermal history introduces a compensatory effect, modulating the direction of the sex ratio shifts based on the inherent sex tendency of the lineage. Furthermore, we identified a clear delay in spermatogenesis in males exposed to elevated temperatures, whereas female gonadal development remained unaffected at this stage of life. The chemical composition of the gonads was primarily determined by sex rather than temperature. These findings highlight the importance of adopting a multigenerational perspective when predicting the demographic fate of species with thermally sensitive sex determination systems under global warming scenarios.
Consistent with previous research on European sea bass and other fish species with GSD + TE or TSD systems, we confirmed that elevated temperatures during the thermosensitive period significantly increase male proportions (Anastasiadi et al.2018; Ospina‐Álvarez and Piferrer2008). This masculinizing effect reflects a widespread reaction norm, posing a potential demographic threat under climate change. However, our three‐generation experimental design uncovers a more intricate picture. The most novel and significant finding is that the effect of parental (F1) temperature exposure on the F2 sex ratio does not follow a simple additive pattern. Instead, it correlates with the sex tendency of the F1 generation: lineages predisposed to producing more males showed a feminizing effect in the F2, while those with a female bias exhibited a masculinizing response. Although this trend is statistically significant only when including the estimate for the missing tank, which is based on the most likely value rather than direct model fit (see methods), the model's overall result differs negligibly, as evidenced by identical slope values with and without the estimate (Figure2c), supporting the observed pattern. In addition, sensitivity analysis shows that other plausible, yet less likely estimates also result in significant correlations (seeSupporting Information). Therefore, it is unlikely that this plasticity in the thermal reaction norm results from stochastic noise. Instead, it more likely reflects a potential adaptive mechanism leading to compensatory adjustments in sex ratio. Although the statistical significance of the correlation depends on the imputed value for the missing tank, the slope of the relationship is virtually identical with and without the imputation, and the result is robust across a wide range of plausible values (seeSupporting Information).
Similar, though not identical, mitigating effects have been observed in other species. For instance, in the coral reef fishAcanthochromis polyacanthus, transgenerational exposure to elevated temperatures partially restored offspring sex ratios that were initially skewed by warming (Donelson and Munday2015). Notably, this study demonstrated that this restoration required that parents experience elevated temperatures throughout their development, not just during reproduction, highlighting the importance of developmental windows in establishing transgenerational plasticity. Our findings extend this concept by showing that the direction of the plastic response (i.e., masculinizing or feminizing) is context‐dependent and linearly linked to the population's sex tendency, a phenomenon not previously described.
According to Fisher's principle, frequency‐dependent selection favors a 1:1 sex ratio to maximize the reproductive value of offspring, since the rarer sex gains a mating advantage (Fisher1930). Persistent masculinization under rising global temperatures would disrupt this equilibrium, skew sex ratios and thereby threaten population viability. The compensatory effects we observe, whereby the thermal history of the parents pushes sex ratios back toward balance, can be interpreted as a mechanism that may have evolved to correct such imbalances. This adjustment appears to respond not only to the immediate environment, but also to the ancestral conditions, potentially optimizing sex allocation based on sex‐specific fitness. Interestingly, our body size data show lineage‐dependent temperature effects. In general, females grew significantly larger under elevated temperatures, whereas males were unaffected. In this context, a parental effect promoting masculinization (as seen in the S4) might seem counterintuitive if the goal is a balanced sex ratio.
In a climate change scenario, persistent masculinization could be catastrophic for many species with thermally sensitive sex determination systems. A strong example comes from reported monosex populations of pejerreyOdontesthes bonariensisin Argentina inhibiting their forthcoming (Cornejo2003). The frequency‐dependent compensatory effect documented in our study offers a glimpse of hope. This suggests that some species may possess an intrinsic adaptive capacity to buffer the impacts of warming on sex ratios. In other words, species with robust compensatory mechanisms may facilitate evolutionary rescue. However, our data also indicate that not all genotypes share this plasticity. The differences observed among the F0 lines suggest that some genotypes may lack the capacity to adapt to successive thermal challenges, potentially leading to demographic bottlenecks and, over longer timescales, even the evolution of alternative sex determination systems adapted to local thermal conditions. Such scenarios are plausible and have been suggested to likely occur in reptiles and amphibia with TSD (Bickford et al.2010; Hulin et al.2009).
The compensatory, frequency‐dependent parental effect likely involves epigenetic mechanisms (Sánchez‐Baizán et al.2025). Such non‐genetic inheritance could provide a rapid population‐level response to environmental changes, outpacing genetic adaptation. Previous studies on European sea bass have shown that early temperature exposure alters the gonadal transcriptome, including genes involved in epigenetic regulation (Anastasiadi et al.2018). Furthermore, studies in other fish and reptiles have demonstrated that environmentally induced epigenetic changes can be inherited for at least one generation, even after the initial stressor is removed (Kelley et al.2021; Shen et al.2023; Valdivieso et al.2020). For example, in reptiles, histone demethylases like KDM6B have been implicated in temperature‐dependent sex determination, suggesting conserved molecular pathways across vertebrates (Ge et al.2018; Whiteley et al.2022). Despite these insights, a major gap remains since the specific epigenetic marks and pathways mediating ancestral temperature effects are still unknown. Thus, future research should focus on identifying these epigenetic signatures, which could provide more insights into the underlying mechanisms of ancestral temperature memory and inform predictive models of species resilience under climate change. If non‐epigenetic mechanisms were instead responsible, the developmental timing and physiological pathways through which the reverse‐direction bias emerges pose a challenge as to how these conflicting cues are integrated within the sex‐determining system at the organismal level. We can say, however, that what we can infer from the PSD architecture is that, due to Fisherian selection, males selected from T progenies (where we consistently see more males than females) should give fewer males in their offspring than males selected from C progenies (where the sex ratio is closer to 1:1). This is because, when more individuals develop as males due to elevated temperature, some that would have been female under a C treatment become males, but retain their additive genetic sex tendency to be females. Thus, in F2, we expect more males in the progeny of XCX than in the progeny of XTX. This is what we see in the S2 progenies, but not in S1, S3 and S4 (Figure2b), meaning something else is acting here. Articulating a developmental and physiological framework is beyond the scope of the current study and will have to be addressed in a future study.
Our investigation about the physiological consequences of thermal stress revealed a sex‐dependent response. Males exposed to elevated temperatures exhibited a significant delay in spermatogenesis, characterized by a significant reduction in advanced germ cell types such as spermatocytes and the complete absence of spermatids at the time of sampling. This suggests that, at one year of age, these males may have reduced reproductive readiness compared to those reared at normal temperatures. This underdeveloped gonadal state may have resulted from a temperature‐induced delay in spermatogenesis and/or germ cell degeneration. It is consistent with what was observed in adult rainbow trout males, which showed quiescent gonads lacking advanced sperm cells, suggesting a long‐lasting response to juvenile heat exposure (Butzge et al.2021). However, it remains unclear whether this represents a permanent impairment or a developmental delay from which recovery is possible at later stages.
In contrast, female gonadal development showed no significant histological differences between the temperature treatments. This may reflect the inherent slower pace of female gonadogenesis in this species at this age, or it could indicate a lower sensitivity of early female development to thermal stress compared to spermatogenesis. Among the various reproductive processes affected by temperature, gametogenesis is particularly susceptible to changes (Alix et al.2020), especially the final stages of oocyte maturation (Bobe2015; Tveiten et al.2001). Therefore, it is possible that effects of temperature on oogenesis emerge at later stages of development, warranting further investigation.
The analysis of the elemental composition of the gonads further supports the conclusion that the primary driver of differences is sex, not the temperature regime. The separation between male and female gonads in the PCA, largely driven by elements such as C and P, reflects fundamental sexual dimorphism in gonadal metabolism and composition. Interestingly, the elevated C levels in males were unexpected, as ovaries usually contain more fatty acids, but may be related to slower maturation and lack of vitellogenic oocytes in females. By contrast, the higher P levels in testes likely reflect the ATP demand needed for sperm maturation, as previously observed in mussels (Kapranova et al.2021). Temperature effects were non‐significant in both males and females, perhaps due to limited sample size.
Overall, the datasets regarding growth, biochemical composition, and histological analyses are not, in isolation, the primary drivers of the central finding concerning reverse‐direction compensatory sex ratio effects. However, these datasets provided essential physiological context to evaluate whether transgenerational temperature effects on sex ratios are accompanied by broader reproductive and metabolic consequences across generations. The histological data are particularly informative, revealing a temperature‐induced delay in spermatogenesis in males, whereas female gonadal development remains largely unaffected. This sex‐specific asymmetry demonstrates that elevated temperature does not uniformly impair reproductive development across sexes, but preferentially affects male gametogenesis. Such impairment may reduce male reproductive fitness and, at the population level, influence the selective pressures driving compensatory, frequency‐dependent sex ratio adjustments across generations. Consistent with this interpretation, the absence of strong temperature effects on gonadal chemical composition, together with pronounced sex‐specific differences, indicates that the observed sex ratio compensation is unlikely to arise from generalized physiological stress or reduced gonadal quality. Instead, these findings support the existence of targeted regulatory mechanisms—potentially epigenetic in nature—that modulate sex allocation without broadly disrupting metabolic homeostasis. Taken together, these results demonstrate that the ancillary datasets strengthen the interpretation that transgenerational plasticity enables compensatory adjustments that enhance demographic resilience under persistent environmental pressure.
This study, by virtue of its complexity and long‐term scope, has inherent limitations. Our focus on paternal inheritance was guided by practical considerations such as the ease of breeding males and the high mitochondrial DNA content in ovaries, as well as prior studies (Sánchez‐Baizán et al.2025). Furthermore, exposure of fish during an early developmental stage implies that males, the earlier‐maturing sex, may be more sensitive to thermal stress, as confirmed by the histological analysis, thereby making paternal effect highly relevant to investigate. As, within a given generation, all males were crossed to a single female, we consider that the differences between groups of offspring do not reflect female effects but only differences between males, as the male and female effects on sex ratio are additive in this species (Vandeputte et al.2007) except in the case of crosses between the Atlantic and Mediterranean lineages (Guinand et al.2017), which is not the case here as only Western Mediterranean broodstock was used. In addition, using only males means that knowledge gained from here can be used for future studies based on sperm analysis, which allow repeated sampling of the same individuals without harming or need for sacrifice and cryopreservation of their sperm (cryopreservation is not possible with fish eggs). Finally, sperm is a liquid sample that also allows the investigation of circulating ncRNAs, which are recognized as key mediators of transgenerational inheritance. Thus, focusing on males is therefore particularly suitable for studies based on a multigenerational approach.
Another limitation of the study is that TTT involved only S3 and S4, whereas CTT involved S1 and S2. This design was dictated by logistical constraints: in the F0 and in each subsequent generation, only two treatments (C or T) can be applied, each replicated with two different males, and the same individual cannot be exposed to both conditions during the sensitive window. Given the large number of animals involved and the use of a large‐growing marine species requiring substantial tank space, inclusion of additional sires was not logistically feasible.
Of note, recent theoretical work on the adjustment of reaction norms in species with TSD in response to changing temperatures has focused on females (Santidrián Tomillo2024), leaving a gap on the role of paternal inheritance that the present study fills. Furthermore, while we identified F0‐lineage specific effects, disentangling grandparental (F0) temperature effects from those of the F1 generation is challenging without an even larger number of lines and crosses, an undertaking that is impractical for a long‐lived species like sea bass. For context, the entire study (F0 to F2) was conducted for over 10 years and encompassed more than 3000 fish.
It is also important to note that we used males that underwent two generations of domestication at most. Nevertheless, it could be argued that they experienced unintended selection pressures that could have yielded lineage‐specific genetic or phenotypic characteristics, leaving unclear whether the compensatory mechanism identified here is representative of wild European sea bass populations. Thus, the possibility that the putative compensatory response reflects a family‐ or strain‐specific phenomenon, rather than a species‐level evolutionary adaptation, cannot be dismissed. However, to account for possible lineage‐specific effects in the founding population, we used four F0 males (two per thermal treatment) to try to avoid this issue of line specificity. Again, a larger experiment would have been better but even more logistically demanding and thus not practically feasible. The domestication effect cannot be entirely ruled out, but since wild females were used at each generation the effects of domestication, if any, are not expected to be too large.
The apparent lack of a strong temperature effect on female gametogenesis could be age‐related, as gonads were sampled at a relatively early stage of maturation. Due to the missing replicate tank for one replicate (CCT, S2), we had to impute the sex proportion, which especially in the case of lower numbers of groups, can introduce bias. We addressed this limitation by thoroughly testing our analyses with a wide range of probable values. Even highly improbable values resulted in significant regression for the main claim in this study (Figure2c), yet there remains the possibility that an extreme outlier would affect the trend. Despite the supporting evidence, this finding could still be an edge‐case for this experiment, genetic background or specific for European sea bass or species with PSD. However, similar observations regarding temperature compensation effects were made in a fish species with GSD, suggesting that this could be a general mechanism (Bonzi et al.2025).
Finally, we strongly advocate for similar multigenerational studies in other fish species with GSD + TE or TSD systems, since our findings could be specific. Determining whether the adaptive, compensatory parental effects we have observed represent a widespread mechanism or a species‐specific phenomenon is essential. Such comparative work will be critical for understanding the evolutionary and ecological resilience of thermally sensitive species in the face of ongoing climate change.
In this study, we systematically compared phenotypic changes, including sex ratio, body size, gonadal histology and gonadal chemical composition, in juvenile European sea bass (F2 generation) exposed to elevated temperatures versus control conditions during the thermosensitive period. Crucially, past generations F0 and F1 were successively exposed to the same thermal regimes, allowing us to identify transgenerational effects of elevated temperatures on key developmental traits. The scale and scope of this study—spanning more than a decade, involving more than 3000 fish in total, and around 1500 analyzed F2 fish in this study across eight different thermal conditions with replicates—enabled robust statistical comparisons, particularly for sex ratios and body size.
Beyond confirming the expected masculinizing effect of direct elevated temperature in F2, we uncovered a novel and significant finding: a linear relationship between the parental (F1) temperature exposure and their sex tendency, revealing a compensatory mechanism that adjusts offspring sex ratios in a frequency‐dependent manner. This transgenerational plasticity suggests that ancestral thermal history can modulate developmental outcomes in a non‐additive, context‐dependent way, providing hope for adaptability to changing environmental conditions during global warming. Moreover, differences between F0 replicates suggest that genotype influences the interaction between developmental and ancestral temperature effects, influencing both sex ratio and body size. Elevated temperatures also led to a delay in spermatogenesis, but oogenesis appeared unaffected, likely due to the premature stage of the fish. However, chemical composition analysis revealed sex‐related differences but no significant differences between temperature groups within each sex.
Taken together, our results provide compelling evidence that transgenerational thermal exposure shapes key life‐history traits in a temperature‐sensitive fish species. This has strong implications for evolutionary biology, climate change ecology, and fisheries management. We advocate the integration of such compensatory dynamics into eco‐evolutionary models to predict long‐term population responses to elevated temperatures. Furthermore, we urge the scientific community to conduct similar multigenerational studies in other species with GSD + TE or TSD systems to determine whether this adaptive mechanism is widespread or species‐specific. Understanding these dynamics is essential for anticipating the resilience or vulnerability of aquatic populations to accelerating climate change.
