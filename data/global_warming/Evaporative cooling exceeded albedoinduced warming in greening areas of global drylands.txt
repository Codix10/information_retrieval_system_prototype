Greening plays a key role in surface-atmosphere energy exchanges, exerting feedback on the climate. While several studies have examined greening feedback in forested regions, the effect on temperature feedbacks in global drylands remain poorly understood. In this study, we used a combination of satellite and reanalysis data to examine the global dryland greening trends over the past two decades and assessed the changes in the biophysical properties. Results show that the greening-induced changes in surface albedo affected net shortwave radiation; however, evapotranspiration linked to changes in soil moisture dominated the temperature feedback, accounting for approximately 54–83% of the feedback signal. This dominance was most pronounced for daytime surface temperature, where the evapotranspiration effect was up 66% more than that of surface albedo. Significant greening was associated with a decrease in daytime surface temperature of 0.53 and 0.8 °C/decade, while significant browning was associated with an increase of 0.86 and 1.32 °C/ decade. Although greening was significant over the global drylands, soil moisture availability strongly controlled evapotranspiration and its contribution to temperature feedback. As dryland continues to warm, moisture availability is important for plant functioning, which impacts the regional climate through surface and atmosphere feedback and, therefore, the sustainability of drylands.

Vegetation is a key component in the global climate system which plays a crucial role in surface-atmosphere energy exchanges1,2. Increase in vegetation enhances evapotranspiration, particularly in well-watered conditions, but may also lead to an increase in absorbed shortwave radiation through reduced albedo. Both processes induce negative and positive feedbacks on the climate3–6, and their effects may vary in different regions depending on the dominance of either of the biophysical parameters, which limits or reduces the effect of the other. In dry climates where moisture is limiting, and the atmosphere is relatively dry, vegetation exhibits control over the loss of water by adjusting the stomata to conserve water7, which limits the evapotranspiration and in-turn exerts feedback on the climate1. Given the unique characteristics of dryland vegetation, understanding the recent changes in vegetation activity across global drylands and their implications for the region has become increasing important.
Global dryland greening, linked to climatic effects and CO2fertilization, has also been reported in recent years8–10. At the same time, significant warming in global drylands has also been documented in several studies11–13. For example, Li, et al.12reported an increase of 1.2 °C in dryland temperature since the late 1940s, while warming of 0.032 °C per decade was also reported by Daramola and Xu13. According to Huang et al.11, warming between 1948 and 2005 in drylands was 2.1 times greater than other areas which contributed largely to the increase in global mean temperature. Such concurrent trends of greening and warming highlights critical questions about how the recent greening in these regions interacts with climate.
Previous studies on greening and climate feedback have been on the biophysical impact in the forested regions14–17. Studies over the years have examined the impact of vegetation change on surface temperature5,15,18–20and near-surface air temperature21–24with most of the focus on the vegetation changes from deforestation. For example, the loss of vegetation cover through forest loss caused an increase in the mean temperature over the tropical region but caused cooling in the temperate and boreal regions18. Similarly, greening in the northern temperate and boreal regions brought about warmer temperatures in the northern hemisphere25. In drylands, while some studies have examined the vegetation-climate feedback, little has been done in this area within the recent decades of the concurrent warming and greening to quantify the warming or cooling effect of greening. For example, Wu et al.26focused on the modulating effect of evapotranspiration on precipitation over the African arid region. Abel et al.27focused primarily on the dryland vegetation resistance to aridity and consistent greening despite aridity increase.
The question therefore remains; how has the recent greening impacted the change in temperature in global drylands? Using a combination of satellite and reanalysis data, we investigate how greening modulates surface and air temperatures, through moisture and radiation feedbacks across the global drylands. This paper is structured in the following ways: first, we investigate greening and browning changes in the drylands and evaluate their biophysical impact. This is followed by the quantification of the warming and cooling effects of greening and browning. Finally, we examined the moisture and radiation effects to determine the dominant driver of vegetation-temperature feedback.
This study covers the global dryland areas, defined by the aridity index (AI = P/PET < 0.65) as areas where precipitation to potential evapotranspiration ratio is less than 0.65. We used the aridity index data obtained from the Consultative Group for International Agriculture Research (CGIAR) Consortium for Spatial Information (10.6084/m9.figshare.7504448.v3) to define the dryland areas. To assess the vegetation dynamics, we used the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), which has been extensively used for global vegetation studies28–30and dryland ecosystem assessment31,32. The NDVI data was obtained from Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS), which is the MODIS/Terra Vegetation Indices Monthly Level 3 (L3) Global 0.05 Deg Climate Modeling Grid (CMG) (10.5067/MODIS/MOD13C2.006). The data has a spatial resolution of 0.05 degrees and comprises cloud-free spatial composites derived from the MODIS Vegetation Indices 16-day products (MOD13A2).
The surface temperature data were obtained from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS). It includes the day and nighttime surface temperature from Terra MODIS (MOD11C3) (10.5067/MODIS/MOD11C3.006) and Aqua MODIS (MYD11C3) (10.5067/MODIS/MYD11C3.006), both of which are composites derived from the daily Land Surface Temperature (LST) products and projected to a geographic grid of 0.05 degree, and are hereafter referred to as LSTdayT(Terra daytime LST) and LSTdayA(Aqua daytime LST). The Aqua MODIS data covers a shorter period from 2003 to 2020. Temperature data was obtained from the Climate Research Unit (https://crudata.uea.ac.uk/cru/data/hrg/cru_ts_4.05/), which is a global gridded data derived from a wide range of observation stations33. Additional reanalysis temperature data was obtained from the ERA5 reanalysis, a product of the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) (10.24381/cds.f17050d7).
Soil moisture data was obtained from the Global Land Evaporation Amsterdam Model (GLEAM v3.5) (https://www.gleam.eu/#datasets)34,35and European Space Agency’s Climate Change Initiative (ESA CCI SM v06.1)36,37. Additionally, GLEAM evapotranspiration data products, including vegetation transpiration and bare soil evaporation, were used. For surface flux, we used the ERA5 reanalysis latent heat flux and sensible flux, a product of the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). We used the MODIS/Terra + Aqua Land Cover Type CMG Yearly L3 Global 0.05 Deg (10.5067/MODIS/MCD12C1.006) to define the vegetation types by the International Geosphere-Biosphere Programme (IGBP) class descriptions. Despite the widely used nature of NDVI, it has some limitations, particularly in drylands. For example, dryland have very short precipitation periods which may not sustain consistent greening for a long period. To account for this, we focused on the peak greening periods, defined as the consecutive months with the highest vegetation activity for each pixel, which were then aggregated to annual means.
For this study, the selection of the vegetated pixels is very important considering the nature of the feedback. As a result, the vegetation index and the land cover data were used for the selection. From the NDVI data, we extracted only the pixels with an average annual NDVI > 0.2. We also used the land cover data to confirm the selection of the vegetated pixels. The reference area of interest, in this case, the non-vegetation, was also extracted using the land cover image and vegetation index. Trend analysis was done using the Mann-Kendall trend test, and the Theil-Sen slope estimator was used to determine the magnitude of change. Using the two-tailed student’s t-test, the trend significance was assessed at 95% and 99% confidence levels.
Another limitation of NDVI in drylands is the possibility of noise introduced into the data from background soil. Dryland are characterized with high proportions of exposed soil which may contribute unwanted signals to the NDVI data. In this study, we defined the area of interest based on vegetation change from the per-pixel trend analysis of the vegetation index. Quantifying how vegetation increase (greening) and decrease (browning) affect temperature feedbacks in global drylands involves multiple interconnected processes. In this study, we use NDVI as a key indicator of vegetation dynamics to examine how changes in vegetation influence biophysical variables such as surface moisture, energy fluxes, albedo, net radiation, and evapotranspiration. Figure1shows the network plot of the correlation between the long-term trends of these variables. NDVI exerts both cooling and warming effects through two main feedback pathways; a moisture-driven cooling pathway, linked to increased soil moisture, evapotranspiration, and latent heat flux; and a radiation-driven warming pathway, associated with reduced albedo and changes in sensible heat flux. The strength of each link is revealed by the color intensity, with strong negative correlation indicated by blue and strong positive correlation indicated by red. This aligns with Charney’s feedback hypothesis42, which points to the vegetation-driven shifts in energy partitioning can produce divergent thermal responses depending on the dominance of moisture or radiation processes.

The NDVI trend showed a significant increase of 0.0073 per decade within the past two decades, between 2001 and 2020 (Fig.2). Although significant NDVI increase (referred to as “greening” hereafter) was observed over the drylands, the spatial distribution also revealed significant decrease in NDVI (referred to as “browning” hereafter) in some areas. Most of the significant greening areas are in western India, Pakistan, northern China, northern Rocky Mountains and Great Plains in US and Canada, Sahel and north-central Africa. Some of the areas that experienced significant browning are Ukraine and southeastern Russia, Western Australia, and Caatinga in Brazil.

We also examined the effect of vegetation change on the variation in biophysical properties. The correlation between vegetation and evapotranspiration trend was 0.54 (Fig.2c), which indicates the effect of vegetation change on evapotranspiration. Increase in vegetation led to an increase in vegetation transpiration (r= 0.63) (Fig.2e), which contributed to the increase in total evapotranspiration. Increase in vegetation also affected bare soil evaporation as bare soil evaporation decreased with increase in vegetation. Increased vegetation shades the bare soil from direct radiation and wind effect, which slows down evaporation and leads to a decline in the evaporation from the bare soil43,44.
Greening impacts the variation of surface heat fluxes, as revealed by the increase in latent and decrease in the sensible heat flux with vegetation increase (Fig.2f). NDVI trend also had a negative correlation of -0.46 with surface albedo trend (Fig.2d), indicating a decrease in surface albedo with greening and increase with browning. The changes in heat fluxes are also associated with changes in soil moisture, revealed by the spatial distribution of sensible and latent heat flux trends (Fig.3). The correlation between soil moisture and surface heat flux trend revealed a positive (negative) correlation for latent (sensible) heat flux, indicating an increase (decrease) in latent heat flux as soil moisture increased (decreased) in the drylands over the past two decades. Examining the changes in surface heat flux at the areas of soil moisture and vegetation change (Fig.4), we observed the limiting effect of soil moisture decline on the latent heat flux, as vegetation increased while soil moisture decreased. In this condition, sensible heat flux increased (1.85 W/m2per decade) but with less intensity compared to that with a simultaneous decline in soil moisture and vegetation (3.52 W/m2per decade). The change in evapotranspiration with greening and moisture change showed that soil moisture played a crucial role in controlling evapotranspiration change (Fig.3). Evapotranspiration decreased (-0.62 mm/month per decade) in conditions where vegetation increased while soil moisture decreased. Although greening occurred, the change in vegetation transpiration was very small, which implies that greening may not necessarily increase evapotranspiration.

We assessed the greening and other parameters relative to the no vegetation change areas (Table1) and found that NDVI over the drylands increased by 0.019 (0.035 over significant greening areas) but decreased by -0.014 (-0.029 over significant browning areas) per decade. The greening of the drylands caused the daytime surface temperatures to decrease by -0.43 °C/decade (LSTdayT) and − 0.13 °C/decade (LSTdayA), while it warmed by 0.42 °C/decade (LSTdayT) and 0.79 °C/decade (LSTdayA) due to browning effect. However, the greening didn’t cause the mean air temperature to decrease as it warmed by 0.14 °C/decade in the greening areas but warmed by 0.26 °C/decade over the browning areas. Considering the areas of significant vegetation change, the daytime surface temperature increased by 0.86 °C/decade (LSTdayT) and 1.32 °C/decade (LSTdayA) from browning and decreased by -0.8 °C/decade (LSTdayT) and − 0.53 °C/decade (LSTdayT) from greening. Significant greening also slowed down mean air temperature as it increased by 0.07 °C/decade relative to the no-vegetation change area. Evapotranspiration decreased by -1.6 mm/month per decade over the browning areas but increased by 2.2 mm/month per decade over the greening areas. Land surface albedo increased by 4.4 × 10− 3per decade and decreased by -0.1 × 10− 3per decade in the browning and greening areas.

We also assessed the effect of greening and browning across vegetation cover types (Table2). The surface temperature increased by 0.68, 0.45, 0.24 °C per decade (LSTdayT), and 0.98, 0.79, 0.075 (LSTdayA) over cropland, grassland and savanna respectively due to browning. These areas experienced browning trends of -0.018, -0.015, and − 0.013 per decade, respectively. With greening trends of 0.029, 0.019 and 0.015 per decade over these areas, surface temperature decreased by -0.55, -0.59, -0.43 °C per decade (LSTdayT), and − 0.28, -0.22, -0.12 °C per decade (LSTdayA) respectively. The mean air temperature increased by 0.34, 0.22, 0.4 °C per decade due to browning and 0.14, 0.11, 0.28 °C per decade due to the greening effect over the cropland, grassland, and shrubland, respectively. The most decrease in evapotranspiration due to browning was in the shrubland (-2.0 mm/month per decade), which also had the least increase in evapotranspiration from greening. This effect was evident in the warming over the area, which was the most intense, as revealed by the mean air temperature trend of 0.4 °C per decade (browning) and 0.28 °C per decade (greening). The intense warming of the shrubland due to the limited evapotranspiration contributed largely to the warming of the drylands.

We examined the dominant drivers of temperature change by comparing the effects of evapotranspiration and surface albedo (Fig.5). The results show that evapotranspiration largely dominated across the drylands. Overall, evapotranspiration accounts for 54–83% of the total contribution to temperature change, with the highest effect on daytime surface temperature, where it was up to 66% more dominant than surface albedo (Supplementary Fig. 3). The dominant effect of evapotranspiration is closely linked to the availability of moisture as changes in evapotranspiration closely followed changes in soil moisture (Fig.6; Supplementary Fig. 1). Separating the per-pixel partial correlation into individual components, the dominant role of evapotranspiration is evident in the surface and air temperature changes (Supplementary Fig. 2). In contrast, the relationship between surface albedo and temperature revealed more spatial variation, with a high correlation in some areas. The high positive correlation was evident in the albedo effect in (Fig.5c, d), which suggests that the surface albedo effect dominated evapotranspiration in some areas. Greening and browning impacted surface albedo (Fig.2d), which corresponded to changes in net shortwave radiation (Fig.7). The net shortwave radiation change was consistent with greening and browning, for example in the western and eastern parts of Asia, the southern part of Africa, and most parts of South America. However, although the net shortwave radiation change was consistent with surface albedo change, the effect of surface albedo was not evident in the temperature feedback in most of the areas. Generally, over the drylands, surface albedo change didn’t substantially impact the temperature change (Fig.1).

This study demonstrates that greening exerts a considerable but highly soil-moisture-dependent influence on temperature feedback signals across global drylands. Using a combination of satellite, reanalysis data and model estimates from 2001 to 2020, this study reveals a significant overall greening trend across the global drylands which is consistent with previous studies that have reported enhanced vegetation productivity over the drylands since the early 2000s45–47. However, our study further demonstrates that this greening is not homogenous as there was co-occurrence of browning, leading to contrasting biophysical responses. Specifically, greening areas exhibited increased evapotranspiration, while browning areas showed reduced evapotranspiration, which is closely linked to the corresponding changes in the soil moisture.
This vegetation-evapotranspiration-soil moisture interaction aligns with findings from earlier studies that soil moisture exerts dominant control over evapotranspiration and surface energy partitioning in drylands48,49. Our result corroborates these findings, demonstrating that increase in soil moisture favors latent heat flux, while drying conditions enhance sensible heat flux, therefore inducing warming feedback. More importantly, this study further demonstrates that greening alone does not necessarily translate into enhanced evapotranspiration. Despite widespread greening, transpiration trends were weak across many dryland areas, indicating that soil moisture availability, rather than greening alone drives evapotranspiration changes. Similar findings have been reported in semi-arid ecosystems where greening did not lead to proportional increase in evapotranspiration due to water stress constraints50, suggesting that this limitation is predominant across dryland regions. In these moisture-limited areas, soil moisture directly regulates evapotranspiration48, as plants adjust their stomata aperture in response to available water, thus limiting transpiration9.
Vegetation greening and browning impact was also evident in the change in surface albedo at the pixel level, where greening reduced albedo and browning increased albedo. This is consistent with previous studies that showed increased vegetation cover reduces outgoing shortwave radiation through changes in the surface reflectivity51,52. Additionally, soil moisture changes contributed to the change in albedo, as wetter soils typically increase net shortwave radiation49,53. This was evident in our findings, where net shortwave radiation increased in greening areas and decreased in browning regions, reinforcing the vegetation-moisture-surface radiative interaction in drylands.
The combined effects of evapotranspiration and surface albedo changes produced important moisture and radiation climate feedback across global drylands. Increased evapotranspiration in greening areas contributed to surface cooling and slowed warming, whereas reduced evapotranspiration in browning regions led to intensified warming. Notably, despite reduced albedo associated with greening, which would typically enhance surface warming, the cooling effect of increased evapotranspiration dominated. This is consistent with regional studies in the semi-arid regions of Africa and Central Asia, which reported that evaporative cooling dominates albedo-induced warming in moisture-limited but vegetated regions54,55. Our result extends these conclusions to global drylands and shows that this dominance is dependent on sufficient soil moisture as the cooling effect is strongly constrained under drier conditions. This aligns with Feldman, et al.56, who showed that although greening has a net cooling effect on tropical warming, vegetation in tropical drylands reduces this cooling effect by 14% due to reduced evaporation and lower albedo. Among vegetation types, shrublands exhibited the strongest warming signals, corresponding with the largest reduction in evapotranspiration.
While evapotranspiration generally dominates the vegetation-temperature feedback, some dryland regions show a stronger influence of surface albedo. The change in surface albedo may be related to variations in soil moisture which is tightly linked to vegetation change, rather than direct reductions in background (soil) due to vegetation cover57,58. Moreover, the positive correlations observed between surface albedo and temperature in these regions likely indicate the influence of other factors, such as ocean dynamics, which affect the climate in drylands through the interdecadal signals of sea surface temperature59. Therefore, although surface albedo decreased, evapotranspiration remained the dominant driver in the vegetation – temperature feedback.
Overall, our findings emphasize that soil moisture availability is the central regulator of vegetation-climate feedback in global drylands. While greening trends are widespread, they do not necessarily mitigate warming unless accompanied by sustained or increased soil moisture. We show that warming is more pronounced in areas experiencing soil moisture decline, reinforcing the feedback effect in which drying soils enhance sensible heat flux and increase surface temperature. Such feedbacks are especially critical in drylands, where precipitation is low and highly variable, and continued warming and drying may push dryland ecosystems towards more degraded states, prolonged dry spells and increased frequency of drought, thereby, threatening ecosystem services and human livelihood60.
While this study provides a comprehensive assessment of greening and browning effects on dryland temperature feedback, there are some drawbacks and limitations of the study. The attribution of temperature signals from greening and browning is complicated by the concurrent influence of large-scale climatic drivers. Our analysis primarily captures the local surface processes, and we acknowledge that the observed signals may also reflect broader dynamical variability such as El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO). These factors may partially mask or amplify the biophysical effects of greening and browning.
Additionally, our analysis relies primarily on satellite-derived vegetation indices, reanalysis data and modelled estimates for the surface and atmospheric variables. Although widely used, these contain uncertainties that may introduced bias into the results in terms of the magnitude of observed trends56. With the sparse nature of in-situ observations in global drylands which limits robust characterization and validation of land surface processes, the observed feedback signals may vary regionally or under difference soil moisture conditions. Further studies integrating long-term ground observations with high-resolution modeling would improve the quality of results and improve attribution of underlying processes.
Finally, it is important to note our analysis did not account for differences that may exist in the grid-cell area; therefore, the results represent unweighted spatial representation as the cells were treated equally regardless of their physical size. This could lead to an overrepresentation of trends in smaller grid cells particularly in higher latitudes, and underrepresentation in larger cells close to the equator. Despite these limitations, and uncertainties, the general spatial patterns and relative contributions of greening and browning to dryland temperature feedback remain robust, supporting the key conclusions of the study while highlighting areas where caution is need in interpreting absolute magnitudes.
