Abstract
Identifying patient diagnoses from hospital discharge letters is essential to enable
large-scale cohort selection and epidemiological research, but traditional supervised
learning approaches rely on extensive manual annotation, which is often impractical
for large textual datasets. In this study, we present a novel weakly supervised Natu-
ral Language Processing pipeline designed to classify Italian discharge letters without
requiring manual labelling. After extracting diagnosis-related sentences, the method
leverages a transformer-based model with an additional pre-training on Italian med-
ical documents to generate semantic embeddings. A two-level clustering procedure
is then applied to these embeddings, and the resulting clusters are mapped to the
diseases of interest to derive weak labels for a subset of data, which are subsequently
used to train a transformer-based classifier. We evaluate the approach on a real-world
case study focused on detecting bronchiolitis in a corpus of 33,176 Italian discharge
letters of children admitted to 44 emergency rooms or hospitals in the Veneto Region
between 2017 and 2020. The pipeline achieves an area under the curve (AUC) of
77.68% (±4.30%) and an F1-score of 78.14% (±4.89%) against manually annotated
data. Its performance surpasses other unsupervised methods and approaches that
of fully supervised models, while maintaining robustness to cluster selection and a

1. Introduction
Electronic Health Records contain a vast and continuously growing amount of
patient information, but a significant portion of this data remains unstructured in
formats like clinical notes, reports, discharge letters and referrals.
Despite con-
taining valuable information, this unstructured data is often underutilized in the
downstream analysis due to their complexity and heterogeneity [1, 2]. While Nat-
ural Language Processing (NLP) has made significant advancements in extracting
and classifying information from text [3, 4], applying these techniques to clinical
narratives remain challenging.
Medical documents are characterized by a highly
specialized lexicon, with ambiguous abbreviations and frequent misspellings, a lack
of standard templates across hospitals and a limited amount of manually labelled
data [5, 6, 7]. Additionally, most NLP tools are optimized for high-resource lan-
guages such as English, limiting their transferability to languages like Italian, where
datasets and domain-specific linguistic resources are scarce [8].
Among the various types of clinical documents, hospital discharge letters play a
particularly important role: they summarize the patient’s condition, diagnoses, and
treatments, representing a relevant source of diagnostic information for both clinical
follow-up and population-based studies. Although international standard such as the
ICD-CM [9] exists for encoding diagnoses, physicians often prefer to provide textual
descriptions of their diagnoses because they are faster to write, more expressive, and
often the only option available in document templates [10, 11]. Even when ICD-
CM codes are included, previous research has shown that they can be incomplete or
inaccurate [12, 13, 14]. Automatic methods capable of identifying diagnoses directly
from unstructured discharge letters are therefore crucial for cohort selection and
epidemiological research [15], reducing the need for manual review, which is labor-
intensive and impractical at scale.
2
In Italy, every hospital or emergency room (ER) admission generates a discharge
letter that should contain at least one diagnosis describing the reason for admission
or discharge. However, these letters vary widely in format and terminology, often
mixing narrative and structured components. Developing robust NLP systems for
such data is challenging: traditional approaches rely on supervised classification
models that require a substantial amount of manually annotated data [16], an effort
that is costly and time-consuming for clinicians. This highlights a significant gap
in the field: the need for methods that can effectively exploit large, unannotated
datasets to extract clinically meaningful information without the burden of manual
labelling.
Previous studies applying NLP to Italian clinical texts have not addressed dis-
charge letters. Lanera et al. [17, 18] worked on supervised models to identify vari-
cella cases from notes of family pediatricians, while Sciannameo et al. [19] used a
BERT-based model to predict rehospitalizations for elderly patients from medication
prescriptions and hospitalization records. Hammami et al. [20] classified pathology
reports for cancer morphology using a rule-based approach, and Viani et al. [21, 22]
extracted entities from cardiology reports through ontologies and recurrent neural
networks. Most international work on discharge letters has been conducted in En-
glish using resources such as the MIMIC database [23, 24] (see [25, 26, 27, 28] for
some examples), but research has also been conducted in other European languages
with limited resources, such as Bulgarian [29, 30], Dutch [31, 32], Czech [33] and
Swedish [34]. These studies, however, all relied on large annotated datasets, making
them difficult to reproduce in settings where manual labelling is infeasible.
Recent work has explored weakly-supervised approaches to mitigate the need
for large annotated clinical corpora. Most of these methods rely on task-specific
heuristics, such as disease-related keywords, regular expressions, or the transfer of
labels from structured data like diagnostic codes or medication records [35, 36, 37,
38, 39].
While effective within narrowly defined clinical settings, such strategies
are difficult to extend to new diseases, domains, or institutions, as they depend on
predefined lexical patterns or on the availability of reliable structured fields.
To overcome these limitations, we propose a weakly-supervised NLP pipeline for
automatically identifying patient diagnoses from Italian hospital discharge letters.
Our approach eliminates the dependency on manually annotated data and offers a
more generalizable solution than previous rule-based methods.
The pipeline first identifies diagnosis-related sentences from a subset of documents
and represents them through contextual embeddings generated by a transformer
model, previously pre-trained with public data in Italian related to the medical do-
main. These embeddings are then grouped through a two-level clustering procedure,
3
after which the resulting clusters are semantically summarized and mapped to dis-
eases of interest to produce weak labels. The weakly labelled data are finally used
to train a transformer-based classifier capable of recognizing those diseases directly
from discharge letters. This design enables large-scale, disease-agnostic classification
of clinical narratives while avoiding the costs of manual labelling.
We evaluate the proposed framework through a real-world case study focused
on bronchiolitis, one of the leading causes of hospitalizations in young children [40].
The dataset includes 33,176 discharge letters of pediatric patients admitted to 44
emergency rooms or hospitals across the Veneto Region (Italy) between 2017 and
2020, collected through the Pedianet network [41]. To the best of our knowledge,
there are no prior studies addressing the identification of bronchiolitis directly from
medical text, whereas existing works have relied on structured data for diagnosis
prediction [42, 43, 44].
In our evaluation, we test the weakly-supervised pipeline against manually la-
belled data, considering different classification backbones and comparing its perfor-
mance with both fully unsupervised and fully supervised approaches. The proposed
method achieves competitive performance, outperforming other unsupervised tech-
niques and showing only a limited gap compared with supervised classifiers.
Its
performance is robust to weak labels selection, and the analysis of the intermediate
steps indicates a strong potential for generalization across diseases.

mostly trained on general-domain web data [51], with limited exposure to biomed-

includes Italian documents from diverse medical sources, such as journal articles,
specialty-school admission tests, patient information leaflets, and medical-science

data to perform an additional pre-training of the transformer-based models we in-
cluded in our pipeline (Step 0.2 in Figure 1). To utilize the entire dataset, which
amounts to 564 MB, we automatically translated the non-Italian documents using
Google Translate’s APIs (Step 0.1 in Figure 1).
2.2. Semi-automatic labelling pipeline (SAL)
Developing a weakly-supervised classification pipeline for discharge letters re-
quires a procedure to derive weak-labels for at least a subset of data. Weak-labels
are labels that can be automatically associated to the data but that might contain
some noise. This section presents a methodology to construct these weak labels by
extracting diagnosis strings from discharge letters and clustering them. This allows
the creation of groups of diagnoses related to the same disease or symptoms that can
be exploited as labels. This step corresponds to Step 1 (SAL) of Figure 1.
6
Our approach combines regular expressions for extracting diagnosis strings and
a customized clustering pipeline based on a transformer model.
In the following
sections, we describe in detail these sub-steps.
2.2.1. Extraction of diagnosis strings
The first step (1.1 in Figure 1.a) is the extraction of diagnosis strings. Italian
discharge letters do not follow a standardized template, as each hospital has its
own, and even different departments within the same hospital may personalize the
template further. Nevertheless, we have observed that these templates often include
a designated section for the diagnosis, where the doctor should report the patient’s
admission diagnosis. This diagnosis indication should contain, in most cases, the
necessary information to determine the label for the discharge summary. However,
there are three main challenges in handling this diagnosis indication:
1. Not all discharge summaries include this indication, as it may be absent or left
empty by the physician.
2. Different templates use varying terminologies and position this indication dif-
ferently in the text.
3. Diagnosis strings are typically written as free text, with the same disease de-
scribed by different strings and different levels of detail
The first point is the primary reason why our pipeline focuses on classifying the text of
a discharge letter rather than just the diagnosis string. To address the second point,
we employ regular expressions to detect the portion of text where they are present.
Regular expressions provide a powerful and flexible approach for pattern matching,
making them suitable for capturing a template field with limited variations, such
as the diagnosis indication in our dataset. Appendix C reports the set of regular
expressions used to extract the diagnosis strings.
After this step, we have, for the majority of the letters, a string, typically a short
sentence, explaining the reasons for the patient’s admission. Due to the third point
mentioned before, it is not possible to directly exploit them as labels. To do this, it
is necessary to cluster them, as detailed in the following paragraph.
2.2.2. Diagnosis Clustering
Once the diagnosis strings are extracted, we use a tailored clustering pipeline to
group them meaningfully, aiming at identifying clusters related to the various types
of diseases and symptoms. This corresponds to Step 1.2 in panel a of Figure 1 and
is expanded in panel b of the same figure.
7
Minor pre-processing steps, including the removal of punctuation, dates, and time
expressions, are applied to facilitate the subsequent construction of a text represen-
tation that captures the semantic content of the document, while excluding elements
that are not informative for discriminating among diagnoses (Step 1.2.1).
After this pre-processing, the pipeline needs to construct an embedding of the
text that a clustering algorithm will subsequently use. This step (1.2.2) exploits the
transformer model we discussed in Section 2.1.
The transformer-based model produces embedding vectors whose size is 768, mak-
ing it necessary to apply a dimensionality reduction technique (PCA) over these
embeddings to use them as input for a clustering algorithm (Step 1.2.3).
After this step, a clustering algorithm can be applied to the resulting embedding
vectors (Step 1.2.4). We selected HDBSCAN [53] because it does not require spec-
ifying the number of clusters a priori, allows leaving part of data unclustered, and
can identify clusters with heterogeneous shapes and densities. These properties are
well suited to transfromer-based text embeddings, which often lie on complex, non-
linear manifolds and may form clusters with varying densities, irregular geometries,
and overlapping boundaries. Several commonly used clustering algorithms, such as
K-Means, Gaussian Mixture Models, and standard hierarchical clustering, are less
suitable for this type of data [54, 55], as further supported by a comparative analysis
reported in Appendix D.
To effectively group strings related to different diseases and symptoms, the clus-
tering must prioritize the intra-cluster similarity against the inter-cluster dissimi-
larity.
This has the drawback of creating multiple clusters associated with each
disease/symptom that are very compact internally, but that should be merged. A
second-level clustering can help merge these clusters that are initially separate, sim-
plifying the identification of the weak labels. To this purpose, a suitable represen-
tation for clusters needs to be produced. We employ a keyword representation in
which, for each cluster, we extract words that appear with significant frequency in
the cluster and do not appear with considerable frequency over the entire dataset
(Step 1.2.5). These keywords can be represented with transformer embeddings in
the same way as the original diagnosis strings and used for subsequent clustering
(Step 1.2.6).
2.2.3. Mapping clusters to diseases
Step 1.3 consists of mapping the clusters to the weak labels that will be used by
the classification model. This requires analyzing the diagnosis clusters produced by
Step 1.2, using their keyword-based representation (Step 1.2.5). The selection can
be fully automated with keyword matching or manual. To automate this step, we
8
propose the following procedure:
1. Definitions: identify a set of definitions for the disease or symptoms of interest.
Each definition includes one or more positive keywords (directly related to
the disease) and, if necessary, negative keywords (terms indicating alternative
diagnoses).
2. Cluster selection: for each cluster, select it if it contains all the positive key-
words and none of the negative keywords, according to at least one of the
definitions.
This procedure requires minimal manual effort upfront for defining keywords and
allows for a more precise selection than other methods, such as using similarity
scores between embeddings of definitions and cluster representations.
A keyword-based selection at this step is more straightforward and effective than
applying keywords directly to the diagnosis strings extracted in Step 1.1. The original
strings are often too heterogeneous, leading to false negatives due to varied termi-
nology and false positives from irrelevant information (e.g., negations, past medical
issues). Similarly, manually reviewing the original diagnosis strings is impractical
due to their large number.
2.3. Classification of discharge letters using the weakly-labelled dataset (WLC)
The classification pipeline that exploits the weakly-labelled data produced by
Step 1 corresponds to Step 2 (WLC) in Figure 1.a. A pre-processing step (Step 2.1)
removes headers and footers that contain information that is non-relevant to our
analysis (see Appendix B for details and results without pre-processing). The clas-
sification model we adopted is based on the fine-tuned version of the UmBERTo
model that we presented in Section 2.1. In this case, the transformer-based model is
enhanced with a linear classification layer that outputs the probability of having the
disease. It is trained with the discharge letters’ text and the weak labels assigned
to the letters based on the clusters to which their diagnoses have been assigned
(Step 2.2).
Given the limitation of RoBERTa-based models in dealing with texts longer than
512 tokens, when this is the case, we truncated the letter text to the first 512 tokens
after pre-processing, possibly including the diagnosis sentences, even if at the end
of the letter (in the setting in which we kept it). This is not a relevant limitation,
considering that 90% of discharge letters in our dataset are within 512 tokens after
pre-processing. In the case of datasets with longer texts, it is possible to chunk them
in portions no longer than 512 tokens, process them in parallel and combine their
outputs, taking the maximum probability.

2.4. Experimental outline
Our main evaluation is at the final classification stage of the pipeline, where we
can compare the results against manually annotated labels. In addition to this, we
evaluated the intermediate steps of the pipeline, in particular the diagnosis extraction
(Step 1.1) and clustering (Steps 1.2-1.3), on a randomly selected subset of 1,000
diagnosis strings that have been manually annotated.
Each of these strings was
marked as correct, partially correct , or wrong with respect to the fact of being a
complete diagnosis string and to the assignments to the first level and the second level
clusters. Additional results on the clustering steps, including robustness analyses
with respect to hyperparameter choices and clustering algorithms, are reported in
Appendix D.
In evaluating classification results, we compare our pipeline with some fully un-
supervised baselines: two rule-based approaches that do not require supervision
and with a zero-shot LLM for the Italian language (LLaMAntino-3-ANITA-8B-Inst-
DPO-ITA) [56]. The two rule-based approaches look for the presence of the terms
corresponding to the definitions of bronchiolitis used in Step 1.3, with the first one
(Rule-based full-text) looking into the entire text of the letter and the second one
(Rule-based diagnosis) looking only in the diagnosis strings extracted by Step 1.1.
The zero-shot LLM is prompted to indicate if the text of the text discharge letter
indicates a diagnosis of bronchiolitis, answering with a “Si” (“Yes”) or a “No”.
Additionally, we assess in detail the performance of the classification model of
our pipeline (Step 2.2) by replacing it with other classifiers:
1. The original UmBERTo model [51], without TPT (WS-UmBERTo)
2. XLM-RoBERTa-base [57], a multilingual version of RoBERTa [48], with (WS-
XLM-RoBERTa-TPT) and without (WS-XLM-RoBERTa) TPT
3. RemBERT [58], an improved version of the multilingual mBERT, with (WS-
RemBERT-TPT) and without (WS-RemBERT-BPT) without TPT
4. An LSTM-based recurrent neural network exploiting Italian Word2Vec embed-
dings [45] (WS-LSTM )
The three transformer-based models with pretraining are also evaluated in a fully
supervised setting, training them on the manually annotated labels (FS-UmBERTo-
TPT, FS-XLM-RoBERTa-TPT, FS-RemBERT-TPT), so as to have a measure of
the performance loss due to weak supervision.
Considering that our weak labels are derived from clusters of diagnosis strings
that we extracted from discharge letters, this might bias the classification models
into looking only into the diagnosis sections of the letters. To assess the presence of
this bias and to verify whether the models can exhibit good performance with the
remaining text of the string, we test the models in two settings:
10
1. With the original letters, possibly including diagnosis strings
2. Removing the diagnosis strings exploited to assign weak labels (when present)
We also measure the performance of the models on the weak labels, to assess the
difference with the ones observed on gold labels, and we verify the robustness of the
results on gold labels to the weak label selection, measuring the effect of removing
single clusters from positive weak labels. To further analyze the noise introduced by
weak labels, we also experiment with replacing a varying percentage of gold labels
with weak labels.
The classification results are evaluated in terms of precision (P), recall (R) and
F1-score (F1) for the bronchiolitis class and area under the receiver operating char-
acteristic curve (AUC). For classifiers producing a continuous score, the classification
threshold is set via Youden’s index [59]. We exclude AUC for rule-based classifiers,
as they do not produce probability estimates, and we do not assess the fully super-
vised models using weak labels. The positive class probability for the zero-shot LLM
is estimated by applying a softmax function over the token-level log-likelihoods of
the tokens “Si” (“Yes”) and “No”, following previous literature [60]. All metrics are
computed with 10-fold stratified cross-validation.
Since our data come from different hospitals and different LHUs, we also perform
additional experiments, reported in Appendix E, to verify the presence of differences
among them.
2.5. Training details
The additional pre-training of the transformer-based models over the E3C corpus
was performed with the masked-word prediction task, with a learning rate of 2·10−5
and weight decay of 0.01, using 20% of the data as a validation set for early stopping.
The LSTM baseline has the frozen Word2Vec embedding layer followed by a
32-unit LSTM layer, a dropout layer, a 16-unit fully connected layer, a dropout
layer and the final output layer. It is trained with Adam optimizer, with a learning
rate scheduler starting from 10−2, a decay rate of 0.7, 100 decay steps, using early
stopping.
The transformer-based classifiers are trained with the AdamW optimizer, using
a linear learning rate scheduler starting from 10−3, with a weight decay of 0.01, for a
number of epochs regulated by early stopping (leading to 6 epochs for the UmBERTo
models, 4 for the XLM-RoBERTa models and 3 for the RemBERT models). Initially,
only the last classification layer is trained, and after two epochs, the last transformer
layer is unfrozen. After four epochs, if reached, the last but one transformer layer is
unfrozen. These hyperparameters have been selected with grid search.

Due to the class imbalance, in the training folds the negative class is undersam-
pled, limiting its size to twice that of the positive class.
3. Results
3.1. Dataset
The dataset for the case study comprises 33,176 discharge letters related to chil-
dren who were admitted to Emergency Rooms and/or hospitalized in the Veneto
Region between 2017 and 2020, with a family pediatrician adhering to the Pedi-
anet network [41]. Inclusion in the Pedianet database is voluntary and parents/legal
guardians must provide informed consent for their children’s anonymized data to
be used for research purposes. The study was performed in accordance with the
Declaration of Helsinki. Ethical approval of the study and access to the database
were granted by the Internal Scientific Committee of So.Se.Te. Srl, the legal owner
of Pedianet.
The dataset covers 44 different hospitals in the region, which can be grouped into 9
Local Health Units (LHUs) and 2 university hospitals. Each discharge letter includes
the complete text of the letter, along with the date of the letter and an anonymized
identifier of the patient.
A manual review of discharge letters was conducted to
extract relevant diagnoses of bronchiolitis, resulting in 335 positive cases within the
dataset. This review was performed by a data manager with a clinical background,
with the support of clinicians for uncertain cases. These labels are the gold standard
to validate our pipeline, and we do not make direct use of them in the pipeline
training (except for the fully-supervised experiments).
Appendix A.1 reports additional plots with data distributions among hospitals
and LHUs. In particular, 10 hospitals cover more than 85% of the records.
The median length of the letters is 4,515 characters (1st-3rd quartiles 3,240-
5,806). An example of a fictitious letter is reported in Appendix A.2.

symptoms. ICD-9-CM codes are present only in 737 diagnosis strings (4.9%), cover-
ing 3,436 discharge letters (10.4%). The most common strings are the simplest, while
more articulate ones have single occurrences. The median length of the extracted
diagnoses strings is 49 characters, with first and third quartiles of 17 and 156 charac-
ters. Among the 335 bronchiolitis-labelled letters, we extracted 138 unique diagnosis

In the first clustering level, the 15,038 extracted diagnosis strings were grouped

to several smaller clusters containing 5 strings. The subsequent second-level cluster-

influenza. A few cases are related to multiple symptoms/diseases not completely

Only one of these 13 clusters (#4) has both “bronchospasm”
and “fever” keywords, and indeed, it contains a high percentage (70%) of bron-
chiolitis, accounting for an additional 15% of total bronchiolitis cases. Thus, these

five clusters are those that, without knowing the gold labels, would be selected
to define the weak labels for the training set, according to the two keyword-based
definitions of {positive={bronchiolitis},negative={}} and {positive={bronchospasm,
fever},negative={}}. Among the respiratory infection clusters, three other clusters
contain bronchiolitis cases (#6, #7, #8), but they are in a limited number.
For the second-level clustering, Table 3 presents clusters related to respiratory
infections. The second level clustering appears to have effectively merged some of
the clusters that were previously separated in Table 2: two related to mild bronchi-
olitis, eight related to bronchospasm, and five about generic respiratory infections.
The selection of weak labels for training remains unchanged since, looking at their
keywords, we select second-level clusters #1, #2, #3, #4 that cover the same records
as the clusters we would have chosen at the first level. Second-level clustering de-
termines a substantial reduction in the number of clusters (approximately 1/5 of
the original number of clusters in our dataset), simplifying manual inspection and
determining a positive effect on diagnosis-cluster mappings as observed in Table 1.
This cluster selection for weak labels covers 80% of the original bronchiolitis cases.
To understand the reason for the false negatives, we reviewed 20 letters labelled as
bronchiolitis in the gold standard but not covered by our weak labels. We found
10 cases where the diagnosis pertains to other symptoms or disease (e.g., otitis,
dyspnea, fever), but the bronchiolitis is mentioned in the letter, at least as a suspect
condition; 4 cases where bronchiolitis is mentioned in the diagnosis alongside other
diseases, leading to assignment to a cluster related to the other disease; 3 cases
of respiratory issues with no explicit mention of bronchiolitis, respiratory syncytial
virus, or bronchospasm and fever; 2 cases with no diagnosis string present; 1 case
where a family member has bronchiolitis, but the patient’s diagnosis is of a different
disease.
These results suggest that the absence of some gold labels in our weak
labels primarily arises from the lack of explicit bronchiolitis indications, with only a
few cases that could have been detected but were missed. They also highlight the
importance of using the full discharge letters for a model aiming to identify diseases
accurately.
To assess the effect of the TPT step on clustering results, Appendix D reports
the clusters obtained using the original UmBERTo model in our clustering pipeline.
The number of clusters related to respiratory infections is higher without TPT (34
vs 27), and their keywords appear more heterogeneous. Selecting weak labels by
applying the same criterion we used on our clusters, we would cover only 47% of
bronchiolitis cases with the clusters from the original UmBERTo model. Moreover,
these clusters are more hetereogenous since they introduce more false negatives in
the weak labels.

Table 4 presents the 10-fold cross-validation results for the classification task,
comparing our weakly-supervised pipeline in versions with different transformer-
based classifiers, fully unsupervised rule-based and zero-shot LLM approaches, and
fully-supervised versions of the transformer-based classifiers. Results are reported
for both the setting where we keep the diagnosis string and the one where we remove
it from the letter. The same results are reported in Table 5 evaluating on the weak
labels. Our weakly-supervised pipeline outperform fully unsupervised approaches
and maintains a limited gap with the fully supervised models. The TPT step con-
sistently improved performance across models: the differences are limited, but there
are no cases in which TPT hurts the performance. Among the transformer-based
classifiers, UmBERTo constantly outperforms RemBERT and XLM-RoBERTa. The
LSTM classifier has a significant performance gap with the transformer-based ones.
Rule-based full-text, which verifies the keywords in the entire letter, shows lower
precision and recall than the majority of the weakly-supervised models, emphasizing
the need for a more sophisticated approach. The results further decline when the
diagnosis string is excluded. Rule-based diagnosis, using only diagnosis strings, yields
better precision than the rule applied to the complete text, though recall remains
similar.
The zero-shot approach with the Italian LLaMAntino-3 LLM does not outperform
our best weakly-supervised results, but shows competitive results.
When comparing the classification results between weak and gold labels, we ob-
serve a decrease, particularly in precision. Notably, LLaMAntino-3 is the only model
which shows better results on gold labels than on weak labels. While this might be
expected for models that were not trained on weak labels, this does not happen with
rule-based methods that are probably more aligned to the way in which weak labels
are identified.
The transformers-based models show limited differences between settings with
and without diagnosis string, while these are larger for the LSTM classifier.
As expected, the fully supervised models demonstrated better overall perfor-
mance, though differences are modest.
In Figure 2, we observe the decrease in performance when we replace increasing
percentages of gold labels with weak labels. This picture is representative of many
real world settings where only a certain percentage of the data can have gold labels,
and shows a smooth and monotonic decrease.

We observed a good mapping between diagnosis strings and their clusters’ de-
scriptions in the manually evaluated subset of strings, and only a limited performance
difference between weak and gold labels (4-7 % of AUC), confirming that the SAL
step can effectively identify relevant cases as weak labels. Despite the inevitable
noise that weak labels introduce, this is limited enough to allow the model to per-
form well when evaluated against gold labels. Additionally, the differences between
settings where we retained the diagnosis strings and those where we removed them
are limited. This indicates that the model is effectively learning from the entire text
of the discharge letters, making it particularly advantageous when the automatic
extraction of diagnosis strings fails. Our manual exploration of some weak labels
further revealed that diagnosis strings do not always mention the disease responsi-
ble for hospitalization. In some cases, they only refer to symptoms, and in cases of
multiple diseases, not all may be reported. These findings highlight the necessity for
a classification model based on the complete text of discharge letters, as the one we
developed.
In contrast, rule-based approaches underperformed because diseases like bronchi-
olitis are not always explicitly mentioned or may appear in negated or hypothetical
contexts. While more sophisticated rule-based systems could address some of these
issues, such approaches lack the scalability and generalizability of our pipeline. Con-
versely, our pipeline can be easily adapted to other diseases, which makes it a versatile
tool for clinical NLP. The zero shot LLM performed better than rule-based and some
weakly-supervised settings, but it did not surpass the best weakly-supervised config-
uration. Despite the reasoning capacities of modern LLMs, their training data rarely
include documents and tasks of this type, limiting performance.
Domain pretraining (TPT) improved results across all models. The best perfor-
mance was obtained with UmBERTo, trained entirely on Italian data, confirming the

advantage of language-specific representations even over larger multilingual models.
Compared with fully supervised classifiers, our weakly supervised pipeline main-
tained competitive performance, with a limited and acceptable loss (< 6% of AUC).
This is particularly notable given the substantial time and effort required for manual
annotation in fully supervised setups: in our case study, annotating the entire dataset
would require approximately 3 minutes per discharge letter, amounting to more than
1,500 hours of expert time. The experiment in which we progressively replaced gold
labels with weak labels (Figure 2) reflects a realistic scenario where only a small
portion of the data can be manually annotated. Even in this mixed-label setting,
performance degraded smoothly and remained close to the fully supervised baseline
as long as a modest fraction of gold labels was available, indicating strong resilience
to label noise. The pipeline has also shown robustness to the cluster selection for
the weak labels. This highlights its value for settings where large-scale labeling is
infeasible.
While our results are promising, several limitations should be acknowledged.
First, the gold labels for bronchiolitis were assigned primarily by a single annotator,
preventing the estimation of inter-annotator agreement and introducing potential un-
certainty in the reference standard. Second, the multi-centric nature of our dataset,
though a strength in its diversity, also presents an imbalance in the number of records
from different hospitals and Local Health Units, which are also all located within a
single Italian region. This is not only due to the geographical distribution of the
population, but might also reflect some biases in the distribution of the family pedi-
atricians adhering to the Pedianet network. Testing the pipeline on a more balanced
dataset, and externally validating it on data from other regions, would strengthen
its generalizability and allow a more precise assessment of performance differences
among hospitals. Finally, the evaluation of intermediate steps relied on the manual
review of a relatively small subset of diagnoses strings. Extending this annotation
to a larger subset and assessing entire clusters would require substantial effort but
could enable a more detailed analysis of the performance of each component in the
pipeline.
Future research can extend this work along multiple directions.
The pipeline
should be validated on multiple diseases, particularly those with overlapping symp-
toms, using larger annotated datasets. Incorporating hospital or LHU identifiers in
hierarchical classification models could further improve performance by accounting
for site-specific documentation practices. Moreover, enhancing the interpretability of
model predictions will be crucial for widespread clinical adoption. Identifying which
portions of each discharge letter most influence the classifier’s decision would help
clinicians assess reliability and build trust. Techniques from explainable artificial
22
intelligence could be applied to reveal the reasoning process behind model outputs
and possibly recognize potential biases present in the training data.
These future developments would strengthen both the methodological robustness
and clinical utility of weakly supervised NLP systems for healthcare text, supporting
scalable and trustworthy solutions for real-world applications.
