Abstract
Feedback is a critical aspect of improvement. Unfortunately, when there is a lot
of feedback from multiple sources, it can be difficult to distill the information into
actionable insights. Consider student evaluations of teaching (SETs), which are im-
portant sources of feedback for educators. They can give instructors insights into what
worked during a semester. A collection of SETs can also be useful to administrators as
signals for courses or entire programs. However, on a large scale as in high-enrollment
courses or administrative records over several years, the volume of SETs can render
them difficult to analyze.
In this paper, we discuss a novel method for analyzing
SETs using natural language processing (NLP) and large language models (LLMs).
We demonstrate the method by applying it to a corpus of 5,000 SETs from a large
public university. We show that the method can be used to extract, embed, cluster,
and summarize the SETs to identify the themes they express. More generally, this
work illustrates how to use the combination of NLP techniques and LLMs to generate
a codebook for SETs. We conclude by discussing the implications of this method for
analyzing SETs and other types of student writing in teaching and research settings.
Keywords: Natural language processing, large language models, generative AI, student
evaluations of teaching, codebook generation, qualitative data analysis

Introduction
Text and speech constitute essential elements of qualitative data utilized across various re-
search domains [1, 2]. These data, including interviews, transcripts, reports, social media
posts, written answers to open-ended response questions, and essays, are traditionally an-
alyzed through methods such as qualitative thematic analysis [3]. However, these analytic
approaches often necessitate significant research investment, incurring costs for both projects
and researchers. For instance, conventional tools like MAXQDA [4, 5] and NVivo [6] come
with price tags and demand extensive research hours and revisions [7]. Moreover, as the
data grow so does the time needed for analysis. Despite these challenges, thematic analy-
sis remains prevalent in qualitative research due to its capacity to extract detailed insights
and themes [1–3]. Nonetheless, the relationship between data size and researcher time is
nonlinear, making large and existing datasets increasingly difficult to manage. Additionally,
datasets containing ill-formatted or unstructured data pose challenges to traditional quali-
tative analysis due to their incompatibility with standard tools or varying datatype content
[8].
During qualitative thematic analysis, researchers scrutinize data to extract or identify salient
themes and concepts, referred to as “codes.” This process involves applying codes based on
choices made by researchers, such as whether to include a theoretical stance or employ an
inductive or deductive coding approach [3].
Inductive coding, a “bottom-up” approach,
derives codes (and resulting themes) from the data itself, allowing the data to dictate the
identification of meaning and interpretation. Conversely, deductive coding involves applying
existing theoretical concepts or theories as a lens to interpret the data - a “top-down”
approach. Thus, inductive and deductive processes differ in the extent to which the data
themselves are dictating the contents of the “codebook”. Nevertheless, both processes are
subject to researcher subjectivity and positional bias, leading to the common suggestion that
multiple researchers should analyze the same data [1, 3].
Qualitative data hold substantial research potential given the density of the information they
contain but entail costs and time-consuming processes. Fortunately, recent advancements in
natural language processing (NLP), a subset of machine learning, integrate large language
models (LLMs) to automate language understanding and synthesis [9, 10]. These tools offer
significant potential for enhancing the accuracy and efficiency of qualitative thematic analysis
[11, 12]. Current NLP applications leverage LLMs to analyze sentiment, synthesize text,
design feedback systems, develop tutoring systems for computer education, and model stance
in student essays [13–20]. Despite these applications, there remains considerable potential
for NLP-directed qualitative data analysis to more closely align with current human-based
approaches.
In this paper, we aim to demonstrate the development and utilization of an NLP-based
method for inductive qualitative data analysis. We demonstrate this method on a set of
5,000 student evaluations of teaching (SETs) containing student feedback for instructors.
Our method generates a codebook that summarizes the prevalent themes and concepts within
these responses. We show that this generated codebook mirrors the codebook produced from
a previously completed traditional thematic analysis on a selection of the same dataset [21,
2
22]. While previous approaches have utilized NLP for deductive qualitative coding [23, 24],
we present a novel method for inductive thematic coding using a robust, iterative process
that extracts, embeds, clusters, and summarizes qualitative data into a codebook akin to
traditional inductive coding processes. This automated, data-secure method employs open-
source LLMs, avoiding proprietary models, like OpenAI’s GPT models and Google’s Gemini
models that may utilize user data for training [25, 26].
Additionally, our programmatic
approach utilizes open-source APIs deployed locally on one’s computer to automate the
coding process without the need for user interfaces like chatbots. Consequently, we anticipate
researchers can employ our approach to conduct meaningful qualitative analysis securely,
efficiently, and expediently. When combined with researcher oversight, there is significant
potential for this approach to broaden the scope of qualitative data analysis.

Literature Review
This section provides insight into the use of NLP techniques for analyzing textual data. We
explore the growing application of NLP in education research, including its use in analyzing
educational content, generating course materials, and assisting learning processes. Further,
we describe SETs and their use in engineering education as an example use case, which were
used to validate the methodology. We aim to establish the theoretical and methodological
foundations for using NLP in analyzing SETs in education by synthesizing these research as-
pects. Furthermore, we suggest that this methodology could be applied to similar qualitative
data beyond this context, which we will explore in a subsequent section.
2.1
NLP Use in Education
Machine learning (ML) continues to be a growing interest within research and industry.
As ML technology has advanced, so too have the applications leveraging its unique pattern
recognition and decision-making capabilities [27, 28]. Like many sectors, education is ripe for
transformation from ML-driven tools. From their capacity to analyze textual data to insights
from unstructured datasets like videos and audio, ML has immense potential to broadly
disrupt learning experiences, instruction methods, and educational systems [10, 15, 16].
Natural language processing (NLP), a sub-field of ML, entails designing algorithms and
models that enable computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language data
[10, 12, 29, 30]. Modern NLP tasks expand beyond single words, allowing computers to
approximate an understanding of the context of words.
Consequently, NLP models can
classify sentences, generate text content, and summarize text, among other tasks. Here,
“models” refer to the sets of rules or parameters that map input data to output predictions
[29, 30]. Simply, a model takes in information (the input) and produces a result or prediction
(the output). The model learns from examples in its training data, adjusting its internal
parameters to improve its predictions over time. Many NLP models are often highly complex,
requiring immense training data and tuning to achieve desired results. However, once trained,
these models can be leveraged for a variety of use cases and analyze or generate text beyond
3
what they were trained on [29, 30]. Of course, these models are limited and may contain
biases or exhibit unexpected behavior, requiring deliberate and careful use when employed
[31–34].
More recently, NLP models have become more effective at generating text given an input
prompt [35]. As many people have experienced with chatbots like ChatGPT [36, 37], NLP
is used to understand user queries, extract relevant information, and generate appropri-
ate responses. This might involve tasks like entity recognition to identify key information,
sentiment analysis to gauge the user’s mood feelings toward specific aspects, and language
generation to craft natural-sounding replies – all carefully designed processes that combine
into a single output [29, 30, 35].
Given these capabilities, NLP models have begun circulating among researchers and prac-
titioners in the educational context. Research leveraging NLP is diverse, encompassing a
range of analysis applications and utilizing its generative capacities [10, 12, 15, 16]. These
applications range from using NLP to extract features from text (e.g., [23, 24, 38, 39]) to
completing engineering assessments (e.g., [40–42]). When used to analyze textual data, the
features, summaries, and categories produced using NLP can then be used for analysis. For
instance, NLP enables the sentiment analysis of text, whereby the model extracts the un-
derlying tonality of a piece of text and outputs whether it is positive, negative, or neutral
[13, 43]. These analyses can then be used to gauge overall impressions and attitudes.
NLP models’ text generation capabilities have also been used in education to design tu-
toring systems, educational content, and personalized learning experiences, among others
[18, 44, 45]. These adaptive systems generate personalized recommendations and feedback
for users, while adjusting the difficulty, level of support, and included material, among others.
Instructors too benefit from NLP models which can automate the creation of educational
content, saving time and resources while producing high-utility materials [46–48].
Given the potential of NLP to analyze text, there is tremendous potential to leverage models
for highly time- and resource-intensive processes, like qualitative thematic analysis. The-
matic analysis is a widely used qualitative research method for identifying, analyzing, and
reporting patterns or themes within data [1]. It is a flexible approach that can be applied to
a range of data sources, including interview transcripts, focus group discussions, open-ended
survey responses, and other forms of textual data [1].
Many of the processes inherent to thematic analysis parallel the features of NLP [10, 38].
Consequently, investigations have used NLP for deductive code analysis [23, 49, 50], whereby
the model is asked whether an existing code applies to a piece of text. However, inductive
qualitative analysis, where the model produces a codebook without a reference, has been
limited [50].
Our objective is to introduce a methodology for generating a thematic codebook utilizing
NLP techniques, showcased through its application on a dataset comprising written feed-
back from engineering students at a research-focused university. In the next section, we
detail these responses, known as student evaluations of teaching, and their significance in
educational settings.
This contextualization serves to lay the foundation for subsequent
sections of our study.

2.2
Student Evaluations of Teaching
Institutions have periodically facilitated SETs [51, 52].
These surveys usually consist of
Likert-scale items that are meant to provide students with the opportunity to ”rate” an
instructor’s teaching effectiveness, as well as open-ended prompts meant to elicit students’
perspectives on their learning experiences, generating quantitative and qualitative data [52,
53]. Quantitative ratings, particularly those meant to measure overall teaching effectiveness,
are commonly used by institutions as input into such administrative decisions as salary,
promotion, and tenure [54, 55]. Students, however, are more likely to engage in the evaluation
process to share their perceptions about their learning experience in the class rather than
how their ratings may influence administrative decisions, and they share these perspectives
primarily through their responses to open-ended prompts [56].
These prompts generate
qualitative data that is challenging to comprehensively distill for instructors teaching large
class sizes, which results in the underutilization of these data. That underutilization results
in a disconnect between how SETs are used in practice and students’ motivation to engage
in the process [54, 55].
Natural language processing (NLP), a subset of machine learning, continues to grow in
education research.
NLP models can analyze and generate human language data, with
applications like classifying text, generating content, and providing personalized learning
experiences. SETs present one such use case for NLP, as the qualitative data from SET open-
ended responses are currently underutilized. The following section describes the development
of an NLP-based methodology for inductive codebook generation that can be used to analyze
SET data more effectively, a process mirroring traditional inductive thematic coding.
3
Methods
In this paper, we are presenting a workflow that involves extracting information, embedding
the extracted information in a high-dimensional vector space, clustering those embeddings,
and summarizing the clusters.
The goal of the extract, embed, cluster, and summarize
(EECS) workflow presented here is to provide an easy-to-use and effective way to extract
information from a large corpus of documents and distill it into a collection of themes. The
process mimics the practice of codebook generation in traditional qualitative data analysis
[57] but on a larger scale. In this case, those documents were SETs from introductory courses
in the College of Engineering and College of Science at a large, research university. In such
settings, it is common to have tens of thousands of SETs to analyze. The EECS workflow is
designed to be scalable so it can be applied to a large corpus of documents beyond education
settings. For demonstration purposes here we use a relatively small corpus of 5,000 SETs,
but in theory, the workflow can be applied to a much larger corpus, depending on computing
resources and the nature of the text units of analysis. Moreover, we believe the workflow
scales beyond this specific context and can be applied to other types of documents, such as
student essays, interview transcripts, and administrative records.
Figure 1 shows a flowchart of the EECS workflow. The workflow begins with the extraction

of ideas from the original documents. The extracted ideas are embedded into a vector space.
The embedded ideas are then clustered into groups of similar ideas. Next, those clusters are

or replaced with a different technique or model as needed. Indeed, there is reason to believe
that optimizing each step along the workflow would yield better results - a fruitful direction
for future work. The workflow is also designed to be scalable so that it can be applied to large
corpora of documents. Finally, we have designed this workflow to use open-source models
and tools for the broader research community’s use. These features allow researchers to run
the EECS process on their own machine, thereby eliminating the need to send third-party
providers, such as OpenAI or Google [25, 26]. The following sections describe each step in
the EECS workflow in more detail.

3.1
Extracting Ideas from Original Texts
The first step in the EECS workflow is to extract the information from the documents we
want to analyze. This step is intended to overcome the varied language found in respon-
dents’ free-form SET responses because excessive variation could cause larger problems in
the downstream analysis if too many similar options are present. This extraction step es-
sentially is a standardization step and a way to isolate each of the ideas expressed in the
SETs. In other words, we want to extract the main ideas from the SETs by disentangling
them into simpler, discrete ideas.
For this extraction step, we use a lightweight generative text model with the prompt given in
the Appendix, section 8.1. The specific model was the dolphin fine-tuned version of Mistral-
7b [58]. The extraction allows us to analyze each idea separately in subsequent iterations. For
example, if a student said, “I liked the in-class demonstrations and availability during office
hours”, we would want to extract the two ideas “in-class demonstrations” and “availability
during office hours”. The prompt is designed to be a general prompt that can be used for
any type of document so that the same model can be used for many other contexts. In
practice, the model generates text from the prompt and original participant response, which
is extracted in a short post-processing step.
6
3.2
Embedding the Extracted Ideas
The next step in the EECS workflow is to embed the extracted ideas into a vector space. By
doing so, we aim to represent each idea as a vector, so ideas can be compared using math-
ematical operations such as cosine similarity, or be clustered together to find semantically
similar ideas. We use a pre-trained text embedding model available from HuggingFace [59]
to embed the ideas into a vector space. The specific model used here was the UAE-Angle
model [60], but other models would also work in this step. We used that model because it
had the highest performance across a range of performance benchmarks at the time among
models that could be run locally on one’s machine. This was an important consideration to
avoid sending data to third parties.
The embedding model uses the text for each extracted idea as inputs and outputs a vector.
The vectors from this UAE-Angle model are 1,024-dimensional, so the text for an idea
is represented as a vector (i.e., collection of numbers) of length 1,024. The goal behind
embedding models is to leverage the theory of distributional semantics, which asserts that
words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings [61, 62]. Using distributive
semantics, we can represent the semantic meaning of words and phrases as vectors. If two
different phrases are semantically similar, as in “used realistic examples” and “gave real-
world scenarios” then the vector representations of these should be close to each other in
that high-dimensional vector space even though the two strings share no words in common.
Working in this vector space allows us to compare the meaning of words and phrases using
mathematical operations such as cosine similarity.
Cosine similarity measures the angle
between two vectors in a high-dimensional vector space [63].
If the angle between two
vectors is small, then the cosine similarity is high, and if the angle between two vectors is
large, then the cosine similarity is low. In text analysis, we can use cosine similarity to
compare the meaning of two phrases. If the cosine similarity between two phrases is high,
then the meaning of the two phrases is similar. If the cosine similarity between two phrases
is low, then the meaning of the two phrases is different. This contrasts older text analysis
methods, such as bag-of-words, which do not capture the semantic meaning of words and
phrases but rely on the presence or absence of words [64]. In that setting, “used realistic
examples” and “gave real-world scenarios” would be treated as completely different phrases
because they have different words, even though they are semantically similar.
In addition to the ability to calculate semantic similarity between pairs of texts, the em-
bedding model also allows us to calculate the similarity between a single text and a set of
texts, as one does when using clustering algorithms. Indeed, clustering is the next step in
the EECS workflow because once we have the vector representations of the extracted ideas,
we have a new problem: how do we find similar ideas so that we can reduce the information
required to represent the SETs into something far smaller, i.e., on the order of 50-100 pieces
of information. An alternative way to frame this problem is similar to principal component
analysis and the idea of finding the dimensions that explain the most variance in a dataset.
In this case, we want to find the dimensions that explain the most variance in the text of
extracted ideas. Clustering can help us step in that direction by finding groups of similar
ideas.
7
3.3
Clustering Embedded Ideas Representations
The next step in the EECS workflow is to cluster the extracted ideas. This is important
because there are many instances of students commenting on similar ideas, e.g., “great notes”
or “the notes added online were very helpful”. To find the unique themes within the SETs,
the extracted ideas must be collapsed into a more manageable number of observations.
This can be accomplished using a clustering algorithm, which groups similar ideas.
We
used the clustering algorithm called hierarchical density-based spatial for applications with
noise (HDBSCAN), which was implemented using the hdbscan Python library [65]. While
clustering, we used conservative parameter settings to encourage many small clusters. The
goal was to have many clusters, each with a few ideas (i.e., ideally one). This was done
to ensure that when we summarize the clusters later, we have a small number of ideas per
cluster to summarize.
3.3.1
Finding Representative of Each Cluster
With the clusters created, we now want to find a single representative member to pass to the
next step in the workflow. For example, if we clustered 5,000 pieces of extracted information
and formed 400 clusters, then we want 400 representatives - one for each cluster - to pass
to the generative model. The goal is to have a more manageable quantity of information
for the model to remember in its prompt. In essence, this step compresses 5,000 pieces of
information to 400, assuming each cluster contains a large amount of redundant information.
Finding a representative reduces this redundancy without losing information.
To identify a representative, we first embedded a concatenated version of the ideas in each
cluster.
For example, if a cluster contained entries such as “helpful office hours”, “lots
of office hours”, and “good office hours”, we could combine them into a single string like
“helpful office hours lots of office hours good office hours”, that we can then embed. These
cluster embeddings were then compared with the embeddings for the original extracted
information to find the most similar piece of original extracted information. We used the
same embedding model as in the prior embedding step and calculated the cosine similarity
between the embeddings for each cluster and the embeddings for the original extracted
information.
Put another way, this representative identification step selects the extracted information with
the highest cosine similarity to the cluster embedding. This “most similar“ idea is assigned
as the representative of that cluster. Let’s imagine that the 295th cluster had grouped six
pieces of extracted information. We concatenate those six ideas into a single string, which we
then embed. We then calculate the cosine similarity between the embedding for that string
and the embeddings for the extracted information from all the 5,000 original documents.
Finally, we select the extracted information that had the highest cosine similarity with the
cluster embedding and assign it as the cluster’s representative. That representative is passed
to the next step in the EECS workflow, summarization.
8
3.4
Summarizing the Clusters
Two more steps remain in the EECS workflow: creating the initial codebook (done in this
summarization step) and simplifying it to remove redundancies and improve its utility. In
the last step, we found representatives for each cluster that encapsulates its main themes.
Now, the representatives are sequentially given to a generative text model with instructions
to summarize it. The aim is to create an initial qualitative codebook with the most common
themes in the SETs. This codebook can then be simplified and used in downstream analyses
to understand the prevalence of the themes in the SETs. The codebook application step is
beyond the scope of this paper and will be described in future work. However, to generate
the codebook, we can use the prompt given in the Appendix, section 8.2.
This initial generative step is part of a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline. One
challenge in generating a codebook is that excessive entries are found, with many being
redundant. Consequently, the prompt included instructions for the model to reference an
existing codebook (i.e., the one we are inductively creating), and decide whether the extant
codes cover the theme(s) in the new text under analysis. If not, a new code entry is warranted
because the existing codes are insufficient.
While it might seem logical to provide the entire codebook to the model as part of the
prompt, our practical experience has shown that it’s more effective to offer the top k codes
from the codebook. By “top k codes,” we refer to the k most probable codes based on
semantic similarity between the cluster text and existing codes. This approach reduces the
memory requirements on the model; rather than memorizing the entire codebook, the model
only needs to recall the top k codes. This is a tradeoff because the model may not always
make an informed decision about the necessity of a new code if it only has the top k codes.
However, in our experiments, the model demonstrated the ability to make such informed
decisions, as evidenced by the reasoning steps it provided in the output.
To determine the top k codes, we embed each entry in the codebook using the same embed-
ding model as in the prior steps and calculate the cosine similarity between the embeddings
for each codebook entry and the embeddings for the cluster representative. Subsequently,
we select the top k entries in the codebook with the highest cosine similarity with the cluster
representative embedding as the top k codes. For instance, if the new cluster representative
is “gave lots of examples in class,” the top k codes might include “in-class demonstrations,”
“real-life scenarios,” and “helpful lectures.” These codes are then included in the prompt
provided to the model.
In response to the prompt (detailed in the Appendix, section 8.2), the model generates a
summary of the cluster with a code, definition, and example if it determines that a new entry
in the codebook is necessary. We subsequently extract the generated text in a short post-
processing step. Ultimately, the text generated after the prompt serves as the codebook
entry, which is utilized in the subsequent simplification step. Despite its complexity, the
process is conceptually straightforward: create a codebook by examining a representative
sample of extracted ideas, while striving to keep it concise by adding new entries only when
essential. This necessity is determined by evaluating the existing content in the codebook.
In essence, the EECS workflow mirrors traditional qualitative data analysis practices.
9
3.5
Simplifying the Codebook
The concluding step in the EECS workflow involves the simplification of the codebook.
This step is crucial as the initial codebook generated can often be lengthy and redundant,
potentially complicating its utility in subsequent analyses. The aim is to streamline the
codebook for easier application in downstream processes.
We employ a generative text model utilizing a prompt outlined in the Appendix, as detailed
in section 8.4. The design of this prompt ensures its adaptability for diverse document types,
enabling the use of the same generative text model across various contexts. In practice, the
model generates text based on the provided prompt and the original codebook. Subsequently,
we extract the generated text, constituting the final version of the inductively constructed
codebook. This approach ensures that the codebook is distilled to its essence, facilitating
its practical application in subsequent analyses.

5
Discussion
5.1
Comparison to Traditional Codebook Generation
These results highlight several important points. First, the EECS workflow can replicate the
process of codebook generation in a traditional qualitative data analysis setting. Not only
did the process reproduce codes from traditional analysis but also it identified new codes with
more granularity than the original codebook. These new codes may be due to the model’s
ability to identify more subtle differences in the data than a human coder. Alternatively,
this may be because we can simply analyze more data. Whereas the original analyses only
looked at a few hundred SETs, the EECS workflow was able to analyze over 4,500 unique
SETs. This is a significant increase in the amount of data that can be analyzed and may
have led to the identification of new codes because something that barely showed up in the
original analyses was now able to be detected when there were more data to consider.
15
Second, as mentioned in the Methods section, the EECS workflow is designed to be scalable.
This is important because the process can be applied to a large corpus of documents. In this
case, we used a relatively small corpus of a few thousand SETs, but in theory, the workflow
can be applied to a corpus much larger, depending on computing resources and the nature of
the text units of analysis. One area for future exploration is the extent to which the workflow
can be used with longer texts in less constrained settings. For example, the workflow could
be used to analyze student essays, research articles, and administrative records. Compared
to those alternative data types, SETs are relatively short and constrained, so it is an open
question whether the workflow would be as effective in those other settings.
5.2
Comparison to Other NLP Methods
While the EECS workflow was able to replicate and extend human coding efforts, it is impor-
tant to consider the workflow’s performance in comparison with other popular approaches to
computer-assisted thematic analysis. One popular method for analyzing text data is topic
modeling. Topic models are statistical models used to identify topics in a collection of doc-
uments. One of the most popular topic modeling algorithms is latent Dirichlet allocation
(LDA) [70]. The LDA model is a generative probabilistic model that assumes that each
document is a mixture of topics and that each word in the document is attributable to one
of the document’s topics. The goal of an LDA model is similar to the goal in the EECS
workflow: identify the latent topics that are giving rise to the observed words in each unit
of analysis (e.g., document, essay, article, SET). In practice, an LDA model is trained on a
corpus of documents and then used to identify the topics in the documents. One advantage
of an LDA model is its quick training - LDA models can be trained in a matter of minutes.
In contrast, the EECS workflow can take hours to run depending on corpus size and compu-
tation resources. Another comparative advantage of topic models is that there are specific
metrics one can use to determine the number of topics to extract from the data [71]. The
EECS workflow does not have such a metric at the moment and instead relies on the user
to determine the number of clusters to extract from the data.
On the other hand, the EECS workflow has several advantages. One big advantage is that
the EECS method relies more on semantics than syntax. Typical topic models rely on word
stems so words like “homework” and “assignments” are treated differently even though
one may want to consider them similar. More modern variations of topic models such as
BERTopic [72] attempt to address this limitation of traditional topic models, to limited
degrees of success. Another advantage of the EECS workflow is the generation of coherent
labels. Often, in traditional topic model implementations, a researcher is given a list of the
most frequent words in each topic and then left to determine what that topic might be about.
Such divination can yield fuzziness in the eventual results. In contrast, the EECS workflow
can not only generate coherent labels but also accompanying definitions.
BERTTopic harnesses the power of transformer-based models, the cutting-edge artificial
neural network architecture that has revolutionized many NLP tasks [30]. Introduced by
Vaswani et al. in their seminal paper [73], transformers have rapidly become the foundation
of state-of-the-art NLP models due to their remarkable ability to capture long-range depen-
16
dencies in sequential data like sentences [30]. By weighing the importance of different words
in a sequence and processing data in parallel, transformers can learn intricate patterns in
language, making them invaluable for NLP applications [10, 30].
Our method, like BERTTopic, employs transformer-based models for the codebook gener-
ation process. However, we are not unique in recognizing the potential of transformers for
NLP-based qualitative thematic analysis. Ganesh et al. [17] introduced the response con-
struct tagging (RCT) task to analyze students’ open-ended responses from course surveys,
focusing on identifying affective states related to transformative experiences and engineering
identity development. Their work showcased the scalability and value of transformer models
like RoBERTa [74] for qualitative coding in educational research contexts.
Similarly, Rahman et al. [75] used transformer-based NLP to extract the required competen-
cies for software testing professionals from online job postings. By comparing these themes
to course syllabi from 20 universities, they found gaps between employer-valued skills and
software testing curricula. This research highlights NLP’s scalability and utility for analyzing
unstructured data and its synergy with traditional thematic analysis techniques.
Beyond theme extraction, Wang et al. [76] explored using ChatGPT to classify YouTube
comments on the MIT OpenCourseWare (OCW) channel. The high correlation between hu-
man inter-rater reliability and human-model agreement across categories suggests that LLMs
mirror human judgment variability in annotation tasks. This work underscores the insights
derived by combining NLP with traditional qualitative methods for large, unstructured text
data in education [75].
While these investigations demonstrated NLP’s supportive role in traditional thematic anal-
ysis, Tai et al.
[23] proposed a novel methodology using LLMs for deductive coding in
qualitative research. Parallel to our research group’s work [50, 77], they developed a deduc-
tive coding scheme with five predefined codes related to scientific identity and input sample
interview excerpts and the codebook into ChatGPT to detect code presence and supporting
evidence. Comparing the results to human coding, the authors found high alignment for
most codes. Interestingly, areas of divergence prompted refinements in code definitions and
applications, showcasing how LLMs can serve as external reviewers to identify potential blind
spots, a finding we also observed. Transformer-based NLP techniques have shown tremen-
dous potential in assisting and guiding qualitative thematic analysis, but research thus far
has been limited to a few approaches and has not ventured into inductive thematic analysis
as our method proposes.
Accordingly, there are several potential reasons our inductive process works. To start, the
initial information extraction step helps to standardize language and disentangle complex
ideas into simpler, singular ideas. This is important because it allows us to analyze each
idea separately in subsequent steps. Without doing this, we would have to analyze the entire
SETs at once, which would be a much more difficult task. Additionally, the embedding step
further helps funnel information into standardized formats because it captures the semantic
meaning of words and phrases. Therefore, we have a way to move from the varied original
text into an abstract space of ideas. Prior methods using dictionary-based approaches simply
were not able to do this. Next, the dimension reduction and clustering steps help to reduce
the information even more than the prior steps. The result is to have a funnel that takes in
17
a large amount of varied information and outputs a small number of themes.
5.3
Humans-in-the-Loop
While our methodology is largely automated and programmatic, human involvement is in-
dispensable. Human expertise and judgment play a pivotal role throughout the process,
ensuring the credibility of the analysis and validating the outputs. For instance, employing
the SETs, our method generated 80 total codes, but five were discarded due to ambiguity or
irrelevance. One such code, “lack of specific feedback,” emerged from instances where stu-
dents either provided “No comment” or left the SET item blank. The EECS process yields a
codebook summarizing the themes and content of the input data, yet it may include themes
and content that are invalid, incoherent, or misaligned with research objectives. We advocate
for researchers to actively engage in the codebook generation process, identifying irrelevant
or misleading codes, manually reviewing and rejecting unnecessary ones, and verifying the
overall quality and validity of the automated analysis.
Code quality also pertains to its usefulness. While the EECS process generates a codebook,
the workflow by default is agnostic about how codes will be utilized - a result of embedding,
clustering, and prompt designs. Hence, researchers must assess the relevance and applica-
bility of generated codes to research questions, considering the required level of granularity
and specificity for meaningful analysis. Establishing criteria for determining the saturation
point in code generation is advised, as this varies between investigations. Researchers must
monitor the codebook to identify when no new themes or insights emerge from the data. In
our case, we observed that the model began outputting overly vague codes, signaling the end
of the generation process. Balancing code granularity and manageability is crucial, as codes
become simpler with each iteration. Consideration for potential consolidation or hierarchical
organization during code review can help conclude the generation process while maintaining
an ideal level of granularity.
The prompts used in the EECS process are detailed in the Appendix and are specifically
tailored for this application. If applying this process to a different context, crafting context-
specific prompts is essential to guide the NLP model effectively. Prompt design is iterative
and should reflect the quality and relevance of generated codes. Balancing specificity and
flexibility in prompt design is crucial to avoid scope limitations or researcher biases [78, 79].
Additionally, there is potential for this method to be adapted to fine-tuned LLMs trained on
domain-specific datasets, potentially enhancing accuracy, relevance, and efficiency [80]. How-
ever, fine-tuning poses challenges such as computational resources, model interpretability,
and ethical considerations, which should also be considered.
The bottom line is this: the EECS workflow works in general. Yet, to maximize utility,
researchers should actively participate in the codebook generation process, from assessing
code quality and utility to designing prompts and selecting models. This method is inherently
reflective, requiring researchers to play an active role in code generation and analysis rather
than passively relying on method outputs.
18
5.4
Limitations
The current workflow presents several limitations, some of which suggest areas for future
exploration.
Firstly, granularity levels within the codebook pose a challenge.
Instances
such as having separate codes for “in-class demonstrations” and “used experiments to show
ideas” may exhibit semantic similarity, suggesting potential consolidation into a single code.
Similarly, codes like “clarity” and “clarity on syllabus” could be collapsed or treated hi-
erarchically, especially if clarity is evident in other aspects of the course. Addressing this
challenge is a focus for future work, necessitating the development of methods to program-
matically handle semantically similar codes, including refining techniques for consolidation
and hierarchical treatment.
Secondly, the efficacy of codebook generation heavily relies on the models employed in the
process. As models evolve, ongoing evaluation of the method’s efficacy and accuracy, partic-
ularly with diverse datasets, is essential. Variations in model size and fine-tuning techniques
can significantly impact outcomes, requiring thorough evaluation across different models and
datasets to ensure robustness and reliability.
Thirdly, the interpretation of codes exhibits context dependency. For instance, the interpre-
tation of “scaffolding” may vary when analyzing SET data compared to comments from a
construction site. Exploring methods to incorporate context into the codebook generation
process, whether through prompt adjustments or alternative approaches, is an avenue for
future investigation.
Finally, while the process mimics human coding efforts, its superiority over alternative
methodologies remains uncertain. Alternative approaches may exist, utilizing different prompts,
variations in the RAG pipeline implementation, or alternative methods of codebook simplifi-
cation. Moreover, advancements in model architectures, such as selective state space models,
may mitigate context window limitations and introduce novel possibilities. While our work
demonstrates the potential of using generative text models and modern NLP techniques with
open-source options to replicate human coding processes efficiently and at scale, there are
diverse avenues for exploration in designing processes for document analysis. We encourage
further research to build upon this foundation, facilitating a range of options for document
analysis across various fields.
6
Conclusion
Recent advances in NLP and LLMs promise opportunities to analyze text data in new ways.
In this paper, we have discussed a new analytic method for such analysis. By combining
information extraction, text embedding, clustering, and summarizing, we have shown that
it is possible to identify themes in a large corpus of documents without the typical time
and labor costs associated with manual coding. The process we have described is scalable
and can be applied to create a codebook that can be used in downstream analyses. We
believe that this method has the potential to be useful for analyzing SETs and other types
of student writing in teaching and research settings. We also believe that this method has
19
the potential to be useful for analyzing other types of documents, such as student essays,
research articles, and administrative records. We hope that this paper will inspire others to
explore the possibilities of NLP and LLMs for analyzing documents in their fields.
