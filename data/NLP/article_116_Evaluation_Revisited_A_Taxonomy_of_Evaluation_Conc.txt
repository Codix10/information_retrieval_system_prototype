Title: Evaluation Revisited: A Taxonomy of Evaluation Concerns in Natural Language Processing
Source URL: http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25923v1
--------------------------------------------------

Evaluation Revisited:
A Taxonomy of Evaluation Concerns in Natural Language Processing
Ruchira Dhar †
Anders Søgaard †
†Department of Computer Science, University of Copenhagen
Abstract
Recent advances in large language models
(LLMs) have prompted a growing body of work
that questions the methodology of prevailing
evaluation practices. However, many such cri-
tiques have already been extensively debated
in natural language processing (NLP): a field
with a long history of methodological reflection
on evaluation. We conduct a scoping review
of research on evaluation concerns in NLP and
develop a taxonomy, synthesizing recurring po-
sitions and trade-offs within each area. We also
discuss practical implications of the taxonomy,
including a structured checklist to support more
deliberate evaluation design and interpretation.
By situating contemporary debates within their
historical context, this work provides a consoli-
dated reference for reasoning about evaluation
practices.
1
Introduction
The rise of performant large language models
(LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020;
Min et al., 2023; Naveed et al., 2025) has been
accompanied by a surge of studies focused on
evaluation (Srivastava et al., 2023; Bommasani
et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2024; Deshpande et al.,
2025; Kazemi et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). As
LLMs exhausted many widely used benchmarks,
researchers began to question what evaluation re-
sults actually indicate (McIntosh et al.; Wallach
et al.; Wei et al.), what kind of claims they support
about model capabilities (Reuel-Lamparth et al.,
2024; Szymanski et al.; Zhou et al.; Singh et al.,
2025), and how reliably they indicate performance
(Eriksson et al.; Weidinger et al., 2025; Rane et al.,
2025; Zhuang et al., 2025). Evaluation has become
a central theme in contemporary LLM research.
Evaluation is not a new concern, however. The
wider field of natural language processing (NLP)
has seen many methodological debates over the

years (Guida and Mauri; Cohen and Howe, 1988;
Jones, 1994; Church and Hestness). Unsurprisingly,
many of the issues highlighted in recent work, were
also discussed decades earlier, but much of this
history is often neglected. Earlier debates, often
relying on slightly different terminology or moti-
vated by different empirical phenomena, are rarely
cited by contemporary work. Ignoring this history
runs the risk of reinventing the wheel.
Our motivation for this work is twofold. First,
we aim to provide a historically grounded reference
for researchers seeking to understand how evalu-
ation debates have shaped NLP, and how current
discussions relate to earlier work. Second, we in-
tend the taxonomy to serve as a practical guide for
model evaluators and benchmark designers, sup-
porting more deliberate evaluation design by mak-
ing underlying assumptions and recurring trade-
offs explicit. By consolidating long-standing evalu-
ation concerns into a coherent structure, this work
seeks to support cumulative progress in evaluation
research, helping the field build on prior insights
rather than encountering them anew in each gener-
ation of models.
2
Methodological Framework
Literature Review
We begin with a scoping re-
view (Pham et al., 2014; Peters et al., 2021) of
prior work that critically examines limitations and
concerns in the evaluation of NLP systems. Can-
didate papers were retrieved through a structured
keyword-based search over the ACL Anthology
and broader ML venues via Semantic Scholar, fol-
lowed by a snowball sampling from citations (John-
son, 2014) and manual screening. Papers proposing
new evaluation artifacts without sustained critique
were excluded. The final corpus comprises 257 pa-
pers published between 1981 and 2024. Full details
of the search, screening, and inclusion criteria are
provided in Appendix A.

Taxonomy Construction
Following corpus com-
pilation, we conducted an iterative qualitative syn-
thesis to derive a structured taxonomy of evaluation
concerns. Four higher-level dimensions emerged as
recurrent organizing axes across the literature: data,
metrics, hypotheses, and reporting practices. These
dimensions capture distinct yet interrelated aspects
of evaluation design and interpretation, rather than
mutually exclusive categories. We refined category
boundaries to maximize explanatory clarity and
analytical coverage, prioritizing dimensions that
account for sustained patterns of concern across
subfields and time periods. In the next sections,
we examine each area in turn, summarizing the
dominant concerns and recurring positions iden-
tified in the literature, and highlighting points of
convergence and disagreement where they arise.
As we discuss each dominant area, we also in-
clude short notes on how recent research keeps
rediscovering these issues — emphasizing the his-
torical continuity of evaluation concerns.
3
Data Concerns
Many critiques of evaluation ultimately trace back
to the data used for evaluating systems. In this
subsection, we organize data-related evaluation
concerns into four high-level categories, reflect-
ing distinct ways in which data can impact model
evaluation.
3.1
Dataset Quality
The first set of concerns examines the quality of
data used to evaluate models- how can we ensure
the data is good? What factors can create problems
in the data?
Construct Validity
The work in this area high-
lights mismatches between the theoretical con-
structs data claims to evaluate and the actual behav-
iors rewarded by the evaluation process (Wagstaff,
2012; Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020; Liao et al., 2021;
Mannarswamy and Chidambaram, 2021; Subramo-
nian et al., 2023; Vida et al., 2023; Fierro et al.,
2024; Reuel-Lamparth et al., 2024). This issue
of leaving the task under constrained has been in-
vestigated to have impacts across different NLP
tasks including structured generation (Finegan-
Dollak et al., 2018), machine translation (Belinkov
and Bisk, 2018), summarization (Kryscinski et al.,
2019), syntactic parsing (Tedeschi et al., 2023;
Martínez et al., 2023), bias exploration (Blod-
gett et al., 2021) etc.
More recent work has
also explored ways of tracking data provenance
(Longjohn et al., 2024), principled dataset construc-
tion (Ribeiro et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021; Bhardwaj
et al., 2024; Saxon et al., 2024; Longjohn et al.,
2024), or use of psychometrics for improving valid-
ity of test data (Lalor et al., 2016; Martínez-Plumed
et al., 2019; Sedoc and Ungar, 2020; Yancey et al.,
2024).

2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Hofmann et al., 2025).

Gold standard data has long
been used for evaluation of NLP systems, yet sev-

Artifact Issues
Evaluation datasets often contain
systematic artifacts that models can exploit, leading
to inflated performance without genuine task un-
derstanding. While earlier work has discussed how
specific feature patterns can create false impres-
sions of success (Globerson and Roweis), more
recent works explore the problem of biases and

ways to mitigate such effects, specifically in the
context of NLU models (McCoy et al., 2019; Clark
et al., 2019; Du et al., 2021).
Contamination Issues
With rise of pretrained
language models, another recent issues is that of
data contamination (Sainz et al., 2023; Oren et al.,
2024; de Wynter, 2025). This refers to when data
already used for training is used for evaluation,
leading to false promises of performance. Some
recent work has considered how to improve collec-
tion and sharing practices to mitigate such effects
(Paullada et al., 2021; Hutchinson et al., 2021).
3.2
Dataset Distribution
Another area of concern has been the distributions
represented by evaluation data, especially in the
context of implicit assumption often made about
how test data should relate to training data and to
real-world inputs.
Test-Train Splitting
Textual data rarely satisfies
IID assumptions, even though many evaluation pro-
tocols implicitly rely on them. Early works have
pointed out that splitting data with IID assumptions
can be problematic for natural language systems
such as overly optimistic estimates of generaliza-
tion (Karimi et al., 2015). Other works show how
the construction of train and test sets should be
nuanced, including issues surrounding use of stan-
dard splits (Gorman and Bedrick, 2019; van der
Goot, 2021; Nie et al., 2022) or even random splits
(Szyma´nski and Gorman, 2020; Søgaard et al.).
This line of work foregrounds data splitting as a
methodological choice with direct consequences
for evaluation validity.
IID vs. Adversarial Data
A recurring concern
in the literature has been the question of whether
IID data is better for evaluation (Jones, 1994; Bow-
man and Dahl, 2021) or if we need adversarial data
(Attenberg et al.; Isabelle et al., 2017; Søgaard,
2017). The latter view is supported by work on ad-
versarial NLI evaluation (Naik et al., 2018; Wallace
et al., 2019; McCoy et al., 2019), which shows that
models achieving high benchmark scores on IID
data can fail under carefully constructed counterex-
amples. Other works show further demonstrate
substantial drops in model accuracy under adversar-
ial testing Mudrakarta et al. (2018), also via input
perturbation (Moradi and Samwald, 2021). The
use of adversarial tests allows for better error anal-
ysis (Rajpurkar et al., 2018) and enables greater
insight into granular model capabilities (Ribeiro

Natural vs. OOD Data
Another tension in the
literature concerns whether evaluation data should
prioritize realistic and representative coverage of
intended natural deployment settings or deliber-
ately include out-of-distribution (OOD) conditions
to test model behavior. Early work emphasizes that
evaluation datasets must be realistic, representative,
and legitimate, cautioning against inducing any
such bases (Sparck Jones, 1994; Siska et al., 2024).
More recent analyses show that dataset selection
practices can bias evaluation toward particular do-
mains or notions of “high-quality” data (Gururan-
gan et al., 2022) and argue for explicitly measur-
ing and enforcing diversity in evaluation datasets
for naturalness (Zhao et al., 2024; Kovatchev and
Lease, 2024). In contrast, a substantial body of
work highlights that strong in-domain performance
often fails to generalize beyond the evaluation dis-
tribution (Gardner et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022; Yuan
et al., 2023; Agrawal et al., 2023; Wang et al.). To-
gether, these works suggest that a tradeoff must
be navigated: evaluation limited to narrow or ho-
mogeneous distributions may overestimate model
capability, while overly controlled realism may ob-
scure robustness weaknesses.
3.3
Dataset Assumptions
This category captures a line of work that inter-
rogates what conclusions evaluation data can le-
gitimately support. Rather than focusing on spe-
cific dataset pathologies, these papers examine the
assumptions underlying benchmark-driven evalua-
tion and the kinds of scientific claims such evalu-
ation is taken to justify. Across the literature, this
discussion is often articulated through a contrast
between empirically driven approaches that empha-
size scale and quantitative performance, and more
rationalist approaches that prioritize explanation
and diagnostic insight.
Empirical Stance
Work adopting an empirical
stance treats evaluation data as a primary source
of evidence for model progress, emphasizing large-
scale datasets, quantitative rigor, and comparability
across systems (Church, 2011; Wang et al., 2019;
Bommasani et al., 2023). Other works in the area
have also considered the way large datasets and
benchmarks can be improved for better evaluation
of systems (Kiela et al., 2021; Bowman and Dahl,
2021; Saxon et al., 2024).
Rational Stance
In contrast, a rationalist stance
emphasizes evaluation as a tool for testing hypothe-
ses and gaining explanatory insight into model be-
havior. Some works argue that evaluation data
should be designed and interpreted in relation to ex-
plicit theoretical commitments, rather than treated
as neutral measurement instruments (Manning,
2011; Gehrmann et al., 2023), some warn against
conflating measurement with understanding, par-
ticularly when aggregate performance numbers are
taken as sufficient evidence of progress (Church; ?;
Church and Hestness; Schlangen, 2021). More re-
cent work has also explored issues surrounding the
validity of benchmark data-based evaluation (Koch
et al., 2021; Raji et al., 2020). Evaluation data
is thus treated as evidence in support of specific
claims, rather than as a comprehensive measure of
capability.

tasks and benchmarks are being introduced (Eriks-

Metric Concerns
We organize metric-related evaluation concerns
into four high-level categories, reflecting distinct
ways in which metrics shape, and sometimes dis-
tort, the interpretation of model performance.
4.1
Metric Validity
A recurring theme across the literature is that
widely adopted metrics often abstract away from
the underlying phenomena of interest, leading to
evaluations that are formally precise but substan-
tively misaligned with real-world or theoretical
goals.
Downstream Validity
It refers to concerns on
whether improvements in a metric correspond to
improvements in the system’s actual end-task per-
formance or practical utility. The question of down-
stream validity of a metric has been repeatedly
raised in different subfields of NLP- starting from
parsing (Headden III et al., 2008) to summarization
(Fum et al., 1985; Radev et al., 2003; Fabbri et al.,
2021), dialogue processing (Walker, 1989; Walker
et al., 2001; Dybkjær and Bernsen, 2001; Dziri
et al., 2019; Corbelle et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2024),
classification (Agarwal, 1995; Powers, 2011; Plaud
et al., 2024), generation (Bangalore et al., 2000;
Reiter and Belz, 2009; Mathur et al., 2020; Rony
et al., 2022), bias judgements (Sheng et al., 2021;
Han et al., 2023; Belém et al., 2024), or robustness
(Yu et al., 2022). As a result, works have suggested
that choosing metrics should be based on: differ-
ent sub-tasks of a system (Sparck Jones, 1994),
different task complexities (Thomas, 1999), mod-
ular vs global performance (Berthelin et al., 2001;
Giménez and Màrquez, 2009), alignment between
task and metric assumptions (Di Eugenio and Glass,
2004), interpretability considerations (McCowan
et al., 2004), or user satisfaction (Hajdinjak and
Mihelic, 2006; Callison-Burch et al., 2006; Tahri
et al., 2023). Recent works have also attempted to
come up with methods for validity judgment (Xiao
et al., 2023) and principled selection of metrics
(Flach, 2019; Thornton, 2023; Xia et al., 2024).
Linguistic Validity
Several works argue that
standard metrics oversimplify complex linguis-
tic judgments: Walker (1989) explores necessity
of qualitative linguistic evaluation, Plank et al.
(2014b) critiques how annotator agreement obscure
genuine linguistic ambiguity, Feng et al. shows
that general-purpose metrics often fail to capture
diagnostic differences between models that rely on
linguistically different mechanisms, and Forde et al.
(2024) shows how metrics can fail in multilingual
settings. Some studies have shown how metrics can
be made more relevant by the inclusion of linguis-
tic information like syntax (Liu and Gildea, 2005;
Padó et al., 2009) or discourse structure (Guzmán
et al., 2014; Muller et al., 2014), while others have
argued that the insight-computational cost trade-
offs of such deeper metrics are not always clear
(Mesquita et al., 2013).
Human Evaluation
The question of how valid
human evaluation is as a metric has long been a
concern in NLP (Howcroft et al., 2020; Clark et al.,
2021; Karpinska et al., 2021; Sottana et al., 2023;
van Miltenburg et al., 2023; Ito et al., 2023; Elan-
govan et al., 2024; Riley et al., 2024; Thomson
et al., 2024), including specific issues around using
solo evaluators (Tetreault and Chodorow, 2008),
problematic correlations between automated and
human evals (Callison-Burch et al., 2007; Chiang
et al., 2008; Graham, 2015; Chaganty et al., 2018;
Chiang and Lee, 2023b), or the opacity of implicit
factors affecting human ratings (Callison-Burch
et al., 2008; Hashimoto et al., 2019; Zhang et al.,
2024). Many works suggest the need for further re-
search into agreement metrics (Amidei et al., 2019),
better rating scales (Belz and Kow, 2011) or fine-
grained guidelines for evaluators (Amigó et al.,
2006; van der Lee et al., 2019; Belz et al., 2020;
Hämäläinen and Alnajjar, 2021; Briakou et al.,
2021; Krishna et al., 2023) to overcome such is-
sues.
4.2
Metric Sensitivity
A second set of concerns focuses on how metrics
behave across tasks, datasets, and model classes,
and on their sensitivity to properties of the evalua-
tion setting.
Data Sensitivity
Several papers highlight that
metric behavior is highly data-dependent. Don-
away et al. (2000) shows how metrics can be highly
sensitive to gold-label data even under slight varia-
tions, Voorhees (2003a); Mathur et al. (2020) and
Lum et al. (2025) shows how precision is sensitive
to character length of output data, and Reichart and
Rappoport (2009) shows how information-theory
based measures depend on the dataset size. Bhan-
dari et al. (2020) show that the correlation between
metrics and human judgments varies significantly
across datasets. As a result, works have also ex-

Task Sensitivity
Many works have investigated
the task sensitivity of metrics Radev et al. (2003);
Babych et al. (2004); Thomson and Reiter (2021)
with significant implications such as obscuring the
desirable behaviours of systems (Lin and Demner-
Fushman, 2006) or invalidity of applying metrics
across tasks — for example, repurposing machine
translation metrics for dialogue evaluation—can
lead to misleading conclusions (Liu et al., 2016).
This concern is echoed by works who argue that
different systems require specialized metrics that
reflect properties not captured by generic measures
(Dziri et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022).
Model Sensitivity
Early works have shown how
metrics can be sensitive to the training objective
of models (Och, 2003), implicit model heuristics
(Belz and Reiter, 2006), model types Kasai et al.
(2022); Liu et al. (2024b), or model compression
(Dutta et al., 2024). More recently, this concern
has been seen in the use of LLMs as judges where
different evaluator LLMs have been shown to have
biases to specific model types (Chiang and Lee,
2023a; Liu et al., 2024a; Deutsch et al., 2022;
Zheng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Parcalabescu
and Frank, 2024; Oh et al., 2024).
A common suggestion has been to use principled
combinations of metrics (Giménez and Màrquez,
2008; Paek, 2001), correlation analysis between
metrics (Lin and Demner-Fushman, 2005; Conroy
and Dang, 2008; Graham and Baldwin, 2014), or
human evaluation correlations (Bangalore et al.,
2000; Elliott and Keller, 2014; Novikova et al.,
2017) for mitigating the effects of metric sensitivity
and gaining a better understanding of how metrics
can be interpreted.

are being increasingly reported for LLM evaluators
(Szymanski et al.; Chehbouni et al., 2025; Li et al.,

4.3
Metric Standardization
A final class of concerns examines how evalua-
tion metrics, once standardized and widely adopted,
shape research practices and incentives at the field
level (Sparck Jones, 1994; Bangalore et al., 2000;
Donaway et al., 2000). One familiar example is that
of BLEU- an NLG metric that was adopted popu-
larly at the time but was later shown to have distinct
disadvantages (Callison-Burch et al., 2006, 2007,
2008). A larger survey discovered that researchers
tended to favour specific evaluation methods based
on publication venues and lead to problems in de-
termining progress in the field (Gkatzia and Ma-
hamood, 2015). Many works have subsequently
warned the community of dangers in such prac-
tices without verifying the efficacy or usefulness of
metrics for specific data and tasks (Caglayan et al.,
2020; Schmidtova et al., 2024). Recent works like
(Reuel-Lamparth et al., 2024) therefore argue that
standardized metric scores should be contextual-
ized using floor, ceiling, and random baselines to
mitigate overinterpretation. Collectively, this lit-
erature suggests that metric standardization does
not merely facilitate comparison; it actively shapes
how progress is defined, incentivized, and commu-
nicated within the research community.
5
Hypothesis Concerns
Evaluation is rarely hypothesis-free: it encodes
assumptions about what constitutes improvement,
and what evidence is sufficient to support claims
about model quality. A recurring concern in the
literature is that these hypotheses are often under-
specified, mismatched with the evaluation setup, or
tested using inappropriate methodological tools.
5.1
Hypothesis Formulation
A first group of concerns focuses on whether evalu-
ation tasks meaningfully instantiate the hypotheses
they are intended to test.
Relevance of Hypothesis
The field has long de-
bated the question of how to formulate relevant
hypothesis about models for improving evaluations
(Thompson, 1981; Mann, 1981; Cohen and Howe,
1988; Hutchinson et al., 2022; Frank, 2023; Chang
et al., 2024). Many have suggested that evalua-
tion hypotheses should go beyond treating task
performance as a sufficient proxy of success and
consider: component-wise performance of the sys-
tem (Walker et al., 1997; Walter, 1998; Bernsen
and Dybkjær, 2000; Dybkjær and Bernsen, 2001;
Yuan et al., 2024) and user relevance of system
(Guida and Mauri; Whittaker and Stenton, 1989;
Sparck Jones, 1994). A suggestion has been to
adopt specific frameworks like the verification and
6
validation (V&V) approach for formulating rele-
vant hypothesis (Gonzalez and Barr, 2000; Barr
and Klavans, 2001).
Task-Specificity of Hypothesis
Several works
highlight that commonly adopted evaluation tasks
encode inappropriate hypotheses about model qual-
ity. Sahlgren (2006) and Schnabel et al. (2015) visit
these issues in the context of word embeddings,
arguing that embeddings cannot be evaluated as
“objectively good” outside of a task-specific hy-
pothesis. Glavaš et al. (2019) show that bilingual
lexicon induction is hypothesized as a proxy for em-
bedding quality despite being poorly aligned with
many downstream tasks. Blodgett et al. (2020) sur-
face these issues in bias measurement in NLP. More
recently, in the context of LMs, works like (Meis-
ter and Cotterell, 2021) have considered aligning
the evaluation hypothesis more precisely with the
task they were trained for (Meister and Cotterell).
These works underscore that evaluation outcomes
depend critically on how “better” is defined.
5.2
Hypothesis Testing
A second group of concerns examines how hypothe-
ses about model improvement are tested, partic-
ularly through statistical significance testing and
related inferential methods.
Reliability of Statistical Tests
Early works have
shown that determining whether observed differ-
ences in model performance reflect genuine im-
provements depends on careful selection of sta-
tistical tests, and that inappropriate testing can
lead to misleading conclusions (Dietterich, 1998;
Demšar, 2006; Yeh, 2000). Several test method-
ologies have been explored that can help improve
results for significance testing in NLP: bootstrap
resampling (Koehn, 2004), controlled replications
for optimizer instability (Clark et al., 2011), ran-
domized significance tests (Graham et al., 2014)
and careful selection of statistical tests (Dror et al.,
2018, 2020).
Parameters of Statistical Tests
Several papers
have question whether standard parameters of sig-
nificance testing practices are sufficient for NLP
evaluation.
Some have argued for careful con-
sideration towards using more conventional p-
value thresholds tailored to NLP tasks (Riezler and
Maxwell, 2005; ?), improving techniques for power
analysis (Koller et al., 2009; Card et al., 2020) and
effect size determination (Søgaard, 2013) of experi-
ments , and incorporating factors like test set distri-
bution in parameter estimation (Berg-Kirkpatrick
et al., 2012).
Such concerns have led to work on trying to sug-
gest possible best practices for hypothesis testing
in NLP(Dror et al., 2018, 2020; Simpson, 2021).
5.3
Model Comparison
Beyond hypothesis formulation and testing, several
papers focus on how evaluation is used to compare
models and to justify claims that one system is
better than another.
Experimental Design
The question of meaning-
ful comparison of models is not a trivial one and
requires evaluation across multiple parameter set-
tings to establish relevance and robustness (Jones,
1994; Salzberg, 1997; Ng and Jordan, 2001; Arlot
and Celisse, 2010; Jansen et al., 2023). Works have
explored how lack of ablation studies across dif-
ferent model parameters can attribute gains to the
wrong factors (Klein and Manning, 2002; Voorhees,
2003b; Linzen, 2020) or how good baselines are
essential to judge task feasibility in the first place
(Berthelin et al., 2008). More recent works have
explored possible mitigation measures like the use
of sensitivity analysis (Jing et al., 1998; Berman
et al., 2024), complementarity scores (Derczyn-
ski, 2016), replicability analysis (Dror et al., 2017),
or the use of performance estimator models (Xue
et al., 2023).
Leaderboard Practices
The use of leaderboards
and model rankings is common in NLP as a way
to signpost progress, but can be misleading if not
done in a principled manner (Church, 2017; Church
and Hestness, 2019; Riezler and Hagmann, 2022;
Futrell, 2023). More specifically, leaderboards are
invalid without applying methods like significance
testing of different ranking methods (Evert, 2004),
statistical paired testing (Rankel et al., 2011), or
data-sensitive pipelines (Tschalzev et al., 2024)
prior to ranking. Hothorn et al. (2005) has pro-
vided a more general framework for improving in-
ference in benchmarking experiments while other
works have suggested alternatives like algorithm-
based rankings (Blum and Hardt, 2015; Kasai et al.,
2022), or the use of psychometrics-based methods
like item-response theory (IRT) (Rodriguez et al.,
2021). More recently, Laufer et al. (2023) also
explores how leaderboards embody complex inter-
actions that has strategic effects on society.
7
→In 2025:
With the increasing use of leader-
boards, practices around modle ranking are being
called into question again (Singh et al., 2025; Hu
et al., 2025).
6
Reporting Concerns
How evaluation findings are communicated mat-
ters: the literature surveyed here highlights that
omissions in reporting are not merely matters of
presentation, but can fundamentally undermine the
evidential value of evaluation.
6.1
Transparency Concerns
Evaluation reports often omit critical contextual
information needed to interpret results meaning-
fully. Early works have shown how reporting of
performance scores is insufficient for accurate con-
clusions on evaluations (Dodge et al., 2019; Palen-
Michel et al., 2021; Goldfarb-Tarrant et al., 2023)
and how omitting information on model size, com-
putational cost, and energy usage substantially low-
ers the utility of leaderboards (Ethayarajh and Ju-
rafsky, 2020). Burnell et al. (2023) similarly doc-
ument that many empirical studies fail to report
crucial experimental details, making it difficult
to assess the reliability or scope of reported im-
provements. More recently, works have proposed
frameworks for improving transparency of report-
ing based on responsibility (Bommasani, 2023; Liu
et al., 2023) and governance considerations (Reuel-
Lamparth et al., 2024).
→In 2025: Recent works have again brought up
issues of transparency in human baselines used in
evaluations of LLMs (Wei et al., 2025).
6.2
Reproducibility Concerns
Insufficient reporting and its impact on repro-
ducibility and comparison across studies has be-
come a significant issue in recent years (Belz et al.,
2021, 2023a; Belz and Thomson, 2024; Laskar
et al., 2024), along with significant impacts for
beginners in the field (Storks et al., 2023). This is
even more problematic in human evaluation stud-
ies which frequently omit essential methodological
details, such as annotator instructions, qualifica-
tion criteria, and aggregation procedures, making
replication difficult (Belz et al., 2023b). As a way
to overcome such issues, works have proposed
measures like reproducibility assessments (Belz
et al., 2022), software release guidelines (Papi et al.,
2024), and using interdisciplinary frameworks (Di-
gan et al., 2021; Belz, 2022).
7
Discussion: Implications for Evaluation
Practices
A taxonomic mapping of evaluation practices and
philosophies over time not only situate contem-
porary thinking, but also prevents us from rein-
venting the wheel. Scientific discoveries are often
re-articulations of older ones (Brown, 2019; Mattei,
2018), obscured by terminology drift (Buckwal-
ter, 2022), explosion of knowledge sources (Bent-
ley et al., 2022), or incentives that privilege nov-
elty over consolidation(Falkenberg, 2021). This
has also been observed for computational sciences
(Denning, 2013; Adalı et al., 2018; Sönning and
Werner, 2021). As evaluation becomes a key con-
cern in ML and NLP (Eriksson et al., 2025; Dietz
et al., 2025; Zhou et al.; Hu et al., 2025; Bean et al.,
2025; Rane et al., 2025), a structured resource of en-
during concerns in the field, such as this taxonomy,
can become valuable for the research community.
This taxonomy is not intended as a prescriptive
framework but rather can serve as a structured lens
for reasoning about evaluation design, adding value
in the following ways: First, as a consolidated ref-
erence for researchers in evaluations, it situates
current proposals within longer-standing debates
across four domains of data, metric, hypothesis and
reporting, clarifying which concerns persist and
how they have evolved across generations. Second,
it can support careful interpretation of evaluation
results, making explicit the assumptions and trade-
offs that have been debated by researchers while
helping analyze how different evaluation choices
can support different empirical claims. To further
support such interpretation, we operationalize the
taxonomy as a structured checklist (Figure 5), in-
tended for evaluators and benchmark designers.
The checklist surfaces questions based on our tax-
onomy that are often implicit in practice and pro-
vides a practical tool for anticipating common eval-
uation failure modes or questions that may surface
regarding their approach to various steps in the
evaluation process.
By consolidating recurring concerns into a co-
herent structure, the taxonomy aims to support cu-
mulative and more self-aware progress in evalu-
ation research. In doing so, it also clarifies the
methodological foundations upon which claims of
empirical progress rest.

Evaluation Checklist
Data Concerns
Dataset Quality
□Is construct validity of the benchmark established?
□Are gold standard reliability issues addressed?
□Are annotation artifacts identified and controlled?
□Has data contamination been assessed and miti-
gated?
Dataset Distribution
□Are train–test partitioning assumptions justified?
□Is the distributional nature (IID/adversarial) speci-
fied?
□Does the data reflect deployment-relevant diver-
sity?
□Is out-of-distribution generalization evaluated?
Dataset Assumptions
□Are empirical claims substantiated beyond aggregate benchmark scores?
□Is diagnostic or explanatory analysis included?
Metric Concerns
Metric Validity
□Does the metric correlate with downstream utility?
□Does the metric capture relevant linguistic phe-
nomena?
□Are established human evaluation protocols fol-
lowed?
Metric Sensitivity
□Is metric stability across datasets verified?
□Is task-domain appropriateness of the metric justi-
fied?
□Are model-specific metric biases accounted for?
Metric Standardization
□Is the selection of evaluation metrics justified?
□Are reference baselines (floor/ceiling/random) re-
ported?
Hypothesis Concerns
Hypothesis Formulation
□Is the evaluation hypothesis explicitly articulated?
□Is the task operationalization appropriate for the
hypothesis?
Hypothesis Testing
□Are statistical tests appropriately selected and ap-
plied?
□Is statistical power adequate for the claims made?
□Are significance thresholds and effect sizes re-
ported?
Model Comparison
□Are ablation studies and control conditions in-
cluded?
□Are ranking claims substantiated beyond raw score
differences?
Reporting Concerns
Transparency
□Are all experimental parameters and conditions
documented?
□Are computational resources (cost, energy, model
size) disclosed?
Reproducibility
□Are human evaluation protocols fully specified?
□Is sufficient detail provided to enable independent
replication?

Appendix A: Survey Methodology
We follow a scoping review methodology (Pham
et al., 2014), including papers until 2024.
We
identify candidate papers through a structured
keyword-based search over two primary sets of
bibliographic sources: ACL Anthology 1 and Se-
mantic Scholar2, enabling coverage of relevant
NLP work in broader ML venues (IEEE, AAAI,
ACM, ICML etc). For ACL Anthology, we ini-
tially we started with grammatical variations of
the keyword evaluation. For Semantic Scholar, we
conducted similar keyword search followed by fil-
tering for Fields of Study (Computer Science and
Linguistics), Date Range (1980-2024), PDF Avail-
ability (Yes), and appropriate journals. Keywords
were iteratively developed to reflect conceptual cat-
egories of evaluation critique, including variations
like evaluation, measurement,testing, benchmark,
etc. This yielded around 6000 papers in total which
were then screened by title or abstract via human
verification based on the following inclusion crite-
ria.
A paper was included if it satisfied all of the
following criteria:
IC1 Evaluation relevance: The paper explicitly
examines evaluation as a methodological con-
cern and addresses at least one of the fol-
lowing: limitations or biases in evaluation
practices; assumptions underlying evaluation
design (e.g., tasks, data, or metrics); misalign-
ment between evaluation results and model
behavior; or normative or methodological ar-
guments about evaluation.
IC2 Substantive focus: Evaluation concerns con-
stitute a central component of the paper’s con-
tribution, rather than a peripheral motivation
or discussion point.
A paper was excluded if its primary contribution
was the introduction of a new artifact without sus-
tained critical analysis of evaluation practices, or
if evaluation was discussed only incidentally. Bor-
derline cases were resolved conservatively, favor-
ing inclusion only when evaluation concerns were
clearly articulated and substantively developed.
This led to a corpus of 194 papers across ACL
and ML venues. The, we conducted citation-based
1https://aclanthology.org
2https://www.semanticscholar.org
snowball sampling to improve coverage. For pa-
pers retained after initial screening, we examined
backward citations i.e references cited within in-
cluded papers. Candidate papers identified through
snowballing were evaluated using same inclusion
criteria applied during the primary screening phase.
This process was intended to mitigate the risk of
omitting influential work that may not have been
captured through keyword search alone, particu-
larly in earlier periods or in papers employing dif-
ferent terminology for evaluation-related critique.
The final corpus consists of 257 papers (190
from ACL and rest from Semantic Scholar), span-
ning over four decades of research on evaluation
in NLP and machine learning from 1981 to 2024.
We acknowledge that this approach may miss rele-
vant work published outside these venues or papers
that do not explicitly use the selected keywords.
However, our goal is not to conduct an exhaustive
systematic review, but to develop a taxonomy of
approaches, characterize progress in this research
area.
